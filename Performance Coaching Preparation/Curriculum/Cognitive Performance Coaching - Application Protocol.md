## LEVEL 1 — FOUNDATION

---

### 1. Metacognitive Awareness `COACH-SAFE`

**Mechanism:** You are not your thoughts — you are the observer of them. Creating distance between self and thought content is the prerequisite for every other cognitive tool. Without this, clients engage techniques intellectually but don't actually apply them.

**Protocol:**
1. Introduce the observer stance: *"You are not the thought. You are the one noticing the thought."*
2. Teach three levels: event → automatic thought → thought about the thought
3. Thought labeling practice: Replace *"I'm going to fail"* with *"I'm having the thought that I'm going to fail"*
4. Daily 5-minute sit: Observe thoughts without engaging or suppressing. Count how many arise.
5. Weekly pattern audit: *"What themes keep appearing in my thinking?"*

**Session Application:** Open every session by asking what thought patterns the client noticed since last session. This builds the habit and generates session material.

**Homework:** Daily thought log — one sentence per significant thought pattern noticed. Morning, not evening — recall is better.

---

### 2. Cognitive Restructuring / Thought Records `⚠️ SUPERVISED`

**Why supervised:** Thought records surface the beliefs beneath surface thinking. Those beliefs are frequently rooted in early experience, trauma, or clinical-level depression. Screen first, supervise always.

**Mechanism:** Aaron Beck's foundational CBT tool. Making automatic thoughts explicit and testable breaks their automatic influence on behavior. Thoughts are hypotheses, not facts.

**Protocol — 7-Column Thought Record:**

| Column               | Question                                                    |
| -------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1. Situation         | What happened? When, where, who?                            |
| 2. Emotion           | What did you feel? Rate intensity 0–100%                    |
| 3. Automatic thought | What went through your mind? What does this mean about you? |
| 4. Evidence FOR      | What supports this thought being true?                      |
| 5. Evidence AGAINST  | What contradicts it? What are you ignoring?                 |
| 6. Balanced thought  | More accurate, complete perspective?                        |
| 7. Outcome           | Re-rate emotion. What action can you take now?              |

**Rules:**
- Column 4 before Column 5 — never skip to the counter-evidence
- Rate emotion before and after — the shift is the data
- Do not help clients fill it in — ask questions, let them write

**Session Application:** Walk through one complete thought record in session on a recent performance-blocking situation. Assign self-directed records as homework after demonstrated competence.

**Homework:** One thought record per day minimum. Graduate to real-time use within 3 weeks.

**Supervision note:** If Column 3 produces content involving hopelessness, worthlessness, self-harm, or early trauma — stop. This is clinical territory. Refer immediately.

### 3. ACT Cognitive Defusion `COACH-SAFE` *(deep belief work: `⚠️ SUPERVISED`)*

**Mechanism:** Steven Hayes — rather than changing the content of a thought, defusion changes your relationship to it. Reduces the literal believability and behavioral grip of unhelpful thoughts without fighting them.

**Protocol — Five Core Techniques:**

**Technique 1 — Labeling:**
- Replace: *"I'm not good enough"*
- With: *"I'm having the thought that I'm not good enough"*
- Practice on every self-limiting statement for one week

**Technique 2 — Naming the Story:**
- Identify the recurring narrative
- Name it: *"There goes my 'not ready yet' story"*
- No argument, no engagement — just recognition

**Technique 3 — Thank Your Mind:**
- *"Thank you, mind, for that thought"*
- Acknowledge without obeying
- Prevents the thought from expanding through resistance

**Technique 4 — Leaves on a Stream:**
- Close eyes, 3 minutes
- Imagine a slow stream with leaves floating past
- Place each thought on a leaf — observe it float by
- When the mind becomes the stream instead of the observer — notice that, return to observer position

**Technique 5 — Silly Voice:**
- State the thought in a cartoon voice
- Breaks literal fusion immediately
- Use for thoughts with high urgency or threat charge

**Session Application:** When client states a limiting belief as fact, interrupt with defusion in real time. *"Before we examine that — can we try something? Say it this way..."*

**Homework:** Labeling technique on every self-limiting statement for 14 days. Brief daily log of thoughts labeled.

**Supervision boundary:** Basic defusion — coach-safe. When defusion begins revealing the origins of deeply held beliefs (shame, identity-level material, early experiences) — supervised territory.

### 4. Values Clarification `COACH-SAFE`

**Mechanism:** ACT-based. Values are chosen life directions, not goals. They cannot be achieved and checked off — they guide continuous action. Behavior aligned with values requires no willpower because it is intrinsically motivated.

**Protocol:**

**Step 1 — Values Inventory:**
Rate each domain for (a) importance and (b) current alignment, 1–10:
- Work / Career
- Relationships
- Health
- Personal Growth
- Spirituality / Faith
- Finance
- Community
- Creativity
- Leisure
- Character

**Step 2 — Eulogy Exercise:**
*"You're at the end of your life, fully satisfied with how you lived it. A close friend speaks about who you were — not what you achieved. What do they say?"*
Write for 10 minutes without stopping. Extract values from the content.

**Step 3 — Clarifying Questions:**
- *"What angers you most when you see it violated in others?"*
- *"What would you do without any concern for money or approval?"*
- *"When have you felt most alive? What were you doing?"*

**Step 4 — Define Behaviorally:**
Not *"integrity"* — *"I honor commitments even when inconvenient, including small ones."*
Write a behavioral definition for each of top 5 values.

**Step 5 — Values-Action Gap Analysis:**
For each value: current alignment score → what behaviors would move it to 10?

**Session Application:** Core intake tool, sessions 1–2. Return to values whenever client is stuck, unmotivated, or making decisions inconsistent with stated priorities.

**Homework:** Daily 2-minute check-in — *"Did today's actions reflect my values?"* Yes/no log.

### 5. Physiological Regulation — Breathing & HRV `COACH-SAFE`

**Mechanism:** Polyvagal Theory (Stephen Porges) — extended exhale activates the parasympathetic nervous system, downregulating threat response. This is not relaxation — it is state management. The nervous system must be regulated before any cognitive tool can work optimally.

**Protocol — Three Methods (teach in order):**

**Method 1 — Extended Exhale (simplest, most evidence-dense):**
- Any comfortable inhale
- Exhale twice as long
- 5 minutes, any time
- This is the foundation. Everything else builds from it.

**Method 2 — Box Breathing:**
- Inhale: 4 counts
- Hold: 4 counts
- Exhale: 4 counts
- Hold: 4 counts
- 4–6 cycles

**Method 3 — 4-7-8 (deeper regulation):**
- Inhale: 4 counts
- Hold: 7 counts
- Exhale: 8 counts
- 4 cycles

**HRV Tracking:**
- Recommend wearable (Garmin, Whoop, Oura)
- Morning HRV baseline = readiness data
- Low HRV day: reduce cognitive demands, increase recovery
- Teach client to read their own data — not compare to population norms

**Session Application:** Teach in session 1. Use at session opening for grounding. Use immediately when client becomes emotionally activated mid-session.

**Homework:** Daily 5-minute practice, morning. Log practice days. After 2 weeks — report what changed.

### 6. Self-Compassion Practice `COACH-SAFE`

**Mechanism:** Kristin Neff — three components: self-kindness (vs. self-judgment), common humanity (vs. isolation), mindfulness (vs. over-identification). Self-compassion consistently outperforms self-criticism for sustained performance. Self-criticism narrows cognitive flexibility and increases threat response.

**Protocol — Three Core Exercises:**

**Exercise 1 — Self-Compassion Break (3–5 minutes, use in any difficult moment):**
1. *"This is a moment of difficulty. This is suffering."* (acknowledgment)
2. *"Suffering is part of being human. I am not alone in this."* (common humanity)
3. Hand on heart. *"May I be kind to myself right now. May I give myself what I need."* (self-kindness)

**Exercise 2 — Critical Self-Talk Rewrite:**
1. Write out the exact self-critical internal monologue verbatim
2. Read it as if a close friend wrote it to you — notice the impact
3. Rewrite it as you would speak to that same friend in identical circumstances
4. Read the rewritten version aloud
5. Note the difference in felt response

**Exercise 3 — Common Humanity Anchor:**
When experiencing failure or struggle: *"What I'm going through right now — how many people have felt exactly this? I am not uniquely broken."*
Used specifically for isolation and shame.

**Session Application:** Introduce when client presents persistent self-criticism, perfectionism, or shame response to failure. Do not introduce prematurely — clients must understand the mechanism first or they will reject it as weakness.

**Homework:** Daily self-compassion break after any setback or self-critical moment. One week minimum.

**Supervision note:** Clients with significant trauma or deep shame may react intensely — resistance, tears, or dismissal. If resistance is severe and persistent — consult supervisor before continuing.

### 7. Expressive Writing Protocol `⚠️ SUPERVISED`

**Why supervised:** Pennebaker's protocol reliably surfaces material the client has not previously examined. Trauma, grief, and clinical-level material emerge regularly. Do not assign without screening for mental health history and confirming active support.

**Mechanism:** James Pennebaker — 4-session protocol reduces psychological distress, improves immune function, and accelerates performance recovery after significant difficulty. Multiple RCTs confirm effectiveness.

**Protocol:**
- 4 separate writing sessions
- 15–20 minutes each
- Minimum 1 day between sessions (spaced over 2 weeks is acceptable)

**Instructions to client:**
*"Write continuously about a difficult experience that has affected you significantly. Include what happened, how you felt, and what it means to you. Write things you haven't told anyone. Don't worry about grammar or spelling. This is for you only."*

**After each session:**
- 5-minute grounding practice (breathing)
- Brief physical movement
- Client should expect temporary emotional discomfort post-session

**After all 4 sessions:**
- Reflection: What patterns emerged? What shifted in how you see it?
- Debrief in coaching session within 1 week

**Homework:** Assigned as homework only — do NOT facilitate in-session without clinical supervision. Assign, check in at next session, do not probe content unless client initiates.

**Supervision protocol:** Discuss client history with supervisor before assigning. Ensure client has either a therapist or strong support network. Have a referral ready.

### 8. WOOP — Mental Contrasting `COACH-SAFE`

**Mechanism:** Gabriele Oettingen — mental contrasting is the evidence-based version of goal visualization. Pure positive visualization reduces motivation by creating premature psychological satisfaction. WOOP activates problem-solving mode and builds realistic commitment. Multiple RCTs across health, education, and performance contexts.

**Protocol:**

**W — Wish:**
State the most important goal clearly and specifically. One sentence.

**O — Outcome:**
*"Imagine the best outcome of achieving this wish. What does it look like? What does it feel like?"*
2–3 minutes of genuine visualization. Immersive, not abstract.

**O — Obstacle:**
*"What is the main inner obstacle — something inside you — that stands between you and this wish?"*
Inner obstacle only (not external circumstances). Feel the reality of it.

**P — Plan:**
*"If [obstacle occurs], then I will [specific behavior]."*
One if-then statement. Concrete and behavioral.

**Full example:**
- Wish: Deliver excellent client sessions consistently
- Outcome: Visualize the depth of impact, the client's progress, your professional confidence
- Obstacle: I procrastinate on session prep when I feel uncertain
- Plan: If I notice I'm avoiding prep, then I will open my notes and write one question for the client

**Session Application:** Use for any significant goal or behavior change. Takes 10–15 minutes. Assign for daily independent use after teaching in session.

**Homework:** Daily WOOP — one wish, 5–10 minutes, each morning. Physical notebook preferred.

### 9. Implementation Intentions `COACH-SAFE`

**Mechanism:** Peter Gollwitzer — if-then plans dramatically increase goal attainment by pre-deciding responses to specific situations. Eliminates reliance on in-the-moment motivation and willpower.

**Protocol:**

**Formula:** *"If [specific situation], then I will [specific behavior]."*

**Design rules:**
1. The situation must be specific and observable — not *"when I feel motivated"*
2. The behavior must be concrete and achievable
3. Write it — do not just state it
4. Mentally rehearse the scenario once after writing

**Obstacle implementation intention (pairs with WOOP):**
*"If [anticipated obstacle], then I will [response]."*

**Examples:**
- *"If it is 6:00am, then I will immediately do 5 minutes of breathing before touching my phone"*
- *"If I feel the urge to check messages during a deep work block, then I will write what I was doing and return to it"*
- *"If a client says something that triggers self-doubt, then I will take one breath and ask a clarifying question"*

**Session Application:** End every session with the client writing at least one implementation intention for their stated next action. Non-negotiable closer.

**Homework:** Write 3 implementation intentions per week. Review compliance at next session — if not followed through, examine the obstacle, don't shame the failure.

### 10. Behavioral Activation `⚠️ SUPERVISED` *(performance-level use: `COACH-SAFE`)*

**Mechanism:** CBT — the inaction-low mood cycle is broken by action, not motivation. Mood follows behavior. Waiting to feel motivated before acting is the cycle's trap.

**Performance-level coaching protocol:**

**Step 1 — Activity Monitoring (1 week):**
Client tracks hourly activities and rates mood (0–10) and energy (0–10) before and after each activity.

**Step 2 — Analysis:**
- Which activities increase energy and mood?
- Which drain them?
- Which are avoided despite being known as activating?

**Step 3 — Activation Scheduling:**
- Deliberately increase activating activities — especially when motivation is lowest
- Start with 2-minute commitment minimum
- Schedule pleasure activities and mastery activities separately — both matter

**Step 4 — Weekly Review:**
- What got done? What was avoided?
- What was the actual effect vs. predicted effect?

**Key principle to teach:** *"You cannot think your way to action. You must act your way to motivation. The feeling follows the behavior."*

**Session Application:** When client is in low-motivation cycles, procrastination, or performance paralysis.

**Homework:** Activity log for one week, then schedule 3 activation activities per day minimum.

**Supervision boundary:** If client presents with persistent low mood, loss of pleasure, withdrawal from relationships, or sleep changes — this is clinical depression territory. Refer. Do not treat.

### 11. Self-Efficacy Building — Bandura's Four Sources `COACH-SAFE`

**Mechanism:** Albert Bandura — self-efficacy (belief in one's capacity to execute) is the single strongest psychological predictor of sustained performance. It is built through four specific sources and can be deliberately cultivated.

**Protocol — Four Sources:**

**Source 1 — Mastery Experiences:**
- Build an *Evidence Log* — running written record of specific accomplishments the client dismisses or minimizes
- Progressive challenge design: engineer achievable wins, scale upward deliberately
- Review log when client is in self-doubt — make the evidence concrete and visible
- Rule: evidence must be specific behavior, not vague outcomes

**Source 2 — Vicarious Learning:**
- Identify 2–3 role models at a comparable level (not unattainably different — this backfires)
- Study their specific behaviors and decisions, not just outcomes
- *"If they could do it from where they were, what does that tell you about what's possible?"*

**Source 3 — Verbal Persuasion:**
- Direct, specific encouragement tied to observable behavior — not generic praise
- *"The way you handled that difficult conversation shows me you have the capacity to..."*
- Teach client to receive and internalize specific positive feedback rather than immediately deflecting it

**Source 4 — Physiological State Management:**
- Link physiological regulation to performance confidence
- Reframe arousal as readiness (see Eustress Reappraisal — Tool 14)
- *"Your body is preparing you, not betraying you"*

**Session Application:** Build the evidence log in sessions 1–3. Reference it whenever the client enters self-doubt. This is a living document, not a one-time exercise.

**Homework:** One evidence log entry per week minimum — one specific thing done well, no matter how small.

### 12. Environmental Design `COACH-SAFE`

**Mechanism:** BJ Fogg, Thaler & Sunstein — behavior is shaped more by context than by motivation or willpower. The environment is either working for the client or against them. Most clients have never deliberately designed it.

**Protocol — Friction Audit:**

**Step 1 — Map the day:**
Client walks through a typical day hour by hour. Where does performance break down? What exists in the environment at that exact moment?

**Step 2 — Remove friction from desired behaviors:**
- Lay out materials the night before
- Open the document before leaving the desk
- Have the first action of each block already defined
- Pre-schedule, pre-prepare, pre-position everything possible

**Step 3 — Increase friction for competing behaviors:**
- Phone in another room during deep work
- App blockers during focus hours
- Remove temptation objects from visible space
- Close browser tabs, email, notifications

**Step 4 — Environmental design principles:**
- Make desired behavior *obvious* (visible cues)
- Make it *easy* (reduce steps required)
- Make it *immediately satisfying* (build in small reward)
- Make competing behavior *unattractive* (increase friction)

**Step 5 — Implementation:**
Client implements 3 environmental changes. Reports results at next session. Adjust based on data.

**Session Application:** When client repeatedly fails to execute stated commitments — audit the environment before assuming a motivation problem.

**Homework:** Identify 3 environmental friction points. Redesign them this week. Report the impact.

### 13. Attentional Control Training `COACH-SAFE`

**Mechanism:** Eysenck's Attentional Control Theory + Wells' Attention Training Technique — attention is a trainable cognitive resource, not a fixed trait.

**Protocol — Three Stages:**

**Stage 1 — Single-Point Focus (weeks 1–2):**
- Set timer for 5 minutes
- Focus only on breath — the physical sensation of inhale and exhale
- When mind wanders: return without judgment
- Count each return as data — not failure
- Build from 5 minutes to 10, then 20, over 4 weeks

**Stage 2 — Open Monitoring (weeks 3–4):**
- Shift from focused attention to open awareness
- Notice whatever arises (sounds, sensations, thoughts) without engaging any of it
- Practice non-attachment — observe the stream without jumping in
- 10 minutes daily

**Stage 3 — Wells' Attention Training Technique (ATT):**
- Focus attention on a specific external sound
- On cue, deliberately shift to a different sound
- Divide attention across multiple sounds simultaneously
- Trains attentional flexibility — the ability to direct, shift, and broaden attention on command
- 10 minutes daily

**Session Application:** Teach Stage 1 in session 2 or 3. Track daily practice. Review focus quality at each session.

**Homework:** 10 minutes daily. Log practice days and duration. Report subjective concentration improvements week over week.

### 14. Eustress Reappraisal `COACH-SAFE`

**Mechanism:** Alison Wood Brooks + Kelly McGonigal — anxiety and excitement share identical physiological signatures. The only difference is the cognitive label. Reappraising arousal as excitement is more effective than attempting to calm down.

**Protocol:**

**Step 1 — Teach the mechanism:**
*"Anxiety and excitement feel the same in your body — elevated heart rate, heightened alertness, physiological arousal. The difference is only the story you tell about it. You can change the story."*

**Step 2 — Reappraisal statement:**
- Replace *"I am anxious"* with *"I am excited"*
- Not *"I am calm"* — the body cannot quickly validate that statement
- Excited is true — the arousal is real — the label is what changes

**Step 3 — Pre-challenge sequence:**
1. Notice arousal: *"My heart is racing. I feel tension."*
2. Label accurately: *"This is arousal. My body is preparing."*
3. Reframe: *"This energy is functional. It sharpens my focus."*
4. State: *"I am excited and ready."*

**McGonigal's stress-as-enhancing belief:**
- Stress responses evolved to help, not harm
- Only suppressed, chronic, unprocessed stress causes damage
- The belief *"stress makes me stronger"* changes the actual physiological stress response — confirmed by research
- Teach this as a factual statement, not positive thinking

**Session Application:** Use immediately before any high-stakes scenario the client describes. Immediate, practical, demonstrable in session.

**Homework:** Apply before every performance moment this week. Report what shifted.

### 15. Tiny Habits — Fogg Behavior Model `COACH-SAFE`

**Mechanism:** BJ Fogg — behavior occurs when Motivation + Ability + Prompt converge simultaneously. Tiny habits maximize Ability (low effort), select reliable Prompts (existing behaviors), and eliminate dependence on variable Motivation.

**Protocol:**

**Formula:** *"After I [ANCHOR BEHAVIOR], I will [TINY BEHAVIOR]."*

**Design rules:**
1. Anchor = existing daily behavior (not a time — existing action is more reliable)
2. Behavior = smallest possible version, maximum 2 minutes
3. Celebrate immediately after — visible physical expression, not internal
4. Scale up only after 14 consecutive days of automaticity

**Examples:**
- *"After I pour my morning coffee, I will write one sentence in my journal"*
- *"After I sit at my desk, I will open my priority task document"*
- *"After I end a coaching call, I will write one observation about the session"*

**Process:**
1. Identify desired behavior
2. Find the strongest existing anchor
3. Shrink the behavior to its smallest viable form
4. Design an immediate celebration (genuine — not performative)
5. Track for 14 days minimum
6. Scale gradually after automaticity confirmed

**Session Application:** When client has repeatedly failed to execute desired behaviors — stop adding motivation. Add anchors, reduce size.

**Homework:** Design 3 tiny habits using the formula. Report anchor choices and consistency at next session.

### 16. Deep Work Protocol `COACH-SAFE`

**Mechanism:** Cal Newport drawing on Csikszentmihalyi's flow research — the ability to concentrate without distraction is a skill that degrades without deliberate practice and builds with it.

**Protocol — Four Disciplines:**

**Discipline 1 — Work Deeply:**
- Protect 60–90 minute uninterrupted blocks
- No notifications, phone away (not silent — away), one task
- Begin with a defined starting point — ambiguity kills the first 10 minutes
- Depth metric: track deep work hours daily

**Discipline 2 — Embrace Boredom:**
- Do not reach for stimulation during idle moments
- This trains attentional endurance
- Boredom is the muscle soreness of attention training

**Discipline 3 — Reduce Shallow Work:**
- Audit: what percentage of work time is shallow (email, reactive tasks, low-value meetings)?
- Set shallow work time budget — contain it to specific hours
- Batch low-cognitive tasks — do not scatter them through the day

**Discipline 4 — Depth Scheduling:**
- Start at 45-minute blocks, build to 90 minutes over 4 weeks
- Recovery: 10–15 minutes genuine rest between blocks — no screen, no content consumption
- Daily ceiling: 4 hours of true deep work is elite level. Start at 60–90 minutes/day.

**Session Application:** Assign depth tracking in week 1. Most clients are shocked by how little actual deep work they're doing.

**Homework:** Track deep vs. shallow work hours for one week. Identify the top 3 shallow work drains.

### 17. Ultradian Rhythm Structuring `COACH-SAFE`

**Mechanism:** Kleitman & Lavie — the brain naturally oscillates between higher and lower alertness in approximately 90-minute cycles throughout the day. Ignoring this creates performance debt. Working with it creates sustainable high output.

**Protocol:**

**Step 1 — Energy Mapping:**
Client rates energy and focus (1–10) every hour for 5 consecutive days. Brings data to next session.

**Step 2 — Pattern Identification:**
- Where are the peak windows? (Usually 2–3 per day)
- Where are the troughs?
- What is the client currently doing during peak windows vs. what they should be doing?

**Step 3 — Schedule Redesign:**
- Highest-cognitive-demand work → peak windows only
- Administrative, routine, low-demand tasks → trough windows
- Protected recovery periods between 90-minute blocks (non-negotiable)

**Recovery protocol (evidence-based options):**
- 10–20 minute nap
- Outdoor walk without phone
- 10 minutes of breathing practice
- Physical movement

**Anti-pattern to break:** Most clients push through troughs with caffeine. This masks the physiological signal and creates performance debt that compounds.

**Session Application:** Assign energy mapping in weeks 1–2. The data is frequently a revelation.

**Homework:** Hourly energy log for 5 days. Bring the data. Do not redesign schedule before the data is collected.

### 18. Identity-Based Behavior Change `COACH-SAFE`

**Mechanism:** Self-perception theory (Bem) + behavioral self-concept research — behavior that confirms a desired identity becomes self-sustaining. Each action is a vote for or against who the person is becoming.

**Protocol:**

**Step 1 — Identity Design:**
- Identify desired behavior: *consistent morning preparation*
- Reverse-engineer the identity: *"What kind of person does this automatically?"*
- State the identity: *"I am someone who begins each day with deliberate intention"*

**Step 2 — Behavioral Definition:**
What does someone with this identity do daily? Be specific and behavioral.

**Step 3 — Vote Casting:**
Every behavior — including small ones — is a vote for or against the identity.
Reinforce: *"You're not doing this for the result. You're doing this because this is who you are."*

**Step 4 — Failure Redirect:**
When behavior fails: *"That's not like me. What would [identity statement] do right now?"*
Identity recalibration without shame spiral.

**Step 5 — Identity Bridge (for resistant clients):**
- If client doesn't yet believe the identity statement, don't force it
- Use: *"I am becoming someone who..."* as bridge
- Collect confirming evidence weekly until the identity feels credible

**Session Application:** Foundational reframe. Return whenever motivation is discussed. Redirect from *"I need to want to"* to *"this is who I am."*

**Homework:** Write 3 identity statements. Collect one piece of confirming behavioral evidence per day. Weekly review.

### 19. Attention Residue Management `COACH-SAFE`

**Mechanism:** Sophie Leroy — when you switch tasks, attention doesn't fully transfer. Residue from the previous task persists and degrades quality and focus on the next task.

**Protocol:**

**Task Completion Ritual (before every task switch):**
1. Write a 1–2 sentence status note: *"Where I am + next step when I return"*
2. This offloads the task from working memory — eliminates residue
3. Close the document or physically put materials away

**Transition Protocol:**
1. Physical movement — stand, walk briefly
2. 60-second breath reset
3. Single declaration: *"Now I am working on [X]"*
4. Do not begin new task until previous task is mentally closed

**Scheduling for residue:**
- Group similar tasks together — reduce switching cost
- Never schedule high-stakes work immediately after emotionally demanding work
- Buffer meetings with 10-minute recovery blocks — no cognitive input between

**Session Application:** When client reports feeling scattered or unable to focus despite apparent motivation. Attention residue is frequently the actual problem.

**Homework:** Implement the task completion ritual for one week. Report subjective change in focus quality.

### 20. Deliberate Practice with Feedback Loops `COACH-SAFE`

**Mechanism:** Anders Ericsson — expertise is built through deliberate practice: focused effort at the precise edge of current ability with immediate, specific feedback and correction. Hours without these elements produce reinforced mediocrity, not mastery.

**Protocol — Four Requirements:**
1. **Specific skill target** — not *"improve communication"* but *"ask follow-up questions that deepen rather than redirect"*
2. **Stretch level** — beyond current comfortable performance, not overwhelming
3. **Immediate feedback** — specific, behavioral, tied to the practice rep
4. **Repetition with correction** — same skill, adjusted based on feedback

**Practice session design:**
1. Identify one specific sub-skill
2. Design a single practice scenario targeting only that skill
3. Execute with full concentration — no multitasking in practice
4. Get feedback immediately: self-assessment, recording review, or mentor observation
5. Identify one specific correction
6. Repeat with correction applied

**For coaching practice development:**
- Record sessions (with client consent)
- Review recordings for one targeted skill only
- Identify one specific correction per review
- Target that correction in next session
- Track improvement over 4-week blocks

**Session Application:** Help clients design deliberate practice routines for their professional skill development.

**Homework:** Identify one specific sub-skill. Design one 20-minute deliberate practice session. Report what feedback was generated.

### 21. Pre-Mortem Analysis `COACH-SAFE`

**Mechanism:** Gary Klein — prospective hindsight. Imagining a plan has already failed increases obstacle identification by approximately 30% compared to standard planning. Bypasses optimism bias.

**Protocol:**

1. State the goal or plan clearly
2. Project forward to the completion date
3. *"It is [completion date]. This has failed completely. What went wrong?"*
   Write every possible failure cause — no filtering — for 10–15 minutes
4. Prioritize by likelihood and impact
5. Redesign plan to address top 3–5 failure modes
6. Build an implementation intention for each top failure mode

**Pre-mortem questions:**
- *"What assumptions am I making that could be wrong?"*
- *"What would have to be true for this to fail?"*
- *"What am I not doing that I should be?"*
- *"Where is my blind spot in this?"*
- *"What would someone who wants this to fail do to make that happen?"*

**Session Application:** Use before any significant client decision, goal commitment, or launch. Takes 20–30 minutes.

**Homework:** Run a pre-mortem on the client's most important current goal. Bring written output to next session.

### 22. After-Action Review (AAR) `COACH-SAFE`

**Mechanism:** Organizational learning literature — structured debriefing within 24 hours of a significant event dramatically accelerates learning from experience. Without structure, the brain narrativizes rather than analyzes.

**Protocol — Four Questions:**
Complete within 24 hours of any significant performance event:

1. *"What was the intention?"* — What was the plan or goal? State it precisely.
2. *"What actually happened?"* — Specific and factual. No editorializing.
3. *"Why was there a difference?"* — Root cause. Ask why 3 times before accepting the first answer.
4. *"What will I do differently?"* — Specific, behavioral, next action.

**Rules:**
- Written, not mental — writing forces precision that thinking doesn't
- No blame, no excuses — factual observation only
- Focus on behavior, not character: *"I rushed the preparation"* not *"I'm undisciplined"*
- One AAR per significant event. Weekly AAR for regular events.

**Failure-specific extensions:**
- *"What did this reveal about my assumptions?"*
- *"What would I tell a colleague in this exact situation?"*

**Session Application:** Review client AARs at every session. The pattern data over time becomes the most valuable diagnostic tool in the coaching relationship.

**Homework:** One AAR within 24 hours of any performance event. Bring to next session.

---

### 23. Weekly Strategic Planning Protocol `COACH-SAFE`

**Mechanism:** Closing open loops reduces cognitive load. Intentional planning dramatically improves goal attainment compared to reactive scheduling. This is the operating system beneath every other tool.

**Protocol — 60–90 minutes, fixed time weekly:**

**Review Phase (30 minutes):**
1. Review previous week's top priorities — what was accomplished?
2. Review all open commitments — what remains incomplete?
3. Process all captures (notes, emails, calendar, random commitments)
4. Conduct a brief AAR on the week overall
5. Values alignment check: *"Was this week consistent with what I say matters most?"*

**Planning Phase (30 minutes):**
1. Identify top 3 outcomes for the coming week — outcomes, not tasks
2. Schedule deep work blocks for highest-priority work — in calendar, not just intention
3. Pre-mortem the week: *"What could derail these priorities?"*
4. Write one implementation intention per major priority
5. Identify explicitly what you will say no to this week

**Capture system requirement:**
All commitments, ideas, and tasks must live in one trusted system. The weekly review is worthless without a reliable capture system to review.

**Session Application:** Teach in month 2. Review planning output at session openings.

**Homework:** First full weekly review this weekend. Bring output to next session.

### 24. Socratic Self-Examination `⚠️ SUPERVISED` *(surface performance beliefs: `COACH-SAFE`)*

**Why supervised:** Systematic questioning of beliefs can rapidly surface core beliefs tied to early experiences, identity-level material, or clinical conditions.

**Mechanism:** Beck Institute CBT — Socratic questioning does not tell the client what to think. It asks questions that expose the structure, origin, and validity of existing beliefs. The client generates their own insight — making it far more durable than externally provided perspective.

**Protocol — Core Questions:**
- *"What is the evidence for this belief?"*
- *"What would you tell a close friend who held this belief?"*
- *"Is this a fact or an interpretation?"*
- *"What are you ignoring or minimizing that contradicts this?"*
- *"What is the most realistic outcome? How would you handle it?"*
- *"What assumptions are underneath this belief?"*
- *"What would change if this belief turned out to be wrong?"*
- *"Where did this belief come from? When did it first appear?"*

**Protocol in session:**
1. Identify the specific belief driving the performance problem
2. State it precisely with the client: *"So the belief is exactly: [statement]?"*
3. Apply 3–4 questions — do not answer them. Wait. The silence is the work.
4. Summarize: *"What do you notice now about that belief?"*
5. Client writes their revised perspective in their own words

**Supervision boundary:** The moment questions begin revealing childhood origins of a belief, or the client becomes significantly distressed, or material involves shame/worthlessness/early relationships — stop the technique. Reflect back. Refer if indicated.

### 25. Behaviorally Anchored Personal Metrics `COACH-SAFE`

**Mechanism:** Behavioral self-monitoring (CBT research) — tracking behavior increases awareness, accountability, and follow-through. Track leading behaviors (actions within control), not lagging outcomes (results dependent on external factors).

**Protocol:**

**Metric design rules:**
1. Behaviors only — not outcomes
2. Fully within client's control
3. Maximum 5–7 metrics (cognitive load limit)
4. Daily data entry — not weekly (recency and rationalization bias)
5. Weekly review for trend analysis — not day-by-day judgment

**Building the dashboard:**
1. Identify 3–5 behaviors most predictive of client's stated outcomes
2. Define each precisely — no ambiguity in what counts
3. Select tracking method the client will actually use (paper, spreadsheet, app)
4. Weekly review: *"What do the numbers show? What do they not show?"*
5. Quarterly audit: adjust metrics as behaviors become automatic

**Common performance coaching metrics:**
- Deep work hours per day
- Sleep hours
- Daily top priority completed (Y/N)
- Session preparation quality (1–5)
- Morning energy baseline (1–10)
- Implementation intentions written (Y/N)

**Session Application:** Build the dashboard in month 1. Review data at the opening of every session. Metrics without review are data collection theater.

**Homework:** Daily log, 60 seconds maximum entry time. Bring data to every session.

### 26. Mortality Awareness Practice `COACH-SAFE` *(conditional — see note)*

**Mechanism:** Stoic memento mori + Terror Management Theory (Greenberg, Solomon, Pyszczynski) — conscious awareness of mortality, when processed deliberately, increases meaning-driven behavior, reduces trivial distraction, and sharpens prioritization.

**Protocol:**

**Daily practice (2 minutes, morning):**
*"I will not live forever. What matters most today?"*
Write one answer. Let it shape the first priority.

**Deathbed Perspective (quarterly, 15–20 minutes):**
1. Project to end of life — honestly, not morbidly
2. *"Looking back at this specific year — what will I wish I had done?"*
3. *"What am I tolerating right now that I will regret?"*
4. *"What am I avoiding that actually matters?"*
5. Write without editing
6. Identify one concrete change based on the reflection

**Seneca's Daily Practice:**
- Morning: *"What if today were the last day I could work on what matters?"*
- Evening: *"Did today reflect what I actually value?"*

**Session Application:** Quarterly check-in tool — not weekly. Powerful calibration for drift. Pairs with values clarification review.

**Supervision note:** Do not use with any client presenting with grief, acute loss, existential crisis, or any history of suicidal ideation. Screen before introducing.

### 27. Motivational Interviewing — Core Techniques `⚠️ SUPERVISED`

**Why supervised:** MI is a clinical communication framework. Misapplication looks like coaching but produces poor outcomes. Requires formal training and feedback on actual session recordings.

**Mechanism:** Miller & Rollnick — change talk (client articulating their own reasons for change) is the strongest predictor of behavior change. Confrontation and persuasion create resistance.

**Protocol — OARS Framework:**

**Open Questions:**
- Questions that cannot be answered yes or no
- *"What would need to be different for you to move forward?"*
- *"What matters most to you about making this change?"*

**Affirmations:**
- Specific acknowledgment of observable strengths and efforts — not generic praise
- *"The way you've continued preparing despite those setbacks shows real commitment"*

**Reflective Listening:**
- Simple reflection: mirror content back — *"So you're feeling stuck"*
- Complex reflection: add meaning or emotion — *"It sounds like it's not the work itself but what it would mean to fail"*

**Summarizing:**
- Periodic synthesis of what's been said, including ambivalence
- *"On one hand... on the other hand... where does that leave you?"*

**Change Talk Elicitation:**
- *"What are the advantages of making this change?"*
- *"How important is this to you, 0–10? Why not lower?"*
- *"What gives you confidence you could do this?"*
- *"What would your life look like if this changed?"*

**Handling Resistance:**
- Never argue
- Roll with it: *"It sounds like you have mixed feelings about this"*
- Explore ambivalence: *"Tell me more about the part that's hesitant"*

**Session Application:** This is a communication framework for the entire coaching relationship — not a single tool. Once learned, it changes how you conduct every session.

**Training required:** MINT network (Motivational Interviewing Network of Trainers). Formal workshop + supervision on recordings.

### 28. Behavioral Experiments `⚠️ SUPERVISED`

**Why supervised:** Behavioral experiments targeting avoidance, fear, or safety behaviors are functionally equivalent to exposure therapy. Misdesigned experiments reinforce the problem.

**Mechanism:** CBT — limiting beliefs are hypotheses, not facts. The most effective way to change a belief is to test it through direct experience.

**Protocol:**
1. Identify the specific belief: *"If I set a boundary with a client, they will leave"*
2. Design the test: *"I will set one boundary in this week's session"*
3. Predict: *"What exactly do I expect to happen?"* — write it before executing
4. Execute
5. Record what actually happened — specifically
6. Compare prediction to reality
7. Update belief based on evidence

**Design rules:**
- Experiments must be specific and observable
- Must be achievable — not overwhelming
- Client must predict outcome before executing — otherwise there is no test
- Review results together without editorializing

**Supervision boundary:** When the belief involves fear, avoidance, social threat, or any behavior functioning as a safety behavior — supervision required.

### 29. Rumination Interruption `⚠️ SUPERVISED` *(performance-level: `COACH-SAFE`)*

**Mechanism:** CBT — rumination maintains and worsens mood and performance by cycling through problems without resolution. It feels like problem-solving but produces no solutions.

**Protocol:**

**Scheduled Concern Time:**
1. Designate 15–20 minutes daily as the only time for ruminative thoughts — same time each day
2. When rumination occurs outside that time: *"I'll address this at [time]"* — note it, redirect
3. During concern time: write thoughts, then either problem-solve or deliberately close the loop

**The 5-Step Interrupt:**
1. Notice rumination occurring
2. Name it: *"I am ruminating right now"*
3. Ask: *"Is this thought actionable right now?"*
4. If yes: take one concrete action or write one next step
5. If no: *"Concern time at [time]"* — redirect to a physical or external focus task

**Behavioral redirect:**
- Immediate physical movement (breaks the physiological loop)
- Any task requiring directed external attention
- ACT defusion technique: label, don't engage

**Session Application:** When client reports being stuck in their head, cycling through the same problems without resolution.

**Supervision boundary:** If rumination is persistent, daily, causing significant distress, and interfering with basic functioning — clinical referral, not coaching protocol.

### 30. Sleep as Cognitive Performance Tool `COACH-SAFE`

**Mechanism:** Matthew Walker — sleep is required for memory consolidation, emotional regulation, decision quality, and cognitive flexibility. Many performance problems are sleep problems misdiagnosed as motivation, discipline, or focus issues.

**Protocol:**

**Step 1 — Sleep Audit:**
- Track sleep hours and quality for 2 weeks
- Track performance quality and mood — correlate with sleep data

**Step 2 — Non-Negotiable Architecture:**
- 7–9 hours is the evidence-based requirement — not 6, not 5
- Consistent wake time is more important than consistent bedtime
- Last caffeine: 10–12 hours before sleep minimum (caffeine half-life is 5–7 hours)
- Temperature: 65–68°F sleep environment
- Eliminate screens 60–90 minutes before bed

**Step 3 — Pre-Sleep Protocol:**
- Consistent wind-down routine: same activities, same order, 30–60 minutes before bed
- Write tomorrow's priorities tonight: offloads working memory, reduces pre-sleep rumination
- Brief reflection practice — not stimulating content

**Step 4 — Nap Protocol:**
- 10–20 minutes maximum (prevents sleep inertia)
- Before 3pm only
- Supplement to night sleep, not substitute

**Session Application:** Assess sleep in session 1. Treat it as a foundational performance variable, not a lifestyle preference.

**Homework:** 2-week sleep audit. Implement one intervention. Bring data.

**Note:** Chronic insomnia (3+ nights/week for 3+ months) requires physician or sleep specialist referral — not coaching intervention.

### 31. Structured Daily Journaling `COACH-SAFE`

**Mechanism:** Externalizing thoughts reduces cognitive load and improves clarity. Structured prompts prevent vague journaling that produces no insight. This is distinct from Pennebaker's trauma-processing protocol — this is forward-focused and performance-oriented.

**Protocol:**

**Morning (10 minutes):**
1. Three things I'm grateful for — specific, not generic
2. What would make today excellent? One sentence.
3. One affirmation grounded in specific evidence — not aspirational, factual

**Evening (5–10 minutes):**
1. Three specific things that went well today — and why they went well (builds self-efficacy)
2. One thing I would do differently and exactly how
3. One insight from today

**Weekly entry (30 minutes, same day each week):**
1. What did I learn this week?
2. Where did I live my values? Where did I fall short — specifically?
3. What is most important for next week?

**Rules:**
- Specific over generic — always. Vague entries produce no insight.
- Honest observation, not performance for an imagined reader
- Physical writing produces deeper processing than digital — supported by research

**Session Application:** Assign from session 1. Review weekly summary entries together. Over months, the journal becomes the primary evidence base for pattern identification.

**Homework:** 15 minutes daily — morning and evening split. Bring weekly summary to sessions.
