## The Honest Starting Point — CPCC Baseline Assessment

### What CPCC Gives You
- Ability to hold a coaching relationship — rapport, active listening, powerful questioning
- Understanding of the coaching container and ethics
- ICF alignment — knowledge of scope of practice (in theory)

### What CPCC Does NOT Give You
- Any clinical psychology training
- Understanding of psychopathology or how to recognize clinical presentations
- CBT theory or technique training
- Research literacy — knowing what's actually proven vs. what's sold
- Competence in cognitive restructuring, defusion, or any evidence-based cognitive tool

### The Core Problem
Co-Active coaching is fundamentally humanistic and relational — it is not cognitive-behavioral. CTI's model is about awareness, fulfillment, and balance. It does not train you in the mechanisms of cognitive change.

The danger zone: you know enough about coaching to feel confident, but not enough about cognitive psychology to know what you don't know. That's where coaches cause harm — not maliciously, but through confident incompetence.

### Transferable Assets
- Coaching relationship skills
- Holding space, asking questions, non-judgment
- ICF ethical framework

### Gaps to Close
- Cognitive psychology theory (from scratch)
- Recognizing clinical vs. coaching territory
- Evidence-based cognitive techniques applied within coaching scope
- Research literacy

## The Evidence Tier — What's Real vs. What's Noise

### Gold Standard — Heavy RCT Evidence
| Framework                                  | Founder                   | Notes                                                                        |
| ------------------------------------------ | ------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| CBT (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy)         | Aaron Beck                | Most studied psychological intervention in history                           |
| ACT (Acceptance & Commitment Therapy)      | Steven Hayes              | Strong empirical base, highly practical                                      |
| DBT (Dialectical Behavior Therapy)         | Marsha Linehan            | Originally clinical, strong emotion regulation evidence                      |
| MBCT (Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy) | Segal, Williams, Teasdale | Strong evidence for relapse prevention and cognitive flexibility             |
| REBT (Rational Emotive Behavior Therapy)   | Albert Ellis              | Predecessor to CBT, direct and confrontational                               |
| Self-Determination Theory                  | Deci & Ryan               | Most robust motivation framework in psychology                               |
| Self-Efficacy Theory                       | Albert Bandura            | Strongest predictor of sustained performance                                 |
| WOOP / Mental Contrasting                  | Gabriele Oettingen        | Multiple RCTs proves that it outperforms positive visualization consistently |
| Implementation Intentions                  | Peter Gollwitzer          | One of the strongest behavior change tools in literature                     |
| Tiny Habits                                | BJ Fogg                   | Empirically grounded behavior design                                         |
| Deliberate Practice                        | Anders Ericsson           | Foundation of expertise research                                             |
| Self-Compassion                            | Kristin Neff              | Outperforms self-criticism for sustained performance                         |
| Expressive Writing                         | James Pennebaker          | Multiple Randomized Control Trials RCTs across health and performance        |

### Approaches to Avoid
- **NLP (Neuro-Linguistic Programming)** — largely debunked, no rigorous evidence base
- **Most "mindset" frameworks** sold in coaching — cherry-picked neuroscience
- **Tony Robbins-style approaches** — compelling but not empirically validated
- **Grit model (Duckworth)** — faces significant criticism; may simply measure conscientiousness
- **Vision casting** — motivational language, not a tool
- **Skill stacking (Scott Adams)** — personal productivity concept, zero empirical basis
- **Dopamine regulation training** — pop neuroscience jargon, not a clinical tool
- **SMART goals** — weak evidence for behavior change; frequently outperformed by WOOP

## Phased Learning Roadmap

### Critical Rule
**Do not use any tool with a client that you have not first applied to yourself for a minimum of 6 months.** You cannot coach what you haven't lived.

---

### Phase 1 — Build the Foundation on Yourself
**Timeline: 3–6 months minimum**

**Books — in this order:**

1. **"Feeling Good" — David Burns**
   Most accessible, evidence-backed entry point. Burns was a clinical psychiatrist at Stanford/UPenn. Not pop psychology.

2. **"Mind Over Mood" — Greenberger & Padesky**
   A workbook. Do every exercise on yourself. Gold standard self-directed CBT tool used in clinical settings worldwide.

3. **"The Happiness Trap" — Russ Harris**
   Best accessible entry into ACT. Introduces psychological flexibility.

4. **"Learned Optimism" — Martin Seligman**
   Empirical work on explanatory style. Seligman ran the UPenn Positive Psychology Center.

**Supplementary:**
- Andrew Huberman podcast — neuroscience episodes on stress, cognition, and behavioral change are evidence-cited
- Michael Gervais — "Finding Mastery" podcast — performance psychology applied to elite performers

---

### Phase 2 — Go Deep Into the Theory
**Timeline: 3–6 months**

5. **"Cognitive Therapy: Basics and Beyond" — Judith Beck**
   Judith runs the Beck Institute (Aaron Beck was her father). This is the textbook. Treat it like one.

6. **"A Liberated Mind" — Steven Hayes**
   Hayes created ACT. His own account of the science and his personal story. Full framework.

7. **"Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy for Depression" — Segal, Williams, Teasdale**
   Dry. Academic. Read it anyway. Tells you what MBCT actually is vs. what people sell as mindfulness.

8. **"Emotion Regulation in Psychotherapy" — Leahy, Tirch, Napolitano**
   Clinician-level. Gives depth on the emotional mechanics underneath cognitive loops.

---

**Timeline: 6–12 months alongside Phase 2**

### Phase 4 — Professional Application
**Timeline: Ongoing**

**Books for applying this professionally:**

9. **"Cognitive Behavioral Coaching in Practice" — Michael Neenan**
   Neenan is a CBT practitioner who bridges the gap between clinical CBT and coaching. Most rigorous coaching-specific text available.

10. **"The Inner Game of Tennis" — Tim Gallwey**
    Foundational performance coaching text. Deceptively simple. Highly cited by elite coaches.

11. **"Performing Under Pressure" — Weisinger & Pawliw-Fry**
    Evidence-based, performance-specific cognitive strategies.

**Ongoing:**
- Read the journal *Cognitive Therapy and Research* — keeps you current on what's actually working [Articles | Cognitive Therapy and Research | Springer Nature Link](https://link.springer.com/journal/10608/articles)
- Get a supervisor or mentor who is a credentialed clinician — protects your clients and your practice

## $50,000 Budget Allocation — Individual Coaching Practice

### What $50,000 Cannot Do
Make you a licensed clinician. That path requires a full master's in counseling or clinical psychology, supervised clinical hours, and state licensure — $80,000–$150,000 and 4–6 years. That is not the right path for this practice.

### What $50,000 Can Do
Make you one of the most rigorously trained non-clinical cognitive coaches in the market. That is a legitimate and valuable position.

### Option A — Academic Route (~$35,000–$42,000)
**UPenn Master of Applied Positive Psychology (MAPP)**
- Seligman's actual program. Most credible non-clinical academic credential available.
- ~$35,000–$40,000 full program
- Online + 2 residential sessions per year
- Gives real research literacy, peer network, and the UPenn name
- Best for: clients and contexts where institutional credibility matters
- Verdict: Highest single credential ROI if targeting corporate clients. For individual-only — less necessary.

### Option B — Stacked Practitioner Route (~$25,000–$35,000 on training)

| Investment | Cost | What It Gets You |
|---|---|---|
| Beck Institute CBT Training (full track) | $5,000–$8,000 | Most credible CBT training outside a clinical degree |
| ACBS ACT Workshops (full sequence) | $3,000–$5,000 | ACT competency from the source |
| UPenn Positive Psychology Certificate | $5,000–$8,000 | Legitimate Seligman-backed credential |
| Personal therapy (weekly, 18 months) | $8,000–$12,000 | Non-negotiable |
| Clinical supervision (monthly, 2 years) | $4,000–$7,000 | Protects clients, accelerates competence |
| Books, materials, conferences | $1,500–$2,500 | ACBS and ABCT annual conferences |

### Recommended: Option C — Hybrid (~$40,000–$48,000)
*Best allocation for individual practice*

| Investment | Cost |
|---|---|

## Individual Client Market Reality

### What Individual Clients Respond To
- Referrals from someone they trust
- Positioning that speaks to their specific problem
- Demonstrated competence in consultation
- Results and reputation over time
They do not hire you because of your UPenn credential.

### The Income Ceiling
Individual-only coaching is a lifestyle business, not a scaling business. Revenue ceiling is approximately **$120,000–$180,000/year** for a well-run individual practice without group programs or courses.

To exceed $300K/year in this model requires eventually adding:
- Group coaching programs
- Digital products or courses
- Workshops or retreats

### The Single Most Valuable Business Asset
**Referral relationships with licensed therapists.**

Therapists regularly have clients who have completed enough clinical work and are ready for forward-focused performance coaching. They need trusted coaches to refer those clients to.

This requires:
- Being trained, ethical, and clear on scope
- Reciprocal referral relationship — you refer clinical presentations back to them
- Building trust over time through consistent professional conduct

This is free client acquisition from pre-qualified, pre-motivated individuals. It is worth more than any credential for the individual market.

## Scripture-Aligned Performance Tool Framework

### Design Principle
Each scripture identifies a performance principle. Each mental tool is the evidence-based means of fulfilling that principle. The scripture provides the why; the tool provides the how.

### Category: Mind & Identity

---

**Romans 12:2** — *"Be transformed by the renewal of your mind…"*

**Performance Principle:** Cognitive renewal precedes behavioral excellence.

**Primary Tool:** Cognitive Restructuring (CBT Thought Records) — Aaron Beck
**Supporting Tool:** Socratic Self-Examination — Beck Institute
**Application:** Daily thought record practice; replace reactive automatic thinking with examined, intentional response patterns.

---

**Philippians 4:8** — *"Whatever is true… honorable… just… think about these things."*

**Performance Principle:** Attention selection determines outcome quality.

**Primary Tool:** Attentional Control Training — Eysenck / Wells ATT
**Supporting Tool:** Directed Attention Practice (mindfulness-based attention regulation)
**Application:** Daily 10-minute ATT practice; eliminate passive attention drift; deliberately curate cognitive input.

---

**2 Timothy 1:7** — *"God gave us a spirit not of fear but of power and love and self-control."*

**Performance Principle:** Emotional regulation under pressure.

**Primary Tool:** Physiological Regulation — Diaphragmatic breathing, Polyvagal Theory (Porges)
**Supporting Tool:** Stoic Pause — stimulus-response gap (Marcus Aurelius, Epictetus); Eustress Reappraisal (Brooks/McGonigal)
**Application:** Daily 5-minute breathing practice; pre-challenge eustress reappraisal sequence; replace fear-based reactions with regulated, deliberate responses.

---

**Proverbs 4:23** — *"Keep your heart with all vigilance…"*

**Performance Principle:** Internal governance precedes external success.

**Primary Tool:** Metacognitive Awareness — Adrian Wells
**Supporting Tool:** ACT Values Clarification — Steven Hayes
**Application:** Daily thought observation practice; weekly values-action gap audit; govern internal narratives before they govern behavior.

---

**Colossians 3:2** — *"Set your minds on things that are above…"*

**Performance Principle:** Vision drives execution quality.

**Primary Tool:** WOOP — Mental Contrasting (Oettingen)
**Supporting Tool:** Implementation Intentions (Gollwitzer); Identity-Based Behavior Change
**Application:** Daily WOOP practice; define future identity standard; operate from that identity through behavioral votes. Replace pure positive visualization — research shows it reduces motivation.

---

### Category: Discipline & Work Ethic

---

**Colossians 3:23** — *"Whatever you do, work heartily, as for the Lord…"*

**Performance Principle:** Internal standard over external supervision.

**Primary Tool:** Self-Determination Theory — Deci & Ryan (autonomy, competence, relatedness)
**Supporting Tool:** Behavioral Activation — CBT
**Application:** Detach performance from applause; build intrinsic motivation through autonomy and competence. Action precedes motivation.

---

**Proverbs 22:29** — *"Do you see a man skillful in his work? He will stand before kings…"*

**Performance Principle:** Mastery creates access.

**Primary Tool:** Deliberate Practice — Anders Ericsson (cite Ericsson directly, not popularizers)
**Supporting Tool:** Feedback Loop Design — immediate, specific, behavioral
**Application:** Daily deliberate practice sessions targeting specific sub-skills with immediate feedback and correction. Structured skill reps, not undirected repetition.

---

**1 Corinthians 9:24–27** — *"Run in such a way that you may obtain it…"*

**Performance Principle:** Intentional training and self-control.

**Primary Tool:** Ultradian Rhythm Structuring — Kleitman / Lavie
**Supporting Tool:** Strategic Recovery — Matthew Walker (sleep as active performance enhancement)
**Application:** Align work intensity with 90-minute cognitive cycles; build recovery as a performance discipline, not an absence of work.

---

**Ecclesiastes 9:10** — *"Whatever your hand finds to do, do it with your might…"*

**Performance Principle:** Full engagement.

**Primary Tool:** Deep Work Protocol — Cal Newport / Csikszentmihalyi flow research
**Supporting Tool:** Cognitive Load Management — John Sweller
**Application:** Protected 60–90 minute deep work blocks; eliminate shallow work scatter; track depth hours daily.

---

**Proverbs 13:4** — *"The soul of the diligent is richly supplied."*

**Performance Principle:** Consistency beats intensity.

**Primary Tool:** Tiny Habits — BJ Fogg (Motivation + Ability + Prompt model)
**Supporting Tool:** Implementation Intentions as habit triggers
**Application:** Anchor micro-behaviors to existing routines; celebrate immediately; scale only after automaticity confirmed.

---

### Category: Focus & Strategic Thinking

---

**Luke 16:10** — *"One who is faithful in very little is also faithful in much…"*

**Performance Principle:** Micro-discipline → macro-trust.

**Primary Tool:** Identity-Based Behavior Change — self-perception theory (Bem)
**Supporting Tool:** Behaviorally Anchored Personal Metrics
**Application:** Frame small commitments as identity votes; audit micro-consistency; what you do when no one is watching determines who you become.

---

**Proverbs 16:3** — *"Commit your work to the Lord…"*

**Performance Principle:** Alignment reduces anxiety.

**Primary Tool:** Structured Transition Rituals — implementation intentions + priming research
**Supporting Tool:** Values Clarification as alignment anchor
**Application:** Begin each work session with intention alignment; pre-task priming ritual; close the gap between stated values and actual work.

---

**Habakkuk 2:2** — *"Write the vision; make it plain…"*

**Performance Principle:** Clarity multiplies execution.

**Primary Tool:** WOOP — written, specific, obstacle-anticipated (Oettingen)
**Supporting Tool:** Goal Hierarchies — connecting daily actions to long-term values
**Application:** Written quarterly WOOP; written weekly priorities; written daily intentions. Unwritten goals are wishes.

---

**James 1:8** — *"A double-minded man is unstable…"*

**Performance Principle:** Divided focus destroys performance.

**Primary Tool:** Attention Residue Management — Sophie Leroy
**Supporting Tool:** Cognitive Switching Cost Awareness — Meyer & Kieras research
**Application:** Task completion ritual before every switch; single-task blocks; group similar work; buffer transitions.

---

**Hebrews 12:11** — *"For the moment all discipline seems painful…"*

**Performance Principle:** Delayed gratification.

**Primary Tool:** Self-Compassion Practice — Kristin Neff (counterintuitive: outperforms self-criticism for sustained performance)
**Supporting Tool:** Reward Delay Training — behavioral psychology
**Application:** Replace punishment-based discipline with compassion-based consistency; restructure reward systems around identity confirmation rather than outcome.

### Category: Endurance & Grit

---

**Galatians 6:9** — *"Let us not grow weary of doing good…"*

**Performance Principle:** Long-term persistence.

**Primary Tool:** Self-Efficacy Building — Albert Bandura (four sources)
**Supporting Tool:** Evidence Log; Vicarious Learning from comparable models
**Application:** Build mastery experiences deliberately; weekly evidence log; long-term mission anchored to values, not outcomes. Note: Duckworth's grit model faces significant criticism in research — use **Bandura's self-efficacy framework as primary.**

---

**James 1:3** — *"The testing of your faith produces steadfastness."*

**Performance Principle:** Stress builds capacity.

**Primary Tool:** Stress Inoculation Training (SIT) — Donald Meichenbaum (clinical origin, strong evidence)
**Supporting Tool:** Eustress Reappraisal — McGonigal / Brooks
**Application:** Controlled challenge exposure with progressive difficulty; reframe stress response as preparation, not threat.

---

**Hebrews 12:1** — *"Let us run with endurance…"*

**Performance Principle:** Remove performance drag.

**Primary Tool:** Environmental Design — BJ Fogg / Thaler & Sunstein nudge theory
**Supporting Tool:** Friction Audit — behavioral economics
**Application:** Systematically remove friction from desired behaviors; increase friction for competing behaviors; redesign environment as performance infrastructure.

---

**2 Peter 1:5–7** — *"Make every effort…"*

**Performance Principle:** Layered growth.

**Primary Tool:** Adjacent Competency Development — expertise research
**Supporting Tool:** T-Shaped Development (depth in one domain, breadth across adjacent areas)
**Application:** Add one targeted competency per quarter; connect new skills to existing strengths; deliberate practice portfolio.

---

**Proverbs 24:16** — *"The righteous falls seven times and rises again…"*

**Performance Principle:** Recovery speed matters.

**Primary Tool:** After-Action Review (AAR) — organizational learning literature
**Supporting Tool:** Expressive Writing Protocol — Pennebaker (supervised)
**Application:** Written failure debrief within 24 hours; four-question format; identify behavioral change, not character judgment. Pattern analysis over time.

### Category: Wisdom & Strategic Leadership

---

**Proverbs 21:5** — *"The plans of the diligent lead surely to abundance…"*

**Performance Principle:** Planning precedes productivity.

**Primary Tool:** Weekly Strategic Planning Protocol — GTD (Allen) + Newport
**Supporting Tool:** Behaviorally Anchored Personal Metrics
**Application:** 60–90 minute fixed weekly review and planning session; outcomes not tasks; scheduled in calendar not just intention.

---

**Luke 14:28** — *"Count the cost…"*

**Performance Principle:** Risk assessment.

**Primary Tool:** Pre-Mortem Analysis — Gary Klein
**Application:** Before any significant decision or commitment — imagine failure, identify causes, redesign plan to address top failure modes. Written output required.

---

**Matthew 25:21** — *"Well done, good and faithful servant…"*

**Performance Principle:** Faithful stewardship.

**Primary Tool:** Behaviorally Anchored Personal Metrics — behavioral self-monitoring (CBT)

**Psalm 90:12** — *"Teach us to number our days…"*

**Performance Principle:** Time awareness sharpens urgency.

**Primary Tool:** Time Blocking + Mortality Awareness Practice
**Supporting Tool:** Deep Work scheduling; Seneca's daily practice
**Application:** Schedule non-negotiable blocks; daily mortality reflection sharpens prioritization; quarterly deathbed perspective exercise.

---

**Joshua 1:8** — *"Meditate on it day and night… then you will make your way prosperous."*

**Performance Principle:** Mental rehearsal and disciplined thought repetition.

**Primary Tool:** Mental Contrasting (WOOP) — Oettingen. Not pure visualization — visualize the goal AND the obstacle.
**Supporting Tool:** Structured Daily Journaling — Pennebaker / CBT self-monitoring
**Application:** Daily WOOP practice; structured morning and evening journaling; cognitive priming through intentional review of values and priorities. Pure positive visualization is contraindicated by research.

## Critical Ethical Boundary — Coaching vs. Therapy

This boundary is not bureaucratic. Violating it harms clients and creates legal liability.

### What a Coach Can Do
- Support clients in identifying and pursuing goals
- Apply evidence-based cognitive tools within a performance framework
- Help clients build habits, systems, and self-awareness
- Challenge thinking patterns in service of performance
- Teach and apply the tools in this framework (coach-safe tools)

### What a Coach Cannot Do
- Diagnose any mental health condition
- Treat depression, anxiety disorders, PTSD, or any clinical condition
- Conduct therapy or use clinical interventions without clinical licensure
- Continue working with a client presenting clinical symptoms without referral

### Warning Signs Requiring Referral
- Persistent low mood lasting more than 2 weeks
- Significant changes in sleep, appetite, or energy unexplained by circumstance
- Hopelessness or statements about life not being worth living
- Panic attacks or severe anxiety interfering with daily function
- Trauma responses (flashbacks, avoidance, hypervigilance)
- Substance use as a coping mechanism
- Relationship to food, body, or self-harm that seems disordered

### The Referral Process
1. *"What you're describing sounds like it may be beyond what coaching can fully address."*
2. *"I want to refer you to someone who has specific training in this area."*
3. Maintain a current referral list of licensed therapists — particularly those who use CBT/ACT
4. Coaching can continue alongside therapy — clarify roles with the therapist

### Building Your Referral Network
- Identify 3–5 licensed therapists in your area who do CBT/ACT work
- Build relationships with them — reciprocal referrals benefit both practices
- Know your scope. Enforce it. Every time.
