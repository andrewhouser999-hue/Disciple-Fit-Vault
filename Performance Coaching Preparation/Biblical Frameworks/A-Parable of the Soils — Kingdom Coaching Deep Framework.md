## Psychological Modalities This Framework Draws From

This framework is genuinely integrative — not one school. Here is the breakdown:

| Framework Element | Psychological Modality |
|---|---|
| Window of tolerance, somatic anchoring, body-as-barometer | **Somatic Experiencing** (Peter Levine) / **Sensorimotor Psychotherapy** (Pat Ogden) |
| Window of tolerance concept specifically | **Interpersonal Neurobiology** (Dan Siegel) |
| Lie work, belief → behavior chains, cognitive restructuring | **CBT** (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy) |
| Schema mapping (unrelenting standards, deprivation, subjugation) | **Schema Therapy** (Jeffrey Young) |
| Secondary gain, defense mechanisms, wound archaeology | **Psychodynamic** |
| Willingness to experience difficult internal states, psychological flexibility | **ACT** (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy) |
| Long story diagnostic, future self narration, narrative identity | **Narrative Therapy** |
| Object constancy, corrective relational experience, attachment mapping | **Attachment Theory** (Bowlby / Ainsworth) |

**The technical term that best names the whole: Somatic-Cognitive Integration** — combining *top-down* approaches (CBT, schema, narrative — working from thought to body) with *bottom-up* approaches (somatic experiencing, sensorimotor — working from body to thought).

Most advanced trauma-informed coaching and therapy operates this way. The technical literature sometimes calls it **integrative somatic therapy** or **embodied cognitive processing**.

What makes this framework distinct is that it layers spiritual warfare and Holy Spirit agency on top of all of this — which no secular modality does. That's the Kingdom Coaching differentiation.

## Part 1: Soil Diagnosis

### Presenting Soil vs. Structural Soil

The first diagnostic discipline is learning to distinguish what a client shows from what actually is.

Clients almost never present their actual soil. They present:
- What they believe their soil is
- What they wish their soil was
- What they've been told their soil is
- A protective performance of a less threatening soil type

**The rule:** When a client presents one soil type, always ask what soil is beneath that one.

| Presenting Soil | Structural Soil Beneath | What's Happening |
|---|---|---|
| Thorny | Rocky | Busyness is covering for a self that has no stable root — the thorns protect them from discovering there's nothing there |
| Rocky | Path | Emotional responsiveness is the last soft layer over deep compaction — breakthrough experiences are the only way the armor temporarily drops |
| Good | Thorny | High functionality masking chronic resource competition — fruit exists but is smaller than it should be and costs more than it should |
| Thorny | Path | The most dangerous pattern — productivity and busyness as armor, emotional flatness beneath the activity |

**How to read the layer beneath:** Stop listening to what the client says about themselves. Watch what they do with truth when it lands. The structural soil shows in the response, not the report.

---

### Structural Diagnostic Framework — Four Dimensions

Instead of symptom matching, read these four structural dimensions:

**Dimension 1: Truth Reception**
How does the client receive new truth?
- Immediate intellectualization → Path (armor engages instantly)
- Immediate emotional resonance that fades by next session → Rocky (no integration architecture)
- Agreement + zero follow-through → Thorny (resources captured elsewhere)
- Wrestles, sits with it, returns having processed → Good Soil

**Dimension 2: Pressure Response**
What happens when the client is under real stress?
- Shuts down, withdraws, becomes cynical → Path
- Collapses, crisis-mode, identity destabilization → Rocky
- Accelerates into busyness or numbing → Thorny
- Slower, more intentional, anchors in foundation → Good Soil

**Dimension 3: God Representation Under Suffering**
Who is God to this client when things are hard?
- Absent, indifferent, or complicit in harm → Path
- Unpredictable — good when things are good, absent when things are hard → Rocky
- Good in theory, irrelevant in practice → Thorny
- Trustworthy even when not making sense → Good Soil

> The single most diagnostic question is not "Do you believe God is good?" — that gets you theology. "Who is God to you right now, in your actual life?" — that gets you soil.

**Dimension 4: Secondary Gain Analysis**
Every soil type has a payoff. Identifying the payoff tells you what you're actually working against.

| Soil | What Staying There Protects Against |
|---|---|
| Path | The risk of vulnerability — if they open, they might be disappointed again |
| Rocky | The terror of an ordinary, unconfirmed faith — the breakthroughs prove they're real |
| Thorny | The emptiness they'd feel if they stopped — and the identity crisis of not being productive |
| Stagnant Good Soil | The discomfort of deeper pruning — fruit exists, but more would cost more |

**The diagnostic question for secondary gain:**
> "If this changed — if this area of your life actually transformed — what would you lose?"

---

### Somatic Diagnostic Indicators

Soil lives in the body, not just the mind.

**Path Soil — Body:**
- Flat affect, minimal facial movement during emotionally loaded content
- Voice stays at the same register regardless of topic
- No visible physical shift when truth lands
- May lean back or create physical distance when vulnerability is approached
- Shallow breathing pattern, particularly during emotional content

**Rocky Soil — Body:**
- High affect, expressive, responds visibly to resonant truth
- Activation is surface — it doesn't go down, it goes out (gesture, expression, volume)
- Physical collapse or slumping when the energy drops
- May cry easily but the tears are dissipating, not processing
- Breathing is reactive — high when stimulated, crashes when the room is ordinary

**Thorny Soil — Body:**
- Chronically activated — slight tension that never fully drops
- Checking phone, time-awareness, subtle restlessness even in engaged moments
- Breathing often chest-held, not belly-breathing
- Physical tension in shoulders, jaw, neck
- Eyes tend to be scanning rather than settled

**Good Soil — Body:**
- Able to slow down and stay present without visible agitation
- Breathing settles in the room
- Can hold physical stillness with truth
- Eye contact is settled, not anxious or deflecting

### The Pressure Test

Don't diagnose soil only from what you see in the room. The room is a controlled environment. Build a protocol for reading how the client functions outside the session.

**Pressure Test Questions:**
> "Walk me through the last time your faith was under real pressure. Not a crisis — just a hard week. What actually happened inside you?"

> "When you read Scripture on your own, with no one watching and nothing to report — what happens? Be honest."

> "What was the last thing God said to you that you actually didn't want to hear? What did you do with it?"

> "When did you last sit in silence with God for more than 10 minutes? What happened?"

---

### Spiritual Warfare Specificity by Soil Type

The enemy's tactics are not generic. He works the terrain that already exists.

**Against Path Soil:**
Satan doesn't need to argue. He confirms. Every new disappointment becomes evidence for the lie already embedded: *God isn't reliable. Opening up gets you hurt. You were right not to trust.* His strategy is reinforcement, not new attack. Watch for clients who experience "coincidental" relational wounds right at the moment of spiritual openness.

**Against Rocky Soil:**
The attack is always against experience — because that's what this soil is built on. Dry seasons, unanswered prayer, emotional flatness, and relational conflict are weaponized. The lie: *The feeling is gone because God is gone. The breakthrough wasn't real. You're not who you thought you were.* The enemy accelerates identity collapse by targeting the experiential foundation.

**Against Thorny Soil:**
The most subtle attack. He doesn't introduce the thorns — he just waters them. Good things become consuming things. Legitimate responsibilities become all-consuming ones. The busyness feels virtuous; the anxiety feels responsible. The lie is embedded in what isn't said: that the cares of this world are simply reality, not a competing kingdom.

**Against Good Soil:**
The attack shifts to pruning — making the client afraid of what God is cutting. Pride in fruitfulness. Comparison with others' growth. Subtle self-reliance disguised as maturity. The lie: *You've arrived. You've built this. You can manage it from here.*

## Part 2: The In-Session Sequence

A 60-minute session has four movements. Not rigid stages — movements. You may spend most of a session in one. You may cycle through all four. Know where you are.

---

### Movement 1: Landing (0–10 min)

Before any soil work, you need to know what ground the client showed up on today — not their overall soil type, but where they are *right now*.

Questions that get real answers (not social answers):
> "Where are you today — not in life, in your body right now?"
> "What's taking up the most internal space coming into this session?"
> "Is there anything that happened since last time that I need to know before we go anywhere?"

What you're doing: checking nervous system state, identifying what's live, and locating any acute material that would override the session agenda if unaddressed.

Also: recalibrate your read of their soil. What you assessed last session may have shifted. Regression is common. Don't assume last week's ground is today's ground.

---

### Movement 2: Diagnosis and Entry (10–25 min)

Identify what soil you're actually working today and choose your entry point.

| What's Live | Entry Point |
|---|---|
| A specific lie active right now | Lie Archaeology — go directly to it |
| Identity collapse or self-report of failure | Identity Installer — which category is destabilized |
| Diffuse anxiety or scattered presentation | Attention Audit — map where resources are going |
| Emotional flatness despite significant events | Somatic entry — bring them into their body before content |
| A breakthrough or positive experience | Root Audit — go underground immediately |

The diagnostic question you carry into every session:
> "What is the soil doing with truth right now — receiving it, bouncing it, growing shallow with it, or choking it?"

**One entry point. One.** The temptation is to address everything you see. That produces sessions that skim the surface of five things and transform nothing.

---

### Movement 3: The Work (25–50 min)

**Go one layer deeper than the client presented.**

- If they brought a behavior → go to the belief beneath it
- If they brought a belief → go to the wound beneath it
- If they brought a wound → go to the agreement beneath it
- If they brought an agreement → go to who God is in relation to it

The movement of the work is always: *surface → root → truth → identity.*

You are not trying to resolve everything. You are trying to reach the actual issue, plant the actual seed, and make sure it reaches actual soil — not just the client's conscious acknowledgment of it.

Watch the window of tolerance throughout. If the client moves into shutdown (path response), flooding (rocky response), or dissociation, pause the work and return to the body before continuing.

---

### Movement 4: Integration and Stabilization (50–60 min)

This movement is not optional and not administrative. It is soil work.

**1. Name what happened**
Not a summary — an identification of the actual soil work done:
> "Here's what I saw happen today. We went from [presenting issue] down to [actual lie/wound/agreement]. That's significant. That's the real thing."

**2. Bridge to the week**
One concrete thing. Not five takeaways. One:
> "Between now and next session, I want you to [single behavioral or observational task directly tied to what was worked today]."

**3. Anticipate resistance**
For every significant session:
> "You should expect some form of counter-pressure this week. It may look like [specific form based on soil type and what was worked]. When that happens — that's confirmation, not contradiction. Come back and tell me what it looked like."

Then close with prayer. Not as ritual — as declaration. You are planting something. Speak over it.

## Part 3: The Transitional Instability Zone

Every meaningful soil transition has a valley between soils.

The path-soil client whose armor is beginning to crack is not yet rocky soil — they are in a state more vulnerable and more dangerous than either. The old defense is gone or weakened. The new root system doesn't exist yet. This is when clients feel *worse* as they get better, and when they are most likely to quit, rearm, or collapse into a crisis that re-cements the old soil.

Name it. For the coach, and for the client.

---

### Path → Rocky Transition

The armor drops and the client suddenly has access to emotion and vulnerability they haven't felt in years. This can feel like breakthrough — and it is. But it's also terrifying. The compacted person who begins to open often experiences:
- Unexpected emotional flooding
- Grief for time lost
- Fear of re-injury without their defenses
- Temporary identity disorientation ("I don't know who I am without the wall")
- Spiritual warfare escalating — the enemy knows the armor is down

**Coach posture:** Slow down. Do not treat the emotional opening as arrival. Treat it as a new soil that needs time and stability. This is not the time for depth work. It is the time for relational reliability.

---

### Rocky → Good Soil Transition

As the shallow-root client begins developing actual identity structure, the breakthroughs become less frequent and less intense. The high-affect faith they built their Christian identity around begins to quiet. This is healthy — but it doesn't feel healthy. It feels like loss of God. Like the faith is dying.

This is not regression. This is the difference between emotional weather and structural climate.

The experiential dependence doesn't disappear without mourning. Moving toward a faith that holds in the ordinary means giving up the extraordinary as the primary proof. That requires grief before acceptance.

**Coach posture:** Normalize the flatness without abandoning the client to it.
> "What you're feeling right now is not evidence that God left. It's evidence that your roots are going somewhere they've never been. Deep root growth doesn't feel like anything — and the absence of the high feels enormous. Hold on."

---

### Thorny → Good Soil Transition

This transition has the most external cost of any. Clearing the thorns is not just an internal decision — it's a set of actual life changes. Reducing commitments. Saying no to things that mattered. Confronting the identity built on productivity or busyness. The choked client who begins to clear may face:
- Relational friction from people who benefited from the thorns
- Financial disruption if their compensation systems are being addressed
- Identity collapse — "If I'm not doing all of this, who am I?"
- Guilt for the rest and whitespace the scaffold creates

**Coach posture:** Help the client understand that the external disruption is part of the process. The thorns had relational and financial root systems of their own. Clearing them disturbs things. That's not a mistake.

---

### Client Language for the Transitional Zone

> "What you're in right now has a name. You're between soils. The old ground is breaking up and the new ground isn't formed yet. That means you'll feel more exposed, more fragile, and possibly worse than you did before we started. That's not failure. That's what breaking up hard ground feels like from the inside."

> "If you quit right now, you'll re-compact faster than you opened. The enemy knows this. What feels like a sign to stop is almost always a sign to hold."

## Part 4: Secondary Gain — Worked

Surfacing secondary gain is the diagnosis. Working it is the surgery.

### Step 1: Validate the Payoff

Before you challenge the secondary gain, honor what it was doing:
> "That makes complete sense. Given what happened, holding onto [this pattern] kept you safe. It cost something, but it also gave you something real. We're not going to pretend it didn't."

Skip this step and the client defends the secondary gain more aggressively.

### Step 2: Make the Cost Visible and Specific

> "Let's make this concrete. What has keeping [the pattern] actually cost you? Not in general — specifically. What relationships? What did you not build? What did you stop reaching for? What version of yourself hasn't existed because this has been in place?"

The goal: bring the loss into the room with the same emotional weight as the protection.

### Step 3: Exegete the Promise

> "This is giving you something. And it's giving you that something because you don't fully trust that [God / reality / relationship] will provide it another way. What is it promising you?"

Then bring the lie framework directly:
> "That promise is connected to a belief. The belief is [name it]. And underneath that belief is the conviction that God either can't or won't [give them what the secondary gain is delivering]."

Now you have the actual lie. This is where the Lie Eliminator engages.

### Step 4: The Invitation, Not the Prescription

> "You've been paying for this protection with [name the cost]. And you've been getting [name the payoff] in return. What I want to ask you is — do you actually want to keep that trade? Not do you think you should. Do you want to?"

Wait. Let it sit. The answer is the most important moment in this work.

- **If yes:** Commit to it. Go to the lie, then identity work, then behavioral displacement.
- **If no, but can't let go:** You've hit the actual wall. The will is divided. Don't rush past it.
  > "You want to want to. That's real. But something is still holding. Let's stay here. What is the thing that feels too risky to give up?"
- **If no, and they're ready:** Move immediately to the specific change. Vague resolutions evaporate.

### Step 5: Behavioral Displacement

The worst approach is to remove what secondary gain was giving without providing something that addresses the underlying need. The need doesn't disappear when you remove the strategy.

| Secondary Gain | Underlying Need | Displacement Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| Busyness as significance | To matter, to have value | Identity work on inherent worth; one practice of receiving rather than performing |
| Emotional flatness as protection | Safety from further wound | Graduated vulnerability in coaching relationship; corrective relational experience |
| Breakthrough-seeking as confirmation | To know the faith is real | Evidence File; object constancy practices; faith in the ordinary |
| Achievement as security | To not be at the mercy of loss | Scripture work on provision; practices cultivating trust in what can't be controlled |

Build a replacement before you dismantle the original.

## Part 5: The Holy Spirit's Agency as Practical Skill

The Spirit is not a passive backdrop. He is the primary agent. The coach who believes this but has no practical skill for tracking and responding to it will functionally operate as a sophisticated psychologist who prays at the beginning and end of sessions.

### Before the Session: Preparation as Positioning

Before the client arrives, you are not preparing your agenda. You are positioning yourself:
> "Lord, I'm not the farmer here. Show me the ground. Show me what you're already working. Give me eyes for what the presenting issue is covering. And give me restraint — don't let me run ahead of what you're doing."

Write down anything that comes to you. Don't filter it yet — evaluate it in light of the session.

---

### During the Session: Three Tracking Channels

Most coaches track one channel: what the client is saying. Skilled coaches track three simultaneously:

**Channel 1: The client's content and affect**
What they're saying, how they're saying it, what their body is doing.

**Channel 2: Your internal response**
What you're feeling, noticing, or drawn toward that doesn't come from the content alone. This is where the Spirit often works — through an impression, a sense of weight on a specific word, a pull toward a question you didn't plan to ask, a restraint from going where you intended to go.

Don't ignore these. Don't trust them uncritically. Hold them lightly and test them.

**Channel 3: What's notably absent**
What isn't being said. What's being skipped around. What emotion should be present but isn't. The Spirit often works through the gap.

---

### The Discernment Practice: Pause Before You Push

When you have an impulse to go somewhere significant — pause:

The question is not *"Should I say this?"*
The question is *"Is this mine or is this being given to me?"*

If the impulse has a quality of being handed to you — a specific word, an unexpected question, a sense of weight that doesn't match your framework — test it gently:
> "I want to ask you about something that may not connect to what we've been talking about. Is [the thing] part of this?"

Watch the response. The Spirit's prompts tend to land with recognition even when unexpected.

---

### Working With Resistance That Isn't Psychological

Sometimes resistance is not secondary gain or window of tolerance — it is spiritual opposition.

Markers that distinguish this from psychological resistance:
- Sudden inexplicable cognitive fog in the client mid-session when they were tracking clearly
- Disproportionate shutdown on a very particular word or framing
- A sense in the room that something shifted atmospherically, not relationally
- The same wall being hit session after session despite correct psychological intervention

When this is present:
> "Something just shifted in the room. Before we go further — let's pray. Not as a routine — right now."

Pray with authority. Not length. Not volume. Clarity and authority. Name what you're asking the Spirit to do. Then return to the work.

---

### The Restraint Skill

Sometimes the Spirit is asking you to do nothing.

To stop talking. To let the client sit in a silence that is not empty but full. To not fill with a question what God is filling with His presence.

**The marker:** the client goes quiet and something is clearly happening internally. Their face shifts. Their breathing changes. There's a quality of presence in the room that is not being produced by your technique.

Don't break it. The skill is to protect it.

When they come back to the room:
> "What just happened?"

## Part 6: The Somatic Framework

### Understanding the Window of Tolerance

Every client has a zone of nervous system activation where they can process — above the freeze/shutdown response (hypoactivation) and below the flood/panic response (hyperactivation). All productive soil work happens inside this window.

**Hypoactivated indicators (below the window):**
- Flat affect, slumped posture, vacant or glazed eyes
- Slowed speech, monotone, minimal response
- Reporting without emotion, even about painful content
- You feel like you're coaching a wall

**Hyperactivated indicators (above the window):**
- Rapid speech, pressured quality
- Eyes wide, scanning, or darting
- Shallow fast breathing, visible tension in jaw/shoulders
- Emotional flooding — crying that is releasing without processing
- Racing thoughts, jumping topics

Neither state can receive or integrate truth. Before deploying any intervention, get the client in their window.

---

### Grounding Techniques by Soil Type

**For Path Soil — bring them up gently**

The goal is to increase activation without triggering the armor.
- Physical movement: "Before we go there — stand up for a second. Stretch your arms out. Feel your feet. Now sit back down."
- Sensory anchoring: "Tell me five things you can see in this room right now."
- Vocal engagement: Ask them to read something aloud rather than process silently.
- Direct curiosity activation: "I want to show you something. Tell me if this is true for you —"

**For Rocky Soil — bring them down without abandoning them**

The goal is to reduce activation enough for integration.
- Extended exhale breathing: "Breathe in for 4, out for 8. Do that three times before you answer."
- Weight and gravity: "Put both feet flat on the floor. Feel the chair supporting you. Let it hold your weight."
- The slow-down instruction: "I'm not going anywhere. There's no rush. Start over, but slower."
- Name the state: "Your system is moving fast right now. That's okay. Let's bring it down just enough to work."

**For Thorny Soil — help them actually arrive**

The goal is to interrupt the background hum of activation.
- Permission to unload: "Before we start — is there anything from this week still running in the background? Let's put it on the table so it's not competing."
- The arrival question: "On a scale of 1–10, how present are you right now? What would it take to get you one point more present?"
- Breath awareness: "Don't control it — just notice what your breath is doing right now."
- Physical inventory: "Starting at your head and moving down — where is your body holding anything right now?"

---

### Somatic Anchoring of Truth

When a significant truth is received, the most critical and most skipped step is somatic anchoring. Truth received only cognitively does not stay.

**The anchoring sequence:**

1. **Slow the moment:** "Stop. Stay with that. Don't move to the next thing."
2. **Locate it physically:** "Where does that truth land in your body? Not what you think about it — where do you feel it?"
3. **Amplify it:** "Put your hand on that place. Breathe into it. Let that truth be in your body, not just your mind."
4. **Name it with language:** "What word or phrase captures what you're experiencing right now?" (Let them find the language, not you.)
5. **Seal it:** "That's real. That just went somewhere. Remember this — when the lie comes back next week, your body knows this is true. Come back here."

This is neuroscience: the integration of cognitive truth with somatic experience encodes memory in a way that is retrievable under stress. Cognitive-only truth is accessible when calm. Somatically anchored truth is accessible when under pressure.

---

### Reading Body Language as Diagnostic Data

- **Breath holds:** Often marks the moment something significant lands — either truth arriving or armor activating. Note exactly what was being said.
- **Jaw tension:** Typically thorny soil — the body managing what the mind is controlling.
- **Eyes going up-left:** Often accessing internal imagery or memory. Something is being retrieved. Go gently toward it.
- **Eyes going down-right:** Often accessing kinesthetic/felt sense. Let it surface before asking.
- **Micro-expressions of contradiction:** Client says something positive with a micro-expression of pain. Don't let it pass: "Your face just did something different from what you said. What was that?"
- **Swallowing or throat-clearing before speaking:** Often precedes something true and difficult. The body is preparing to say something the mind isn't sure it wants to say.

---

### The Somatic Recovery Protocol

When a client has been brought into dysregulation by the work:

1. **Stop the content work entirely:** "We're going to stop here. Not because something is wrong — because your system needs to come back before we close."
2. **Physical grounding:** Both feet on floor, hands on knees, three slow exhales.
3. **Orientation:** "Look around the room. Name three things you can see. You're here. You're safe."
4. **Narrative containment:** "What we opened today is real and we will come back to it. For now, I want to put it somewhere safe."
5. **Resourcing:** "Before you leave — tell me one thing that is true, solid, and good in your life right now. Not related to what we worked. Just something stable."
6. **Concrete transition:** Give them something physical and ordinary to do in the next hour. Not processing. "Go eat something. Take a walk. Be with a person you trust."

## Part 7: The Crisis Protocol

### Type 1: Window of Tolerance Breach — Flooding

**Immediate response:** Stop all content work completely.
> "I'm stopping here. Not because something went wrong — because your system just hit a wall. We're going to come back to your body before we go anywhere else."

Deploy somatic recovery protocol. Do not attempt to complete the session agenda. The session agenda is now: stabilize and close safely.

After stabilization:
> "What just happened is actually important information — not about what's wrong with you, but about how deep this material goes. We found something significant. We'll go back in when you're resourced."

Do not pathologize the flooding. The client's interpretation will be shaped heavily by your response.

---

### Type 2: Acute Spiritual Attack in Session

**Markers:**
- Client suddenly cannot track what they were tracking
- Extreme shame or condemnation flooding in immediately after a breakthrough
- A client who was engaged becoming suddenly vacant, hostile, or confused — not in a psychological-pattern way but in a discontinuous, abrupt way
- Physical symptoms without physical cause — sudden headache, nausea, pressure

**Response:** Pause the content. Pray with authority and brevity. Name specifically what you are asking the Spirit to do and what you are commanding to stop.

If continuing — do not return to the same material in the same session. Plant a seed, close well, and prepare them for counter-attack in the days ahead.

---

### Type 3: Disclosure of Crisis or Safety Concern

If a client discloses active suicidality, self-harm, abuse, or acute danger:

1. Stop the session agenda
2. Acknowledge and stabilize: "I hear you. I'm not going anywhere. Let's slow down."
3. Assess the level: Is there a plan? Immediate danger? Or disclosure of past or passive ideation?
4. Know your referral pathways before you need them — a licensed therapist you trust, a crisis line, clarity on when you contact authorities

A Kingdom Coaching framework does not make you qualified to treat acute psychiatric crisis. Know the boundary between your role and clinical care, and protect the client by staying inside it.

After safety is addressed:
> "What you just trusted me with matters. We are going to make sure you have the support this requires — and that includes more than what I can provide alone."

## Part 8: Client-Facing Translation

The framework is coach-internal. The client needs a way to understand their own terrain without being clinically labeled or positioned as a subject being analyzed.

### When to Introduce the Framework

Not session one. Introduce when:
- The client is experiencing repetitive patterns they can't break
- They're confused about why growth isn't holding
- You have enough relational trust that they can receive self-knowledge without feeling diagnosed

The soil framework is most powerful when the client *discovers* their soil type rather than being told it.

---

### Client-Facing Language for Each Soil

**Path Soil — the language of armor:**
> "Some of us, through no fault of our own, developed a way of relating to truth where it lands on the outside but doesn't get in. Not because we're hard-hearted — but because we got hurt enough times that our interior learned to protect itself. The protection worked. It also has a cost. Part of what we're doing here is finding whether it's safe to open a little."

Never use "hardened" without this context. "Armored" lands as acknowledgment; "hardened" lands as judgment.

**Rocky Soil — the language of roots:**
> "Some of us respond to truth and God with a lot of feeling — and that feeling is real. But feeling is not the same as formation. What we're looking for isn't more intensity — it's depth. Roots that hold when the emotion is gone and the week is hard and God seems quiet. That's a different kind of work than what you're used to."

**Thorny Soil — the language of resource competition:**
> "Some of us genuinely believe, genuinely want to grow, and are genuinely stuck — not because we don't care, but because everything we have is captured. Attention, energy, time, anxiety — all of it is going somewhere, and that somewhere is not God. The word Jesus uses is 'choked.' Not killed. Choked. The life is there — there just isn't room for it to grow."

Deliver this without guilt. The thorny-soil client is already carrying chronic low-grade guilt. Adding to it shuts the door.

**Good Soil — the language of cultivation:**
> "What I see in you is ground that is actually ready to receive. That doesn't mean there's no work — it means the work we're about to do has somewhere to go. The question isn't whether you can grow. It's what the fruit looks like when we give it the right conditions."

---

### The Client's Self-Diagnostic Tool

Four questions, answered honestly:

1. *"When I read Scripture or receive truth — what actually happens inside me?"*
   (Bounces off / lands emotionally but fades / takes root but gets crowded / grows and stays)

2. *"When I'm under real pressure — what happens to my faith?"*
   (Shuts down / collapses / gets buried under busyness / holds, even when hard)

3. *"Who is God to me right now, today — in my actual life?"*
   (Absent or untrustworthy / present only when things are good / good in theory but not in practice / trustworthy even now)

4. *"What would I have to face if the noise in my life stopped?"*
   (Nothing — I don't know / the fear that my faith isn't real / the emptiness beneath the productivity / I think I can sit with that)

Not a score. Not a label. A mirror.

### The Ongoing Shared Vocabulary

Once a client has internalized the framework, shorthand moves quickly without losing depth:
> "What soil are you in with that?"
> "Is that landing or bouncing?"
> "What's under the busyness right now?"
> "Is this emotion with roots or emotion without them?"
> "What thorn is capturing the most resources this week?"

This creates a language the client can use outside the session — in their journal, in their own self-observation. The framework becomes a tool they carry, not just a lens you hold.

## Part 9: Contraindications

What NOT to do, and why:

| Soil | Contraindicated Intervention | Why It Fails |
|---|---|---|
| Path | Emotional pressure / forced vulnerability | Deepens compaction; confirms that opening is dangerous |
| Path | Information-heavy sessions | Feeds the intellectualization armor without touching it |
| Rocky | Celebrating breakthroughs heavily | Reinforces experience-dependence; confirms the feeling is the thing |
| Rocky | Moving to new content before integrating old | Adds more soil to surface without addressing the rock |
| Thorny | Agenda-heavy, output-driven sessions | You become another demand; models the problem instead of the solution |
| Thorny | Broad lifestyle overhaul recommendations | Overwhelms the already-overwhelmed system; produces guilt, not change |
| All | Working above the nervous system's window of tolerance | Truth received in dysregulation does not integrate |
| All | Coaching faster than the Holy Spirit is moving | You are working the soil; you are not the seed, the rain, or the sun |

## Part 10: The Coach's Soil

The question you must hold continuously:

**What soil are you in, in relation to this client?**

Your compaction will prevent you from going where they need to go. Your shallow roots will make you match their enthusiasm without anchoring them. Your thorns will make you hurried and agenda-driven when they need protected space.

The most important soil work in the room is always your own.

---

## The Farmer's Posture

The farmer in the parable scatters seed generously across all four soils. He doesn't pre-qualify ground before planting. He plants everywhere — and trusts the process.

Your job is not to decide who is "worth" the work or who is "ready enough." Your job is to scatter seed generously while simultaneously working the ground. Some clients will respond faster than you expect. Some will frustrate every expectation.

But the farmer doesn't determine the harvest. He prepares the soil, plants the seed, and trusts the God who makes things grow.

> *"So neither the one who plants nor the one who waters is anything, but only God, who makes things grow."* — 1 Corinthians 3:7

Your tools, your frameworks, your psychological insight — all of it is soil work. The life in the seed is God's.

## For Further Study — Additional Coaching Models

Organized by function. Study in priority order listed at the bottom.

---

### Conversation & Session Structure Models

**CLEAR Model** (Peter Hawkins)
Contract, Listen, Explore, Action, Review. More relational than GROW — the Contract phase establishes what the client needs from *this session* before any work begins. Better for path soil where GROW's directness can feel too exposed too fast. The Listen phase is explicitly about presence and space, not information gathering.

**TGROW**
Topic added before Goal in the GROW sequence. Forces coach and client to agree on what the session is actually about before pursuing a goal. Prevents the common failure of working the presented topic when the real issue is underneath it. Essential for rocky soil clients who present an experiential symptom when the structural issue is the actual territory.

**OSKAR Model** (O'Connor & Lages)
Outcome, Scaling, Know-how, Affirm & Action, Review. Solution-focused structure — builds on what's working rather than diagnosing what isn't. The Know-how phase draws out the client's existing resources before introducing anything new. Communicates that the client already has what they need.

---

### Diagnostic & Assessment Models

**Stages of Change / Transtheoretical Model** (Prochaska & DiClemente)
Five stages: Pre-contemplation, Contemplation, Preparation, Action, Maintenance. The critical insight: match your intervention to the client's readiness stage. Pushing an Action-stage intervention on a Pre-contemplation client produces resistance, not change.

Soil mapping:
- Path soil → Pre-contemplation or early Contemplation
- Rocky soil → oscillates between Preparation and Action without reaching Maintenance
- Thorny soil → Contemplation — they know, they want to, they're not moving
- Good soil → Action and Maintenance

**SPACE Model** (Edgerton & Palmer)
Social context, Physical, Action, Cognition, Emotion. Five-domain assessment that ensures the coach reads the full landscape — not just cognitive content. Most coaching overweights cognition and underweights the physical and social domains, both load-bearing in soil work.

---

### Motivation & Change Models

**Motivational Interviewing** (Miller & Rollnick)
The most evidence-backed tool for working with ambivalence — the defining feature of path soil. Four processes: Engaging, Focusing, Evoking, Planning. The spirit: partnership, acceptance, compassion, evocation. The coach does not argue for change — they evoke the client's own motivation for it. The client, not the coach, makes the case for why change matters. *Requires specific training — see your vault's supervision tags.*

**WOOP** (Gabriele Oettingen)
Wish, Outcome, Obstacle, Plan. One of the most empirically validated goal-achievement frameworks in psychology. The key differentiator: WOOP requires the client to identify the *internal obstacle* before planning. Research shows positive visualization alone decreases motivation. Mental contrasting (desired outcome + honest obstacle) significantly increases follow-through. Directly applicable to thorny soil intervention.

**Implementation Intentions** (Peter Gollwitzer)
Not a full model — a technique. "When X happens, I will do Y." Transforms vague intentions into if-then behavioral scripts. Dramatically evidence-backed for closing the intention-action gap. Every coaching commitment should end in an implementation intention, not a general resolution.

---

### Identity & Being Models

**Ontological Coaching** (Flores, Echeverría, Olalla)
Works at the level of *being* — the observer the client is — rather than behavior or cognition alone. Three domains: Language (words shape reality), Body (embodied posture as identity), Emotions (moods as predispositions for action). Premise: you cannot sustainably change what you do without changing who you are being. Most philosophically aligned model to the Identity Installer work.

**Narrative Coaching** (David Drake)
The story is the soil. Every client is living inside a narrative about themselves, God, and the world — and that narrative determines what they see, what they do, and what's possible. Works at the story level: identifying the dominant narrative, finding the gap between the story and lived experience, and co-creating a new narrative the client can inhabit. Direct parallel to the Lie Eliminator in coaching language. Key tool: *re-authoring* — the client is not a victim of their story; they are the author who can revise it.

**Strengths-Based Coaching** (Buckingham, Clifton — CliftonStrengths)
Sustainable growth comes from developing strengths, not fixing weaknesses. Particularly effective for path soil — enters through what's working rather than what's broken. The coach is not fixing a deficit; they are building from existing ground.

**Co-Active Coaching** (Kimsey-House, Whitworth — CTI)
One of the most widely taught professional coaching frameworks. Works on three axes: Fulfillment (living in alignment with values), Balance (holding multiple perspectives), Process (being present to what's happening right now). Core stance: the client is *naturally creative, resourceful, and whole.* They are not broken. Deeply compatible with the Kingdom Coaching posture.

---

### Spiritual & Transpersonal Models

**Transpersonal Coaching**
Explicitly addresses the spiritual dimension of human experience as a coaching domain — not as a religious overlay but as a legitimate domain of growth. Works with meaning, purpose, peak experiences, and transcendent values. The most directly compatible secular coaching framework with Kingdom Coaching. Provides coaching-appropriate language for what the Holy Spirit does in a session.

**Integral Coaching** (Ken Wilber's AQAL framework)
Four quadrants: Interior-Individual (I — interior experience), Exterior-Individual (It — behavior), Interior-Collective (We — culture, relationships), Exterior-Collective (Its — systems). Growth in one quadrant without corresponding growth in others is partial and unstable. Relevant for thorny soil clients who change internally but not their external environment or relational systems — which will re-create the thorns.
