# Christian Responses to Suffering — Prescriptions Over Presence

A Christian behavioral analysis of what is revealed when a believer responds to another's suffering with: *"Read your Bible more," "Pray more," "Get out and serve more."*

---

## 1. Anxiety Management Masquerading as Pastoral Care

The suffering of another person creates discomfort in the observer. These three prescriptions — Bible, prayer, service — function as **anxiety regulators for the responder**, not care for the sufferer. They restore the responder's sense of order and control. The formula: suffering = spiritual deficiency → more input → less suffering. Clean, manageable, and emotionally costless to deliver.

The tell: the response comes quickly. Presence is slow. Prescription is fast.

---

## 2. Functional Theology Contradicts Stated Theology

The person may confess grace, but the response reveals a works-based operating system underneath. If the solution to suffering is always *more doing*, their functional belief is: **God's engagement with you is proportional to your output.** This is not the gospel they would defend in a theology discussion. It is, however, the gospel their nervous system actually runs on.

This is Kegan's "subject-object" gap in religious form — they haven't made their functional theology an object they can examine; they are *subject* to it.

---

## 3. Avoidance of Lament

These responses short-circuit the lament arc entirely. They jump from suffering to resolution without moving through the valley. This almost always reflects one of two things:

- The responder has **never been given permission to lament** — their own suffering was similarly managed with prescriptions, and they absorbed the frame
- They have **active, unprocessed grief** that proximity to another's suffering threatens to activate

The prescriptions are defensive. They protect the responder from the destabilizing pull of another's unresolved pain.

---

## 4. Low Differentiation of Self (Bowen)

Differentiated people can remain emotionally present with another's suffering without needing to resolve it. Undifferentiated people experience another's distress as a demand on their own nervous system that must be neutralized. The prescription *neutralizes* — it moves the problem back to the sufferer's court.

These responders are not being cruel. They are being fused and reactive while appearing helpful.

---

## 5. Implicit Shame Transfer

The prescriptions carry an embedded diagnosis: *your suffering indicates your spiritual practices are insufficient.* Whether intended or not, the sufferer receives: **you brought this on yourself by not doing enough.** This is the logic of Job's friends. It is also, functionally, the logic of shame — your pain is evidence of your deficiency.

Curt Thompson would note: this is how shame spreads interpersonally — the responder, unaware of their own shame, exports it through the interaction.

---

## 6. Underdeveloped Theology of Suffering

The prescriptions assume suffering is primarily a problem to be solved through spiritual inputs. A mature theology of suffering holds that:

- Suffering can be *formative* (Rom 5:3–5)
- Suffering can be *redemptive* without explanation (2 Cor 4)
- Suffering sometimes has no satisfying cause (Job's final verdict)
- Lament is itself a legitimate spiritual response, not a station on the way to something better (Ps 22, 88)

The person who jumps to prescription has not integrated that **God can be fully present in suffering that does not resolve.** They have a theology of deliverance but not a theology of the wilderness.

---

## 7. The Incarnation Gap

This is the deepest diagnostic level. The Word became flesh and *dwelt among* — not prescribed from a distance. Jesus at Lazarus's tomb already knew he was about to raise him, and he still wept. He asked blind Bartimaeus "what do you want me to do for you?" — the answer was obvious. He asked anyway because presence precedes prescription.

These responses reveal a Christianity that has absorbed the *content* of the gospel but not the *form* of it. The form of the gospel is incarnational: move toward, stay with, bear weight. The prescriptions are the opposite form — they redirect weight back to the sufferer and allow the responder to exit the discomfort cleanly.

---

## 8. What a Healthy Response Looks Like

The inverse of every diagnostic above is instructive. A healthy response is not the absence of Scripture, prayer, or service — it is the refusal to use them as distance-creating devices.

**Move toward before speaking.** Physical and emotional proximity precede any word. The first act is to close the distance, not fill it with counsel. This is the incarnational pattern: the Word *came* before he *spoke*.

**Ask rather than assume.** "What do you need right now?" is not therapeutic hedging — it is what Jesus asked blind Bartimaeus when the answer appeared obvious (Mk 10:51). It honors the sufferer as the authority on their own experience rather than imposing a diagnosis from the outside.

**Tolerate unresolved pain without neutralizing it.** The healthy responder can sit in the silence of an unanswered *why* without reaching for a prescription. Brueggemann's lament tradition requires this: God can be fully present and suffering can continue simultaneously. The two are not in contradiction. The ability to hold that tension is a sign of theological and emotional maturity.

**Lament with, not past.** When words come, the first movement is joining, not redirecting. *"This is terrible"* is a complete pastoral response when it is true. It names reality rather than bypassing it. Allender's witness framework is relevant here: the sufferer needs to be *seen* before they need to be *helped*.

**Differentiate without detaching.** Presence does not mean absorption. A differentiated responder (Bowen) remains emotionally engaged without losing their own footing — they bear weight without either collapsing under it or pushing it away. This is not detachment; it is stability that allows them to stay when an undifferentiated person would flee.

**Defer Scripture and exhortation until they can land.** Biblical truth has a right moment, and it is rarely the first one. The problem with "read your Bible" is not that the Bible is unhelpful — it is that it skips past the person to the prescription. When Scripture is eventually offered, it functions as companionship (*"here is where I find God in what you're describing"*), not correction.

**Check the shame load before speaking.** Thompson's frame applies: before any response goes out, ask — will this land as *you are not alone*, or as *you are deficient*? If the answer is the latter, it does not go out yet.

The operative diagnostic question throughout is: *Am I doing this for them, or to manage myself?*

**Caveat — The Problem of Automatic Patterning Under Dysregulation**

Knowing what a healthy response looks like does not guarantee access to it in the moment. The prescriptive responses described in sections 1–7 are not primarily failures of knowledge — they are automatic patterns, and automatic patterns do not yield to good intentions when the nervous system is dysregulated.

Shame is the most reliable dysregulator in pastoral contexts. Another person's suffering can activate a helper's own unprocessed shame (inadequacy, helplessness, past failures, fear of being exposed as insufficient), and when it does, the automatic pattern fires before conscious intention can intervene. Polyvagal terms are useful here: co-regulation — the capacity to remain present and stabilizing with a suffering person — requires ventral vagal access. Dysregulation collapses that access and the higher-order responses it enables. What emerges instead is the pattern that was already grooved: deflect, prescribe, manage.

This means that the work described in section 8 cannot be performed on demand. It must be prepared for. Siegel's window of tolerance applies: the capacities required for presence — differentiation, lament tolerance, shame awareness, attunement — are only available inside the window. Outside it, a person defaults to whatever automatic structure their history has built.

The implication is direct: **doing your own interior work is not self-care that precedes ministry — it is ministry preparation.** A helper who has not processed their own suffering, examined their functional theology, sat with their own unresolved lament, and had their own shame witnessed will, under pressure, export all of it to the person they are trying to help. Willard's framework applies: the capacities required for a particular response must be cultivated before the moment demands them. You cannot access under pressure what you have not formed in preparation.

The practical question for anyone who intends to minister to suffering people: *Where in my own history did I receive prescription when I needed presence — and have I ever gone back for what I actually needed?*

---

## Summary Diagnosis

You are not looking at bad people or cold people. You are looking at people whose own suffering was managed rather than witnessed, whose Christianity was primarily activistic, and who have not yet been invited into a theology that can sit with unresolved pain without flinching. The prescription is a window into their own interior — what they were given when they needed presence, and what they now give in return.
