# Samuel Cooper — 6-Week Marriage Prep Coaching Plan

**Client:** Samuel Cooper, age 19, U.S.-born male
**Coach:** Kingdom Coaching
**Engagement:** 6-week individual coaching
**Context:** Preparation for marriage to a 22-year-old Hungarian woman currently living in Austria with her parents on a work visa. Client is confident, no career, low-salary job, no college, strong adoptive parental support. Proposal imminent.
**Optimization target:** Expand understanding of responsibility, mindset work, identify historical failure patterns as future risk indicators, prepare for husbandry.

---

## Coaching Framework (Kingdom Coaching)

Each session is built on a three-part model:

1. **Locate Lie Nukes** — deeply held lies ready for circumstantial detonation
2. **Address eternal foundation** — confirm and build daily spiritual scaffolding
3. **Build wound awareness** — identify internal terrain inhospitable to stability, sound perspective, peace, resiliency, and connection to God

**Adoption/complex trauma profile** is active context for all sessions. Handle with care, but never skip.

---

## Session Arc Overview

| Week | Title | Primary Goal |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Identity Audit | Honest baseline: who is he, really |
| 2 | Pattern Archaeology | Surface historical failure modes |
| 3 | Cross-Cultural Reality Check | What he knows vs. what he's assumed |
| 4 | Financial and Career Reckoning | Honest numbers, real plan |
| 5 | Relational Competencies | Actual partnership skills, Husband Contract |
| 6 | Integration and Readiness Assessment | Synthesize, commit, or pause |

# Week 1 — Identity Audit

## Session Goal
Get an honest baseline on who Samuel actually is — not who he presents himself to be. Establish his self-concept, test it against evidence, surface the adoption layer without forcing it, and lay the foundation for everything that follows. Assess whether his spiritual foundation is load-bearing.

---

## Opening Frame (5–10 min)

Open with one direct prompt:

> *"Tell me who you are — not what you do, not your story. Who are you?"*

Let him talk uninterrupted for 2–3 minutes. Listen for structure, not content. Does he anchor his identity in relationships? Achievements? What others say about him? Faith? His own self-perception? What he leads with tells you where his identity is rooted — and where it's vulnerable.

---

## Core Exploration (40–50 min)

### Block 1 — Self-Concept vs. Evidence

- *"Where does your confidence come from? What's it built on?"*
- *"Tell me about a time your confidence was right — you believed you could do something and you did."*
- *"Tell me about a time your confidence was wrong — you were sure, and it didn't go the way you expected."*
- *"What's the hardest thing you've ever had to do — and did you finish it?"*

**What to listen for:** Does he have a track record that supports his confidence, or is his confidence mostly untested self-belief? A young man consistently affirmed by loving adoptive parents may have internalized capability that hasn't been stress-tested by real-world failure. Affirmed but untested confidence collapses differently than earned confidence when marriage applies pressure.

---

### Block 2 — The Adoption Layer

Approach as curiosity, not excavation.

- *"Tell me about your family — what does family mean to you?"*
- *"What did being adopted shape in you — for better or worse?"*
- *"Is there anything about being adopted that still sits with you — questions, feelings, anything unresolved?"*
- *"When you imagine your own family — you, her, kids someday — what does that feel like?"*

The last question matters most. A healthy answer sounds like vision. A wound-driven answer sounds like relief.

**Lie Nukes to watch for:**
- *"I just want a family of my own"* — legitimate desire that becomes idolatry if it's the primary driver
- *"She makes me feel complete"* — emotional fusion; a person cannot be the source of what only God provides
- *"I need to build something real"* — identity through achievement/family as substitute for settled identity in Christ

**Wound awareness:** Adoption, even loving adoption, can install questions — *Am I truly wanted? Do I belong? Can people actually stay?* These questions don't go away at the altar. They go into the marriage and wait for a hard season.

Ask directly but gently: *"When you're honest with yourself, is any part of what's driving this marriage about finding a place where you fully belong?"*

This is not a trap — it's an invitation. If he answers yes, it's workable. If he gets defensive, that's data.

---

### Block 3 — Values Audit

- *"What are your top three values — not what you think they should be, what they actually are based on how you live?"*
- *"What is something you said you believed but acted against in the last year?"*
- *"What does integrity mean to you in practice — not as a definition, but in your actual life?"*
- *"What does your faith mean to you day to day? Is it a foundation or a resource you call on when you need it?"*

**The faith question is non-negotiable.** Samuel's eternal foundation is the scaffolding everything else is built on. If his faith is transactional — God-on-demand rather than daily walk — the spiritual warfare dimension of this marriage is unprotected.

Ask: *"If God said 'not yet' to this marriage — would you hear Him? Would you obey?"*

His answer tells you the condition of his heart more than any other question in Week 1.

---

### Block 4 — Marriage Definition

End here. This is a baseline to return to in Week 6.

- *"What is marriage to you? Define it — not theologically, personally."*
- *"What are you walking into? What does day-to-day husbanding look like in your mind?"*
- *"What are you most afraid of about being married?"*
- *"What do you think will be the hardest part?"*

**Write down his answers to the last two verbatim.** Return to these in Week 6 to measure movement.

---

## Closing Assignment

Before Week 2:
1. Write a one-page answer to: *"Who am I, and where did I come from?"* — reflection, not biography. Include what adoption shaped in him.
2. List 5 commitments made in the last two years — rate each: kept, partially kept, or broken. No explanations yet.
3. Spend one day asking himself when he feels most like himself, and write down the answer.

---

## Successful Session Marker
Samuel leaves with more questions about himself than he arrived with. He feels assessed and seen, even uncomfortably. If he leaves feeling validated and unchanged, the session went soft.

# Week 2 — Pattern Archaeology

## Session Goal
Surface Samuel's historical failure patterns with specificity — not to shame him, but to give him a map of his vulnerabilities before marriage applies pressure to them.

---

## Opening Frame (5–10 min)

Review the 5-commitment list from Week 1 homework. Read it back to him and ask:

> *"What do you see when you look at this list?"*

Let him name the pattern before you do. If he sees it himself, he owns it.

---

## Core Exploration (40–50 min)

### Block 1 — The Quit Map

- *"Tell me about something you started and didn't finish — work, a goal, a commitment, anything."*
- *"What made you stop? What was the moment you decided to quit?"*
- *"Is there a pattern to when you disengage? What has to be present for you to give up on something?"*
- *"When things get genuinely hard — what's your first instinct?"*

**Mapping his quit profile.** Common disengagement triggers in young men with Samuel's profile:
- Stops feeling competent
- Feels unseen or unappreciated
- Expectations aren't clear
- Shame is activated

Knowing which one (or combination) applies tells you exactly what marriage will trigger.

---

### Block 2 — Responsibility History

- *"Tell me about a time someone was counting on you and you didn't come through. What happened?"*
- *"How do you handle it when you've let someone down?"*
- *"Do the people who depend on you trust you to do what you say? How do you know?"*
- *"Have you ever held ongoing responsibility — a job, a role, a relationship — for longer than a year? What did that look like?"*

**About the job specifically:**
- *"How long have you been at your current job?"*
- *"Have you been promoted or given more responsibility? Why or why not?"*
- *"What's kept you there, and what's kept you from moving up or moving on?"*

The job is a microcosm. What he's doing (or not doing) professionally is a preview of behavior under routine low-grade marital pressure.

---

### Block 3 — Relational and Conflict History

- *"Tell me about a significant conflict with someone you care about. How did it start, what did you do, how did it end?"*
- *"When someone you love is disappointed in you — what happens inside you? What do you do with that?"*
- *"Have you ever been in a relationship where things got hard and you pulled back? What happened?"*
- *"How do you handle being wrong? When did you last admit it to someone who mattered to you?"*

**Lie Nukes to watch for:**
- *"I don't run from conflict"* — almost always stated by someone who runs non-obviously (silence, withdrawal, deflection through humor)
- *"I just need time to process"* — legitimate need or trained avoidance? The difference matters enormously in marriage.
- *"I've never really had major relationship problems"* — at 19, this is evidence of limited exposure, not capacity.

**Wound awareness — adoption specific:**
- Fear of conflict because conflict = potential loss of relationship
- Over-accommodation (saying yes when he means no to preserve connection)
- Preemptive withdrawal (pulling back before someone can leave him)
- Anger that comes from shame rather than actual grievance

Ask: *"Is there anything you avoid saying or doing with her because you're afraid of how she'll respond? What are you protecting?"*

---

### Block 4 — Building the Pattern Map Together

Synthesize out loud with him at the end:

> *"Here's what I'm hearing. Tell me if this is accurate or if I'm missing something."*

Name 2–3 specific behavioral patterns — not character verdicts. Give him the chance to correct, refine, or confirm. The goal is a shared map, not a verdict.

---

## Closing Assignment

Before Week 3:
1. Write his **top two historical failure patterns** in his own words.
2. For each pattern: *"In marriage, this could show up as ____."*
3. Write one paragraph answering: *"What is the hardest truth about myself that she needs to know before we marry?"* — He does not have to share it yet. He has to be able to write it.

---

## Successful Session Marker
Samuel has named his failure modes himself, in his own words, with specific examples. He is reflective, not defensive. If he says something like "I never saw it that way before" — or goes quiet in a way that indicates processing rather than shutdown — the session worked. If he was cheerful and untouched throughout, it went soft.

# Week 3 — Cross-Cultural Reality Check

## Session Goal
Surface what Samuel actually knows versus what he's assuming about the woman he's marrying, her world, and the practical terrain of a transnational marriage. Identify where romantic confidence is masking information gaps.

---

## Opening Frame (5–10 min)

Open cold:

> *"Before we get into anything today, I want you to answer one question: What do you actually know about what her parents expect from the man who marries their daughter?"*

Let him answer without coaching. Listen for:
- Does he speak about her parents as stakeholders or obstacles?
- Does he have specifics, or does he generalize?
- Does he say "I don't know" honestly, or fill silence with confidence?

---

## Core Exploration (40–50 min)

### Block 1 — What He Knows vs. What He's Assumed

**Cultural terrain:**
- What do you know about Hungarian family culture — specifically around gender roles, a husband's responsibilities, and a wife's relationship to her parents after marriage?
- Her parents are in Austria. What is her relationship with them like? Do they have influence over her major decisions?
- What's her relationship to her faith, if any? Does it match yours?

**The hard conversations — has he had them?** Ask directly: *Have you and she explicitly discussed each of these? Yes or no.*
- Where will you live — U.S., Austria, or elsewhere?
- Who works, who earns, what's the financial expectation?
- Children — how many, when, how raised (faith, culture, language)?
- Her parents — how involved are they in the marriage? Who do you go to in conflict?
- What does submission and leadership mean to each of you?
- What happens if she gets homesick and wants to move back?

**Lie Nukes:**
- *"Love will navigate the differences"* — romantic bypass of real incompatibility
- *"God brought us together so it will work out"* — faith used to avoid due diligence
- *"She'll adapt"* — male assumption that the woman absorbs the relocation burden
- *"I know her well enough"* — confidence substituting for actual knowledge

---

### Block 2 — The Immigration and Legal Reality

- Do you know what visa pathway she would use to come to the U.S.? *(K-1 fiancée visa — filing fees ~$800–$1,000, total with legal help = $4,000–$12,000+, timeline 6–18 months)*
- During that waiting period, she likely cannot legally work in the U.S. Who supports her?
- Alternatively — if you go to her: Do you have legal right to work in Austria? Do you speak German or Hungarian?
- Has either of you consulted an immigration attorney?

**Wound awareness marker:** If he has no answers here, this is avoidance, not a knowledge gap. Confident men under pressure often don't research what they don't want to see.

---

### Block 3 — Her World, Not Just His Plan

> *"I want you to think about this from her side. She is 22, living with her parents in Austria on a work visa. What are you asking her to give up, and what are you offering in exchange?"*

Let him sit with this.

- What does she say she's afraid of about this marriage?
- What does she say she needs from you that you're not currently giving her?
- Have you asked what her dream life looks like in 10 years — and listened without immediately mapping it onto your own plan?

**Kingdom Coaching lens:** The adoption wound can create a pull toward building family that is *about his need for belonging* more than *serving her needs well*. Name this gently but directly.

Ask: *"Is there any part of this relationship where you're more focused on what you'll gain than what you'll give?"*

This is a lie nuke detonator. If there's an agreement hiding there, this question will crack it open.

---

## Closing Assignment

Before Week 4:
1. Have one conversation with her where he only listens — does not answer, advise, or solve. Ask her:
   - *"What are you most afraid of about marrying me?"*
   - *"What do your parents think about this, honestly?"*
   - *"What would need to be true about our life for you to feel safe and at peace?"*
2. Look up K-1 fiancée visa requirements on USCIS.gov. Bring back 3 facts he didn't know.
3. Write one paragraph: *"What am I asking her to give up, and what am I offering?"*

---

## Successful Session Marker
He leaves with discomfort, not reassurance. He should have questions he didn't have before and a specific homework gap requiring him to do something he's been avoiding. If he leaves fully confident, the session went soft.

# Week 4 — Financial and Career Reckoning

## Session Goal
Build an honest, number-based picture of his current capacity. Expose where he's using faith or parental support as a substitute for personal responsibility. Begin constructing a real 2-year plan.

---

## Opening Frame (5–10 min)

Open with the Week 3 homework debrief — specifically what she said in response to his three questions. This isn't just check-in. What she said informs the financial priorities of this session.

Then pivot:

> *"Today we're going to talk about money and work. Not your dreams — your current reality. I'm going to ask you for numbers, and I need you to be exact, not approximate."*

---

## Core Exploration (40–50 min)

### Block 1 — The Honest Financial Snapshot

Build the numbers together in writing:

- Current monthly income (after tax, actual take-home)
- Monthly expenses — housing, food, transportation, phone, subscriptions, everything
- Savings — how much, right now, exactly
- Debt — credit cards, loans, anything owed
- Net monthly surplus

Then: *"Can you support yourself fully — without any parental help — on your current income?"*

If no: *"What would need to be true for that answer to be yes, and by when?"*

**Critical question:** *"If your parents stepped back entirely — no financial support, no housing subsidy, nothing — what does your month look like?"*

A husband cannot be financially dependent on his parents while leading a household.

---

### Block 2 — The Cost of This Marriage

Give him real numbers. Do not let this be abstract.

**Immigration (if she comes to U.S.):**
- K-1 fiancée visa: filing fees ~$800–$1,000, total with legal help = $4,000–$12,000+
- After arrival: Adjustment of Status (green card) = $1,440+ in filing fees alone
- Timeline: 6–18 months during which she likely cannot work

**Wedding:** Minimal ceremony in the U.S. = $3,000–$8,000 realistic

**First year of married life:**
- Modest apartment: $1,200–$1,800/month depending on city
- Two people eating, transportation, healthcare

Ask: *"If you add it up — immigration, wedding, and first 12 months of shared living — what number are we talking? Have you ever sat down and estimated it?"*

**Lie Nukes:**
- *"God will provide"* — true statement weaponized to bypass preparation. The parable of the talents is more relevant here than manna from heaven. God providing does not mean God will do what Samuel refuses to plan for.
- *"I'll figure it out when I get there"* — not faith. Avoidance dressed in confidence.
- *"My parents will help"* — delegation of husbandly provision to adoptive parents creates structural dependency, stunted maturity, and eventual marital resentment.

---

### Block 3 — Career and Income Trajectory

- What is your job? What does it pay? What does it cap out at?
- What would you need to earn to support a wife and household without parental help? (Build the number from Block 1)
- What's the gap between what you earn now and what you need to earn?
- What are your options for closing that gap? (Trade/vocational training, different job/industry, starting something, military)
- What have you already tried or explored? What have you avoided and why?

**Do not allow:** *"I'll get a better job"* without a specific path. What job? What does it pay? What qualifications? What's the timeline?

**Wound awareness:** Underachievement in young men with complex trauma backgrounds is often not lack of ambition — it's fear of failure, fear of visibility, or unconscious belief that they can't sustain success.

Ask: *"What happens in your mind when you think about building something big — a career, a real income? What's the first thing that comes up?"*

Do not interpret his answer. Let him hear himself.

---

### Block 4 — Parental Support: Asset or Crutch?

- What does their support look like concretely?
- What's their stated view on his readiness to marry?
- Have you talked to them honestly about what this marriage will cost and what your plan is?
- Is there any part of you that assumes they'll catch you if this gets financially hard?

Then: *"Do you think your parents see you as ready — not supportive of you, but genuinely ready?"*

This is different from "are they supportive." Their opinion is data, even if not determinative.

---

## Closing Assignment

Before Week 5:
1. Build a written budget — current income, current expenses, and a projected married-life budget. Bring it to Week 5.
2. Research one specific career/income path that could close the financial gap — job title, required steps, realistic timeline, realistic income ceiling.
3. Have one conversation with his adoptive parents where he tells them his financial plan (not asks for approval — tells them the plan) and asks for their honest assessment.
4. Write one sentence: *"The version of me that is ready to be a husband earns ___ per month and has ___ in savings."*

---

## Successful Session Marker
He has a real number — the gap between his current financial reality and what marriage actually costs. And he has at least the beginning of a plan to close it. The session is successful if he is sober, not deflated. The goal is to replace unexamined confidence with grounded confidence that has something real under it.

# Week 5 — Relational Competencies

## Session Goal
Assess the actual skills of partnership — not the desire to be a good husband, but the demonstrated capacity. Move from theory to behavioral evidence. Introduce the Husband Contract as a commitment artifact.

---

## Opening Frame (5–10 min)

Open with her answers from the Week 3 assignment. Ask Samuel to read back what she said, in her words. Then:

> *"What did you feel when she said those things? What did you do with that?"*

His emotional response to her vulnerability is live data. How he received what she said tells you about his capacity to receive her in the marriage.

---

## Core Exploration (40–50 min)

### Block 1 — Communication Under Pressure

- *"When you're stressed or overwhelmed, what does your communication look like? Does she know that about you?"*
- *"Tell me about a conversation with her that went wrong. What happened on your end?"*
- *"When she's upset — really upset — what do you do? What's your instinct?"*
- *"Do you fix, flee, or stay present? Be honest."*

Most 19-year-old men fix or flee. Staying present with a woman's emotion without solving it or shutting down is a learned skill almost no young man has without deliberate work. This is not a character flaw — it's a gap. Name it as such, and name that it's closable.

**Kingdom Coaching lens:** A husband's presence is a form of ministry. *"Husbands, love your wives as Christ loved the church"* — Christ did not fix or flee. He showed up, bore the weight, and stayed. Samuel should know what standard he's working toward.

---

### Block 2 — Conflict Resolution

- *"Have you and she had a serious disagreement — not small friction, a real conflict? What happened?"*
- *"How was it resolved? Who moved first? What did resolution look like?"*
- *"Is there anything between you that isn't fully resolved — set aside rather than worked through?"*
- *"When you are wrong in a conflict, what does your apology sound like? Say one out loud — pick a real situation."*

**Make him say the apology aloud in the room.** You will immediately see whether it's a real apology (takes responsibility, names the impact, no self-defense) or a performance apology (sorry you felt that way, sorry but...).

**Wound awareness:** For someone with an abandonment wound, conflict resolution is loaded. Apologizing = vulnerability = exposure = potential rejection. Watch for apologies designed to end the conflict quickly rather than repair the relationship genuinely.

---

### Block 3 — Emotional Regulation

- *"When was the last time you lost your temper — or went cold — with someone you love? What triggered it? What did you do after?"*
- *"What do you do when you feel disrespected? Unseen? Like a failure?"*
- *"What does shame feel like in your body, and what do you do when you feel it?"*
- *"Does she know how to reach you when you've shut down? Have you ever taught her how?"*

Most young men expect their partners to intuitively know how to reach them. They have not built a language for their own interior experience and have not translated it into a map their partner can use. A husband who cannot be reached in his own interior is not leading — he's hiding.

---

### Block 4 — The Husband Contract

Introduce it:

> *"I want you to write a husband contract. Not legal — personal. This is you, to her, in writing: what you commit to, what you stand for, and what you will not be as her husband. This is not vows — those come later. This is the man you are deciding to become before you make the vows."*

Give him 10 minutes in-session to write a first draft. It doesn't need to be polished — it needs to be honest.

**Three sections:**
1. *What I commit to give you* — presence, provision, protection, honesty, pursuit
2. *What I commit to work on* — name the patterns from Week 2 directly
3. *What I will not be* — name the failure modes and avoidances he's choosing to fight against

**Kingdom Coaching lens:** This is a declaration — structurally parallel to the Identity Installer format (What I'm Putting On / What I'm Putting Off). The act of writing it is formative, not just documentary.

He takes it home, refines it, and brings the final version to Week 6.

---

### Block 5 — Does She Know Who She's Marrying?

End here.

- *"Does she know your failure patterns — the ones we identified in Week 2?"*
- *"Have you told her the hard truth you wrote at the end of Week 2?"*
- *"Is there anything you've been hoping she won't find out until after the wedding?"*

If he hasn't told her the hard truth — this is the assignment. Not optional.

---

## Closing Assignment

Before Week 6:
1. Finish the Husband Contract. Bring the final version to Week 6.
2. Tell her the hard truth from Week 2's assignment — actually tell her, not perform it. Report back what happened.
3. Ask her: *"Is there anything I do that makes you feel unsafe, unseen, or unheard? I want to know."* Listen. Write down what she says. Bring it.

---

## Successful Session Marker
He has a written Husband Contract in hand. He has had the hard conversation with her. He understands that communication, conflict, and emotional presence are learnable disciplines — not just personality traits. He is no longer operating on the assumption that love is sufficient.
