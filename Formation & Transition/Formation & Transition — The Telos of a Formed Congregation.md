# Formation & Transition — The Telos of a Formed Congregation

---

## Purpose

This document answers one question for the Formation & Transition track: what is a formed congregation actually displaying, and why is that communal expression distinct from the aggregate of individually formed disciples?

It is the theological floor every Phase 2 design decision rests on. When a design choice is evaluated — *does this architecture move the congregation toward its telos?* — this document supplies the answer to what that telos is.

What this document does NOT cover:
- How individual or communal formation works mechanically (→ *Trained, Not Consumed*)
- What a congregation must exhibit before genuine ekklēsia function is possible (→ *Assembled, Not Attending*)
- What the one-another functions require to operate (→ *Formation & Transition — The Allēlōn Functions*)
- What historical architectures have produced formation at scale (→ *Formation & Transition — Scaling Mechanism Research*)
- What modern Western constraints any formation architecture must work within (→ *Formation & Transition — Modern Western Constraint Matrix*)

Read in conjunction with those documents. This document governs the direction they are all serving.

## The Communal Foundation — Image-Bearing Was Never Solitary

The image-bearing vocation of Genesis 1:26–28 is architecturally communal from its first statement:

> "Let us make man in our image, after our likeness. And let them have dominion over the fish of the sea and over the birds of the heavens and over the livestock and over all the earth and over every creeping thing that creeps on the earth." (Genesis 1:26, ESV)

The deliberation is plural; the commission is given to *them*. The mandate that follows — "Be fruitful and multiply and fill the earth and subdue it" — cannot be fulfilled by a single person. It is reproductive, territorial, and multigenerational by design. The image-bearing vocation requires a community that extends across time and place, and no individual can bear the image into creation in the way the mandate envisions.

This is not a secondary observation about Genesis 1. It is the foundational structure: the image of God is given to humanity as a community and is meant to be displayed by humanity as a community. The lone image-bearer, however well-formed, is not the completed picture.

The Fall fractures this in both directions simultaneously. Genesis 3–4 is not a single story of moral failure but two: the broken vertical (Adam hiding from God, the source of the image) and the broken horizontal (Cain killing Abel, the image-bearing community destroying itself from within). Image-bearing collapses in both directions at once, and its recovery requires both dimensions to be addressed.

Israel receives the image-bearing vocation in explicitly corporate form:

> "You shall be to me a kingdom of priests and a holy nation." (Exodus 19:6, ESV)

Neither designation is individual. A kingdom of priests means the entire community functions as mediators — representing God to the nations and the nations to God. Israel's corporate life, in its visible way of being together, is to display to surrounding nations who the covenant God is. The Isaiah servant-figure extends this vocation to the nations: "I will make you as a light for the nations, that my salvation may reach to the end of the earth" (Isaiah 49:6, ESV). The witness is the community's shared life, not only the proclamation of its members.

The ekklēsia inherits this vocation. It does not invent it.

## The Ekklēsia as New Humanity

Three New Testament texts carry the primary weight for understanding what the ekklēsia is and what it displays.

### 1 Peter 2:9 — Corporate Identity Stated

> "But you are a chosen race, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people for his own possession, that you may proclaim the excellencies of him who called you out of darkness into his marvelous light." (ESV)

Peter applies Exodus 19:6 to the ekklēsia without modification. Every designation is corporate. The telos is proclamation — but notice the form: it is the community's visible existence and way of life, not only its verbal testimony. The ekklēsia proclaims by being what it is together. The corporate life is the witness.

### Ephesians 2:19–22 — The Joined Community as God's Dwelling

> "So then you are no longer strangers and aliens, but you are fellow citizens with the saints and members of the household of God, built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets, Christ Jesus himself being the cornerstone, in whom the whole structure, being joined together, grows into a holy temple in the Lord. In him you also are being built together into a dwelling place for God by the Spirit." (ESV)

Paul's verb is *synoikodomeisthe* — being built together simultaneously. The structure exists only when the stones are joined. A dispersed collection of Spirit-indwelt individuals is not the same thing as the ekklēsia assembled and joined: the dwelling of God in the Spirit is located in the joined community in a way that the aggregated individuals, unjoined, do not replicate. This is Paul's point — not that individual Spirit-indwelling is denied, but that the *together* is where something occurs that the scattered parts do not produce. [*Interpretive note: Reformed theology generally affirms corporate indwelling while maintaining individual Spirit-indwelling as prior. This document reads Ephesians 2:22 as making a genuine additional corporate claim — both are true, but the corporate is Paul's argument here, and softening it loses the force of the passage.*]

### Ephesians 4:11–16 — The Corporate Telos Named

> "...to equip the saints for the work of ministry, for building up the body of Christ, until we all attain to the unity of the faith and of the knowledge of the Son of God, to mature manhood, to the measure of the stature of the fullness of Christ... the whole body, joined and held together by every joint with which it is equipped, when each part is working properly, makes the body grow so that it builds itself up in love." (ESV)

The telos is named: *mature manhood, the measure of the stature of the fullness of Christ.* But it is a corporate telos — "we all attain." No individual bears the fullness of Christ. The range of gifts (apostle, prophet, evangelist, shepherd-teacher) is distributed across the body precisely because no single member carries the full representational weight. The fullness of Christ is displayed by the whole body functioning together: differentiated gifts operating in unity, every joint contributing, the body building itself up in love. [*Interpretive note: "We all attain" has a legitimate alternative reading — all individual believers reaching eschatological completion rather than the congregation displaying Christlike fullness corporately now. The communal reading is adopted here as the more contextually supported reading given Paul's body-metaphor argument throughout Ephesians 4:1–16, but this is an argued position, not exegetical consensus.*]

1 Corinthians 12 reinforces the same architecture. The *sympatheia* Paul identifies in verse 26 — shared suffering and shared honor — is not an incidental feature of Christian community but a designed property of the body. The body cannot function with the eye telling the hand it has no need of it (v. 21). The mutual dependence is structural, not optional. Individual formation is the necessary precondition of body function; communal function is the designed output.

## The Telos — What a Formed Congregation Displays

A formed congregation is not a collection of formed individuals. It is a body whose joined life displays what no individual formation alone can produce:

**God's presence in the assembled life.** The joined structure is the dwelling (Ephesians 2:22). A formed congregation is not merely a place where God's people gather; it is a community whose joined life is the location of God's manifest presence in a way that dispersed individuals do not replicate. This is why the gathering matters — not for attendance but because the *together* is where something happens that the separated parts do not produce.

**The fullness of Christ through differentiated unity.** No individual bears the fullness of Christ (Ephesians 4:13). The range of gifts, formation stages, temperaments, and capacities in a body functioning together approximates a fullness that no single Christlike person can display. This is the theological reason why formation of the whole body — not merely formation of the pastor or the spiritually gifted — is the design. The undeveloped member is a missing note in what the fullness requires.

**Corporate witness legible to the world.** "By this all people will know that you are my disciples, if you have love for one another" (John 13:35, ESV). The world's recognition is triggered by the *together* of the community's life. This is not an individual virtue the world notices; it is a communal display that cannot be faked by isolated individuals performing well. The early church's witness was the visible fact of their common life — Jews and Gentiles, slave and free, at the same table — not primarily their doctrinal claims.

**The eschatological preview.** "Behold, the dwelling place of God is with man. He will dwell with them, and *they* will be his people" (Revelation 21:3, ESV). The covenant formula in its final form is plural. The formed congregation is not yet the eschatological community — but it is its visible beginning, its present sign, its already-and-not-yet expression. A functioning ekklēsia previews the new creation community in which the image-bearing vocation is permanently and perfectly fulfilled.

## Virtue as Reflex — What the Telos Looks Like in Practice

N.T. Wright's recovery of New Testament virtue ethics (*After You Believe*, 2010) supplies the practical precision the telos statement requires.

The New Testament's formation goal is not rule-following by willpower, nor case-by-case moral deliberation under pressure. It is the formation of character — *hexis* in the Aristotelian sense, *paideia* in the NT sense — in which Christlike response has become the trained reflex of a formed person. Hebrews 5:14 names the mechanism: "solid food is for the mature, for those who have their powers of discernment trained by constant practice to distinguish good from evil." The word *trained* is *gumnazō* — the gymnasium word; discernment is produced by repeated exercise, not by a single act of instruction.

Ephesians 4:13 applies this to the community: "mature manhood, to the measure of the stature of the fullness of Christ." A mature community does not have to deliberate in the moment whether to respond to conflict with patience, to suffering with compassion, to the outsider with welcome. These are the reflexive expressions of a community whose character has been formed by sustained practice over time.

This is the telos this track is building toward. It cannot be produced in a short program. It cannot be measured by doctrinal knowledge or attendance. It is visible in how a community functions under pressure, in conflict, with strangers, and in grief — specifically in whether those responses are labored (trying to comply with a standard) or natural (expressing a formed character).

## The Spirit as Primary Agent

Every formation mechanism this track identifies — *sympatheia*, *allēlōn* functions, disciplined practice, communal accountability, the small-group container — creates conditions. The Spirit is the one who forms.

> "And we all, with unveiled face, beholding the glory of the Lord, are being transformed into the same image from one degree of glory to another. For this comes from the Lord who is the Spirit." (2 Corinthians 3:18, ESV)

The passive voice is the key: *are being transformed.* The congregation beholds; the Spirit transforms. The congregation creates conditions — sustained attention to Christ, embodied practice, communal accountability, between-session disciplines — and the Spirit works through those conditions. No architecture, however faithfully designed, produces the telos this document describes. The architecture removes obstacles and opens space; formation is received, not manufactured.

This is the mechanism check every Phase 2 design decision must pass: *does this structure create conditions for the Spirit's forming work, or does it position the structure itself as the transformation agent?* The second posture is not a minor error — it is a categorical mistake about who is doing the forming.

## The Diagnostic Layer — What Blocks the Telos

The existing track documents name the congregational diagnostic layer: the structural patterns that block the communal telos from being displayed, regardless of the theology the congregation holds or the intentions of its leaders.

**Consumption architecture** (*Assembled, Not Attending*) — the congregation organized around the delivery of religious goods to passive recipients. In a consumption architecture, the body cannot build itself up through every joint's contribution (Ephesians 4:16) because the structure suppresses contribution in favor of reception.

**Parallel presence** (*Formation & Transition — The Allēlōn Functions*) — physical co-location without the mutual knowledge that the *allēlōn* commands require to function. People occupying the same space without the registration of one another's actual states cannot bear one another's burdens (Galatians 6:2), mourn with those who mourn (Romans 12:15), or stir one another up to love and good works (Hebrews 10:24).

**Platform delivery** (*Assembled, Not Attending*) — formation channeled through a single communicator rather than distributed through the body's differentiated gifts. Platform delivery is not inherently wrong; it becomes a structural block when it is the *only* formation mechanism, because the fullness of Christ distributed across the body's gifts cannot be carried by any single voice.

These are not merely strategic problems. They are diagnostic indicators that the image-bearing telos is being blocked at the structural level — that the congregation is organized in ways that make the communal display impossible regardless of the spiritual quality of its individual members.
