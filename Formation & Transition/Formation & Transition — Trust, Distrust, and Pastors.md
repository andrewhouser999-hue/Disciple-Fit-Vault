# Trust, Distrust, and Pastors

**What this is:** A plain-language synthesis of three related questions about trust and pastors — why congregations distrust pastors, why pastors distrust others, and how much pastors actually understand about their own distrust. **When to use it:** as background understanding before or during any conversation, briefing, or working relationship with a pastor in the Formation & Transition track. **Why it matters for this track:** the track's whole premise is that congregations can't be formed past a leader who hasn't been formed himself, and trust — both how much a pastor is trusted, and how much he trusts others — is one of the clearest outward signs of whether that inner formation has actually happened. **Who this is for:** Andrew's own working understanding, not client-facing material.

This document does not try to resolve anything theologically. It lays out three separate but related pictures — the trust congregations extend to pastors, the trust pastors extend to others, and the evidence (or lack of it) about whether pastors see any of this in themselves — and it is honest, throughout, about which parts are well-supported by evidence and which parts are reasonable inference stacked on top of related but not identical research.

---

## Part 1: Why congregations distrust pastors

Trust in pastors, as a category, has genuinely declined. Gallup's long-running "honesty and ethics" poll — which asks Americans to rate the honesty of people in various professions — showed clergy drop out of the "high trust" tier for the first time in the poll's history in the early 2000s, and clergy trust has not recovered since. This isn't a vague cultural mood; it traces to a specific, stacked sequence of real events, plus a few structural conditions that make the damage stick once it happens.

**1. A dense run of high-profile scandals, not just occasional ones.** Over the last twenty-five years, several major, well-documented failures have hit in close succession: the Catholic Church's abuse crisis, which broke into public view with the *Boston Globe*'s 2002 investigation; the Southern Baptist Convention's own abuse crisis, documented in the *Houston Chronicle*'s 2019 "Abuse of Faith" series, which found roughly 380 credibly accused pastors and youth leaders over twenty years; and a string of celebrity evangelical pastors whose personal conduct publicly contradicted their platform — Mark Driscoll and the collapse of Mars Hill, Bill Hybels and Willow Creek, Ravi Zacharias, Carl Lentz at Hillsong. Any one of these, on its own, would be an isolated story about one bad leader. Piled up over two decades, they function differently: they turn "a pastor did something wrong" into "pastors, as a category, are a risk" in the public mind.

**2. The pastoral role has no fallback trust to rely on.** A CEO's authority rests mostly on competence — if his personal life is a mess but the company performs, people often still trust his judgment at work. A pastor's authority is different: it rests on being a credible moral and spiritual example, not on a separate, independent skill set. That means when a pastor's private conduct contradicts what he teaches, there's no separate "he's still good at the job" fallback to preserve. The failure doesn't just damage that one relationship — it can undercut the entire basis on which people were trusting him in the first place.

**3. The fastest-growing part of American Protestantism has the least outside accountability.** Independent, non-denominational churches — the model growing fastest today — typically answer to no outside body. The elders or board members who could remove a pastor are usually people he selected himself. Older denominational structures (Presbyterian, Methodist, Catholic) at least have some outside authority that exists independent of the local pastor, even when that outside authority is slow-moving or self-protective. The newer, more autonomous model concentrates a lot of unchecked power in one person — which is both a cause of some of the worst failures and a reason those failures produce lasting distrust once they surface.

**4. Nothing stays local anymore.** Before the internet, a pastoral failure was mostly a local, containable event — known to that congregation, maybe that town, and then it faded. Now it's a permanent, searchable national story. This probably hasn't changed how often pastors actually fail; it's changed how visible and how permanent each failure is once it happens, which functionally raises how risky pastors seem as a group, even if the underlying rate of misconduct hasn't moved much.

**5. Pastors lost their "neutral" status by becoming visibly partisan.** Especially among white evangelical clergy, the last two decades saw pastors increasingly read as political actors — endorsing candidates, weighing in on culture-war fights from the pulpit — rather than as neutral moral voices standing outside political tribes. That cost them a specific kind of trust: the trust that comes from being above the fight. You can't be seen as a partisan combatant and a trusted neutral authority figure at the same time; those two identities compete for the same credibility.

**6. Ordinary people now have language for manipulation that used to go unnamed.** Books like David Johnson and Jeff VanVonderen's *The Subtle Power of Spiritual Abuse*, and more recent work by trauma specialist Diane Langberg, gave lay Christians vocabulary to describe controlling pastoral behavior — guilt used as a compliance tool, "shepherding" that's really control — that previously had no name and so went unrecognized. Once people have the words for a pattern, they start noticing it in pastors who may not be doing anything different than pastors always did; the behavior may be old, but the *recognition* of it is new, and it drives distrust up independent of any actual change in pastoral conduct.

**Bottom line for Part 1:** the decline in trust toward pastors is real, measurable, and traceable to specific causes — not just "people are more cynical now" in the abstract.

## Part 2: Why pastors distrust others

This is a different and less discussed question — not why people distrust pastors, but why pastors, as a group, often carry a real guardedness or distrust toward the people around them. This isn't primarily a personality trait. It's substantially something the role itself produces, through a set of structural conditions that would push almost anyone in that position toward guardedness over time.

**1. The relationship only runs one direction.** Congregants confide in the pastor — about their marriages, their finances, their doubts, their failures. The pastor is not expected to confide back at the same depth. Over years, that one-way flow of disclosure never builds the kind of mutual vulnerability that ordinary friendship and trust are made of. Barna's "State of Pastors" research found that a majority of pastors report having few or no close friends they can be fully honest with — including inside their own congregations. That's not incidental; it's what the structure of the role produces, regardless of the individual pastor's personality or effort.

**2. Pastors carry secrets they can never set down or trade back.** Confession, marital crises, financial trouble — pastors accumulate other people's most sensitive information constantly, with no equivalent flow coming back to them, and usually no one they're allowed to process any of it with. That relentless one-directional weight wears down a person's general expectation that relationships will be reciprocal, which is part of what trust actually requires.

**3. Many pastors have lived through a specific betrayal, not an abstract risk.** A meaningful share of pastors experience a forced termination, a no-confidence vote, or a board takeover at some point in their careers — often initiated by people who had, until that point, been trusted allies. For a pastor who's lived through this, wariness toward "the elder board" or "the leadership team" isn't paranoia in the clinical sense — it's a learned response to something that actually happened to them, once or more than once.

**4. Some people approach a pastor with an agenda, not a relationship.** A portion of the access people seek to a pastor is instrumental — someone wanting a past decision reversed, wanting the pastor's authority borrowed to win an internal argument, wanting cover for a personal agenda. Pastors learn to spot this fairly quickly, and the skill of spotting it tends to generalize outward: new people can get read through a lens of "what does this person actually want from me" before an authentic relationship has a chance to form on its own terms.

**5. Pastors are the default target when something in the church goes wrong.** Worship style, a financial decision, a staffing choice, a doctrinal disagreement — whatever's unhappy in a congregation often gets routed back to the pastor personally as the cause, even when the actual decision was made collectively or by someone else. Years of absorbing blame that isn't really about him personally teaches a pastor to expect criticism disguised as feedback, which naturally makes him less inclined to take people's stated concerns fully at face value.

**6. The job doesn't allow him to look weak.** Pastoral authority requires projecting stability and confidence; showing real doubt, fear, or need is often professionally risky, because congregations frequently expect their pastor to be the strong one. That forces a kind of ongoing performance between the pastor and everyone around him — and it's hard to trust people when you can't tell whether they like *you* or just the composed version of you they're allowed to see.

**7. In some traditions, distrust of people gets theological cover.** In more Reformed or conservative theological streams, a strong doctrine of human sinfulness ("the heart is deceitful above all else," Jeremiah 17:9) sometimes gets applied not just to salvation but to everyday relationships — producing a working framework where guardedness toward people gets treated as spiritually appropriate rather than examined as a wound that needs attention. That theological framing can let real relational distrust hide in plain sight, dressed up as doctrinal faithfulness instead of being named as something that needs healing.

**8. For some pastors, the distrust predates the pulpit entirely.** Ministry disproportionately attracts people whose early life shaped them toward caretaking or hypervigilance as a way of surviving — always managing, always composed, always available. The role rewards exactly that pattern instead of surfacing it for repair, so for these pastors, the guardedness toward people didn't start in ministry; ministry just gave it a permanent stage that never requires it to be examined.

**Bottom line for Part 2:** almost none of this is really about pastors being unusually suspicious people by nature. It's a role that structurally punishes vulnerability, rewards guardedness, and delivers at least one real experience of betrayal to most people who stay in it long enough. The distrust is a reasonable adaptation to real, lived conditions — not a character flaw to be judged.

## Part 3: How much do pastors actually understand about their own distrust?

This is the sharpest and least answerable of the three questions, and it deserves an honest answer about the limits of what's actually known, rather than a confident-sounding one.

**There is no research that directly measures this.** No study asks, and answers, "how self-aware is a given pastor about his own pattern of distrusting others, and why he distrusts them." What exists instead is a set of related, indirect bodies of evidence — each of which tells you something adjacent to the question, but none of which actually answers it. They're worth walking through individually, because they point in different directions and it matters which kind of evidence you're relying on.

**1. Self-report surveys measure the experience, not the insight.** Barna and similar surveys ask pastors to report things like isolation, loneliness, or a lack of close friendships — and pastors report these things at high rates. But saying "I don't have close friends" or "I keep my guard up around my congregation" is not the same as saying "I understand that I distrust people, and I understand why." A pastor can accurately report the symptom (loneliness) while having no real insight into it as something he himself is doing — he can explain it entirely as "ministry is just lonely" or "people just aren't trustworthy," which is itself a way of not seeing his own part in the pattern. Surveys can't tell the difference between genuine self-understanding and a well-rehearsed, professionally acceptable story about how hard ministry is.

**2. Studies of leaders in general (not specifically pastors) suggest people are worst at seeing exactly this kind of thing about themselves.** In organizational research on leadership, when you compare how leaders rate themselves against how the people around them (staff, peers) rate them — what's called a 360-degree or multi-rater comparison — the biggest gaps consistently show up on relational qualities: how open the leader is, how well he takes feedback, how defensive he gets. On task skills — is he good at the actual job — self-ratings and other-ratings tend to line up much more closely. People are relatively accurate about their competence and relatively inflated about their own openness and trust. No one has run this specific kind of study on pastors directly, as far as this synthesis found — but there's no obvious reason pastors would be an exception, and some reason to think the gap might be worse for them, since the job itself requires *appearing* warm and open regardless of whether that's actually true underneath.

**3. A major theological framework used in pastoral consulting assumes low self-awareness as the normal condition, not the exception.** Edwin Friedman (*Generation to Generation*, *A Failure of Nerve*) and Peter Steinke (*Healthy Congregations*) built entire frameworks for working with church leaders around the idea that most leaders — pastors very much included — operate with a low level of what they call "self-differentiation," meaning their anxiety-driven patterns, including guardedness and distrust, run largely below the level of conscious awareness. This is taken seriously across the field of church consulting, and it's the source of the "regulatory capacity" language already used elsewhere in this track. But it's important to be clear about what kind of evidence this actually is: it comes from clinical case observation generalized into a theory, not from a study that measured a large group of pastors and reported a number. It's a respected framework claim, not a data point.

**4. The training model used to form pastors is itself built on distrusting self-report.** Clinical Pastoral Education (CPE), which many ministers go through as part of ordination training, is built entirely around having a supervisor review specific, word-for-word transcripts of a minister's actual pastoral conversations and point out patterns the minister didn't see in himself. The whole design assumes that reflection alone — a pastor simply thinking about his own behavior — isn't reliable enough on its own; it requires an outside observer looking at specific evidence. That the field built this model at all is a meaningful signal: the people who train pastors for a living don't trust self-assessment as sufficient. But again, this tells you what the training establishment *believes* about self-awareness — it isn't itself a direct measurement of how self-aware any given pastor actually is.

**5. Attachment research suggests the specific pattern in question may work by hiding itself.** If a pastor's guardedness comes from what attachment researchers call a "dismissing" or avoidant attachment style, the defining feature of that style is that it works by keeping a person's own relational needs and threat-responses out of conscious awareness — that's the actual mechanism, not a side effect of it. This is well-established in general adult attachment research (particularly Mary Main's work using the Adult Attachment Interview), but essentially no one has studied clergy specifically through this lens. It's a strong theoretical prediction — if this is what's driving a pastor's distrust, the pattern would be specifically designed, in a sense, to stay invisible to him — but it's borrowed from research on the general population, not evidence about pastors themselves.

**Bottom line for Part 3, stated plainly:** what actually exists is (a) self-report data that can't tell real insight apart from a practiced narrative, (b) general leadership research suggesting this exact kind of self-knowledge is where people are least accurate, (c) a respected theological framework that assumes low self-awareness as the default rather than the exception, and (d) a whole training system for ministers that was built specifically because self-reflection alone wasn't considered trustworthy. None of that is a direct measurement of any individual pastor's self-awareness. The most honest reading is this: the field's own response to the problem — building training models that rely on an outside observer rather than on a pastor's ability to see himself — is itself the best evidence available, and what it shows is that self-awareness in this area can't be assumed. It has to be checked from the outside, case by case, rather than taken at a pastor's own word.

## What this means going into any working relationship with a pastor

Put together, these three parts suggest a specific posture rather than a specific technique: assume that whatever a pastor says about his own openness, trust, or relational health is a starting hypothesis, not a settled fact — not because pastors are dishonest, but because the role itself both produces guardedness and makes that guardedness hard for the person carrying it to see in himself. The congregation's distrust of him (Part 1) and his own distrust of others (Part 2) can both be true and largely invisible to him at the same time (Part 3) — and the way to find out which is actually happening is through direct observation of how he behaves under real relational pressure, not through asking him and taking the answer at face value.
