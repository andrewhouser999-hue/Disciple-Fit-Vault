# Swing Trading Reading Curriculum

Beginner → Intermediate → Active Trader. No fluff. Build a real edge or don't trade.

> [!danger] Core Warning
> Reading these books in isolation = 50% knowledge, 0% edge. Any single layer alone is insufficient. The combination, applied in sequence, is the actual edge.

## Phase 1 — Foundation + Early Application

*Read, then paper trade simultaneously.*

---

### 1. Stan Weinstein — Secrets for Profiting in Bull and Bear Markets (1988)

[Buy on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Secrets+Profiting+Bull+Bear+Markets+Weinstein)

> [!info] Who Is Weinstein
> Former publisher of the *Professional Tape Reader* newsletter for 20+ years. Not an academic — a practitioner who watched institutional money move in and out of stocks over decades.

**Core Framework: Stage Analysis**

Every stock and sector moves through four stages:

| Stage | Name | What's Happening | Action |
|-------|------|-----------------|--------|
| 1 | Base / Accumulation | Price flat, volume quiet. Institutions quietly buying. | Wait. Do not buy. |
| 2 | Uptrend / Advancing | Price breaks above 30-week MA with volume expansion. | **Only stage to buy longs.** |
| 3 | Top / Distribution | Price stalls near highs, erratic volume. | Exit longs. Do not add. |
| 4 | Downtrend / Declining | Price collapses below 30-week MA. | Short or stay out entirely. |

**Key Tools:**
- 30-week moving average (primary filter — is price above or below?)
- Relative strength versus the market (is the stock leading or lagging?)
- Volume confirmation (expands on breakouts, contracts on pullbacks)
- Sector analysis (buy leading stocks in leading sectors, not laggards)

**Stage 2 Buy Criteria:**
- Stock has formed a base for several months
- Price breaks out above the base on above-average volume
- 30-week MA is flattening or turning up
- Stock acting better than the market

**Strengths:**
- Most actionable framework in this entire curriculum. Open a charting platform and apply it today.
- Forces institutional-flow thinking, not retail noise.
- Applies equally to stocks, sectors, and market indices.
- "Only buy Stage 2" alone eliminates the majority of losing trades beginners make.

**Weaknesses:**
- Published 1988. No ETFs, no options, no algo discussion.
- 30-week MA is slow — Stage 2 confirmation means you've already missed some of the move.
- No position sizing, risk management, or psychology.
- Less clear in choppy, range-bound markets.

> [!tip] How to Read It
> Cover to cover. Written plainly, ~300 pages, readable in 2–3 weeks. Pay extra attention to the sector analysis chapters — most readers underinvest there.

> [!danger] Brutal Reality
> This was the most glaring omission from the original book list. Without stage analysis as a primary framework, you are a chart reader without a map.

### 2. John Murphy — Technical Analysis of the Financial Markets (1999)

[Buy on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Technical+Analysis+Financial+Markets+John+Murphy)

> [!info] Who Is Murphy
> Former CNBC technical analysis commentator, CMT. Career spent translating institutional TA for broader audiences.

**What It Teaches:** The TA textbook. Covers nearly every classical concept — Dow Theory, trendlines, support/resistance, reversal and continuation patterns, volume, moving averages, RSI/MACD/Stochastics, intermarket analysis, Elliott Wave intro, candlestick intro.

**Most Valuable Sections for Swing Traders:**
- Trendlines and channels
- Support and resistance
- Volume analysis
- Moving averages
- Oscillators — what RSI, MACD, Stochastics actually measure mechanically
- Intermarket analysis — bonds ↔ stocks ↔ commodities ↔ currencies

**Strengths:**
- Intermarket analysis chapters provide macro context most purely technical traders ignore entirely.
- Comprehensive enough to answer most foundational questions without multiple books.
- Oscillator chapters explain indicator mechanics, preventing misuse.

**Weaknesses:**
- Charts from the 1980s–1990s. Algo-dominated markets behave differently in ways that matter.
- Zero probability data. Describes patterns, not how often they succeed.
- No position sizing, trading system, or risk management framework.

> [!tip] How to Read It
> **Do not read cover to cover.** This is a reference book. Read: trendlines, support/resistance, volume, moving averages, and the intermarket analysis section. Reference everything else as questions arise while trading.

### 3. Mark Douglas — Trading in the Zone (2000)

[Buy on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Trading+in+the+Zone+Mark+Douglas)

> [!info] Who Is Douglas
> Trading psychology coach and consultant for 20+ years. Former futures trader who experienced significant losses early on, driving his research into the psychological dimension of trading.

**Core Thesis:** Markets are random at the individual trade level. Any single trade can go either way regardless of setup quality. Over a large sample size, a positive-expectancy system produces consistent results — *if* the trader executes without emotional interference. Human beings are not wired for probabilistic thinking, and that disconnect destroys accounts.

**Key Frameworks:**

*The Five Fundamental Truths:*
1. Anything can happen
2. Every trade outcome is unique
3. An edge is a higher probability, not a certainty
4. There is a random distribution between wins and losses for any given edge
5. You don't need to know what the next trade will do to make money consistently

*The Four Primary Trading Fears:*
- Being wrong
- Losing money
- Missing out
- Leaving money on the table

Each fear produces specific, documented self-destructive behaviors.

*Thinking in Probabilities:* Your job is not to predict the outcome of this trade. It is to execute a process across many trades.

*The Trading Paradox:* The more you need the trade to work, the more you interfere with your own execution.

**Weaknesses:**
- Repetitive by design — makes the same points multiple ways for internalization.
- No technical content. Pure psychology.
- The gap between intellectually understanding "think in probabilities" and actually doing it under real P&L pressure is enormous. Reading does not close this gap alone.

> [!tip] How to Read It
> Cover to cover. ~200 pages. Re-read the second half after every significant emotional trading mistake — you will find different things landing each time.

> [!danger] Brutal Reality
> Psychology is absent from most self-directed trading curricula. This is not optional. Technical mastery without psychological discipline is just expensive education. Traders who dismiss psychology as "soft" are almost universally the ones who blow up.

## Phase 2 — Process + Edge Development

*While paper trading or trading small size.*

### 4. Van Tharp — Trade Your Way to Financial Freedom (2nd ed. 2007)

[Buy on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Trade+Your+Way+Financial+Freedom+Van+Tharp)

> [!info] Who Is Tharp
> Trading coach and researcher. Interviewed hundreds of top traders over decades. Not a trader himself — a researcher who modeled what separates consistently profitable traders from losing ones.

**Core Thesis:** Most traders are obsessed with entries (when to buy). Entry is one of the least important variables. **Position sizing — how much to trade — is the primary driver of long-term performance.**

**R-Multiples:**
Define your risk before entry. If you risk $500 on a trade, that is 1R. A $1,500 return = 3R winner. A $500 loss = -1R. This normalizes all trades regardless of dollar size or account size.

**Expectancy Formula:**
```
Expectancy = (Win% × Avg Winner in R) − (Loss% × Avg Loser in R)

Example: 40% win rate, avg winner 3R, avg loser 1R:
(0.40 × 3) − (0.60 × 1) = 1.2 − 0.6 = +0.6R per trade

→ Profitable at a 40% win rate.
  Most beginners focus on win rate. Expectancy is what matters.
```

**Position Sizing Formula:**
```
Position Size = (Account × Risk%) ÷ (Entry Price − Stop Price)

"I'll put in $5,000"              ← NOT a position size
"I risk 1% of $50,000, stop is
$2 below entry, so I buy 250 shares" ← IS a position size
```

**Weaknesses:**
- Some system development content is abstract until you have real trading data to apply it.
- Occasionally veers into New Age framing that some readers find off-putting.
- Optimal f and Kelly criterion math is theoretically interesting but practically dangerous for retail traders.

> [!tip] How to Read It
> Cover to cover once for the framework. Then use position sizing chapters as active reference when building your system. Do the expectancy math on your own paper trades before risking real money — this is not optional.

> [!danger] Non-Negotiable
> You must be able to calculate your position size before every trade. If you can't define your R before entry, you are gambling, not trading.

### 5. Mark Minervini — Trade Like a Stock Market Wizard (2013)

[Buy on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Trade+Like+Stock+Market+Wizard+Minervini)

Follow-up: [Think and Trade Like a Champion](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Think+Trade+Like+Champion+Minervini)

> [!info] Who Is Minervini
> Four-time US Investing Championship winner including a 155% return year. Former professional fund manager. One of the few documented and audited retail-to-professional success stories in trading literature.

**Core Methodology: SEPA (Specific Entry Point Analysis)**

The most complete and actionable swing/momentum trading framework in popular literature.

**The Trend Template — All 8 Must Be Met:**
- [ ] Price above 150-day and 200-day moving average
- [ ] 150-day MA above 200-day MA
- [ ] 200-day MA trending up for at least 1 month (ideally 4–5 months)
- [ ] 50-day MA above both 150-day and 200-day MA
- [ ] Price above 50-day MA
- [ ] Price at least 25% above 52-week low
- [ ] Price within 25% of 52-week high (ideally within 15%)
- [ ] Relative strength rank above 70

If a stock fails any criterion, skip it. This eliminates ~90% of stocks immediately.

**The VCP (Volatility Contraction Pattern):**
His primary base pattern. Stock forms a multi-week base where successive corrections get smaller in price depth and duration. Volume contracts as the base tightens. Final shakeout on low volume just before breakout. Breakout occurs on significant volume expansion.

*Logic:* Smart money accumulates quietly. When supply is exhausted, modest buying demand breaks the stock out.

**Entry and Risk Rules:**
- Buy as close to the pivot point as possible
- Initial stop below the base low or key technical level
- Risk 1% of account per trade; stop typically 5–8% below entry
- Add to positions only after they prove themselves, not before
- Partial profits at 20–25% gain; hold remainder for larger moves
- Sell into parabolic climax runs
- Stop hit = exit immediately, no exceptions

**Weaknesses:**
- Growth stock / bull market focused. Setup frequency drops dramatically in bear or sideways markets.
- No options integration, no hedging strategies.
- VCP is presented as the primary pattern but real markets offer a wider range of valid setups.

> [!tip] How to Read It
> Cover to cover (~300 pages). Then build his trend template criteria into a stock screener and run it weekly. Read *Think and Trade Like a Champion* immediately after.

### 6. Thomas Bulkowski — Encyclopedia of Chart Patterns (3rd ed. 2021)

[Buy on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Encyclopedia+Chart+Patterns+Bulkowski+3rd)

> [!info] Who Is Bulkowski
> Retired software engineer turned full-time trader and independent researcher. Self-funded back-testing across thousands of stocks over multi-decade datasets. No institutional affiliation.

**What It Teaches:** Statistical performance data for every major chart pattern. For each pattern: identification criteria, success/failure rates, average gain/loss, throwback frequency, volume behavior.

**Key Insight — Busted Patterns:** When a pattern fails and reverses through itself in the opposite direction, the subsequent move is often *larger* than the original expected move. Documented edge that most traders ignore.

**Weaknesses:**
- Historical data. Algorithms have degraded many classical patterns by specifically fading them.
- Aggregate statistics can't account for your specific instance's context.
- Beginners who read front-to-back often develop "pattern hunting" syndrome — trading patterns mechanically without context. This destroys accounts.
- No psychology, no position sizing, no macro integration.

> [!tip] How to Read It
> **Do not read cover to cover.** Read the methodology introduction chapters. Then use as a lookup reference — when you see a pattern in your trading, look it up to understand its historical success rate and failure modes. This is a dictionary, not a textbook.

### 7. Steve Nison — Japanese Candlestick Charting Techniques (2nd ed. 2001)

[Buy on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Japanese+Candlestick+Charting+Techniques+Nison)

Follow-up: [Beyond Candlesticks](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Beyond+Candlesticks+Nison)

> [!info] Who Is Nison
> The person who introduced Japanese candlestick charting to Western markets in the early 1990s. Before Nison, Western markets used only OHLC bar charts. The Japanese methodology dates to the 1700s for rice futures trading.

**Key Patterns:**

*Single Candle:* Doji, Hammer, Inverted Hammer, Shooting Star, Hanging Man, Spinning Top, Marubozu

*Two Candle:* Bullish/Bearish Engulfing, Harami, Piercing Line, Dark Cloud Cover, Tweezer Tops/Bottoms

*Three Candle:* Morning Star, Evening Star, Three White Soldiers, Three Black Crows, Abandoned Baby

> [!warning] Critical Rule Most Readers Miss
> Candlestick patterns are only meaningful at significant technical levels — support, resistance, Fibonacci retracements, trendlines. A hammer in the middle of a trend range is not a signal. A hammer at a major support level after a prolonged downtrend, confirmed by a subsequent up-close candle, is a signal.

**Weaknesses:**
- Standalone patterns have poor statistical reliability — barely above random without additional context.
- Pattern identification is subjective; the line between patterns is sometimes ambiguous.
- Beginners trade patterns in isolation without confirmation and lose money on this methodology consistently.

> [!tip] How to Read It
> Cover to cover once. Keep the pattern reference pages accessible while reviewing charts for the first few months.

### 8. Constance Brown — Technical Analysis for the Trading Professional (2nd ed. 2012)

[Buy on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Technical+Analysis+Trading+Professional+Constance+Brown)

> [!info] Who Is Brown
> Professional market analyst and institutional technical analyst. Known for research on how traditional indicators behave differently across different market environments.

**Most Important Insight — RSI Market Regimes:**

Standard teaching: RSI above 70 = overbought, below 30 = oversold. **This is wrong as a universal rule.**

| Market Regime | RSI Range | "Oversold" | "Overbought" |
|---------------|-----------|------------|--------------|
| Bull Market | 40–90 | ~40–50 | ~80–90 |
| Bear Market | 10–60 | ~10–20 | ~50–60 |

*Implication:* Fixed 70/30 thresholds cause you to sell too early in bull markets and buy too early in bear markets. Identify the regime first.

**Other Key Content:**
- Multi-timeframe analysis — how to align signals across timeframes
- Advanced Fibonacci — price and time projections, level clusters
- The Composite Index — her proprietary indicator that catches RSI divergences RSI itself misses
- Market geometry and cycles

**Weaknesses:**
- Very dense. Requires fluency with RSI, MACD, Stochastics, and classical chart patterns before reading.
- Should not be read until you are actively trading and encountering the specific problems she is solving.

> [!tip] How to Read It
> Not linearly. Read the RSI chapter first — immediately applicable. Then multi-timeframe analysis. Treat the rest as reference you return to as specific questions arise from active trading.

### 9. Options — Three-Tier Approach

Do not skip ahead. Build sequentially.

---

#### Tier 1 — Tastytrade (Free)

Website: [tastytrade.com](https://tastytrade.com)

Platform built by Tom Sosnoff (co-creator of thinkorswim) specifically to educate retail options traders. Premium-selling focused.

**Core Concepts:**
- **Implied Volatility Rank (IVR):** Current IV relative to its own 52-week range. IVR above 50 = options expensive, sell premium. IVR below 25 = options cheap.
- **Probability of Profit (POP):** Theoretical probability a trade expires profitable based on option delta.
- **Mechanical rules:** Sell premium in high IVR. Take winners at 50% of max profit. Size so no single loss exceeds 2% of account.

**Best Shows to Start With:**
- *Options for Dummies* — foundational mechanics
- *From Theory to Practice* — connecting concepts to live markets
- *Market Measures* — data-backed empirical tests (watch regularly, they think empirically)
- *Best Practices* — rules of thumb for position management

> [!warning] Limitation
> Tastytrade's framework is premium-selling focused. For directional options use as a swing trader — buying calls on breakouts, buying puts as hedges, legging into spreads — their framework requires translation. Risk management principles are solid; directional application is thin.

---

#### Tier 2 — Guy Cohen — The Bible of Options Strategies (2nd ed. 2015)

[Buy on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Bible+Options+Strategies+Guy+Cohen)

Structured reference covering both directional and non-directional strategies. For each: construction, when to use it, risk/reward profile, Greeks behavior over time, exit strategies. More balanced than Tastytrade, more accessible than McMillan. The bridge between basic options literacy and professional-level reference.

---

#### Tier 3 — Lawrence McMillan — Options as a Strategic Investment (5th ed. 2012)

[Buy on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Options+Strategic+Investment+McMillan+5th+edition)

The professional desk reference standard. ~1,000 pages covering every options strategy in exhaustive detail — all spreads, combinations, synthetics, index options, futures options, tax treatment, hedging strategies. Real trade examples throughout.

> [!danger] Critical Warning
> Do not use McMillan as a teaching text before you are actively trading options. It is a reference — reach for it when you encounter a situation you don't know how to handle. Reading it as a beginner before active trading is an expensive way to be confused.

## Ongoing — Macro Environment

*Read alongside active trading. Not prerequisites.*

---

### 10. Howard Marks — The Most Important Thing (2011)

[Buy on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Most+Important+Thing+Howard+Marks)

> [!info] Who Is Marks
> Co-founder and co-chairman of Oaktree Capital Management — one of the world's largest alternative investment managers (credit and distressed debt). Managed hundreds of billions across multiple full market cycles.

**Key Concepts:**

- **Second-level thinking:** First-level = "the company's prospects are good, I'll buy." Second-level = "the prospects are good, but is that already priced in? What does the consensus expect, and where might it be wrong?"
- **Market cycles:** Markets cycle between greed and fear, risk-tolerance and risk-aversion. Where you are in the cycle determines how much risk to take.
- **Risk:** Not volatility — the probability of permanent loss. High prices = high risk, regardless of how calm recent markets have been.
- **Patient opportunism:** Best opportunities arise when others are forced to sell, not when they're eager to buy.

**For Swing Traders Specifically:** When valuations are extreme, sentiment euphoric, credit flowing freely, volatility suppressed — reduce aggressiveness. When fear is dominant, volatility spiking, credit tight — increase aggressiveness and position size.

> [!warning] Limitation
> Marks is a credit/value investor with decade-long timeframes. Translating "we're late in the cycle" to specific swing trade adjustments this week requires bridging with shorter-term technical and market breadth indicators.

### 11. Ray Dalio — Macro Framework

[Buy Principles for Navigating Big Debt Crises on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Principles+Navigating+Big+Debt+Crises+Dalio)

> [!info] Who Is Dalio
> Founder of Bridgewater Associates, the world's largest hedge fund. Known for systematic macro investing. Managed through multiple major market cycles.

**Start Here — Free:**
Search YouTube: *"How the Economic Machine Works — Ray Dalio"* (30-minute video). Watch this before reading anything else. Explains the credit cycle, deleveraging, and economic expansion/contraction at the most accessible level Dalio has ever presented.

**Then Read: Principles for Navigating Big Debt Crises (2018)**

Detailed analytical history of major debt crises (1920s Germany, 1930s US Depression, 2008 Financial Crisis) with a framework for understanding where economies are in the debt cycle.

**What It Teaches Swing Traders:**
- How credit expansion and contraction drive risk appetite and sector rotation
- Deflationary vs. inflationary deleveraging — how assets behave differently in each
- Currency cycle dynamics — how dollar strength/weakness affects equities, commodities, and emerging markets
- Why different asset classes perform at different macro cycle stages

> [!warning] Limitation
> Dalio's framework operates on timescales of years to decades. Use it to calibrate directional bias and risk tolerance, not to time individual trades. Macro context informs the backdrop; technical analysis times the entry.
