# Threshold Discernment — Practitioner Calibration for Reading Interior States

---

## Document Purpose

**What this is:** A formation guide for the practitioner's attunement — the quality of attention that allows you to read where a person actually is in the diagnostic-to-formation arc, particularly at the transition from surfacing false identity to receiving covenant truth.

**When used:** Pre-session preparation and post-session supervision. Not for in-session reference — consulting a document mid-session breaks the presence this work requires. The Quick-Reference Card at the end is the exception: one scan, then set it aside.

**What this is not:** A diagnostic protocol. It does not give you certified indicators for declaring a person to be "in state X." The threshold between states is a judgment call requiring Spirit-dependent attunement, not a clinical test. This document trains the attunement; it cannot substitute for it.

**Scope flag — non-negotiable:** This document is written for non-acute coaching contexts. If a client presents indicators of trauma — dissociation, flooding, fight/flight escalation under normal session conditions, significant unprocessed trauma history — the protocols here do not apply as written. Shame rooted in acute trauma requires clinical treatment before coaching tools are appropriate. When in doubt, refer. What follows is written for coaches, not therapists.

**Governing mechanism:** The Spirit is the primary agent of transformation. The coaching relationship creates formation context. Diagnostic work surfaces false identity and disordered affections. Covenant truth is applied and received. Between-session disciplines sustain what was received. This document serves the middle transition — between surfacing and receiving — where the practitioner's attunement determines whether the right tool reaches the right person at the right moment.

---

## 1. The Attunement Question

The question this document trains is not: *How do I avoid misreading interior states?*

That question produces a practitioner who is checking indicators rather than seeing people — technically informed, but observing from a distance. It is, ironically, the same defended posture in the practitioner that prevents reception in the client.

The question is: **What quality of presence allows me to see what is actually happening in this person, in this moment?**

That is a formation question, not a technique question. The observable indicators below serve a practitioner who has been developing this quality of attention. They are reference points, not a checklist. Checklists produce false confidence at exactly the moment genuine attunement is required.

Reading a person well requires four things in the practitioner:

**A regulated interior.** You cannot attune to another person's state from a dysregulated one. A practitioner in low-level anxiety, urgency, or discomfort will read the room through their own state, not the client's. Your regulated presence is not a virtue — it is a functional precondition for seeing accurately. (See Daniel Siegel's interpersonal neurobiology for the mechanism; see Section 6 for the formation pathway.)

**Non-agenda presence.** The practitioner who needs the session to go a certain way — to produce movement, to reach resolution, to get to the formation layer — will read the person through that need. The practitioner's agenda is always visible to the client's nervous system, even when invisible to the practitioner themselves.

**Familiarity with their own interior states.** You can only guide someone through territory you have walked. The practitioner who has not sat in genuine collapse will misread it in others — not out of inattention, but out of inexperience. Their frame for what brokenness looks like will be theoretical rather than embodied.

**Willingness to hold a provisional read.** Many people present in mixed states. Certainty about a person's interior state is usually a sign of premature closure. The practitioner's read is always probabilistic, always subject to revision as new information arrives in the session. The most dangerous read is the confident one.

## 2. Five Orientations — What You Are Reading

These are orientations and tendencies, not discrete clinical categories. A person can be genuinely broken about one thing and defended about another in the same session. Shame and brokenness coexist. Performed brokenness often has a shame substrate underneath it. What you are reading is the dominant orientation in this moment, with the knowledge that it can shift and that secondary states are almost always present.

---

### Genuine Brokenness

**Core dynamic:** The false structure has already collapsed — not because you pressed it, but because life, consequence, or the Spirit's conviction has already done the excavating. The person is no longer defending what was wrong. The hand is open.

**Orientation of attention:** Outward — toward God and toward whoever or whatever was broken. The person is not primarily concerned with what this says about them.

**Language signals:**
- Grief language that references outside the self: "I broke something," "I hurt them," "I walked away from God"
- Questions directed outward: "What does God say about this?" "Can I come back from this?" "What do I do with this?"
- Spontaneous disclosure — the person brings material without being pressed

**Somatic signals:**
- Open posture; nothing is guarded
- Eye contact that is *seeking* rather than avoiding — looking for something, not hiding from something
- Tears present but not flooding — weight without overwhelm
- Present rather than dissociated

**When covenant truth is spoken:** It lands somewhere. There is visible registration — the body opens slightly, the person leans in, the words reach something. Not theatrical; quiet and real.

**Distinguished from shame:** The grief is oriented toward what was broken in the relationship — with God, with others — not toward the self's image or exposure. The primary question is "What have I done?" not "What does this say about me?"

---

### Shame

**Core dynamic:** Self-protective collapse around a wound. The person is still self-referential — the primary concern is what this reveals about them, not what was broken with God.

**Orientation of attention:** Inward and self-condemning. The circle of concern is the self's exposure and what others now see or think.

**Language signals:**
- Subject is always *I*, predicate is always condemnation: "I can't believe I did that," "I'm such a failure," "I'm disgusting," "What is wrong with me?"
- "I don't deserve..." — applied to anything offered, including covenant truth
- Absence of the outward-facing question; the grief doesn't press toward God

**Somatic signals:**
- Eye contact avoidance — shame is fundamentally about hiding from the gaze; the person cannot meet your eyes when the wound is exposed
- Physically smaller: shoulders in, head down, arms crossed or pulled in
- Voice drops or becomes flat

**When covenant truth is spoken:** Bounces — "I don't deserve that" — or inverts into new self-condemnation: "I know I'm *supposed* to feel like a beloved child but I don't, which must mean something is even more wrong with me." The declaration becomes a new standard to fail against rather than truth received.

**Distinguished from brokenness:** The grief is circling inward, not pressing toward God. The primary breach named is the self-image, not the relationship. In brokenness, the person says "I broke something with God." In shame, the person says "I am broken" — and the concern is the self's condition, not the relationship damaged.

**Coaching implication:** Shame requires a different prior move before declarations are possible. The person must first experience the practitioner's non-anxious, non-flinching presence with their exposure — acceptance received relationally — before they can receive truth about themselves. Declaration into active shame becomes new evidence of failure. (See Section 5 for the recovery move.)

---

### Performed / Managed Brokenness

**Core dynamic:** The person is presenting what they believe brokenness is supposed to look like. Fear-driven — fear of consequences, relational loss, or the practitioner's assessment.

**Orientation of attention:** Toward the practitioner's response. The person is calibrating the performance to the audience, monitoring whether what they are offering is landing appropriately.

**Language signals:**
- Technically correct but hollow in register: "I know I was wrong," "I've been convicted about this," "I realize I need to change"
- Formulaic — delivered without the weight that genuine disclosure carries
- The right words, in the right order, without cost

**Somatic signals:**
- Emotion appears and disappears too quickly — controlled rather than genuine; feelings managed rather than expressed
- Discrepancy between verbal content and physical register: saying the right things while the face and body are neutral or closed
- The person is watching your face to read whether the performance is working

**When covenant truth is spoken:** Accepted readily — perhaps too readily. Agreement without cost. The agreement doesn't seem to land anywhere specific; it is absorbed efficiently and nothing shifts.

**Distinguished from brokenness:** Voluntary disclosure is absent — the person responds but does not initiate. Gentle deepening either produces performance escalation or deflection. The fundamental tell is the monitoring of your response: the broken person is addressing God or naming what happened; the performing person is managing you.

---

### Despair

**Core dynamic:** Collapsed but not praying. The distinction from genuine brokenness is the absence of reaching. Brokenness still moves toward God even in collapse; despair has stopped moving.

**Orientation of attention:** Inward and static — not cycling like shame, but arrested. The person is not protecting anything, not performing anything — they have simply stopped.

**Language signals:**
- Certainty about the irreparable: "There's no coming back from this," "God won't..." "I've gone too far," "It's over"
- Present-tense certainty about a closed future — not fear, but settled conclusion
- Absence of question — nothing is being sought

**Somatic signals:**
- Flat affect, distinct from the tender grief of brokenness — heavier, more static
- Energy is low and does not lift when truth is spoken
- May be dissociated — present in the room but not reachable

**When covenant truth is spoken:** Does not register. Not rejected — simply doesn't reach. The person may nod, but the words find nowhere to land.

**Distinguished from brokenness:** Psalm 51 is speech toward God — brokenness addresses someone, seeks something. Despair has gone silent or turned entirely inward. Brokenness has orientation; despair has lost it. The question that distinguishes: *Is this person still reaching toward anything?*

**Coaching implication:** Despair often requires clinical referral or extended presence before formation tools apply. The immediate work is reestablishing the person's capacity to address God — often through lament more than declaration.

---

### Still Defended

**Core dynamic:** The false structure is still operating. The person is protecting something — a false covenant object, a self-narrative, a right to their anger. The hand is closed.

**Orientation of attention:** Toward self-protection. The person is asserting, not receiving. The James 1:20 anger is operating just beneath the surface.

**Language signals:**
- "It wasn't that bad," "Yes, but..." — minimization with a counter
- Agreement without landing: "You're right" — but nothing changes in posture or energy
- Intellectualization: analyzing the problem from a safe cognitive distance rather than sitting in it
- Topic drift — movement away from vulnerable material as it gets close

**Somatic signals:**
- Reactive energy — subtle tension, forward lean that is pressing rather than seeking
- The face is engaged but the engagement is evaluative, not receptive
- A quality of *managing* the conversation rather than inhabiting it

**When covenant truth is spoken:** Absorbed without movement — agreed with verbally, but produces no shift in posture, no registration, no landing.

**Distinguished from performed brokenness:** The defended person is not trying to look broken — they are not pretending. They are actively protecting. The performed person is performing for you; the defended person is protecting from you (or from what you represent).

## 3. At the Threshold

When the person has moved from defended to broken — or arrives in session already broken — the practitioner's role changes. Not because of a technique decision, but because of what the person's state actually requires.

The corrected claim: **Diagnostic pressure on a person who is already open is misdirected.** It is not that diagnostic questions are categorically prohibited. Elijah in 1 Kings 19 was broken — God asked him two diagnostic questions ("What are you doing here, Elijah?") before food, rest, and mission were given. The text does not prohibit the question; it illustrates the priority. The primary movement God makes toward the broken person is toward revival (Isaiah 57:15: "to revive the spirit of the lowly, and to revive the heart of the contrite"), not toward further excavation. The practitioner who keeps pressing when the person is already open has missed the shift — not violated a rule.

**What changes at the threshold:**

The practitioner's primary mode shifts from questioner to *witness and truth-speaker.* The questions stop not because they are forbidden but because the moment doesn't call for them. What it calls for:

**Presence precedes declaration.** Isaiah 57:15 is precise: God *dwells with* the contrite before he revives them. The practitioner's non-anxious, non-flinching presence with the person in their brokenness is itself the first form of covenant truth received. You are embodying what God does before you say what God says.

**Covenant truth becomes the content.** Not generic declarations, but specific truth that counters what the collapsed structure was built on. The person's particular false covenant object has just failed — the covenant truth that lands is the one that speaks directly to what they were trusting and what they now need instead.

**The pace slows.** Brokenness is not a moment to move through efficiently. The practitioner who rushes toward resolution is serving their own discomfort, not the person's formation.

**The Spirit determines what lands.** The practitioner speaks; the Spirit determines what the person can actually receive in this moment. Trusting that completely — without anxiously checking whether it worked — is itself a form of the meekness James 1:21 requires of the receiver. The practitioner needs the same posture they are cultivating in the client.

## 4. When the Read Was Wrong — Orienting Moves

Every move listed here creates conditions. The Spirit determines what the person can actually receive in the moment. These are not corrective techniques; they are how the practitioner reorients when attunement was off.

Three scenarios. Each leads with the single move the moment requires.

---

### Scenario 1: Moved to Covenant Truth Before the Person Was Ready (Still Defended)

**Single orienting move: Name that you moved too fast. Return.**

The declarations you spoke are true; don't retract them. But name the disconnect without making the person feel accused: *"I want to slow down for a minute — I think I moved before something was ready."* Return to the diagnostic layer, but with wondering questions rather than pressing ones: *"What's still sitting with you about this?"* The defended person doesn't need harder pressure — they need a question they don't feel compelled to defend against.

*Signal you called it wrong:* The person brightened too quickly. Agreement came without cost. The next session opened exactly where the last one closed, with no movement between them.

---

### Scenario 2: Kept Pressing Diagnostic When the Person Was Already Broken

**Single orienting move: Stop asking. Start speaking.**

Shift your posture visibly before you shift your words — the change needs to be legible to the person before it is verbal. Acknowledge what is already in the room: *"I've been asking a lot. I want to do something different."* Then name what you observe: *"You're not defending anything right now. You've named something real."* Then speak covenant truth — slowly, specifically, not generically. The person's open hand is what you are speaking into.

*Signal you called it wrong:* The person got smaller as the session progressed rather than beginning to lift. They stopped volunteering material and started waiting to be asked. The energy in the room dropped further rather than moving.

---

### Scenario 3: Spoke Covenant Truth Into Shame Instead of Brokenness

**Single orienting move: Companion before countering. Presence before declaration.**

The declaration has been spoken; don't retract it. But recognize it has not landed and may have become new evidence against the person. Slow the pace and lower your register — the nervous system reads tone before content. Your willingness to sit with the person without flinching at what they've exposed is the first intervention. Don't rush past it. Only once the shame has been companioned — once the person has experienced your non-anxious presence with their exposure — return to the truth. Speak it again, more slowly, more specifically.

*Signal you called it wrong:* The covenant truth produced more self-condemnation, not relief. The person agreed verbally but contracted physically. "I know I'm supposed to believe that" was the response — not reception, but distance.

## 5. What Creates Misreads — Self-Supervision and Coach Formation

This section serves post-session reflection. Its goal is not simply to identify what went wrong — it is to name what in the practitioner needs formation, and to suggest the pathway for that formation.

**The self-supervision question that opens this work:**

*Was the grief in that room oriented toward God — or toward the self?*

If you can answer that clearly from what you observed, your attunement is functioning. If you're uncertain, the uncertainty itself is diagnostic data: either the person was in a mixed state, or your attention was elsewhere during the session.

---

**Interference factor 1: Discomfort with prolonged brokenness**

If sitting with collapse is uncomfortable, you will move toward hope too fast to relieve your own distress. The speed of your move to covenant truth is sometimes more about you than about the person.

*Formation pathway:* Develop a personal practice with lament. The Psalms of lament prayed slowly — particularly Psalms 88, 22, and 77, which do not resolve in the way most devotional reading expects — form the practitioner's capacity to stay in the dark without urgency toward resolution. The practitioner who can remain in Psalm 88's silence without rushing to Psalm 89's praise is being formed for this attunement.

---

**Interference factor 2: Over-training in diagnostic work**

If excavation is your primary competence, you will keep excavating past the point of usefulness because it feels productive and it's what you know how to do.

*Formation pathway:* In post-session supervision, identify one moment where you asked a question and the person had already answered it with their posture. What would you have said instead of asking? The capacity to speak rather than ask develops through deliberate practice. Bring one such moment to supervision each session and draft the declaration you didn't make.

---

**Interference factor 3: Unprocessed personal brokenness**

You can only guide someone through territory you have walked. A practitioner whose own experience of collapse ended in shame, or was never processed toward revival, will misread the distinction in others — not out of inattention, but out of inexperience. The internal frame for what genuine brokenness looks like will be theoretical rather than embodied.

*Formation pathway:* This is the most significant factor and the hardest to address by technique. What it requires is your own ongoing formation — your own history with collapse, with covenant truth received in open-handed brokenness, with revival experienced rather than described. The practitioner's own spiritual direction is not optional equipment; it is the formation context for this capacity. If it is absent, the limitations will surface in your most important sessions.

---

**Interference factor 4: Session urgency**

Time pressure creates the impulse to produce movement within the hour. Brokenness does not produce on a schedule, and pushing the pace is always the practitioner's agenda, not the Spirit's.

*Formation pathway:* Before each session, a brief and specific surrender of outcomes. Not a phrase recited but a posture practiced: *this session belongs to the Spirit; I am not responsible for where it ends.* This takes deliberate repetition before it becomes a genuine interior posture rather than a spoken intention.

---

**Interference factor 5: Theological eagerness**

Knowing Isaiah 57:15 deeply can produce a premature move to revival before the person has actually arrived at the broken posture the promise addresses. The promise is true; it does not override the pace at which the Spirit brings the person there.

*Formation pathway:* Develop the habit of a single checking question before moving to covenant truth: *Is this person actually there yet?* Not as a checklist item, but as a genuine pause in the practitioner's interior. The practitioner who waits with the person rather than waiting for the right moment to speak is being formed in the patience that attunement requires.

## 6. Source Material — Tiered

### Read First — Highest Yield, Immediately Applicable

**Dan Allender & Tremper Longman — *The Cry of the Soul* (1994)**
The definitive integration of emotion, the Psalms, and the soul. Allender distinguishes shame, contempt, and grief in ways that directly sharpen the state distinctions above. The treatment of shame as self-protective hiding — specifically its relationship to the gaze — is essential grounding for the shame/brokenness distinction. This is the primary source for that section.

**Bill Miller & Stephen Rollnick — *Motivational Interviewing* (3rd ed., 2012)**
MI distinguishes sustain talk (defending the status quo — the defended state) from change talk (moving toward change). The righting reflex — the practitioner's impulse to fix and correct — is specifically identified as the most common error skilled practitioners make and the primary thing that stalls movement in a defended person. When a person has moved into change talk, the practitioner follows; redirecting back to excavation is a misread with a known cost. Directly applicable to the diagnostic-to-formation transition.

**David Benner — *Sacred Companions* (2002)**
Attunement to where the Spirit is already working rather than imposing a session agenda. Benner's treatment of the companion's role — as one who accompanies rather than directs — is the clearest pastoral articulation of what changes at the threshold. The practitioner's receptivity to where God is moving is a prerequisite for reading the person accurately.

---

### Reference Later — Foundational, Requires More Investment

**Stephen Porges — *The Polyvagal Theory* (2011)**
Provides a neurological framework for understanding why the practitioner's regulated presence is a functional precondition, not a relational nicety. The three autonomic states (ventral vagal: engaged and receptive; sympathetic: fight/flight; dorsal vagal: shutdown) map broadly onto the orientations above. Use as a descriptive framework for understanding the mechanism — not as a clinical procedure. Note: Porges's anatomical claims have been contested in neurological literature; the conceptual framework remains useful even where the specifics are debated.

**Bessel van der Kolk — *The Body Keeps the Score* (2014)**
The body as the site of emotional state, not just the mind. Useful for understanding why somatic indicators (posture, breath, eye contact, voice register) are often more reliable than verbal content. Important scope note: much of this book addresses trauma treatment — read with the scope flag from the purpose section in view.

**June Price Tangney & Ronda Dearing — *Shame and Guilt* (2002)**
Empirical research distinguishing shame (self-focused, withdrawal, concealment) from guilt (behavior-focused, repair-oriented). Provides research grounding for the shame section above. More technical than Allender; useful for precision on the distinction.

**Daniel Siegel — *The Developing Mind* (2nd ed., 2012)**
Interpersonal neurobiology: how attunement between two people shapes what can happen between them. The practitioner's regulated interior is not only a spiritual discipline — it is, neurologically, what makes co-regulation possible and therefore what makes the receptive state accessible to the client. Foundational for understanding why the practitioner's formation is clinical infrastructure, not optional virtue.

**Gerald May — *Addiction and Grace* (1988)**
The dynamic of attachment (addiction in the broad sense — equivalent to the false covenant object framework) and how surrender is the precondition for grace to work. May's treatment of willingness versus willfulness is the psychological parallel to meekness versus defensiveness in James 1:21. Short and accessible; a bridge text between the psychological and theological frames.

**Ignatius of Loyola — *Rules for the Discernment of Spirits* (from the Spiritual Exercises)**
The most sophisticated pre-modern framework for reading interior movements. Ignatius distinguishes consolation (expansion, orientation toward God) from desolation (contraction, closing, movement away). His rules for what not to do in desolation — specifically, do not make major decisions or act from the contracted state — map directly onto the broken/despair distinction and the recovery protocol for Scenario 2. Rule 5 of the First Week is the entry point.

**Note on Ekman:** Paul Ekman's work on micro-expressions (*Emotions Revealed*, 2003) is frequently cited for distinguishing genuine from performed emotion. The underlying concept — that the face registers emotion before conscious control can manage it — is real and worth knowing. However, applying micro-expression reading reliably requires extensive trained practice that self-study does not produce. Know the concept; don't expect practical skill from the book alone.
