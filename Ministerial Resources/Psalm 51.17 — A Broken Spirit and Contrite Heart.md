# Psalm 51:17 — A Broken Spirit; a Broken and Contrite Heart

> *The sacrifices of God are a broken spirit; a broken and contrite heart, O God, you will not despise.* — Psalm 51:17 (ESV)

---

## 1. Text & Context

Psalm 51 is one of seven Penitential Psalms (6, 32, 38, 51, 102, 130, 143). The superscription roots it in the most catastrophic moral collapse of David's life — after Nathan confronted him following the Bathsheba affair (2 Samuel 11–12). This matters: the psalm isn't abstract piety. It's a man under the weight of adultery, murder-by-proxy, and abuse of sovereign power.

**Structure:**
- vv. 1–2: Appeal to covenant mercy (*hesed*, *rahamim*)
- vv. 3–6: Acknowledgment of sin — "against you, you *only*, have I sinned" (v. 4)
- vv. 7–12: Prayer for cleansing, joy, and Spirit renewal
- vv. 13–17: Vow of response — teaching, praise, sacrifice
- vv. 18–19: Petition for Jerusalem (widely considered a later liturgical addition)

Verse 17 is the climax of the vow section and arrives precisely when David is asking: *what does God actually want from me?* The verse immediately precedes vv. 18–19's reference to burnt offerings — a deliberate tension between interior disposition and exterior ritual.

## 2. Language

**Hebrew text:** זִבְחֵי אֱלֹהִים רוּחַ נִשְׁבָּרָה לֵב-נִשְׁבָּר וְנִדְכֶּה אֱלֹהִים לֹא-תִבְזֶה

### רוּחַ (ruach) — spirit
Semantic range: breath, wind, inner disposition, volitional center. Not primarily *emotion* — ruach is the motivational core of a person, closer to *will* than *feeling*. Proverbs 16:18–19 contrasts *shefal-ruach* (lowly spirit) with haughtiness. The ruach is where self-sufficiency or its absence lives.

### שָׁבַר (shabar) — broken (Niphal passive participle: *nishbar*)
Root: to shatter, smash, break to pieces. Used of pottery (Jer 19:11), bones (Ps 34:20), military defeat, the breaking of a yoke. The Niphal participle signals a *condition*, not a moment — the spirit that *has been brought to ruin*, now existing in a state of having been shattered. Not a mood. A state.

### דָּכָה (dakah) — contrite (Niphal: *nidkeh*)
Stronger than *shabar*. Root: to be crushed, beaten fine, ground to powder. This is not broken — this is pulverized. The same root appears in:
- Isaiah 53:5, 10 — the Servant is *crushed* (דָּכָא) for our iniquities
- Isaiah 57:15 — God dwells with the "contrite and lowly in spirit" (*daka u'shefal-ruach*)
- Isaiah 66:2 — the same cluster

### לֵב (lev) — heart
Not emotion alone. In Hebrew anthropology, the heart is the unified center of cognition, will, affection, and character — where decisions originate and where worship actually happens. When lev is broken *and* crushed, the whole interior person — not just feelings — is what is described.

### בָּזָה (bazah) — despise
To treat as worthless, contemptible. The negative ("will NOT despise") is the key: what would seem contemptible by any external standard — a man with nothing to offer — is precisely what God refuses to treat as worthless. The costly animal sacrifice could be despised. The broken spirit cannot.

**Note on the two-line structure:** The second line (*lev nishbar wenidkeh*) intensifies the first. Some commentators read this as synonymous parallelism (saying the same thing twice for emphasis, as Hebrew poetry does). The better reading, given *dakah*'s semantic weight, is *escalating* — not just broken but ground to dust.

## 3. Historical-Cultural Context

In the Ancient Near East sacrificial system — including Israel's Levitical cult — maintaining relationship with the deity operated through ritual sacrifice: burnt offerings, guilt offerings, peace offerings, blood mediation through the priest. This was the mechanism of atonement and restored standing.

Here is the problem David is sitting inside: Numbers 15:30–31 is clear that deliberate, "high-handed" sin (sinning with a raised fist, knowing defiance) may have placed a person *beyond* the reach of the sacrificial system. The prescribed offerings covered unintentional sins, inadvertent violations. David committed adultery and orchestrated murder — intentional, power-enabled, high-handed. No bull, no ram, no goat covers this by the book.

This is the weight behind vv. 16–17. David isn't offering a generalized spiritual principle. He may be acknowledging: *there is no sacrifice in the catalog that reaches where I am.* What God wants, then, must be something that goes beneath ritual to the interior — the condition of the person bringing the offering.

This connects to a strand of prophetic critique throughout the OT: God's weariness with hollow sacrifice from people whose hearts are elsewhere (Isaiah 1:11–17; Amos 5:21–24; Micah 6:6–8; Hosea 6:6 — "I desire steadfast love and not sacrifice"). The prophets aren't abolishing sacrifice; they're insisting the interior reality must accompany the exterior act. Psalm 51 is not anti-sacrificial (vv. 18–19 explicitly anticipate restored burnt offerings in a rebuilt Jerusalem). The broken spirit is the *condition under which sacrifice becomes real*.

## 4. Intertextual Connections

**Isaiah 53:5, 10** — The Servant is *crushed* (*dakah*) for our iniquities. The same word used for what God values in the human heart is the word used for what God does to his Son on our behalf. This is not accidental. The covenant exchange runs through this vocabulary.

**Isaiah 57:15** — "I dwell in the high and holy place, *and also* with him who is of a contrite and lowly spirit, to *revive* the spirit of the lowly, and to revive the heart of the contrite." God's transcendence and his proximity to the crushed are held together in a single statement. He doesn't merely tolerate the broken person — he *goes there*. And he goes there to revive, not to leave them crushed.

**Isaiah 66:2** — "But this is the one to whom I will look: he who is humble and contrite in spirit and trembles at my word." The gaze of God oriented toward the crushed.

**Psalm 34:18** — "The LORD is near to the brokenhearted and saves the crushed in spirit." *Nishbar lev* and *daka ruach* — the identical vocabulary of Psalm 51:17 — appear here. God's nearness is definitionally oriented toward the broken.

**Matthew 5:3–5** — The Beatitudes open the Sermon on the Mount recapitulating this cluster: *ptochos* (utter poverty, not just lacking) in spirit; the meek (those who have relinquished self-insistence). Jesus defines the character of Kingdom inhabitants using the vocabulary and logic of Psalm 51 and Isaiah.

**Luke 18:9–14** — The Pharisee and Tax Collector is a dramatization of verse 17. The Pharisee's unbrokeness is explicit: "God, I thank you that I am not like other men." The tax collector: "God, be merciful to me, a sinner." The broken one goes home justified. The unbroken one does not.

**2 Corinthians 7:10** — Paul distinguishes *godly grief* (*lype kata theon*) from *worldly grief* (*lype tou kosmou*). Godly grief produces repentance that leads to salvation without regret; worldly grief produces death. This is the precise diagnostic distinction the psalm creates.

**James 4:6–10; 1 Peter 5:5–6** — "God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble." The theological logic of Psalm 51:17 codified as a principle: brokenness/humility is the condition that opens the person to receive what God gives.

## 5. Theological Weight

**a. The interior is the site of covenant faithfulness.** This is Deuteronomy's direction all along: "Circumcise your heart" (Deut 10:16; 30:6). Jeremiah's new covenant writes the law on the heart, not stone (Jer 31:33). Psalm 51 is not innovating; it is clarifying what was always true about the sacrificial system — it was always meant to express an interior reality, not substitute for one.

**b. God is drawn toward the broken — not repelled.** The word *bazah* (despise) makes this sharp. The unbroken person might assume God treats the crushed as contemptible: weak, failed, disgraced. The psalm refutes this directly. What God values — what he accepts as true sacrifice — is precisely the person who has nothing left to offer.

**c. Brokenness is the precondition for formation, not the end state.** This is critical. The broken spirit is not the goal. Isaiah 57:15 makes the purpose explicit: God goes to the crushed *to revive*. Psalm 51:12 prays for *joy*. Verse 13 vows *teaching other sinners*. The broken spirit is the open hand — the posture that can receive what the unbroken spirit cannot: grace, covenant identity, restoration not earned.

**d. The broken spirit is not despair.** Despair would say: "I am crushed and there is no way back." The broken spirit prays. It addresses God. Verse 17 is itself a speech-act of trust — David brings his brokenness *to* God, which presupposes some functional belief that God receives it. The crushed spirit is not hopeless; it has simply given up on helping itself.

**e. Redemptive arc — the cross makes brokenness safe.** Isaiah 53 connects to Psalm 51 through *dakah*: because the Servant was crushed in our place, we can stand before God as crushed people without the crushing destroying us. Genuine repentance is only "safe" because another has borne the crushing we deserved. The cross is what prevents the broken spirit from collapsing into judgment.

## 6. Interpretive Debate

**Contrast or condition?** Does v. 17 say the broken heart *replaces* sacrifice, or that it is the *condition* making sacrifice genuine? The "prophetic abolition" reading (brokenness instead of ritual) is common but weak — vv. 18–19 explicitly anticipate restored offerings. The better reading: brokenness is what makes any offering acceptable. An unbroken spirit corrupts the sacrifice; a broken spirit redeems it.

**Can the spirit break itself?** The Reformed tradition tends to read brokenness as wholly Spirit-initiated — sovereign conviction and humbling. The Wesleyan tradition emphasizes human response and cooperation. The psalm doesn't settle this directly. Verse 10 ("Create in me a clean heart, O God") implies that at minimum, restoration is God's work. But the psalm is itself David's response — suggesting genuine human movement, even if Spirit-enabled.

**Escalation or synonymous parallelism?** "Broken spirit" and "broken and contrite heart" — same thing said twice (parallelism), or intentional intensification? Given *dakah*'s semantic range (ground to powder vs. shattered), intensification is the stronger reading. The second line adds both *lev* (heart, the unified person) and *dakah* (crushed further) to the already-broken *ruach*.

**Verses 18–19 as secondary addition?** Most critical scholars see the Jerusalem petition as post-exilic — David is praying about walls not yet destroyed. If so, the psalm accumulated liturgical use across Israel's history. This doesn't affect the exegetical weight of v. 17, but it means the psalm may have been canonically shaped for corporate as well as individual use.

## 7. Coaching Translation

### Primary diagnostic layer — Biblical Counseling

The broken spirit is the collapse of a false covenant object. David's sin grew from functional idolatry — unchecked power, unaccountable desire, the idol of sovereign will. The *unbroken* spirit is the spirit that still trusts its functional gods: self-sufficiency, control, performance, image management. The *broken* spirit is the one that has lost confidence in those objects. They failed. The cost appeared. The idol didn't deliver.

This is the key distinction from shame: **shame circles inward; brokenness moves toward God.** Verse 4 — "against you, you only, have I sinned" — is diagnostic. The broken spirit locates the primary breach in its relationship with God, not in its self-image. Shame's primary concern is the self. Brokenness's primary concern is the One sinned against.

2 Corinthians 7:10 is the clinical tool here: *What direction is the grief oriented?* Toward God, with openness to restoration = brokenness. Circling inward, self-condemning, paralyzed = worldly grief. These look similar from the outside and require entirely different responses.

### Affections layer

The broken spirit is evidence of *reordered affection*. When the heart's affections are still primarily oriented toward the false covenant object, conviction doesn't produce brokenness — it produces defensiveness, minimization, or performed contrition. Genuine brokenness signals that the person now cares more about what was lost (relationship with God, integrity, others harmed) than about protecting the self-narrative the sin was serving. The affections have begun to shift. That shift is not the person's accomplishment — it is the Spirit's work creating the precondition for formation.

### Formation layer

The broken spirit is the entry point into a deeper formation stage, not a stopping point. The unbroken person cannot receive certain things: unearned grace lands as threat (it exposes the false covenant object), covenant identity feels fraudulent, declarations don't stick because the interior scaffolding rejects them. The broken spirit is the open hand — the interior condition that can actually *receive* what God offers.

Isaiah 57:15 is the formation anchor: God revives the contrite. The broken spirit is where formation becomes possible. A client who has moved from defensive self-management into genuine brokenness has crossed a threshold. What they need now is entirely different from what they needed before.

### Psychological description (subordinate)

In attachment terms, the broken spirit resembles the collapse of a dismissive or self-reliant internal working model — the moment when "I can manage this" fails. In CBT terms, it's the disruption of a core belief in self-adequacy. These are useful *descriptions* of a spiritual reality but are not its cause or cure. Don't lead with them.

## 8. Session Application

### Diagnostic use

When a client presents grief or remorse, the diagnostic question is: *What is this oriented toward, and what is it about?*
- Grief about consequences, reputation, or relational loss = may be worldly sorrow
- Grief that presses toward God and opens to restoration = brokenness
- Grief that cycles into self-condemnation and paralysis = shame

"Against you, you only" (v. 4) is a useful frame: *Where does the client locate the primary breach?* If the answer is primarily "I let myself down" or "I can't believe I did that" — the posture is still self-referential. If the answer is "I broke something with God, and with them" — that's a different orientation.

### Formation use

Psalm 51 as a formation practice — not only for crisis moments but as a recurring posture. The broken spirit is not a one-time event; it's an ongoing disposition of the person who has relinquished self-sufficiency. Between sessions: *What in me is still trusting something other than God? What am I managing that I haven't handed over?*

Isaiah 57:15 as a declaration of God's proximity: "I dwell with the crushed." This is formative truth, not mere comfort — it repositions the client's understanding of where God is when they are at lowest capacity.

### Lie Eliminator entries

- Lie: *"God turns away from me when I fail."* — Countered directly by v. 17 (he will NOT despise) and Isaiah 57:15 (he dwells with the contrite)
- Lie: *"I have to get myself together before I can come to God."* — The entire posture of the psalm refutes this; David comes broken, and that is precisely what is accepted
- Lie: *"I am too far gone."* — Countered by the cross-shaped logic: the Servant bore the crushing (Isa 53) so the crushed can come without being destroyed

### Framework anchor

The broken spirit marks a coaching threshold. A defended client needs diagnostic work — surfacing false covenant objects, naming affections, exposing what is actually being trusted. A genuinely broken client needs something different: formation, reception of covenant truth, identity anchored in what God has declared rather than what the client can produce. The coach's entire posture shifts at this threshold. Pushing diagnostic work on a broken person is pastoral malpractice — they need covenant truth and the revival Isaiah 57:15 promises.
