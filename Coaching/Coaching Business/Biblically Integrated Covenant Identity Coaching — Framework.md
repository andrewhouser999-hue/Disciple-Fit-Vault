# Biblically Integrated Covenant Identity Coaching — Framework
*A conceptual framework for a distinct coaching model. Sources: Scripture, Co-Active Coaching methodology, Schema Therapy, Narrative Identity Theory, IFS, Attachment Theory, CBT, Immunity to Change, Kegan, Brueggemann, Reformed sanctification theology.*

---

## I. What It Is — The Core Claim

Covenant Identity Coaching is a coaching model in which:

- **Covenant** is the organizing theological structure — not a metaphor, but the actual framework for understanding the client's identity, the coaching relationship, and the change process
- **Identity** is the primary terrain — the central work is not behavior change but identity integration: growing into who the client already is before God
- **Coaching** is the methodology — client-agenda-driven, generative, forward-focused, with the coach as evoker and challenger rather than teacher or director
- **Integration** means genuine synthesis — not Bible verses bolted onto psychology, but a unified epistemology in which Scripture governs the understanding of human nature and psychology provides descriptive precision

The core claim: **The client's deepest problem is almost never a behavior problem or even a motivation problem. It is an identity problem — a failure to live from who they already are in covenant with God.** The coaching work is closing the gap between declared covenant identity and lived, felt identity.

---

## II. Why Covenant Specifically — Not Just "Biblical"

"Biblical coaching" could mean many things. The covenant frame is specific and carries distinctive weight.

**What covenant is:**

Ancient Near Eastern suzerainty covenants (the form used repeatedly in Scripture) followed a consistent structure:
1. **Preamble** — Who the covenant-maker is ("I am the LORD your God")
2. **Historical prologue** — What the sovereign has already done for the vassal — the record of grace that grounds the relationship
3. **Stipulations** — How the vassal is to live *in light of* the covenant
4. **Blessings and curses** — What follows faithfulness or unfaithfulness
5. **Witnesses and renewal provisions**

The critical structural feature: **stipulations follow the historical prologue.** Obligation follows gift. Identity is declared before behavior is required. The Ten Commandments begin: "I am the LORD your God who brought you out of Egypt, out of the house of slavery. *You shall*..." The indicative always precedes the imperative. This is the grammar of covenant — and it is the structural opposite of every performance-based system.

**What this means for coaching:**

- The client's identity is *given before it is grown*
- Belonging precedes behavior — the client does not earn their way into covenant identity through performance
- The coaching work is not helping the client become someone new; it is helping them inhabit who they already are

This is categorically different from secular coaching's "who do you want to become?" It is more like: "Who has God already declared you to be — and what is in the way of living from that?"

---

## III. The Indicative / Imperative Grammar — The Structural Spine

This distinction is the hinge on which the entire model turns.

| Grammar | Form | Example |
|---------|------|---------|
| **Indicative** | Statement of what is already true | "You are a new creation" (2 Cor 5:17) |
| **Imperative** | Call to action based on what is true | "Put on the new self" (Eph 4:24) |

In Scripture, the imperative is always grounded in and made possible by the indicative. "Be who you are." The command is an invitation to inhabit a reality already established, not to construct a new one through effort.

**Legalism reverses this grammar:** Perform → then receive identity/belonging. "Do enough and you will be acceptable." This produces the shame cycle, the treadmill, the persistent sense of never being enough — regardless of how much is accomplished.

**Covenant Identity Coaching restores the grammar:** Identity declared → belonging established → behavior flows from that ground.

Every client presenting with stuck patterns, persistent shame, compulsive behavior, chronic under-performance, or spiritual dryness is — at some level — operating from reversed grammar. They are trying to perform their way into an identity they haven't received. The coaching work is identifying where the grammar flipped, what caused it to flip, and restoring it.

---

## IV. The Epistemological Foundation — What Makes This Different

Three types of Christian coaching, distinguished by epistemology:

**Type 1 — Secular coaching with Christian assumptions:**
The coaching methodology is entirely secular. Biblical worldview operates in the background but doesn't structurally shape the work. Scripture is occasionally referenced. The client's agenda and values are the sole reference point for what counts as good.

**Type 2 — Directive biblical coaching (nouthetic tradition):**
Scripture is authoritative and prescriptive. The coach/counselor is the expert who applies biblical truth to the client's situation. The client is primarily receptive. This produces good outcomes in some contexts but violates the coaching methodology's core architecture (client self-determination, evocative rather than instructive).

**Type 3 — Covenant Identity Coaching:**
The client is the authority on their *experience*. God is the authority on the client's *identity*. These are distinct and both honored. The coach holds both — fully respecting the client's experience without reducing identity to it.

**What this looks like in practice:**

When a client says: "I feel completely worthless."

- Type 1 coach: Validates the feeling and explores it. Does not bring covenant truth. May help the client *understand* the feeling but has no authoritative reference point beyond the client's own perception.
- Type 2 coach: Corrects the feeling with Scripture — "But the Bible says you're fearfully and wonderfully made." Well-intentioned. Usually produces shutdown or performance agreement. The client learns to say the right thing while feeling the same.
- Covenant Identity Coach: Honors the feeling as real data ("That sounds like a deep and familiar weight"). Gets curious about its source. Holds covenant truth as the orienting frame without weaponizing it: "I'm curious — when you feel that way, what does it feel like about who God says you are?" Works in the gap between felt experience and declared reality.

The gap is not resolved by dismissing either pole. It is the primary coaching terrain.

## V. The Covenant Identity Architecture — What the Client Has Actually Received

Covenant identity is not a single statement. It is a layered architecture, each layer addressing specific schemas and wounds.

| Layer | Declaration | Schema It Counters |
|-------|------------|-------------------|
| **Imago Dei** (Gen 1:26-27) | You bear the image of God. Your worth is ontological, not earned. | Defectiveness / Shame |
| **Known Fully and Chosen** (Ps 139, Rom 8:29) | God sees everything about you — including what you hide — and chose you anyway. | Shame + Abandonment |
| **New Creation** (2 Cor 5:17) | The old is gone. The new has come. This is positional fact, not aspiration. | Failure / Permanent Defectiveness |
| **Adopted Sonship/Daughtership** (Rom 8:15) | You are not a servant earning favor. You are a child with full inheritance rights. | Subjugation / Conditional Worth |
| **Co-heir with Christ** (Rom 8:17) | The full inheritance of Christ is shared with you. | Deprivation / Scarcity |
| **Indwelt by the Spirit** (1 Cor 6:19) | The very presence of God lives in you. You are not abandoned to your own resources. | Abandonment / Isolation |
| **Seated in Heavenly Places** (Eph 2:6) | Your current status before God is secure. This is not future — it is now. | Vulnerability / Instability |
| **Loved as the Father Loves the Son** (John 15:9) | The quality and depth of love directed at you equals the love between the Father and Son. | Emotional Deprivation |
| **Ambassador / Sent One** (2 Cor 5:20) | You carry God's presence and message into specific people and places. | Meaninglessness / Purposelessness |
| **Being Conformed to Christ's Image** (Rom 8:29) | You are not a finished product. You are a person in a divinely-superintended formation process. | Failure / Impatience with growth |

Each layer is not simply a cognitive correction. It is a covenant declaration — an ontological claim about who the client is in reality, regardless of how it feels.

The coaching work is identifying which layers the client has received and integrated, which are cognitively assented to but not embodied, and which are actively disbelieved or unfelt.

## VI. The Psychological Integration — Where the Frameworks Map

The psychological frameworks do not contradict Scripture — they describe the same underlying reality from different vantage points. Psychology provides descriptive precision for what Scripture names theologically.

### Narrative Identity Theory (McAdams)

Humans form identity by constructing an internalized story — a narrative that integrates past, present, and anticipated future into a coherent self. The client's presenting problem is almost always a narrative problem: a story being told about themselves that is either false or incomplete.

McAdams identifies **narrative contamination** — stories dominated by decline, victimhood, or shame — and **redemptive narratives** — stories that move through suffering toward meaning. The biblical meta-narrative is the original redemptive narrative: creation → fall → redemption → restoration. The client's personal story is a chapter in that larger story, not a standalone.

**Coaching application:** Help the client identify the current story they're telling. Then ask: where does that story intersect with the larger story? What chapter are they actually in? What has been redeemed that they haven't named yet?

---

### Schema Therapy (Young)

Early maladaptive schemas are deeply held beliefs and emotional patterns about self, others, and the world, developed in response to unmet core needs. They operate as assumed truth rather than as beliefs, and they resist change precisely because they feel like reality.

The schemas most relevant to identity work:
- **Defectiveness/Shame** — "I am fundamentally flawed at the core"
- **Abandonment/Instability** — "The people I love will ultimately leave"
- **Subjugation** — "My needs, wants, and feelings don't matter to others"
- **Failure** — "I have failed, am failing, and will inevitably fail"
- **Emotional Deprivation** — "No one will ever truly see or meet my needs"
- **Unrelenting Standards** — "I must achieve constantly or I am worthless"

These are the psychological mechanism of what Paul calls "strongholds" (2 Corinthians 10:4-5). "Taking every thought captive to obey Christ" is schema-level cognitive work.

The covenant identity declarations are structurally opposed to each schema. This is not proof-texting — it is theological schema replacement: displacing the operational belief with the covenant reality, practiced until it becomes the default.

**The process:**
1. Identify the active schema
2. Name its origin (where did this become true for you?)
3. Name the covenant truth that directly contradicts it
4. Build embodied practice of living from the covenant truth
5. Track evidence — where is the covenant truth proving itself true in your experience?

---

### Internal Family Systems (Schwartz)

IFS identifies the **Self** (capital S) — the undamaged, untraumatized core of the person — and multiple "parts": protectors (managers and firefighters) and exiles (wounded parts the protectors defend against).

**The theological mapping:**
- The Self maps onto the *imago Dei* — the original, unmarred image of God within the person, never fully extinguished
- The exiles map onto the wounded inner self — places of unmet need, shame, and grief locked away
- The protectors (managers) map onto flesh patterns (Galatians 5) — adaptive strategies that served survival but now obstruct life
- The firefighters map onto compulsive patterns — impulsive responses to exile pain

**The IFS goal:** Unburden the exiles and help all parts relate to the Self as leader. **The theological goal:** Crucify the old self; walk in the new creation (Galatians 2:20, Ephesians 4:22-24). Both describe the same interior movement.

**Coaching application:** When a client is stuck in a pattern they genuinely want to change, IFS language gives them access to their interior without pathologizing it: "What part of you is driving that? What is that part trying to protect? What would it need to trust the new creation self to lead instead?"

---

### Attachment Theory (Bowlby, Ainsworth)

The client's experience of God is almost always shaped by their experience of early attachment figures. God-representation is not purely theological — it is also relational and experiential.

A client with avoidant attachment often experiences God as distant or uninterested. An anxiously attached client often experiences God as unpredictable or easily disappointed. These experiential templates operate beneath theological belief — the client may cognitively affirm God's love while emotionally experiencing abandonment.

The covenant identity contains the authoritative God-representation: the Father who runs toward the prodigal, the shepherd who leaves the ninety-nine, the God who is simultaneously transcendent and "closer than our next breath" (Psalm 139:7-10).

Covenant Identity Coaching identifies where the client's experiential God-representation diverges from the revealed covenant God, and builds practices that create new attachment experiences: contemplative prayer, lament, Ignatian imagination, Psalm engagement.

---

### CBT and Cognitive Restructuring

Romans 12:2 — "be transformed by the renewing of your mind" — is cognitive transformation as the mechanism of spiritual formation. "As a man thinks in his heart, so is he" (Proverbs 23:7).

The work is not generating positive thoughts — it is replacing schema-level operational beliefs with covenant truth, practiced until the new belief becomes the automatic default. Cognitive assent alone does not produce transformation. Repetition, embodiment, and lived experience of the covenant truth are all required.

---

### Immunity to Change (Kegan & Lahey)

The competing commitments and big assumptions uncovered in immunity mapping are the active counter-testimony to the covenant identity. The big assumption ("If I assert myself, people will reject me") is an operational lie — a belief formed in wound and held as reality.

The coaching work in this model goes one level deeper than standard ITC mapping: not just "is this assumption true?" but "what would it mean about who God is and who you are if this assumption were wrong?" The theological dimension of the assumption is often the load-bearing structure. Addressing it only psychologically leaves the theological root intact.

## VII. The Primary Coaching Terrain — Working the Gap

The central territory of Covenant Identity Coaching is the gap between:

**Declared covenant identity** ← → **Felt, lived, operational identity**

This gap is not a sign of failure — it is the normal condition of the Christian life and the exact terrain where formation happens. The gap is the coaching space.

The coach holds both poles with respect and works between them — treating the gap as the productive tension that generates growth. Not: "Just believe what God says and stop feeling that way." Not: "Your feelings are your truth." Instead: honor both, and work the distance between them.

**Diagnostic questions for working the gap:**
- "When you feel [false identity], what does that mean about who God is to you?"
- "What would it cost you to believe [covenant truth] fully and live from it?"
- "Where have you seen [covenant truth] prove itself true in your actual experience?"
- "What story would you have to give up if [covenant truth] were operating?"
- "What would change about your life if you woke up tomorrow fully convinced of [covenant truth]?"

## VIII. The Distinctive Practices

### Lament — Not Optional, Structural

The Psalms are approximately 40% lament. Lament is not complaint or despair — it is a structured practice of bringing grief, anger, confusion, and suffering directly to God *within* the covenant relationship. It is the opposite of spiritual bypassing.

**The structure of biblical lament (Brueggemann):**
1. **Address** — turning toward God, invoking the covenant relationship as the container
2. **Complaint** — bringing the full weight of suffering without sanitizing it
3. **Petition** — asking for specific intervention
4. **Confession of trust** — reorientation toward God *despite* unresolved suffering
5. **Praise** — often anticipatory, before the situation changes

A client who cannot lament has almost certainly engaged in spiritual bypassing — using theological categories to avoid the emotional reality of pain. This produces brittle faith that shatters under real suffering, and a distorted identity: the client learns to present a sanitized self to God and the coach, which means the actual self — wounded, angry, confused — never gets brought into the covenant.

Lament is the biblical permission to bring the full gap — unedited — into the presence of God. It is the practice of remaining in covenant while being honest about the distance between covenant truth and lived experience.

---

### Covenant Identity Declaration — Not Affirmation

Distinct from positive affirmation (which asserts desired feelings), a covenant identity declaration is a speech act rooted in biblical authority. *Homologia* — the root of "confession" — literally means "to say the same thing." Confession of covenant truth is agreeing with what God has declared about you.

"I am a new creation in Christ. The old is gone. The new has come. Therefore I will not live today from the old story."

**The practice is:**
- Specific — addressing a particular schema or wound, not generic
- Anchored — tied to specific covenant text
- Embodied — spoken aloud, written, tied to physical practice
- Rehearsed — repeated until the new narrative becomes the default operating system

---

### Embodiment Work

Covenant identity is not only cognitive. The body holds the false identity — shame lives in the posture, the gaze, the voice.

Coaching questions:
- "Where do you feel [false identity] in your body? Where does it live?"
- "If [covenant truth] were fully true for you right now, what would your body feel like?"
- "What posture do you take when you believe the lie? What posture belongs to the covenant truth?"

Embodied practices that reinforce covenant identity: receiving posture in prayer (open hands, lifted head), speaking aloud rather than only thinking, physical engagement with lament, Communion as covenant renewal — the physical eating and drinking as bodily re-enactment of covenant belonging.

## IX. The Holy Spirit as Active Agent — Not Background Assumption

Any model claiming biblical integration must grapple structurally with the Holy Spirit's agency. This is not a theological add-on — it changes the architecture of the coaching.

The Spirit is:
- **Paraclete** (John 14:16) — the one called alongside. The coach is not the paraclete; the coach is a human instrument through whom the Paraclete works.
- **Spirit of truth** (John 16:13) — guides into all truth. Powerful questions create openings; the Spirit provides revelation.
- **Spirit of adoption** (Romans 8:15-16) — bears witness with the client's spirit that they are children of God. The deepest identity confirmation is not from the coach but from within the client.
- **The one who intercedes in weakness** (Romans 8:26-27) — present and active precisely where the client feels most stuck.

**Practical implications:**
- Prayer is not decorative — it is the coach's primary orienting practice before and during sessions
- The coach attends to both the clinical picture and what they sense the Spirit doing in the room
- Frameworks are held loosely — subject to interruption by the Spirit's movement
- The coach is not the primary agent of change; they are a means of grace operating within the Spirit's sovereign work

Discernment is a coaching competency: knowing when to follow the clinical framework and when to follow the Spirit's lead even when they diverge. This requires ongoing spiritual formation in the coach, not just methodological training.

## X. Sanctification as the Long-Term Arc

Covenant Identity Coaching is a contribution to the lifelong arc of sanctification — understood theologically in three tenses:

- **Definitive sanctification** — already accomplished positionally in Christ ("you were washed, you were sanctified, you were justified" — 1 Corinthians 6:11). This is the covenant identity. It is done.
- **Progressive sanctification** — ongoing in actual experience ("work out your salvation with fear and trembling, for it is God who works in you" — Philippians 2:12-13). This is the coaching terrain.
- **Eschatological sanctification** — completed at glorification ("when he appears, we shall be like him" — 1 John 3:2). This is the trajectory.

Coaching operates in the progressive dimension — it is a means of grace, not a substitute for grace. The change it produces is real. But the ultimate agent is God. The coach creates conditions in which the Spirit's transforming work becomes more accessible.

This humility is structural. It protects both coach and client from the performance pressure that would make coaching itself a new form of reversed grammar.

## XI. What This Is Not

**Not nouthetic/biblical counseling:** Not directive, not primarily sin-focused, does not position the coach as the expert applying Scripture. The client's own discernment and the Spirit's guidance within them are primary.

**Not discipleship or teaching:** Not content-transmission, not curriculum-driven. The client drives the agenda.

**Not spiritual direction:** Spiritual direction is contemplative and non-structured. Coaching has forward-focus, specific commitments, structures, and accountability. Complementary but distinct.

**Not therapy:** Does not diagnose or treat clinical conditions. Does not process trauma directly. Knows when clinical issues are present and refers. When identity work opens a wound that is clinical in depth, the coach holds the container and creates a bridge to clinical care.

**Not prosperity theology:** Covenant blessings are relational and eschatological — not promises of material comfort, health, or success. The covenant includes "participation in his sufferings" (Philippians 3:10). Lament is built in precisely because suffering is real and expected.

**Not moralistic therapeutic deism:** MTD says God wants you to feel good about yourself. Covenant Identity Coaching says God has acted decisively in history through Christ, has declared your identity through covenant, and is actively conforming you to his Son — which is often uncomfortable, requires death of the false self, and moves *through* suffering, not around it.

## XII. How It Differs from Existing Models

| Model | Strengths | What Covenant Identity Coaching Adds |
|-------|-----------|--------------------------------------|
| Secular Co-Active | Strong methodology, client agency | Covenant identity as authoritative reference; theological telos; Holy Spirit agency |
| Generic Christian Coaching | Faith worldview present | Covenant as *structural* frame (not decorative); indicative/imperative grammar; schema-covenant mapping |
| Biblical Counseling | Scripture authority, directness | Client agency; coaching methodology; psychological precision; lament; non-directive evocation |
| Spiritual Direction | Spirit-attentiveness, contemplative depth | Forward-focus; structure; commitments; accountability; behavioral integration |
| CBT-based Christian Counseling | Cognitive precision, skill-building | Identity-level (not just thought-level) work; covenant as the content of cognitive replacement; embodiment |

## XIII. Structure of an Engagement

**Phase 1 — Covenant Orientation**
*Establishing the ground before building the structure*
- Narrative inventory: What story have you been telling about yourself?
- Covenant identity inventory: Which layers have you received and integrated? Which are cognitively held but not felt? Which are actively disbelieved?
- Identifying the primary schemas/lies in operation
- Establishing the coaching container: How will we work? What does accountability look like here?

**Phase 2 — Wound Mapping**
*Honoring what shaped the terrain before asking the client to move through it*
- Where did the false narrative come from? When did you first learn that about yourself?
- What needs were unmet? What agreements were made with lies?
- Grief and lament work: bringing the wound into the covenant rather than around it
- Identifying competing commitments — what is protected by not changing?

**Phase 3 — Identity Installation**
*Growing into what is already declared*
- Targeted covenant truth work: specific declarations addressing specific schemas
- Embodiment: What does it feel like in your body to live from [covenant truth]?
- Behavioral experiments: acting from covenant identity before it fully feels true, and observing what happens
- Acknowledgment: naming identity votes, championing who the client is becoming in Christ
- Tracking the gap: is it narrowing? Where? What is still wide?

**Phase 4 — Mission and Fruitfulness**
*Covenant identity sent outward*
- Telos work: What are you constitutively for? What is the specific corner of the world you are sent to?
- Ambassador framing (2 Corinthians 5:20): How does your covenant identity send you into your specific relationships, work, and community?
- Building sustainable covenant renewal rhythms: prayer, community, Sabbath, lament, Scripture engagement
- Sanctification as the long arc: this coaching engagement is a chapter, not the whole story

---

## XIV. Summary Statement — The Core Thesis

Covenant Identity Coaching is the practice of helping a person close the gap between who God has declared them to be and how they actually live — using coaching methodology as the vehicle, covenant theology as the epistemological ground, psychological frameworks as descriptive tools, and the Holy Spirit as the active agent of transformation.

Its central claim is that the client's most persistent problems are identity problems — rooted in a failure to inhabit the covenant reality already established by grace — and that closing that gap is not primarily a matter of effort, strategy, or behavioral correction. It is a matter of receiving, embodying, and growing into a self that has already been given.

The coach's role is not to produce this transformation. It is to be a faithful human instrument in the hands of the one who does.
