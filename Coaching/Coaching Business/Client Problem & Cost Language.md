# Disciple Fit — Client Problem & Cost Language

**What this is:** The 17 core problems Disciple Fit exists to solve, written in client voice with the felt cost that creates motivation to invest. Built from ICF coaching demand research, counseling-scope research, and Disciple Fit niche analysis.
**Who it's for:** Andy — intake design, marketing copy, discovery call framework, social content, website
**How to use:** Lead with the felt cost in marketing and intake — not the root. The spiritual/identity root is the door you open after the client recognizes their cost.
**Date:** April 2026

---

## The Rule

**People pay when they feel the cost of the problem — not when they see the root.**
Name the felt cost first. Reveal the root second.

---

## Part 1 — Pain Away (Escape Motivation)

*Problems where something is breaking, drifting, or stuck.*

---

### 1. My marriage feels like coexistence, not partnership — and I don't know why

> "We don't fight. We don't connect either. We're roommates who share a bed and a calendar. I thought marriage would feel like something. I'm starting to wonder if this is just what it is — or if something is quietly dying."

**Cost:** Loneliness inside the relationship they built their life around. The fear that the drift is permanent. The slow grief of an unfulfilling marriage they're too committed to leave and too exhausted to fix alone.

---

### 2. I know what I believe but I can't seem to live it

> "I have the theology. I've heard the sermons. I can tell you what's true. But Monday comes and I'm anxious, reactive, and running on empty again. It doesn't translate. And I don't understand why."

**Cost:** Quiet despair that knowledge isn't enough — and the creeping suspicion that something is wrong with them, not just their effort. Leads to disengagement from faith or performance of it.

---

### 3. Anxiety and shame that my faith keeps failing to touch

> "I've prayed. I've repented. I've claimed the promises. The anxiety comes back. The shame comes back. I feel like everyone else got something I didn't — some switch that actually works. I'm exhausted from trying."

**Cost:** Spiritual disillusionment. Private hopelessness. The cost of living in a body that won't calm down despite a faith they genuinely hold. Secondary shame: "What's wrong with my faith?"

---

### 4. I don't know who I am outside of what I do or what others expect

> "I perform well. People have a good image of me. But take away my role, my title, my usefulness — and I genuinely don't know what's left. I don't know what I actually want. I'm not sure I have a self underneath all of it."

**Cost:** Fragile identity that collapses under failure, loss of role, or transition. Relationships feel transactional because they're built on performance. Deep loneliness. Fear of being truly known.

---

### 5. I keep making the same mistakes — nothing actually changes

> "I've repented of this a hundred times. I've made the decision, started strong, and ended up in the same place again. I'm starting to believe I'm just broken in a way that doesn't get fixed."

**Cost:** Eroded self-trust. Hopelessness about real change. Often leads to either spiritual withdrawal ("what's the point") or compulsive religious effort that produces exhaustion without transformation.

---

### 6. I'm isolated — performing fine but known by no one

> "I have people around me. I show up to community. But no one actually knows what's happening inside me. I've never let them. I'm not sure I know how — or if it's safe. So I keep performing okay while feeling completely alone."

**Cost:** The specific pain of loneliness inside community — which feels worse than regular loneliness because it should be different. Leads to hollow relationships, disconnected marriage, and a growing internal life no one has access to.

---

### 7. I feel spiritually stuck and I don't know what's in the way

> "I'm not in crisis. I'm not walking away from God. I'm just... flat. I've been at the same place for years. I do the things. Nothing moves. I don't know if the problem is discipline, or something deeper, or me."

**Cost:** Wasted years. Spiritual boredom masking real blockage. The cost is invisible until a crisis hits and the shallow root system fails — then it's catastrophic. The patient person paying here is the one who senses that and doesn't want to wait.

---

### 8. I'm burned out on ministry or leadership and losing faith in the process

> "I built my life around serving. I believed in it. Now I resent it. I'm going through the motions, protecting my image, and privately questioning everything. I'm afraid to say that out loud to anyone inside the church."

**Cost:** Vocational collapse. Spiritual crisis. Relational isolation — they can't be honest with the people most likely to help because those people are their congregation or team. The loneliness of leading while falling apart.

---

### 9. I'm heading into a major life transition with no solid foundation

> "Everything is about to change — marriage, a new role, a move, a loss. I know I should feel ready. I don't. I don't trust myself under pressure. I don't have a clear enough sense of who I am to know how I'll hold up."

**Cost:** Entering a defining season unprepared. The fear that the cracks they've managed in normal life will become fractures under pressure. Marriage prep, leadership transition, and parenting launch are all high here.

---

### 10. The gap between my Sunday self and Monday self is widening

> "Who I am at church and who I am the rest of the week are becoming two different people. I know it. It's starting to feel dishonest. But I don't know how to close the gap — and I'm not sure anyone around me is actually living differently."

**Cost:** Integrity erosion. Private cynicism about faith. The gap, left unaddressed, produces either full disengagement from church or full performance of it — neither of which is life. This is the slow drift toward nominal Christianity.

## Part 2 — Gain Toward (Aspiration Motivation)

*Problems where something is beginning and the person wants to enter it well.*

---

### 11. I'm stepping into a leadership role and I don't trust myself at this level

> "I got the promotion. The title. The responsibility. And the first thing I felt wasn't excitement — it was dread. I don't know if I actually have what it takes. I've been faking competence at the lower level for a while. Now the stakes are real and I can't afford to fake it."

**Cost:** The gap between the role and the identity that inhabits it. Leaders who don't close this gap either perform and burn out, or shrink and underdeliver. For Christians: the added weight of "God gave me this" makes the stakes feel even higher.

---

### 12. I'm getting married and I want to enter it whole, not just hopeful

> "I love this person. I want this. But I also know I carry things — patterns, wounds, ways of shutting down or overreacting — that I haven't dealt with. I don't want to bring that into the marriage and spend the first five years figuring out what I should have figured out before."

**Cost:** Entering the most important covenant relationship carrying unexamined baggage. The cost is paid later — in conflict cycles, emotional distance, and patterns that compound over years. Clients here are paying to prevent a future cost rather than escape a present one.

---

### 13. I want to be a different kind of parent than I had — and I don't know how

> "I swore I wouldn't do what was done to me. And then I hear my father's words come out of my mouth. Or I check out the same way my mother did. I don't want to pass this down. But wanting it to stop and knowing how to stop it are two completely different things."

**Cost:** Generational transmission. The thing they survived becoming the thing their child grows up in. For Christians: the theological weight of generational patterns makes this even more urgent and personal.

---

### 14. I sense a calling I haven't had the courage or clarity to pursue

> "There's something I keep coming back to. A direction I feel pulled toward. I've dismissed it, prayed it away, told myself it's not practical. But it won't leave. And I'm starting to realize the problem isn't the calling — it's that I don't believe I'm the kind of person it happens to."

**Cost:** The unlived life. Not dramatic — quiet. The slow accumulation of years spent building something safe while the real thing waits. For Christians: the theological weight of stewardship — "what did you do with what I gave you?"

---

### 15. I want my life to actually mean something — and I'm not building toward that

> "I'm not depressed. I'm not in crisis. I'm just... going through motions that don't add up to anything I care about. I do the next thing. Then the next thing. And occasionally I stop and think — is this it? Am I building toward something? Because it doesn't feel like it."

**Cost:** Accumulated meaninglessness. Years of competent, successful motion that doesn't compound into anything that matters. For Christians this is especially sharp: they believe life has purpose — they just can't find the thread of it in their actual week.

---

### 16. I'm launching something and the internal resistance is the problem

> "I've planned it. I know the steps. I've started three times. Every time I get close to real exposure — putting it out, asking for the sale, stepping into the room — something shuts down. I tell myself it's the strategy. But I know it's me. I'm the obstacle."

**Cost:** The idea that never becomes the thing. The ministry that stays a vision. The business that exists in a Google doc. The book that's been "almost ready" for two years. The growing suspicion that they'll always find a reason to stop short.

---

### 17. I know what I need to do — I just can't do it alone

> "I don't need someone to tell me what to do. I've read the books. I know the framework. What I need is someone who will actually hold me to it. Someone who asks the hard question, sees through the excuse, and doesn't let me off the hook. I've never had that."

**Cost:** Wasted self-knowledge. This person is unusually self-aware — which makes it more painful, not less. They can see exactly what they're not doing and why. For Christians: this is also a discipleship deficit — the absence of anyone who knows them well enough to speak into their actual life.

---

## What the Full List Reveals

Across all 17 problems, two things are almost always true:

1. **The real problem is identity** — who they believe they are, what they believe they deserve, what they believe is possible for them specifically
2. **The real deficit is relationship** — no one safe enough, honest enough, equipped enough to go there with them

That is Disciple Fit's entire value proposition in two sentences.
