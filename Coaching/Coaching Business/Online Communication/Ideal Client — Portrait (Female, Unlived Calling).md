## What Keeps Coming Back

---

Twelve years I have been carrying this.

Not loudly. That is not how it comes. It comes the way light comes in early morning — patient, without apology, through the same window every day whether I pull back the curtain or not. I make the coffee and it is there. I drive the children to school and come home to the quiet house and it is there: the sense of something asking to be done that I have not done.

I was supposed to write. Or teach. Or build something that has not yet been built. I am not precise about the form — the form keeps shifting. But the direction is constant. There is a thing I am supposed to do with my life and I have been not doing it for twelve years, while doing other things quite well.

The other things are not nothing. I have raised two children with real attention. I have loved my husband imperfectly and returned when I wandered, and I believe that matters. I have served at my church and meant it. These are not empty years. I will not say they are empty years.

But something in me knows the difference between the life I have lived and the life I was asked to live. They are not opposites. They are not even far apart. But they are not the same, and I have been telling myself for twelve years that they are close enough.

I have reasons. The reasons are real. The children are young. The money is not enough to take risks with. My husband's work is demanding and the household requires managing. There is always a season coming that will be better — a clearing in the calendar, a year when the conditions will be more suitable for beginning. I have been waiting for the conditions to become suitable for twelve years. I have become more adept at explaining why they are not.

What I have not been able to silence is this: the sense that the waiting is a choice, and the choice is fear, and the fear has a face, and its face is this — I do not believe I am the kind of person this happens to. Other women write the book. Other women build the ministry, launch the thing, step into the room and fill it. I watch them and I think: yes, for them, of course, look at them. And I go home and the quiet thing is still there in the morning, patient as a bird on the same branch, and I have run out of reasons that satisfy even me.

I am forty-one. I do the math sometimes. Not morbidly — practically. If I begin now. If I begin now, what is still possible. The math is not discouraging. What is discouraging is twelve years of not beginning. The self I have quietly confirmed, year by year, by not beginning.

I read once that the most devastating question at the end of a life is not what you did wrong, but what you left in you undone.

I have underlined that sentence. I believe it. I have not moved.

There is a difference between knowing the truth and inhabiting it. I have been on the wrong side of that difference for twelve years, and I am tired of having good reasons for staying there.

## What Coaching Did

The first thing coaching named was not a goal or a plan — it was a belief: *I am not the kind of person this happens to.* Asked to say it out loud and then asked where that verdict came from, she heard it for what it was. Not a fact. Not a reasonable assessment of her capacity. A schema — old, pre-faith, assembled in a family where ambition in women was quietly discouraged and visibility carried a cost no one named but everyone enforced.

CBT mapped the belief and traced its behavioral consequences: the procrastination, the perpetual preparation, the last-minute retreat from every threshold. Scripture reframed the weight of it — not the pressure of potential but the stewardship of the gift, the image of the servant who buried what he was given and the one who did not. The coaching relationship added what neither could deliver alone: someone who asked the next question and did not accept a more comfortable answer in its place.

She did not launch everything in six months. She wrote the first three pages of the thing she had been not writing for twelve years. She sent them to one person.

The person asked for more.

That is where things that matter begin — not with the ideal conditions, but with the one true move made in the absence of them.
