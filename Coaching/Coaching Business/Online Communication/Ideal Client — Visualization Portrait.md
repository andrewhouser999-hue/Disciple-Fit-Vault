**Segment:** Sunday-Monday Christian (Primary Target)
**Style:** Frederick Buechner — confessional, luminous, honest about the weight of things without melodrama.

## The Man Who Has Everything Working

---

I am good at this. I want you to know that first, because it matters that you understand what kind of man I am before I tell you what kind of man I am. I show up. I have always shown up. Sunday I lead the men's Bible study at 8 a.m. before the main service — fifteen men around a table, coffee in paper cups, me with my notes prepared, asking the right questions, holding the silence the right length of time before offering the observation that opens something up. They trust me. I know they trust me because I can feel the weight of it, the way they lean toward me when I talk, the way Jim — who lost his job eight months ago and hasn't told his wife how bad it is — will sometimes catch my eye across the table as if to ask whether I see him. I do see him. I am good at seeing people. I just don't know what to do with being seen.

Monday I am at my desk at 7:15 a.m. The house is quiet. My wife is still sleeping. The coffee is hot. I open my Bible because I am the kind of man who opens his Bible in the morning, and I sit there — and I want to be careful here, I want to be accurate and not dramatic — I sit there and something has gone. Whatever it was on Sunday morning, whatever moved through the room when we talked about the Father's love and I could almost feel the truth of it pressing against my chest, it is gone by Monday at 7:15 a.m. Not gone like a fire gone out. Gone like a word you were certain you knew until you needed it, and then you stood there in the middle of the sentence with your mouth open and nothing.

I have been a Christian for twenty-two years.

I know what I believe. I could write a short systematic theology from memory, not complete but enough — the nature of grace, the mechanics of justification, the inner witness of the Spirit, the difference between guilt and shame and why one is from God and the other is not. I know it. I have taught it. I believe it, I think, in whatever part of me belief happens. But here is what I cannot tell you: what it would feel like to live inside it. To actually inhabit the thing I can describe. To be the man the doctrine is describing when it says I am loved, when it says I am new, when it says the Spirit dwells in me and makes intercession with groanings too deep for words, because my groanings lately have no words and I am not sure that's the same thing.

My wife asked me last week — she was standing at the kitchen sink, not looking at me, which is how she asks the real things — she asked me if I was happy. I said of course. I said it before I thought about it. And she nodded, not the nod of someone who believes you but the nod of someone who has decided not to press. I watched her back and thought: she knows. She doesn't know what she knows, she can't name it, but she knows the man she is living with is not all the way here. I am not all the way here. I have not been all the way here for — I started to count and stopped counting.

This is the thing I cannot say to anyone: I am performing. Not lying, not pretending exactly, because I believe the things I say when I say them. But I have built something — a self, a reputation, a role — and I maintain it the way you maintain a house, not from love of the house but from the understanding that if you stop maintaining it, something will be lost that you cannot get back. The men's group trusts me. My team at work trusts me. My kids think I am solid. My wife thinks — I don't know anymore what she thinks. I think she is lonely in a way she cannot name and I am the reason and I do not know how to stop being the reason without first knowing who I would be without the performance.

There is a verse I have read a hundred times: *Behold, you desire truth in the inner parts.* I have preached on that verse. I have read the commentaries. What I have not done, what I cannot seem to do, is sit with the full weight of it — the implication that He is looking at the inner parts, that what He wants from me is not my study notes or my Sunday morning or the right answer given in the right tone of pastoral warmth, but something true from somewhere I have never allowed anyone, including Him, to actually enter.

I have prayed. I want to be clear: I have prayed. Not the formal prayers of a man who knows how prayer should sound, though I have prayed those too. I mean the desperate kind, 11 o'clock at night in the garage sitting in the car in the dark because I couldn't explain why I needed to sit in the car and I didn't want to explain. *Fix this. Whatever this is, fix it. I don't know how to name it. I have everything. I have everything and something is terribly wrong and I don't know if that makes me sick or just ungrateful.* Both, maybe. The answer to both prayers is the same silence, and I have learned to be okay with the silence, which may be the problem. I have learned to be okay with too many things.

My son is seventeen. He watches me. I notice it sometimes at dinner — the way he watches me answering his mother's question, the way he is taking notes on something, logging data, building his model of what a man is by watching me be one. And there is something in that gaze that terrifies me because I know what he is seeing. He is seeing the performance. He is seeing the maintenance. He is seeing a man who does all the right things and feels almost none of them, and he is deciding whether that is what manhood is, whether that is what faith is, whether the whole thing is just a very sophisticated act that you learn to sustain. I do not want him to decide that. I do not want that for him. I do not know how to give him something different when I don't have it myself.

Last Sunday I drove home from church and I sat in the driveway for seven minutes. Not doing anything. Not on my phone. Just sitting. And something in me — not a voice, not a vision, nothing that dramatic — something in me said: *you cannot keep going like this.* And I knew what it meant. It meant that the version of me I have been managing is not actually sustainable, which I think I have known for a long time, and the only question is whether the collapse, when it comes, will be chosen or whether it will arrive the way collapses arrive when you don't choose them, in the worst possible moment, taking the things you love down with it.

I am not walking away from God. I want to be clear about that too. I am not having a crisis of belief. I believe. I believe in the resurrection, in the Word made flesh, in the whole enormous impossible claim. What I don't know is what to do with the fact that believing it and being changed by it have turned out to be different things — that I can hold the truth in my head and still be running on something else entirely down in the machinery of me, something old and afraid and relentlessly competitive and desperate for approval, the same gears that were turning before I ever heard the gospel, turning still, and the gospel somehow sitting above them rather than inside them.

I am forty-four years old. My father is sixty-eight and I can see him in me now in ways I couldn't see at thirty. The same efficiency, the same competence, the same loneliness that he called independence. I do not want to be sixty-eight and look back at this decade and know that I stayed on the surface of my own life because the surface was safe, because I was good at the surface, because no one asked me to go deeper and I never volunteered.

I need someone who will ask.

I need someone who knows the difference between the man I appear to be and the man I actually am — not to condemn the distance between them but to walk with me into it. Someone who has been inside the machinery of a Christian life and knows that knowledge is not the same as formation, that performance is not the same as presence, that the gap between what I know and who I am is not a moral failure but it is a wound, and wounds do not close by themselves, and I have been waiting for mine to close by itself for twenty-two years.

I am good at this. And I am so tired of being good at this. And I am ready — which is not the same as unafraid — I am ready, if someone shows me the door, to walk through it.
