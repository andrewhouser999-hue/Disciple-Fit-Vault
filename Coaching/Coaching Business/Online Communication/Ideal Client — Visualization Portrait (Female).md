## The Woman Who Is Fine

---

I am fine. I want to start there because I say it approximately forty times a day and it has become the sentence my life is organized around. Fine when my husband asks. Fine when my mom calls. Fine when Brittany from women's Bible study squeezes my arm in the hallway and tilts her head and says how are you doing, really — which is the thing women do when they want to seem like they're asking the real question without actually wanting the real answer, and I know this because I do it too, I do it all the time, I am very good at the tilted head. I am fine. Everything is fine. And if I say it one more time I think I might come apart at the seams in the Chick-fil-A drive-through, which is where I do most of my crying because it is the one place I am reliably alone.

I have two kids, a nine-year-old and a six-year-old, and a husband who is a genuinely good man, which matters, which I know matters, and I hold onto that fact the way you hold onto a railing on a boat — not because the water is rough exactly, but because something in you knows it could get rough and you don't fully trust your own legs. He is good. We are fine. We have not had a real conversation in something like eight months, not one where I said what was actually happening inside me, because what is actually happening inside me is not something I can explain without sounding ungrateful or broken or like someone who needs to be fixed, and I already feel like someone who needs to be fixed, I don't need to also say it out loud and watch his face try to arrange itself into the right response.

I became a Christian at twenty-six, which felt like — I don't know how to say this without sounding like a Sunday school answer — it felt like someone turned a light on in a room I had been navigating in the dark my whole life. I am not being dramatic. It was real. It is still real, in whatever part of me real things live, which lately feels like a very small part, a closet in the back of the house that I keep meaning to get to. I believe in Jesus. I read my Bible. I go to church. I lead the Thursday morning women's study and I am good at it, I am genuinely good at it — I prepare, I ask good questions, I notice when someone is hurting and I follow up — and I drive home from it every Thursday morning feeling completely empty in a way I cannot explain, like I poured everything out and there was nothing underneath it to refill from.

Here is the thing nobody tells you about being a Christian woman in your thirties: the performance required is total. It is not enough to show up — you have to show up grateful. Joyful. Joyful is the word. The fruit of the Spirit is love joy peace patience and I have ranked them and joy is where I fail, joy is the one that mocks me, because I am doing everything I know to do and there is this flatness underneath it all that doesn't move, that doesn't respond to prayer or Scripture memory or the worship playlist I keep on while I fold laundry, and I don't know what's wrong with me, I genuinely don't know, and the not knowing is its own kind of hell because at least if I knew I could fix it, I could repent of it, I could put it on the prayer request list. But this — this flat gray thing that lives in my chest — has no name that I've been given permission to use.

I think about my mother a lot lately. The way she kept the house perfect and smiled at my father's work friends and then sat at the kitchen table alone after we went to bed, just sat there, not reading, not doing anything, just sitting. I swore I would be different. I said it to myself at my wedding, I said it when I held my daughter for the first time — I am going to be present, I am going to be real, I am not going to disappear inside the performance of a life. And then somewhere between the second kid and the small group we host and the volunteer coordinator role I said yes to at church because no one else would do it, I looked up and realized I had disappeared anyway. Just differently. With better theology.

The anxiety started three years ago and I would like to talk about the anxiety because I think it is important and because no one in my life has let me talk about it without immediately offering me a solution. It lives in my chest. It shows up at 3 a.m. and it shows up in the school pickup line and it shows up during worship sometimes, which feels like a particular cruelty — standing there with my hands open, singing about the peace that passes understanding, and my nervous system doing whatever it is doing, just completely ignoring the part where peace is supposed to be passing through me. I have prayed about the anxiety. I have claimed the verses. I have done the deep breathing. I have confessed every sin I could locate. It comes back. It always comes back. And the thing underneath the anxiety, the thing that makes the anxiety so exhausting, is the secondary shame about it: what does it mean that I believe what I believe and I am still this afraid? What does it mean about my faith? What does it mean about me?

I don't say any of this on Thursdays. I say: God is so faithful. I say: this passage wrecked me this week in the best way. I say: I'm still working through some things but I know He's in it. All of which is true. None of which is what's actually happening. And the women nod and write things in their journals and I watch them and I wonder if any of them are doing what I'm doing, if any of them go home and sit in the car in the driveway for a few extra minutes before going inside because they need just a moment, just one minute, of not being needed and not performing, before they open the door and become Mom again.

I love my children so much it frightens me. That is not the problem. The problem is that I have built my entire sense of self on being needed by them, and occasionally — in the 3 a.m. moments, in the Chick-fil-A moments — I feel the terror underneath that arrangement. Because what happens when they don't need me the same way? What happens when the need that has been my identity for nine years slowly redistributes itself into their friendships and their own lives and their own stories, and I am left standing in a kitchen I kept clean for a decade, and I have to answer the question my life has been organized around not answering: who am I when I'm not being useful to someone?

I don't know. I actually do not know. I have been a daughter, a wife, a mother, a Bible study leader, a volunteer coordinator, a good friend, a church member, a woman who brings meals to people when they have babies or surgeries, a person who remembers birthdays, a person who sends the encouraging text — and underneath all of those I do not know who I am. I don't know what I like, really like, not what I'm supposed to like. I don't know what I want for my life in any sense that doesn't immediately route back through what other people need from me. I lost that thread somewhere and I'm not sure I ever had it to begin with, I'm not sure I was ever taught that it was mine to have.

My daughter asked me last week what my favorite color was. She is nine. She asked casually, the way nine-year-olds ask things, not expecting the question to land in me like a stone. And I started to answer and I — I didn't know. I have a favorite color that I say, blue, but I said it and I thought: is that true? Is that actually true or is that the answer I've been giving since someone asked me in fourth grade and I haven't revisited it since? And I know it sounds ridiculous to say that a question about a favorite color undid me, but it wasn't about the color. It was about the realization that I might be living a life made entirely of answers I gave a long time ago and never went back to check.

I am so tired. Not the tired that sleep fixes. The tired of maintaining. The tired of being simultaneously the most responsible person in every room I'm in and the loneliest. The tired of sitting in a Bible study full of women who love me and feeling completely, hermetically alone, because love and being known are different things and I have a lot of the first and almost none of the second and I didn't realize for a long time that you could have one without the other.

I want someone to know me. The real version, the 3 a.m. version, the one who doesn't know what her favorite color actually is, the one who is failing at joy and scared and exhausted and still — still — believes, stubbornly, embarrassingly, believes that God is real and present and that something in this life is supposed to be more than what I've made of it. I want someone who can hold all of that at the same time without fixing it too fast, without handing me another verse, without tilting their head. Someone who has sat in their own car in their own driveway and knows what that feels like and came out the other side of it changed.

I am so tired of being fine.

I am ready to be something true instead.
