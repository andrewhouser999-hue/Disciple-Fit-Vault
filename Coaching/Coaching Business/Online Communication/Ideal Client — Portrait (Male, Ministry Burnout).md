# Ideal Client — Portrait (Male, Ministry Burnout)

**Segment:** Wilderness Christian — Male
**Core Problems:** #8 Burned out on ministry, #7 Spiritually stuck, #4 No self beneath the roles
**Style:** Cormac McCarthy — spare, declarative, the weight of things stated simply and left to stand
**Date:** April 2026

---

## The Man Who Poured It All Out

---

Eleven years. He gave those years the way a man gives years when he believes the thing he's giving them to is worth more than the cost. He still believes that. He needs you to understand that first.

He just doesn't know who he is without it.

He was an elder for six of those years. Youth ministry before that, then small groups, then oversight of the entire discipleship structure of a church of eight hundred people. He built it. He loved building it. He loved the men who grew under the structure — who came in one way and left another, who sat across from him in coffee shops and said things they had never said to anyone. He was good at that. At receiving the thing people had never said to anyone.

Then the thing with the senior pastor. He will not relitigate it. He knows what happened. He knows what was done and what was said and he has forgiven it, he thinks, he is working on forgiving it, which is not the same thing but it is what he has. What he cannot work past is this: that the men he had discipled chose the institution over the truth. That they sat in that room and heard what was being done and did not speak. He had believed, without knowing he believed it, that the community he had built was the kind of community where the truth gets spoken.

He was wrong. That is the thing that broke.

He left. Quietly. He told his wife it was time for a change. He has not told her what broke.

Sunday mornings now he sits in the back of a church he chose because it is large enough that no one knows him. The anonymity was supposed to feel like rest. Mostly it feels like exile. He sings the songs. He listens to the sermon. The sermon is fine. Everything is fine. Nothing touches anything.

He prays in the mornings. Honest, inarticulate prayers — the kind that feel like standing at a door and knocking without being certain anyone is on the other side. He believes someone is on the other side. He believes it the way he believes in gravity. It doesn't require his mood or his certainty. It simply is. But the warmth is gone. The sense of being known by the God he is praying to. The sense that any of this is going anywhere.

He is fifty-one. He looks at the years behind him and they do not add up to what he thought they would. He looks at the years ahead and there is a blankness he does not know how to enter. He has spent thirty years being useful to God inside structures. The structures failed him. He does not know how to be useful outside them. He does not know, honestly, if useful is even the frame anymore — or if that was always the problem, and what was broken was already broken before the betrayal gave it a name.

He needs someone who knows the difference between a man whose faith is failing and a man whose faith has been stripped of everything it was propped against — and who can see that what remains is real.

---

## What Coaching Did

Coaching gave him a distinction he could not make alone: the difference between God and the institution through which he had experienced God. They had become so fused, after thirty years, that when the institution wounded him he felt it as abandonment by God. A skilled coach — working from Scripture and schema therapy simultaneously — separated the two. The wound belonged to the institution. The relationship with God was older than the wound and had survived it.

What followed was slower: excavating the identity that had been built entirely on ministry output. The schemas from boyhood — worth earned through service, love expressed through productivity — had been spiritually baptized rather than healed. Coaching named them and began to address them at the root, not with information, but with the sustained attention of a relationship that did not require his performance to continue.

He is not in formal ministry. He may not return. But he knows who he is for the first time in his adult life in a way that does not depend on a title, a structure, or the approval of men who chose the institution over the truth.

That is not a small thing. That is everything.
