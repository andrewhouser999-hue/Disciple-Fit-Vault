## The Woman Who Saves Men

---

I know exactly what I do. I want to start there because I am tired of the version of this story where I pretend I didn't see it coming. I saw it coming with Marcus. I saw it coming with Derek. I am watching it happen right now with Joel and I am doing it anyway, which is the part I cannot explain — and the part I most need someone to help me explain.

What I do is this: I find men who are drowning and I become the reason they don't. I am extraordinarily good at finding them — I could charge for it — and I am extraordinarily good at the saving, the steadiness, the practical wisdom, the middle-of-the-night phone calls, the way I can sit with someone in the worst of themselves without flinching. I was told my whole life this was a gift. It is a gift. It is also the thing that has cost me every relationship I have ever tried to build.

Marcus got sober. We dated for three years while he was getting sober and I was incredible, I will say that honestly — patient, consistent, I loved him well. And when he was sober and stable and no longer needed the kind of love I know how to give, he fell in love with a woman from his home group who needed nothing from him, and he married her. I went to the wedding. I smiled the entire time. I am very good at performing okay.

Derek was a different flavor of the same thing — not addiction but failure, a man whose business had collapsed and whose confidence had collapsed with it. I talked him back to himself. I prayed for him, genuinely. I believed in him when he couldn't believe in himself. I watched him rebuild and become someone who didn't need me anymore. He was very kind about it. I wish he had been cruel. It would have been easier to carry.

Joel is not drowning. Not exactly. He is just diminished — capable of more than he's living, and I can see the more the way I always can, like a frequency I'm tuned for. And I am already doing the thing. Already making myself the steadying presence, already organizing my interior life around his becoming. And I know — I know — that in two years I will be watching him become, and I will not be in the picture.

I am thirty-eight years old and I do not know how to be loved by someone who does not need me. I am not sure I believe I deserve to be. When a man is steady and healthy and wants me simply because he wants me — not because I'm useful, not because I can see things in him he can't see yet — I get bored, or scared, or I find the subtle thing wrong with him and I widen it until it's a reason to go.

I am a Christian. I believe God loves me. I believe it the way I believe a lot of things — in the part of me that processes information about God and agrees with it. Whether I believe it in the part of me that makes decisions about what I deserve, who I am allowed to want, what I am worth when I am not being useful to someone — that is a different question.

The answer to that question is apparently no. Not yet.

And that not yet is the thing I cannot pray my way through alone.

## What Coaching Did

The pattern had a root she had not reached. Coaching found it: an identity built entirely on being needed, assembled in a childhood where love was conditional on attentiveness — on reading the room, managing the mood, becoming useful before the withdrawal came. The schema, stated plainly: *I am only as valuable as what I give. Relationships are only safe when I am the one with more to offer.*

CBT named the distortion and traced it from childhood forward through three relationships. Scripture named the lie at the center: that she was a servant before she was a daughter, that her worth had to be earned inside every relationship including the one with God.

Coaching held both and gave her something neither therapy nor church alone had provided: a framework in which her worth was declared before she did anything — and a relationship that proved it by not requiring performance to continue.

The savior pattern did not end in a session. It ended slowly, as she began to practice staying in discomfort without rescuing, receiving care without deflecting, letting a relationship exist without organizing her identity around its survival.

She did not find the right relationship in coaching. She became a woman who could recognize one.

That was the beginning.
