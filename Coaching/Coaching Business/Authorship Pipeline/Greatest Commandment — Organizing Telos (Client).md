# What All of This Is For

*Coach reference version: [[Greatest Commandment — Organizing Telos (Coach)]]*

You have been in church thirty years. Raised your kids in the faith. You have served — the worship team, the small group, whatever the committee was that no one else would take — probably more than one role at a time. Your quiet times are real. You know your Bible. You have heard the sermons.

And somewhere in the last few years, something stopped adding up.

Not dramatically. No collapse, no crisis. Just a quiet ceiling. You keep adding things — another discipline, another study, more service, more commitment. The return keeps declining. You are functioning well by every visible measure. But function is not what you were promised, and somewhere beneath the discipline you know it.

When you get honest, you describe it as stuck. Or flat. Or like going through motions you cannot stop performing because you do not know what you would be without them.

What you may not know is that the problem is not effort. You have plenty of that.

The problem is that no one has told you what you are building toward.

---

Thirty years of forward motion with no named destination produces exactly this. Theology accumulated, disciplines sustained, service records built, relationships invested in — and eventually a ceiling that more accumulation cannot break. The ceiling is not made of effort. It is made of orientation. You have been moving with discipline and without direction, and at some point those two things produce the same wall.

Jesus named the direction. He was asked which commandment was greatest — the organizing principle beneath everything else. His answer was not a discipline, not a doctrine, not a behavior.

*Love the Lord your God with all your heart, soul, mind, and strength. And love your neighbor as yourself.*

You have heard this hundreds of times. You can say it before the question is finished. And it has likely become what church answers become after thirty years: something you can say correctly without it meaning anything on a Tuesday morning.

That is precisely what needs to change.

---

The commandment is not asking you to try harder.

This is what most people at this stage miss — and it is the thing most worth saying directly. The Greatest Commandment is not a higher bar set above the ones you have already been failing to clear. It is a description. A picture of what a person looks like whose interior world has been cleared enough for love to actually move through it.

You have spent your life building things that actually last — family, faith, relationships, service records. That is not a criticism. It is how people like you raise kids, sustain communities, and keep commitments that other people let fall away. Building is what you know.

But you cannot build toward this.

The person who loves God with everything they have is not loving harder. They have less in the way. The decades of effort that produced function without formation — theology without translation, belief without felt reality, the Sunday self and the Thursday self that keep diverging no matter how much you intend otherwise — are not evidence of insufficient effort. They are evidence of obstacles that effort cannot remove by adding more effort on top of them.

What is in the way is specific. It is identifiable. And it can be cleared.

---

Here is what is almost always true at this stage.

The ceiling is not intellectual. You have the theology. The ceiling is not behavioral. You have the disciplines. The ceiling is in the places you have never looked at directly — because there was always something more urgent, something more productive, something that did not require sitting still long enough to notice what was underneath the motion.

The beliefs about God that were formed not from Scripture but from the years when God did not show up the way you expected. The identity that was built on what you produce or who you care for rather than what you have received — functional for decades, but increasingly costly as the roles that have defined you begin to shift. The relational patterns that keep you performing in every room rather than present in any of them. The grief you never processed. The slow drift between what you believe on Sunday and how you actually live by Thursday.

These are not failures of effort. They are obstacles.

And every one of them sits directly between where you are and the love the commandment describes.

The love is not the destination you reach after clearing the obstacles. The love is what is already there when the obstacles are gone. You are not building toward it. You are clearing toward it.

---

There is a second half to the commandment that tends to get underweighted at this stage: *love your neighbor as yourself.*

After thirty years of serving your church, this can sound like a category you are already in. You serve. You give. You show up.

But the commandment is not about service output. It is about presence. The neighbor it describes is not the beneficiary of what you produce. It is the person in the room you are actually in — and the question is whether you are actually there, or whether the performance of being there has quietly replaced the thing itself.

Most people at this stage have gotten very good at being in rooms. Fewer have learned to be present in them.

The gap between those two things is one of the most honest measures of where the love work stands.

---

Every piece of work in this process is pointed here. Not at being better — at being clearer. Not at adding more — at identifying what is in the way.

The question is not *How do I love God more?*

It is: *What is blocking me from receiving the love that makes loving possible?*

That question has a specific answer. The answer changes. The direction does not.
