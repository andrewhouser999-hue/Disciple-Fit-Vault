# Do It Again: On Holy Repetition, the Stuck Soul, and the Long Work of Becoming

*In the voice of G.K. Chesterton | Film: Groundhog Day*

---

I want to draw attention to a film that is, so far as I can tell, one of the great works of modern theological comedy — which is only fitting, since comedy is itself a theological form and the moderns have mostly forgotten this.

The film is *Groundhog Day*, in which a man named Phil Connors — a television weatherman of profound and carefully cultivated unpleasantness — finds himself condemned to relive the same day in the same small town, apparently forever, without any apparent escape and without any apparent reason.

Most people, when they describe this film, call it a fantasy or a comedy. They are not wrong. But they are, I think, missing the deeper game. *Groundhog Day* is a film about sanctification, which is to say it is a film about the most important and least glamorous process available to a human being: the slow, repeated, frequently failing, ultimately irreversible work of becoming someone worth being.

---

Let us begin with what is obvious and therefore almost always overlooked. Phil Connors is not trapped in a terrible day. He is trapped in a perfectly ordinary one. The same alarm clock. The same song. The same Pennsylvanian groundhog. The same conversations, the same puddle to avoid, the same citizens of Punxsutawney going about their thoroughly unremarkable business.

The torment is not that the day is bad. The torment is that the day is *the same*. And it occurs to me that this is a remarkably precise picture of the complaint that most people have about their lives — not that anything has gone catastrophically wrong, but that everything has gone comprehensively predictable. The same breakfast. The same commute. The same arguments. The same loop.

The film's genius is that it takes this very ordinary complaint and makes it literal, and then does something no ordinary complaint ever does: it asks what would happen if the loop continued long enough that escape became impossible and only transformation remained.

---

Now I have written — somewhere in a book about Christianity that many people have read without quite understanding — that it is possible God says every morning "Do it again" to the sun. That the monotony of sunrise is not mechanism but delight. That what we take for the automatic behavior of a universe on rails may in fact be the inexhaustible appetite of a God who has never grown tired of the same morning and therefore continues, with what one can only describe as divine enthusiasm, to produce it. It may be, I suggested, that God makes every daisy separately and has never got tired of making them. That He has, in short, the eternal appetite of infancy — for we have sinned and grown old, and our Father is younger than we.

The reason I raise this here is that Phil Connors goes through precisely the stages we go through when confronted with any repetition we did not choose: first rage, then exploitation, then despair, then — and this is the part that takes the longest and costs the most — transformation.

The rage is comprehensible. The exploitation is instructive. Watch what Phil does when he realizes the rules have changed and the usual consequences no longer apply: he uses the day. He seduces. He steals. He lies with impunity, because tomorrow the lie will not exist. He is, in short, a perfectly representative human being operating in a world without accountability — which is another name for a world without meaning.

And then — this is the most important moment in the film, and it happens quietly, almost accidentally — he runs out of things to exploit. He has done everything one can do with a lawless day. He has experienced every pleasure the town can offer and found that none of them last past midnight. He has, in a word, discovered that the problem was never the day.

The problem was Phil.

---

This is the discovery that is perpetually available to us and perpetually avoided. Most of us spend considerable energy diagnosing the loop — the repetitive patterns of our lives, the same argument that keeps happening, the same job that keeps disappointing, the same relationship dynamic that keeps emerging regardless of which relationship we are in. We are very good at describing the loop. We are considerably less good at entertaining the possibility that the loop is not primarily a feature of our circumstances.

The loop is a feature of us. Or rather, it is the curriculum we keep generating for ourselves because we have not yet learned the lesson it is trying to teach.

I do not mean this cruelly. The loop is not punishment. It is — if I may push the theological metaphor to its proper conclusion — more like a patient teacher who knows that the student has not yet understood and continues to present the material, in slightly varied forms, until the understanding arrives. The insults of the loop are real. But so is its instruction. And the instruction is always the same: not *what must you do differently* but *who must you become*.

---

Phil becomes a pianist. He becomes a sculptor in ice. He learns to speak French and to catch a boy who falls from a tree at precisely the right moment because he has watched the fall enough times to know exactly when it will happen. He becomes, over the course of an unspecified number of days that the film wisely declines to count, someone who can be fully present to the people around him — not because the people have become more interesting, but because Phil has become more capable of interest.

This is sanctification. I will not apologize for the word. It is the word for the thing, and the thing is real, and it looks almost exactly like this: not a dramatic single conversion but a patient, repeated, often tedious accumulation of small choices toward love until the small choices become, without anyone quite noticing, a character.

The day breaks when Phil breaks — not in despair, which he has already tried, but in openness. When he stops trying to escape the day and starts trying to inhabit it. When the repetition ceases to be a prison and becomes, instead, the particular gift of being given the same twenty-four hours again until you learn to use them for something other than yourself.

God says every morning, Do it again. Phil Connors, eventually, begins to understand what this means.

---

The coaching implication of all this is not complicated, though it is demanding. Most people who seek out coaching are in a loop. They know they are in a loop. They have tried, often many times, to exit the loop by changing the external conditions — the job, the relationship, the city, the routine. And the loop has followed them, because the loop is not the conditions. It is the self that generates them.

A skilled coach does not help a person escape the loop. A skilled coach helps a person *learn the loop's lesson* — which is always, underneath the specifics, something about what the person believes about themselves and what they are capable of and whether they are worthy of the kind of life they are, despite everything, still reaching for.

This is slow work. It is not slow because the coach is inefficient. It is slow because transformation is slow — because the self changes the way a glacier moves, imperceptibly in any single moment and dramatically over time, and because there are no shortcuts, however much we would prefer them.

What the coach provides, in the midst of the loop, is what Phil finally found: a reason to care about the day that has nothing to do with escaping it. A reason to learn the piano, and save the boy from the tree, and be fully, warmly, generously present to the people who are, after all, right there — in the same town, under the same February sky — waiting to be seen.

The day you are in is the day you have. And the person you become in it is the only person who will ever walk out the other side.

Do it again. And again. Until you mean it.

## Author's Notes

This piece addresses the **stuck-in-loops / sanctification-as-non-linear** client — the one who feels like nothing is changing, who has tried to fix the external conditions repeatedly, who cannot see that the direction is being set even when the scenery looks identical.

- Chesterton's "Do it again" passage from *Orthodoxy* is the theological engine of the piece and can be quoted directly in sessions or content.
- The piece reframes the coaching relationship from "how do I escape this" to "what is this trying to teach me" — a significant shift for clients who are spiritually sophisticated but behaviorally stuck.
- Phil's arc through rage → exploitation → despair → transformation is a reliable map for clients in the middle stages of change who are losing faith in the process.
- The Chesterton voice is the most surprising and energetic in the series — highest potential for shareability with readers who are fatigued by earnest Christian content.
- Pull quote: *"The loop is not the conditions. It is the self that generates them."*
