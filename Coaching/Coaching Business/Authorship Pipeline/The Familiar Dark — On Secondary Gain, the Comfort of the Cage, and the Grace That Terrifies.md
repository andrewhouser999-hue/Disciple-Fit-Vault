# The Familiar Dark: On Secondary Gain, the Comfort of the Cage, and the Grace That Terrifies

*In the voice of Brennan Manning | Film: The Shawshank Redemption*

---

There is a scene in *The Shawshank Redemption* that I find almost unbearable to watch. An old man named Brooks is released from prison after fifty years. He has served his time. The doors open. The world is waiting. And he falls apart.

He carves his name into a rafter of his halfway house — *Brooks Was Here* — and hangs himself.

I want to sit with that for a moment, because I think we are too quick to call it tragedy and move on. What happened to Brooks is not a mystery. It is one of the most honest pictures of the human soul I have encountered outside the Scriptures. Brooks did not die because freedom was denied him. He died because freedom was *given* to him, and he could not receive it.

The prison had become his home. The walls that once confined him had, over fifty years, become the definition of safety. Outside those walls, he was nobody — a frightened old man in a world that had moved on without him, that did not know his name, that owed him nothing. Inside, he had been the librarian. He had mattered. He had a role, a rhythm, a reason to get up in the morning. The cage had given him all of that.

This is what the therapists call secondary gain, and what I would simply call the comfort of the known dark. It is the reason people stay in situations that are clearly killing them. It is the reason the man who says he wants to change is the first one to sabotage the change when it finally arrives. The prison is terrible. But the prison is known. And the known terrible is always, at some animal level, preferable to the unknown good.

Let me be ruthlessly honest with you: I know Brooks. I have been Brooks. I spent years choosing the familiar dark of the bottle over the terrifying brightness of sobriety, not because alcohol was good but because it was *mine*, and I knew how to live inside it. The cage had formed around me so gradually that I had long since stopped noticing it was a cage. I called it comfort. I called it coping. I called it the only thing standing between me and a world I did not know how to navigate sober.

Grace was the threat. Not the warden.

---

This is precisely what the Scriptures are describing when they speak of the Israelites longing for Egypt. These are people who were slaves. Who were beaten, who watched their sons drowned in the Nile, who cried out to God for generations. And when God delivers them — when the sea parts and the army drowns and the wilderness opens before them — they look at the manna and the quail and say: *we remember the fish we ate in Egypt for free, the cucumbers and the melons and the leeks and the onions and the garlic.*

The onions of Egypt. The memory is of the food, not the whip. The soul under long captivity does not remember its captivity accurately. It remembers the familiar. It has built a life inside the walls, and what it fears most is not more suffering. What it fears most is having to become someone it does not yet recognize.

This is not weakness. It is not a character defect, though it produces terrible consequences. It is what happens to a soul that has survived by adapting to circumstances that should have broken it — and in adapting, has come to need the very thing it endured. The cage becomes companion. The wound becomes identity. The sorrow becomes so well-known that joy, when it comes, feels like the stranger.

---

The film gives us another man — Andy Dufresne — who is imprisoned unjustly and spends nearly twenty years inside Shawshank without ever, not for a single day, accepting it as his permanent address. He keeps a poster on his wall. He plays Mozart through the prison loudspeakers until they shut him down. He writes letters every week until the library gets funded. He maintains, at great personal cost, an interior freedom that the prison cannot confiscate because the prison does not know where he keeps it.

And he tunnels. Through twenty years and six inches of rock and ocean upon ocean of excrement, he tunnels — not because he can see the ocean on the other side, but because he refuses to let his outer circumstances become his inner ones.

Andy represents what it looks like when a person refuses the secondary gain of the cage — when they will not let comfort calcify into identity, when they insist, against all the evidence of their circumstances, that they belong to a different story than the one being told about them.

Red, by contrast, is institutionalized. He knows it. He says it plainly, with no self-pity: *I've been in here most of my life. I'm an institutional man now.* He has made peace with the cage, not because he loves it, but because making peace with it was the only way to survive it. When he is finally released, he nearly becomes Brooks. He stands at the edge of the road with a pistol and considers it seriously.

What saves Red is a promise Andy made him — a specific place, a specific instruction, a specific invitation issued long before Red was ready to receive it. Someone had already seen beyond Red's institutionalization to the man he could still become, and had left him coordinates.

This is the office of a guide. This is what a coach does, in the serious and demanding sense of the word.

---

I want to name what secondary gain actually protects, because if we are going to address it we must understand it. It is not laziness. It is not cowardice, exactly. It is the soul's very reasonable attempt to survive an environment that made survival costly. The cage formed around real wounds, real losses, real betrayals. The adaptation was intelligent. It worked, for a season, the way all our defenses work — by keeping out something that would have damaged us further.

The problem is that defenses don't know when to stand down. They don't recognize when the war is over. They go on protecting a self that no longer needs protecting in the old way — and in doing so, protect it from exactly the things that could heal it: genuine intimacy, genuine risk, genuine hope.

The man who says he wants to change and then quietly dismantles every opportunity for change is not lying, exactly. Part of him wants to change. But another part — the part that has organized itself around the cage, that has found its dignity within the walls, that has carved its name into the rafters — that part is in genuine terror of the freedom being offered. And terror has a vote.

A skilled coach does not argue with the terror. Terror cannot be argued with. It can only be accompanied — walked alongside until the person begins, gradually and at their own pace, to discover that the freedom on the other side of the wall is not the chaos they feared, but a country they were always meant to inhabit.

---

Red makes it to Zihuatanejo. The last scene of the film is of two old friends, on a beach, under a blue sky. It is not sentimental. It is not easy. It is simply true — the truth that was always there, waiting past the end of the tunnel, patient as the ocean.

Abba is like that. He is not standing at the mouth of the cage, demanding that we exit at a pace that suits him. He is standing on the other side of the wall — or perhaps, as the mystics suggest, inside the very walls themselves — carving coordinates into the rock: *Get busy living, or get busy dying.*

The familiar dark is real. The comfort of the cage is real. And the grace that calls us out of it is more real than both.

It has been waiting for us since before the cage was built.

---

The work of a skilled coach is not to pry the cage open from the outside. The cage does not yield to force — it only tightens. The work is something far more patient and far more precise: to sit alongside the imprisoned soul until it begins to notice, perhaps for the first time, that the door has been unlocked for quite some time. And then to stay — through every moment of re-incarceration, every Brooks-like retreat to the familiar dark, every quiet return to what is known — until the person finds, at last, that they want the ocean more than they want the walls.

That wanting is everything. And almost nothing can give it to a person except time, presence, and someone who has already made it through the tunnel themselves.

*Brooks was here. But he didn't have to stay.*

## Author's Notes

This piece addresses the **secondary gain / resistance to freedom** client — the one who says they want change and quietly dismantles every opening toward it. Manning's own history with alcoholism and self-sabotage gives him authority here that no other voice carries; his confessional tone disarms the very defensiveness the piece is describing.

- **"Brooks Was Here"** — the name carved in the rafter — works as a standalone pull quote or section header for content on stuck clients.
- The piece is also useful for positioning coaching not as accountability pressure but as accompaniment — the coach as the one who leaves coordinates before the client is ready to use them.
- Strongest audience: clients who intellectually want freedom but emotionally keep choosing the known.
