# Where Your Gladness Lives: On Calling, Shame, and the Pleasure of God

*In the voice of Frederick Buechner | Film: Chariots of Fire*

---

There is a moment in *Chariots of Fire* that I have never been able to forget, and I have tried, because it is the kind of moment that makes a person uncomfortable in the way that only truth can.

Eric Liddell — Scottish missionary, Olympic sprinter, a man of fierce and peculiar conviction — is trying to explain to his sister why he cannot give up running, even for the mission field, even for God. She is impatient with him. The mission field is waiting. People are dying without the gospel. What is running, compared to that?

And Liddell says: *"I believe God made me for a purpose, but he also made me fast. And when I run, I feel his pleasure."*

I have been thinking about that sentence for years. Not because it is eloquent — though it is — but because it points toward something that most of us spend our lives avoiding: the possibility that our gladness and God's purposes are not, in fact, in competition.

---

The film gives us two runners. They are both fast. They are both driven. And they could not be more different in what drives them.

Harold Abrahams runs from something. He is a Jewish man in England in 1924, navigating a world that is politely, persistently unwilling to fully receive him. He runs to silence the voice that tells him he is not enough, not welcome, not quite. *"I'll be what I am,"* he says — but what he is, is a man in flight from his own insufficiency, using his speed to put distance between himself and the verdict that haunts him.

He is brilliant. He is ferocious. And there is something in his running that is recognizable to most of us, because most of us have organized our ambitions the same way — not around what delights us, but around what will finally, definitively, silence the voice that says we are not enough.

Eric Liddell runs toward something. Or rather, toward Someone. He runs because running is, for him, a form of prayer — a way of being fully in the body God gave him, doing precisely what that body was made to do. The pleasure he speaks of is not vanity. It is participation. It is the sensation of being exactly where you are supposed to be, doing exactly what you were designed for.

The difference between these two men is not their speed. It is the engine behind the speed. And that difference, I want to suggest, is one of the most important distinctions a person can make about their own life.

---

I have spent a great deal of time thinking about what I have called, in one place or another, the intersection of gladness and hunger. The gladness I mean is not the shallow cheerfulness that comes from things going well. It is the deeper gladness — the sense of being, in some fundamental way, *yourself* — that arises when you are doing what you were made to do, regardless of whether the circumstances around you are convenient or comfortable.

Liddell has this gladness when he runs. You can see it in his face — the famous upturned face, the arms pumping wildly, the stride that is inefficient and glorious. He is not performing. He is *being*. And there is a kind of pleasure in that which the watching world can feel even when it cannot name.

Abrahams does not have it. He has something else — the electric charge of competition, the relief of winning, the temporary silencing of the inner voice. These are not nothing. But they are not gladness. They are its sophisticated imitation, and like all imitations, they require constant renewal. The silence never lasts. The voice always returns. The next race is always waiting, because no number of races is ever quite enough to settle the question that was never really a racing question.

---

I want to speak carefully here, because I know that someone reading this will object: but surely we can't just do what makes us glad. The world has needs. People are suffering. Is not our gladness a luxury?

This is precisely Liddell's sister's objection. And it is worth answering precisely.

The gladness I am describing is not self-indulgence. It is, in fact, the opposite. It is the gladness that arises when the gift you have been given meets the need it was designed to meet. Liddell's running is not escapism. It is, for him, an act of worship that is inseparable from his witness — a demonstration, in his body, that the God who made him made him *whole*, and that wholeness includes swiftness and joy and the particular pleasure of a foot striking the ground at full speed.

The man who has found the intersection of his gladness and his calling does not do less. He does more, and does it from a source that does not deplete. The man running from shame — however fast, however decorated — eventually runs dry, because shame is not a renewable resource. It consumes. It does not replenish.

---

Here is what I have observed, over many years of watching people try to find their way to a life that feels like their own: the gladness is usually not mysterious. It is usually not hidden. It is usually something the person has already felt, in moments — a particular quality of aliveness that appears when they are doing a specific thing, in a specific way, with a specific kind of attention.

What is hidden is the permission to take it seriously. The permission to believe that the gladness is a signal rather than a distraction, that the pleasure of God is not reserved for the obviously spiritual but extends to the sprinter and the cellist and the teacher and the father building something with his hands in the garage on a Saturday morning.

*When I run, I feel his pleasure.*

The extraordinary thing about this sentence is not its theology, though the theology is sound. The extraordinary thing is that Liddell says it simply, without embarrassment, as though it is the most natural thing in the world to locate the pleasure of God in the particular physical sensation of running fast. As though the body is not a distraction from the spiritual life but a participant in it. As though gladness is not suspect but is, in fact, one of the ways God speaks.

---

A good coach — and I mean a genuinely good one, not the kind that provides enthusiasm and accountability structures — is someone who has learned to listen for the gladness beneath the noise.

Most of us come into a coaching conversation talking about what we need to do, what we ought to do, what we are failing to do. We bring our Abrahams-selves: driven, competent, haunted. We know how to run. What we have lost track of — or perhaps never fully found — is what we are running *toward*, and whether the running itself is giving us life or slowly consuming it.

The coach who asks the right questions can find the gladness. It is almost always there, buried under the obligation and the performance and the long habit of not asking whether what we are doing is what we were made for. It shows up in the slight change of voice when the right subject is touched. It shows up in the energy that appears from nowhere when a particular topic arrives. It shows up in the way a person talks about the thing they love doing when they have forgotten, for a moment, that they are supposed to be talking about the thing they are failing at.

That is the gladness. And a skilled coach knows how to follow it.

The place God calls you to is the place where your deep gladness and the world's deep hunger meet. Not the place where your anxious striving and your need for validation meet. Not the place where your father's expectations and your cultural programming meet. The place where your gladness — your particular, embodied, God-given gladness — meets a need that is real and waiting.

Eric Liddell found that place on a track in Paris, in 1924, running a race he had not planned to run, in an event he had not prepared for, and winning it — and feeling, in the winning, the unmistakable pleasure of a God who is not, as it turns out, indifferent to the way a particular man runs.

Harold Abrahams won the same gold medal and sat alone in his room afterward, in silence, waiting for the feeling to arrive.

It did not.

The work of finding your gladness is not easy work. But it is the most important work there is. And it is almost impossible to do alone.

## Author's Notes

This piece addresses the **performance trap / identity-before-outcome** dimension of coaching — the Abrahams client who is driven and accomplished and quietly running on empty, versus the Liddell client who has begun to discover what running *toward* feels like.

- The Buechner gladness/hunger framework ("the place where your deep gladness and the world's deep hunger meet") is the diagnostic tool embedded in the piece — coaches can use it directly as a session question.
- The two-runner contrast maps cleanly to two recurring client types in faith-based coaching: the shame-driven achiever and the person beginning to act from identity.
- Strong publication angle for Christian audiences tired of hustle-culture and performance-based Christianity. The Liddell story is historical and credible to skeptical readers.
- *"When I run, I feel his pleasure"* — strongest pull quote in the piece; works as a standalone prompt for social content.
