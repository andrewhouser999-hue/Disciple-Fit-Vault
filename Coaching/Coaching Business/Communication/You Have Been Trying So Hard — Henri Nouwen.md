# You Have Been Trying So Hard
*In the voice of Henri Nouwen*

---

I want to speak to you about something you already know, but may have never been given words for.

You are tired.

Not the tiredness that sleep fixes. Something deeper — the tiredness of a person who has been working very hard, for a very long time, to be acceptable. To be safe. To be enough. You may not have named it that way. You may have called it ambition, or discipline, or faithfulness. But underneath those words, if you are honest, there is the hum of a question that has never fully gone quiet: *Am I loved? Am I truly, finally, unconditionally loved?*

And because that question has never been answered in the place where it lives — not in the mind, but in the body, in the bones, in the place below argument — you have kept working. Kept performing. Kept managing the impression you make on others, and on God, and most exhaustingly, on yourself.

I know this because I have lived it. And I know that what you are longing for is not more information about love. You are longing to *feel* it. To stop bracing. To come home.

I want to tell you what home looks like.

---

## The Voice That Has Changed

There is a voice inside you that has been speaking for as long as you can remember. You may have learned to live with it the way you live with background noise — so constant that you stopped noticing it as noise. But it has been shaping everything.

It tells you that you are behind. That others are disappointed in you. That if people knew — really knew — what you were like on the inside, they would withdraw. It picks up every failure and holds it to the light. It anticipates rejection before rejection has a chance to arrive, which means you are often, without realizing it, living in a future that has not happened — bracing for a wound that may never come.

This is not weakness. This is what the mind does when it has learned, early and painfully, that love is conditional. The mind is a pattern-maker, and it has made a pattern from the evidence it was given.

But here is what I want you to know: that voice is not the truest voice.

There is another voice. It has been speaking since before you were born. It does not compete with the critical voice for airtime — it simply is, underneath everything, the way a river is underneath the noise of traffic. And what it says is this: *You are my beloved. On you my favor rests.*

The person I want to describe to you is someone who has, over time, learned to hear this voice. Not because they were taught a technique — though the slow work of honest reflection, of bringing their thoughts into the light and asking whether they are true, has been part of the journey. But because they allowed themselves to be loved long enough, and consistently enough, by people and by God, that the deeper voice has become audible.

And when that voice becomes audible, everything changes.

---

## What the Relief Feels Like

Let me try to describe it.

It is a Saturday morning, and this person wakes slowly. There is no jolt of anxiety to meet them at the threshold of consciousness. This is not because their life is without difficulty — it is not. There are real things to face, real problems unsolved, real losses not yet healed. But the morning does not feel like a test they might fail. It feels like a day they are invited into.

They lie still for a moment and notice something: they are not rehearsing. They are not already three conversations ahead, managing outcomes, preparing defenses. They are simply here. The light through the curtain. The sound of the house. Their own breath.

This is new. Or rather — it is very old. It is the kind of presence that was natural before the world taught them to be afraid.

When they get up and look in the mirror, the voice that responds is not the one that catalogues deficiencies. It is something quieter, something almost amused — *there you are.* There is a kind of tenderness toward themselves that is not complacency. They know their failures honestly. They are not performing self-acceptance. But they have stopped using their failures as evidence for a verdict that was never true.

When a thought comes that says *you are not enough*, they have learned to pause. To ask: *Is this true? Where did this come from? What does it cost me to believe it?* And increasingly — not always, not perfectly, but increasingly — they can choose not to follow the thought all the way down. They can let it pass the way a cloud passes, without needing to live inside it.

This is what the shift in the interior life feels like. Not the absence of hard thoughts. The absence of the compulsion to obey them.

---

## The God Who Has a Face

Here is where I must be very direct with you.

Many of us who have grown up in the faith carry a God who is primarily a watcher. A recorder of debts. A deity whose love is real but somehow always slightly withheld — available in full only when we have performed adequately. This God makes us afraid in ways we have learned to call reverence. This God keeps us striving in ways we have learned to call devotion.

But this is not the God of the Gospels.

The God of the Gospels runs. Did you notice that? In the story of the prodigal son, the father does not wait with folded arms for the son to complete his confession. He sees him while he is still a great way off, and he runs. He throws his arms around him. He calls for the robe, the ring, the fatted calf — before a word of apology has been spoken, before any restoration has been earned.

The person I am describing has encountered this God. Not merely read about him — encountered him. Something in their inner world has shifted from surveillance to accompaniment. They no longer experience God as primarily behind them, watching for failure. They experience God as beside them, even ahead of them, already in the future they are afraid of.

When they pray, there is a directness in it. A kind of holy ease. They do not have to construct their words carefully to manage the impression they make on the divine. They can come as they are — tired, confused, angry, grateful, undone — and they trust that what they bring will be received. Not fixed immediately. Not explained. But received.

This changes the inner monologue entirely. Because when you believe, in your body and not just your mind, that you are accompanied by a God who delights in you — who *sings over you*, as the prophet says, with joy — the long project of earning your own acceptability begins to lose its urgency. You can afford, for the first time, to stop.

---

## The Body That Has Come to Rest

You may not have thought much about your body as a spiritual matter. But I want to suggest to you that the body tells the truth about what we believe.

A person who is not sure of their welcome walks through the world differently than one who is. The shoulders know. The belly knows. The jaw knows. The body holds what the mind has not yet processed, and it releases only when safety has been experienced — truly experienced, not merely argued for — enough times to be trusted.

The person I am describing has, slowly and often painfully, given their body permission to relax.

This did not happen through willpower. It happened through relationship. Through the experience of being with people who did not withdraw when the real self appeared. Through the practice of sitting with difficult emotions long enough to discover that they were survivable — that anger did not destroy, that grief did not drown, that fear, when brought into the open, lost some of its power to govern from the shadows.

They have learned something that sounds simple but costs everything: that they can feel a feeling without becoming the feeling. That they can be sad without sadness being the final word. That they can be afraid and still choose. In the language of the mind's own science, we might say that the gap between stimulus and response has widened — that there is now, in that space, something that looks remarkably like freedom.

And because the body has learned to rest, something else has returned that the defended self cannot access: wonder. The capacity to be stopped by beauty. To receive a sunset, a piece of music, a child's face, a line of Scripture, as *gift* — without immediately processing it, categorizing it, moving past it. This person can be moved. They have not armored themselves against being moved. And this, I want to tell you, is one of the signs of a life being restored to its truest shape.

---

## The Grief That Was Finally Wept

I want to say something now that may surprise you.

One of the clearest signs that a person has moved toward wholeness is that they have learned to grieve.

Not the performance of grief. Not the management of grief. Actual grief — the kind that bends you over, the kind that asks something of the body, the kind that requires you to stay present to a loss rather than converting it quickly into a lesson.

Many of us were not given permission to grieve. We were given comfort too quickly, or we were taught that grief was a failure of faith, or we simply had no one who could sit with us in it without becoming uncomfortable and moving away. So we learned to carry our losses silently. To fold them neatly and put them somewhere out of sight.

But what is folded and put away does not stay put. It shows up in the way we cannot fully receive good news, because somewhere underneath we are waiting for it to be taken. It shows up in the way we hold people at a slight distance, because we know by now what loss feels like and we have decided, below the level of consciousness, to get ahead of it.

The person who has grieved — really grieved, brought their losses before God with the kind of raw honesty we find in the Psalms, where the writer does not clean up his words before approaching the divine — this person has been lightened. Not because the loss was undone, but because it was witnessed. Because it was held. Because they discovered that God does not turn away from tears. That the man of sorrows, acquainted with grief, can be trusted with ours.

And from this place, something remarkable becomes possible: they can be fully present with another person's pain. Not anxious to fix it, not frightened by it, not rushing past it. They can sit with someone in the dark because they have sat in the dark themselves and found that they were not alone there.

---

## The Self That Came Home

Let me tell you what this person is like to be around.

They are not the most impressive person in the room. They are not performing. They do not fill silence with the sound of their own adequacy. But there is something in their presence that is difficult to name — a quality of being genuinely *there*, genuinely interested, genuinely available — that draws people toward them without quite knowing why.

It is because they are no longer divided. The gap between the self they show and the self they are has narrowed dramatically, and the energy that was spent maintaining that gap has been freed for something else. For curiosity. For generosity. For the particular kind of love that is possible only when you are no longer afraid.

Their thoughts about themselves are honest without being cruel. When they fail — and they do fail, regularly, because they are human and will remain so — the response is no longer a spiral of self-condemnation that can last for days. There is something quicker now: a naming, an owning, a turning. *I got that wrong. I want to do better. I am still loved.* Three movements, not a season.

They have learned to want things. This matters more than it sounds. The person who has lived under shame has often learned to suppress desire — because desire makes you vulnerable, because wanting something means you might not get it, because hoping is a kind of risk. But desire is the language of the alive. And this person has begun, carefully at first and then with more confidence, to say *I want. I long for. I hope.* And to trust that the God who made them with desires is not threatened by them.

---

## The Invitation

I have been describing someone you might become.

Not through trying harder. Not through the accumulation of more knowledge about healing. But through the slow, relational, embodied work of allowing yourself to be known — by a community that will not abandon you, and by a God who has already decided that you are worth running toward.

The journey is not short. There will be seasons of two steps forward and one step back. There will be mornings when the old voice is louder than the new one. There will be moments when God feels far and the silence feels like absence.

But you will not be the same person who began. Each step toward the truth of who you are — each moment of honest reflection, each grief honestly wept, each shame brought into the light and found to be survivable, each encounter with a love that did not withdraw — changes something. Not just in the mind. In the body. In the pattern. In the deep grammar of how you move through the world.

You were made for this. Not the striving — this. The rest. The presence. The freedom of a person who knows, without needing to earn it again today, that they are held.

Come home.

The Father is already running.

---

*Henri Nouwen (1932–1996) was a Dutch Catholic priest, professor, and author of more than forty books on the spiritual life, including* The Return of the Prodigal Son, Life of the Beloved, *and* The Wounded Healer.
