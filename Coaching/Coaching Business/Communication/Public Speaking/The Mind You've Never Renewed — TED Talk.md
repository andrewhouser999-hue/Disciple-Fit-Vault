# The Mind You've Never Renewed
**TED Talk — 15 Minutes**
**Format: Ministry podcast / public teaching**
**Audience: Spiritually serious, 35+, church or podcast listener**

---

## Modified Delivery Prompt

**Format:** 15 minutes (~1,950 words at TED pace with pauses)
**Voice:** Personal, revelatory, accessible. You are sharing a discovery, not preaching a sermon. The idea carries the weight — Scripture supports it, does not anchor it.
**Key shift from sermon:** No scripture reading. Verses are woven in as evidence, briefly, then released. Accessible to spiritually serious people who may not be in a church.
**Opening:** Provocative observation — not a question to the audience. Drop them into the insight immediately.
**Central metaphor:** *The layer* — return to it at least four times.
**Personal story:** Not a placeholder you work around. In TED format it is load-bearing. Place it at the center, where the God-representation concept lands. 2–3 minutes, concrete and specific.
**Close:** Vision + invitation. Coaching placeholder below.

**Delivery for TED format:**
- Move — don't stand in one place the entire talk
- No notes visible
- Shorter sentences than the sermon; more white space
- Slower at the central idea, slightly faster through the supporting material
- The God-representation moment is the slowest point in the entire talk
- Close with warmth and forward motion, not heaviness
- The invitation is an outstretched hand, never a pitch

---

## Talk

*[DELIVERY NOTE: Begin still. No greeting, no preamble. First sentence is the hook.]*

The people who struggle most to feel loved by God are often the ones who have spent the most time in church.

I know that sounds backward. But I have seen it too many times to dismiss it.

And I finally understand why.

*[PAUSE]*

I want to share one idea with you today. Just one. But it has the potential to reframe everything you thought you understood about why spiritual growth stalls — and what it actually takes to get it moving again.

Here it is.

There are two minds. And most of us have only been renewing one of them.

*[DELIVERY NOTE: State it simply. Let it sit. Do not rush to explain it.]*

---

I work with people who are stuck. Not beginners. People who have been in the faith for years — sometimes decades. People who know the Bible well. Who have sat under good preaching, sought counsel, made commitments, kept showing up. Genuinely serious people.

And almost all of them describe a version of the same experience.

They know God is loving. They cannot feel loved by Him.

They know they are forgiven. They still respond to failure like someone who is not.

They know He is near. He feels far away.

*[PAUSE]*

For a long time I thought this was a discipline problem. Pray more. Study more. Get more accountability. Go deeper in the Word. And those things matter — they are doing real work. But they were not closing the gap. Something was still missing, and I could not name what it was.

Then I started paying attention to something that neuroscience has been pointing to for decades, and that Scripture, I believe, has been pointing to all along.

The mind is not one thing.

*[DELIVERY NOTE: Slow down here. This is the pivot. Land it clearly before moving forward.]*

There is the conscious mind — the part that reads, reasons, decides, follows an argument, listens to a sermon. This is the layer we almost always address when we talk about spiritual growth. Memorize this verse. Believe this truth. Make this decision. These are all conscious-layer interventions. Good ones. Necessary ones.

And then there is a deeper layer.

Sometimes called the subconscious. Sometimes called the implicit system. What it is, is the part of you that formed before you had language. Before you could reason. Before you could choose what went into it. It formed through experience — through what it felt like to be small and dependent. Through what love and authority actually looked like in your earliest years. Through whether the world felt safe or dangerous, whether you were truly known or only conditionally accepted.

This layer runs faster than your conscious mind. It processes your environment before your reasoning wakes up. It determines your instinctive responses to failure, to intimacy, to silence, to threat. It drives more of your daily behavior — and more of your daily experience of God — than most people realize.

A brain surgeon named W. Lee Warren has written about how the mind is actually beyond the brain itself — a deeper layer of meaning and identity that the brain serves but cannot fully account for. The point is not complicated. The mind goes deeper than we have been treating it.

And here is where everything changes.

*[PAUSE]*

This deeper layer is where your God-image lives.

Not the God you can describe theologically. Not the God you would write in a statement of faith. The God you respond to instinctively.

The God you expect when you fail.
The God you expect when prayer goes quiet for a long time.
The God you expect when you feel ashamed of something you cannot seem to change.

That God was not formed by your theology. He was formed before your theology. Through experience. Through relationship. Through the accumulated weight of what love actually felt like when you were too young to have words for any of it.

And for most of us — and I am including myself here — the God in that deeper layer is not fully the Father of Jesus Christ.

He is smaller. Harder. More conditional. More distant than Scripture says He is.

And you have been trying to grow closer to God primarily at the conscious layer, while this other God — the one your instincts actually believe in, the one your nervous system responds to — has been quietly running the show.

*[DELIVERY NOTE: This is the center of the talk. The slowest moment. Do not rescue the audience from it too quickly. Let it land.]*

*[PERSONAL STORY — 2–3 minutes. A specific, concrete moment — your own or an anonymized client's — when the gap between the God of your theology and the God of your gut became undeniable. This is not optional in TED format. The talk needs a human anchor exactly here.]*

---

Now, I want to be careful, because this is where things can go in the wrong direction.

This is not an argument against the church. Sermons, Bible study, faithful counsel — these are reaching the conscious layer, and that work is real and necessary. Keep receiving it.

And for people carrying significant wounds from early in life, working with a licensed counselor or a biblically-grounded therapist is often essential. Please hear that clearly. That work is not a detour. It is often exactly what the deeper layer needs first.

What I am naming is the gap that remains — the layer that cognitive renewal alone cannot reach.

Because the deeper layer does not change through argument.

It has already heard the right things. It is not persuaded by information because it does not operate in propositions. It operates in experience. In relationship. In what it has felt to be safe, or known, or held.

It changes through encounter.

Relational. Experiential. Often slow. The truth that God is safe must not just be believed — it must be received at the level where your nervous system actually registers safety. And that is a different kind of work than anything the conscious layer alone can do.

*[DELIVERY NOTE: Pick up energy slightly. Not volume — forward motion.]*

There is a passage in Hebrews 4 that names this more precisely than I can. The writer says the Word of God is sharp enough to pierce to the division of soul and spirit — and that it discerns both the *thoughts* and the *intentions of the heart*. Two different words. Two different layers. The deliberate reasoning mind, and the deep motivational pulse underneath it.

The writer assumes both exist. He assumes the Word is sharp enough to reach both. But the question is whether we know how to position ourselves to receive it at both layers.

And when Jesus stood in Nazareth and said He had come to proclaim liberty to the captives — the Greek word for captives describes someone held prisoner, someone who cannot decide their way out — He was not describing a theological misunderstanding. He was describing an interior that is in bondage. A layer that requires liberation, not merely information.

In Matthew 15, He looked at the most theologically precise people of His day and said: *"This people honors me with their lips, but their heart is far from me."* The lips — the conscious, public, verbal layer — were performing perfectly. The heart — the interior — was somewhere else entirely.

He was not satisfied with that gap. His entire mission was oriented toward closing it.

*[PAUSE]*

This is what Covenant Identity work is built to do.

God has not spoken to us only propositionally. He has spoken covenantally — in relationship, binding Himself to us with His own person as the guarantee. And your identity before God is not simply declared to you from a distance and left for you to believe harder. It is meant to be formed — through encounter, through truth reaching the layer where distorted God-images have lived for years, and meeting them with something they may have never fully received.

A love that holds, regardless of what you bring to it.

The work is structured. It is Scripture-grounded. And it moves toward one outcome: the God of your gut beginning to match the Father of Jesus Christ.

*[DELIVERY NOTE: You are at the close. Lift — warmer, not louder. Vision now.]*

Think about what becomes possible when that happens.

Not gritting your teeth to stay in the Word — but wanting to be there. Not prayer as something you are trying not to neglect — but prayer as homecoming. Not seeking God because you know you should — but seeking Him because the God your gut believes in is finally the same God your theology describes.

Safe. Present. Permanent. For you.

This is not a new achievement to add to your list. This is what Jesus came for. The captive layer, set free. The heart of stone, given a pulse. The God of your gut, finally beginning to match the Father.

*[DELIVERY NOTE: Invitation. Outstretched hand.]*

If what you heard today opened something — if you found yourself thinking, *that is the thing I could not name* — I want to invite you into a next step.

*[GROUP COACHING PLACEHOLDER — Insert program name, format, and call to action once built.]*

The deeper layer did not form overnight. It will not transform overnight. But it does transform.

And it is worth finding out that it can.

*[END]*

## Delivery Notes (Summary)

- **Spoken word count:** ~1,950 words including personal story at 2–3 min — target 14–16 minutes at TED pace
- **The personal story is the emotional center** — if it is thin or vague, the talk does not land
- **Four returns to "the layer":** formation, God-representation, encounter, and Covenant Identity
- **Slowest moment:** the God-representation section — do not rush past it
- **The close** should feel like arrival, not summary
