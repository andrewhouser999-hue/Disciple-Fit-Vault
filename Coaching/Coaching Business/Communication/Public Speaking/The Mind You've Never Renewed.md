# The Mind You've Never Renewed
**Text: Hebrews 4:12 (ESV)**
**Format: 35-minute teaching sermon — ministry podcast**
**Audience: 35+, 10+ years in faith, church or podcast listener**

---

## MOVEMENT 1 — The Ceiling Nobody Talks About

*[DELIVERY NOTE: Slow. Conversational. You are with them, not performing for them. Let pauses breathe.]*

Let me ask you something.

How long have you been a Christian?

For some of you it's been five years. For some of you it's been thirty. And for those of you who have been walking with the Lord for a long time — there is something I want to name. Something you may have felt many times but have never quite said out loud.

You know the Word.

You've been in Bible study. You've sat under good preaching. You have people in your life who love you enough to speak truth to you. You've gone to your elders. You've sought counsel. You've repented. You've prayed. You have genuinely tried.

And there is still something in you that doesn't move.

Not everything. There has been real growth. You are not who you were. But there is a particular thing — maybe it's how you respond to failure. Maybe it's the distance you feel from God even when you're doing everything right. Maybe it's a pattern that keeps returning no matter how many times you've taken it to the cross. Maybe it's this quiet but persistent sense that the intimacy with God you've been promised — the love, the joy, the freedom — is something you know about but haven't fully found.

*[PAUSE. Let this land.]*

And if you've felt that, you've probably asked yourself: what am I doing wrong? Am I not praying enough? Is there sin I haven't dealt with? Is this just what the Christian life actually feels like — close but not quite?

I want to suggest something different today.

I want to suggest that you may not be doing anything wrong. You may simply be doing everything right — at one layer of who you are — while leaving another layer almost entirely untouched.

*[DELIVERY NOTE: Let that settle before you continue.]*

The people who have spoken into your life — your pastors, your elders, your Bible study leaders — they are not the problem. Sermons are not the problem. Scripture-based counsel is not the problem. These are genuine gifts from God and they are doing real work. I am not here to set any of that aside.

But here is what I want to propose this morning: all of those practices, as good as they are, are primarily reaching one layer of who you are. And there is another layer underneath that most of us have barely touched.

Let me explain what I mean.

The mind is not a single room.

When most of us think about the mind, we picture the part that reasons. The part that follows an argument, memorizes a verse, weighs a decision, listens to a sermon. That is a real and important part of your mind. But it is not the whole of it.

Beneath that reasoning layer, there is another layer. Neuroscientists and psychologists have been studying it for decades. It is sometimes called the subconscious. Sometimes called the implicit system. What it is is the part of you that formed earliest — before you could reason, before you had language, before you could form a theological sentence about anything. It formed through experience. Through relationship. Through what it felt like to be young and small and entirely dependent on the people around you.

And here is what matters most: this layer runs faster than your conscious mind. It processes your environment before your reasoning layer even wakes up. It determines your instinctive responses — to threat, to intimacy, to failure, to silence. It drives more of your daily behavior, and more of your daily experience of God, than most people realize.

A brain surgeon named W. Lee Warren has written about how the mind is not simply identical to the brain — there is a meta-layer, a depth of meaning, memory, and identity that exceeds what neurons alone can account for. The point is not complicated. The mind goes deeper than we have been treating it.

Now here is why this matters for you as a follower of Jesus.

Paul says in Ephesians 4 that we are to put off the old self, which belongs to our former manner of life — corrupt through deceitful desires — and be renewed in the spirit of our minds, and put on the new self, created after the likeness of God in true righteousness and holiness.

We have been reading that and applying it almost entirely to the conscious layer. To wrong beliefs we can reason our way out of. To bad habits we can decide our way past. To theological errors we can correct with better information.

But the old man is not only what you consciously believe.

The old man is the whole implicit system — the patterns of response formed before you were redeemed, and often long before you understood what redemption meant. You cannot think your way out of all of it. Because not all of it lives in the thinking layer.

And if we are going to be renewed — truly renewed, not merely informed — we need to know the other layer exists. And we need to understand how to engage it.

## MOVEMENT 2 — What Hebrews 4:12 Is Actually Telling Us

I want to take you to a passage you have almost certainly read before.

Hebrews 4:12.

*[DELIVERY NOTE: Read deliberately. Treat this as if it is new. Pause after "thoughts" and again after "intentions of the heart."]*

*"For the word of God is living and active, sharper than any two-edged sword, piercing to the division of soul and of spirit, of joints and of marrow, and discerning the thoughts and intentions of the heart."*

Read it again.

*"Living and active, sharper than any two-edged sword, piercing to the division of soul and of spirit, of joints and of marrow, and discerning the thoughts and intentions of the heart."*

*[PAUSE.]*

I want to move slowly through this because there is something here that I think we pass over too quickly.

The writer says the Word of God is living and active. The Greek word for living — *zōn* — carries the sense of something that is not static, that has agency, that is at work. And *energēs*, active, is where we get our word energy. The Word of God is not information waiting on a shelf. It is alive. It is moving. It is doing something.

Then: it is sharp enough to pierce to the division of soul and spirit.

Notice what the writer assumes. He assumes you are not a flat, single-layer person. He assumes you go deep. He assumes there are layers to be divided. And he is telling you that the Word of God reaches those layers — not just the surface, but the division between them.

Look at the physical image he uses: joints and marrow.

Think about a bone. The joint is visible — you can see it move, you can observe it. But the marrow is hidden inside, deep within the bone structure, invisible from the outside. The marrow is where blood cells are formed. It is where life is generated from the inside out. And the writer says the Word of God is sharp enough to reach the marrow. Not just the joint you can see. The interior. The hidden, life-generating place.

That is not decorative language. That is a description of a Word that goes to the deepest layer of who you are.

*[DELIVERY NOTE: Slow down here. This is the pivot of the sermon.]*

Now look carefully at the last phrase: "discerning the thoughts and intentions of the heart."

Two things are named. Not one. Two.

Thoughts — the Greek word here refers to deliberate reasoning, conscious cognition. The kind of thinking you do when you follow an argument, when you reflect on a passage, when you reason through a decision.

And then — intentions of the heart. The Greek word is *enthumēsis*. It is not the same word. It points to the deep motivational pulse beneath thought — the thing that is already driving what you do before your conscious reasoning catches up. The desire layer. The instinctive layer. The part of you that is already responding before you have decided anything.

The Word of God discerns both.

Which means both exist. Which means they are different. And which means that the transformation Paul describes in Romans 12 — "be transformed by the renewing of your mind" — is not simply the renewal of the reasoning layer. The Greek word Paul uses, *metamorphoō*, is where we get metamorphosis. Not a decision. A transformation from the inside out. And the Greek word for mind, *nous*, carries a breadth that includes the whole inner faculty of moral perception and response — not only what you think, but what you are oriented toward at the deepest level.

Full renewal means the full layer. Including the layer below.

*[PAUSE.]*

Now I want to name something that I believe is at the center of why sincere, faithful people hit a ceiling.

Buried in the deeper layer — in the subconscious, implicit layer — is your God-image.

Not the God you can describe theologically. Not the God you affirm in a statement of faith. But the God you respond to instinctively.

The God you expect when you fail.
The God you expect when you go silent in prayer and nothing seems to return.
The God you expect when you feel ashamed, or afraid, or alone.

That God was not formed by your theology. He was formed before your theology. Before you had words for any of it. He was formed through experience — through what it felt like to be small and dependent. Through what love and authority actually looked like in your earliest years. Through whether the world felt safe or dangerous, whether you were welcomed or managed, whether you were truly known or only conditionally accepted.

And for most of us — and I include myself in this — the God who lives in that deep layer is not fully the Father of Jesus Christ.

He may be smaller. Harder. More conditional. More distant than the Scripture says He is. And you have been trying to grow closer to God — through every sermon, every Bible study, every faithful practice — primarily at the conscious layer. While this other God, the one formed in the deep layer, the one your instincts actually believe in, has been running the show.

*[DELIVERY NOTE: Pause here. This is where people feel seen for the first time. Do not rush to rescue them. Give it room.]*

This is why you can know that God loves you and still not feel loved by Him.

This is why you can affirm that you are a new creation and still respond to failure like someone who is not.

This is why you can believe in the nearness of God and still experience Him as far away.

The conscious layer and the deeper layer are not automatically in sync. The practices that reach one do not automatically reach the other.

And Jesus knew this.

When He stood in the synagogue in Nazareth and unrolled the scroll of Isaiah, He read: *"The Spirit of the Lord is upon me, because he has anointed me to proclaim good news to the poor. He has sent me to proclaim liberty to the captives and recovering of sight to the blind, to set at liberty those who are oppressed."*

Liberty to the captives.

The Greek word for captives — *aichmalōtos* — refers to someone taken prisoner. A prisoner of war. Someone who is not free because something holds them. Jesus did not come primarily to inform people who were misinformed. He came to liberate something that is in bondage. And what He described being in bondage was not primarily theological error. It was the interior. The layer beneath the surface.

In Matthew 15, Jesus quotes Isaiah directly at the religious leaders of His day: *"This people honors me with their lips, but their heart is far from me."*

He is diagnosing the exact split we are talking about. The lips — the conscious, public, verbal layer — are saying the right things. All the right things. But the heart — the interior, the *enthumēsis* layer — is somewhere else entirely. Jesus names this gap. He calls it a problem. He is not satisfied with cognitive religion that leaves the interior untouched.

And in John 8, He says: *"You will know the truth, and the truth will set you free."* But then He does not stop there. He goes further. *"Everyone who practices sin is a slave to sin."* You cannot be a slave and simply decide your way out of it. A slave needs to be set free — not just told they are free. Knowing and experiencing are two different things.

The whole arc of Jesus' mission is oriented toward the interior. Not only the confession. The heart. The layer below. The part that actually runs the life.

And the writer of Hebrews tells us that the Word of God is sharp enough to get there — to the division of soul and spirit, to the marrow, to the thoughts and the intentions beneath the thoughts.

The Word reaches that layer. The question is whether we know how to position ourselves to receive it there.

## MOVEMENT 3 — A Different Kind of Renewal

*[DELIVERY NOTE: Lift here. Not volume — warmth. This is morning breaking. Still measured. But the heaviness of Movement 2 begins to resolve.]*

So where does that leave us?

Let me be clear about what I am not saying.

I am not saying your church has failed you. I am not saying that sermons, or Bible study, or the counsel of faithful elders is something you should step away from. Keep receiving those. They are doing real work.

And I am not saying therapy is not needed. For those of you who carry deeper wounds — wounds that have shaped you profoundly and early — working with a licensed counselor, or a biblical counselor who understands the intersection of psychology and faith, is often exactly what is needed. If that is you, I want to encourage you to pursue it. That work is real and it is worth the investment.

What I am saying is this: there is a layer of the mind that most of the tools we reach for in the Christian life simply do not address. Not because they are insufficient in themselves. Because they were not designed for that layer.

The deeper layer does not change through argument.

It does not change through more information, more discipline, or more willpower applied at the conscious level. It has already heard those arguments. It is not persuaded by them because it does not work in propositions.

It changes through encounter.

It changes relationally. Experientially. Often slowly. It changes when the truth that God is safe is not merely believed but experienced at the level where your nervous system actually registers safety. It changes when the gap between the God of your theology and the God of your gut is named — honestly, carefully, without shame — and met with something that can actually reach that deep.

God Himself has always been aiming at this.

In Ezekiel 36, He makes a promise that goes far beyond cognitive renewal: *"I will give you a new heart and a new spirit within you; and I will remove the heart of stone from your body and give you a heart of flesh."*

A heart of stone is not just a theological error. It is a dead interior response system. It does not feel. It does not receive. It processes life at the surface but does not open to it at depth. And God says — I will not merely inform it. I will replace it. New heart. New interior. Not just new ideas about Me — a new capacity to receive Me.

In Psalm 51, David does not say: *"You delight in truth in my correct theology."* He says: *"You delight in truth in the inward being, and you teach me wisdom in the secret heart."* The inward being. The secret heart. These are the layered places — not immediately visible, not consciously managed, not reachable by better arguments alone. This is where God wants to work. This is what He has always been after.

*[PAUSE.]*

This is what Covenant Identity work is built to do.

The foundation is this: God has not spoken to you only propositionally. He has spoken covenantally — in relationship, binding Himself to you with His own person as the guarantee. Covenant is not primarily a legal architecture. It is the form that love takes when it intends to be permanent. When it will not walk away regardless of what you bring to it.

Your identity — who you actually are before God — is formed in that covenant relationship. Not just declared to you once from a distance and left for you to believe harder. Formed. Through encounter. Through truth reaching the layer where distorted God-images have been living, and meeting them with something they have never fully received: a love that holds.

*[PERSONAL STORY PLACEHOLDER — a brief story from your own experience of hitting the ceiling and finding the deeper layer, or an anonymized client account. 3–5 minutes.]*

The work is structured and Scripture-grounded. It takes the layered inner person seriously — because Jesus took it seriously, because Hebrews takes it seriously, because the prophets took it seriously. And it moves toward one outcome: that the God of your gut would begin to match the Father of Jesus Christ.

Not perfectly. Not all at once. But genuinely. Experientially. At the layer where it actually matters — where the behavior comes from, where the responses form, where the desire to seek Him lives or does not live.

*[DELIVERY NOTE: Slow here. You are painting a picture. Let them see it before you speak the invitation.]*

Think about what becomes possible when that happens.

Not gritting your teeth to stay in the Word — but actual desire for it. Not prayer as a discipline you are trying not to neglect — but prayer as homecoming. Not seeking God because you know you ought to — but seeking Him because the God your gut believes in has finally begun to match the God your theology describes. Safe. Present. Permanent. Genuinely for you.

Not more striving. A different kind of transformation. The kind that Jesus came for. The kind that reaches the captive layer and sets it free. The kind that gives the heart of stone a pulse.

This is what the Father has been after from the beginning.

*[DELIVERY NOTE: Pause. Warm, not urgent. The invitation should feel like an outstretched hand.]*

If what you've heard today has opened something in you — if you have found yourself thinking, yes, that is the thing I could not name — I want to invite you into a next step.

*[GROUP COACHING PLACEHOLDER — Insert program name, brief description of format, and specific call to action once the medium is built. Suggested register: "I am building a small group experience structured around exactly this work — [name]. A small number of people, working through this together, with space and structure to engage the deeper layer. If that's you, I would love to have you. [Insert link or contact.]"]*

I want to be honest with you: this is not a quick fix. The deeper layer did not form overnight, and it will not transform overnight. But it does transform. People do find that the God of their gut begins to shift — that the intimacy they have been reading about becomes something they actually experience. That the desire to seek Him, to stay in the Word, to pray — begins to come from a different place inside them.

That is worth the work.

---

*[DELIVERY NOTE: Close slowly. One final image. Let it breathe.]*

The writer of Hebrews says the Word of God is living and active.

Sharp enough to reach the division of soul and spirit.

Sharp enough to reach the marrow — the hidden, interior, life-generating place — not just the joint you can see.

He wrote that because he believed the Word actually goes there.

The question before us is whether we know how to receive it there.

My hope — and the work I want to invite you into — is that you would find out that you can.
