Most of us, if we're honest, were not formed through a clear, intentional pathway of understanding God. We were exposed.

We heard sermons---some excellent.\
We joined studies---often chosen by others.\
We read Scripture---frequently starting in different places.

And over time, we accumulated truth.

But accumulation is not the same as formation.

What many Christians end up with is not a coherent understanding of God, but a collection of truths without a governing framework. And when there is no framework, something else takes its place.

That "something else" is the person's existing schema---their internal model of reality shaped by experience, attachment, authority, and identity.

So when we say, "God is loving," that truth doesn't land on neutral ground. It lands on someone who may already believe love is conditional.

When we say, "God is just," it lands on someone who may already associate authority with harshness or unpredictability.

Which means this:\
People are not just learning theology---they are interpreting it through distortion.

And if we don't account for that, we unintentionally allow people to build a relationship with a version of God that is partially true... and deeply misinterpreted.

That's why two people can sit under the same teaching for years and walk away with entirely different experiences of God---one marked by trust, the other by fear or distance.

So what's missing?

From a pedagogical standpoint, two things:

First, structured, schema-first learning\
Second, intentional engagement with the learner's internal framework

In any domain---medicine, law, engineering---experts don't train people through random exposure. They design scope and sequence. They start with first principles, because those principles organize everything that follows.

Christian formation often skips that.

We don't always start with:\
Who is God---fully, clearly, systematically?\
What is true of Him that gives meaning to everything else?

Instead, we move quickly to application, behavior, or isolated doctrines.

But without a clear, accurate understanding of God's nature, every other truth becomes unstable.

So the first part of the solution is this:

We must build learning around foundational schemas, beginning with the nature of God, and then intentionally layering truth in a way that is coherent, cumulative, and reinforcing.

But even that is not enough.

Because the second issue remains:\
People don't just lack structure---they bring distortion.

So alongside structured theology, we introduce a second track:

Ongoing reflection that helps learners recognize where their perception of God is being shaped by something other than truth.

This creates a powerful moment:

The learner begins to see the gap between who God is and how they experience Him.

And that gap is where transformation happens.

The model becomes this:

We don't just teach truth. We sequence it carefully.\
We don't just deliver content. We build frameworks.\
We don't ignore the inner life of the learner. We help them recognize distortion.

Over time:

Truth becomes integrated.\
God becomes coherent.\
Faith becomes relationally grounded.

And people begin to experience a clearer, more secure relationship with God---because they are relating to Him more as He is.

If we care about forming leaders---not just informed students, but people who know God clearly and represent Him accurately---then we cannot leave their formation to randomness.

We have to design for it.
