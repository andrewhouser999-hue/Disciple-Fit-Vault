# CIC — Evidence Case for the Model (Internal)
*Internal — does the evidence actually justify building this, and what exactly does it justify*

**What this is:** An honest evidence audit answering one question: does external research support building Covenant Identity Coaching as currently scoped? Written to pressure-test Andrew's own conviction, not to persuade a prospect, partner, or reader.
**When used:** Before committing further build time, budget, or public positioning to CIC as a distinct offering; revisit when new evidence (client outcomes, additional research) becomes available.
**Who it's for:** Andrew only. Not client-facing, not for referral partners. The marketing-adapted version of the same evidence lives in [[Coaching Demand Research — Why People Hire Coaches]] Part 3B.

---

## The Question This Document Answers

Two separate claims are easy to collapse into one, and the whole point of this document is to keep them apart:

1. **Is the problem CIC targets real and measured?** — i.e., is there good evidence that Christians broadly experience a gap between professed belief and lived transformation, of the kind CIC's mechanism is designed to close?
2. **Does CIC's specific mechanism close that gap?** — i.e., is there evidence that implicit/explicit diagnostic sequencing, memory-reconsolidation-informed Covenant Truth work, and the CIC stage model specifically produce the transformation that generic discipleship, counseling, or coaching doesn't?

The honest answer, upfront: **(1) is well-evidenced. (2) is not evidenced at all — it's a working thesis.** Everything below explains why, and what that does and doesn't justify.

---

## Part 1 — What Is Well-Evidenced

### The gap is real, named, and measured by sources with no stake in CIC existing

- **Willow Creek's REVEAL study** (2004–2007, expanded to 30 congregations): increased church activity involvement did not predict or drive long-term spiritual growth. This is an institutional insider — one of the most program-driven megachurches in America — finding this against its own model, not a critic asserting it. [Christian Century](https://www.christiancentury.org/article/2008-01/willow-creek-finds-limits-its-model)
- **Barna's worldview tracking**: only 6% of Americans (9% of self-identified Christians, per one Barna/CRC measurement) hold a biblical worldview — defined as consistency between professed belief and actual thought/behavior. Barna's own title for this line of research: "Faith Has a Limited Effect On Most People's Behavior." [Barna](https://www.barna.com/research/faith-has-a-limited-effect-on-most-peoples-behavior/)
- **Barna's pornography/wellbeing data**: 54% of practicing Christians report viewing pornography, versus 68% of non-Christians — a narrow gap on a behavior explicitly named as sin in a population professing transformed identity. Outcome measures (life purpose, goal accomplishment, self-criticism, fear of failure) track the same direction regardless of religious identity. [Barna](https://www.barna.com/trends/over-half-of-practicing-christians-admit-they-use-pornography/)

**What this justifies:** building and testing an offering aimed at this gap is a reasonable bet. The market need is not invented — it's independently measured by three unrelated research efforts with no reason to inflate the finding.

**What this does not justify:** any claim that *this particular gap responds to coaching intervention at all*, let alone to CIC's specific mechanism. REVEAL and the Barna data describe the problem's existence and scale. None of them test an intervention.

### Limits on this evidence, stated plainly
- REVEAL measured church-program involvement, not coaching, counseling, or any 1:1 intervention — it's evidence the *content-delivery model* fails, not evidence about what succeeds.
- The Barna worldview and pornography data are population-level correlational snapshots, not before/after intervention studies — they establish the gap exists, not what closes it.
- None of this evidence is specific to the demographic CIC is actually targeting (stuck-but-functional Christians willing to pay for coaching) — it's general-population or general-congregation data.

## Part 2 — What Is Not Evidenced

### CIC's mechanism efficacy has never been tested

No outcome study — internal or external — has measured whether CIC's diagnostic sequence (Affections Discovery → false identity naming → Covenant Truth → Stage 4a/4b resolution) produces different or better results than generic Christian coaching, biblical counseling, spiritual direction, or simply time and a good friendship. This is not a criticism of the model — it's the honest status of a young, un-outcome-tested practice. Treat every current claim that "CIC works" as a working thesis under active construction, not a demonstrated result.

### There is indirect mechanism-plausibility evidence — but it's borrowed, not CIC-specific

The reconsolidation research CIC's Stage 3 mechanism draws on (Ecker, Nader & LeDoux — see [[Covenant Identity — What Moves Covenant Truth from Registration to Reception]]) is empirically grounded in its own field (memory science) but has not been independently validated in a Christian-coaching application. This is the strongest available support for *why the mechanism might plausibly work* — it is not evidence that *it does* work as deployed in CIC sessions specifically. That document's own Contested Ground note says the same thing; this document doesn't add new certainty, it just makes sure the two evidence types (market-need evidence vs. mechanism-plausibility evidence) aren't quietly merged into one "the research supports CIC" claim.

---

## Part 3 — Verdict

**The market need: justified.** Build and test against it. The evidence that professed belief doesn't reliably produce lived transformation is about as solid as population-level social science research gets — three independent sources, no shared incentive to agree, converging on the same finding.

**The mechanism claim: not yet justified — testable, not tested.** CIC's causal chain (Theory of Change) is a coherent, well-reasoned theoretical synthesis of real fields (interpersonal neurobiology, memory reconsolidation, spiritual formation theology) — that's a legitimate basis for *building* the model. It is not yet a basis for *claiming* the model works better than alternatives, publicly or privately. The gap between "theoretically well-grounded" and "empirically validated" is real and currently unclosed.

**What would close it, if you want this case to get stronger before scaling:** track actual client outcomes against a defined marker (even informally — pre/post self-report on the specific false-identity/functional-trust shift CIC targets). Right now the case for CIC rests entirely on the plausibility of the theory and the reality of the problem it targets — not on any evidence that it's the thing that solves it. That's a normal place for a new practice to be. It stops being normal if the public-facing claims start outrunning it.
