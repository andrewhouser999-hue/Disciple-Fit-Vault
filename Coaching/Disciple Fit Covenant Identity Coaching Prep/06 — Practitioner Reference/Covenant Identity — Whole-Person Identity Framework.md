# Covenant Identity — Whole-Person Identity Framework
*Practitioner Reference — the anatomy of human identity: what it is, how it distorts, and what the coach is looking for in session*

*Related: [[Covenant Identity — Diagnostic Lens Transition Logic]]; [[Covenant Identity — Character Wound Diagnostic Tool]]; [[Affections Discovery Process — Affections, Trust & Priorities]]; [[Covenant Identity — Integration Rationale]]*

## Framing Note

This framework draws on four contributors — Curt Thompson, Dan Allender, Eric L. Johnson, and Gary Collins — alongside Scripture as the governing plumbline. Psychological contributions are included only where they describe mechanisms Scripture assumes but does not explain, or where they independently confirm what Scripture says about human nature. ## Part 1 — Governing Anthropological Statement

### What Identity Is

Human identity is not self-constructed. It is given, distorted, and being renewed.

> **Given → Distorted → Being Renewed**

- **Given** — Every person is created as an image-bearer (*imago Dei*). Identity is grounded in God's act of creation, not in the person's achievement, performance, or others' perception. This is the ontological foundation beneath every client's dignity and every coaching conversation. The image cannot be earned or lost — it can only be lived from truthfully or distorted.
- **Distorted** — The fall did not destroy the image but corrupted it. Every person carries a disordered identity organized around false foundations rather than the one given by God. This distortion is not incidental — it is structural, pervasive, and expressed across the whole person.
- **Being renewed** — The telos of identity formation is conformity to the image of Christ (Romans 8:29). Coaching partners with what God is already doing in the client's formation toward that telos. The coach does not construct the client's identity — the coach serves its emergence.

---

### The Anatomy of the Whole Person

Identity is not stored in one faculty — it operates across all of them simultaneously. These faculties are interdependent: distortion in one affects all others. Renewal is also whole-person.

| Faculty | What It Is | Distorted Form |
|---|---|---|
| **Intellect** | Knowing, understanding, interpreting reality | Darkened understanding; false beliefs about self, God, and world |
| **Will** | Agency, choice, directedness toward ends | Disordered willing; bondage to patterns that contradict stated desire |
| **Affections** | Loves, desires, what the heart is drawn toward and trusts | Misplaced trust; organized around substitutes for God |
| **Imagination** | Capacity to envision what is not yet present | Foreclosed or captive imagination; cannot see a different story |
| **Memory** | The self's relationship to its own story and history | Distorted narrative; shame-encoded history; fragmented or over-controlled story |
| **Body** | Embodied existence as part of the whole person | Somatic encoding of identity distortion; held in tension, withdrawal, or collapse |

## Part 2 — The Five Dimensions of Identity Function

Identity distortion does not live in one area — it operates across the whole person simultaneously. Each dimension below corresponds to the soul's faculties above. The coach tracks all five as a unified picture, not as separate presenting issues.

---

### Physical

The body participates in identity. Chronic tension, depletion, physical withdrawal, and somatic constriction are often expressions of identity distortion encoded in the body before the mind has language for them. The body communicates what is held implicitly.

*Distortion signals:* chronic fatigue from a performance-driven identity demanding unsustainable output; physical collapse or withdrawal from a shame-based identity that has retreated from life; constriction or agitation when identity is questioned or threatened.

*Health markers:* physical presence and groundedness; capacity for rest without guilt or restlessness; the body is not braced against being known.

> *Coach boundary:* observe somatic signals as identity data — do not intervene directly in somatic patterns. Refer when somatic symptoms are clinically significant.

---

### Emotional

Emotions are not noise — they are identity data. They reveal what the person functionally believes about themselves, God, and the world. A single emotional moment is less diagnostic than emotional patterns across sessions.

*Distortion signals:* flat affect (implicit identity suppressing emotional access); chronic anxiety (performance or threat-based identity); contempt — of self or others (shame being managed rather than faced); an absence of genuine joy or rest (identity organized around striving or hiding rather than being received).

*Health markers:* access to the full emotional range; congruence between what is felt and what is said; capacity to stay with difficult feeling without collapse or immediate deflection.

---

### Cognitive / Mental

Thought patterns and core beliefs are the most verbally accessible dimension. Identity distortions produce characteristic thinking patterns that operate automatically — often before conscious awareness — and filter incoming experience to confirm what is already believed.

*Distortion signals in language:*
- All-or-nothing framing — *"I always," "I never," "I can't"*
- Personalization — *"Everything that goes wrong is my fault"*
- Mind-reading — *"They think I'm ___"*
- Catastrophizing — *"If that happens, everything is over"*
- A core belief that, if surfaced, reads as: *"I am not enough," "I am too much," or "I am fundamentally alone"*

*Health markers:* cognitive flexibility; capacity to hold complexity without resolving it prematurely; thought patterns that are becoming aligned with God's stated reality about the person.

---

### Relational / Social

Identity is formed in relationship and expressed in relationship. The coaching relationship is a live relational context — how the client relates to the coach is data about how they relate in general. The false self will perform, manage, defer, or withdraw here as it does everywhere else.

*Distortion signals:* performance for the coach (managing the coach's perception rather than engaging honestly); reflexive deference without genuine agreement; withdrawal from honest disclosure when topics approach the wound; inability to receive direct feedback without collapse or deflection.

*Health markers:* capacity to be genuinely known without the session becoming a managed presentation; relational presence without performance; differentiation — the self remains stable and accessible across different relational contexts.

---

### Spiritual

The integrating dimension. Spiritual health is not one area alongside the others — it is the dimension that orders and gives meaning to all others. A client's functional experience of God (distinct from their theology of God) reveals the identity distortion's deepest layer.

*Distortion signals:* a God-image that is distant, punishing, or never satisfied — often incongruent with stated theology; prayer as transaction or performance; Scripture received as law rather than personal address; inability to rest in God's acceptance without immediately qualifying it.

*Health markers:* growing capacity to be known by God without hiding; prayer as honest exchange rather than managed presentation; Scripture received as address to a specific person; rest in identity as received, not achieved.

> *Coach diagnostic move:* Ask about the client's *experience* of God — not their theology of God. The experienced God reveals what the identity distortion has done to the person's functional picture of who God is toward them.

## Part 3 — How Identity Forms and Why Distortion Persists

### Identity Forms Through Relational Attunement

The self is not self-constructed. Identity is shaped through being seen, known, and responded to by another — beginning in earliest development and continuing across the lifespan. When relational attunement is absent, distorted, or conditional, the self forms around compensatory patterns. This is the mechanism behind the biblical reality that humans are made for knowing and being known (John 17:3; 1 Corinthians 13:12).

The practical implication: every client carries a relational history that has shaped the architecture of their identity. The wounds in that history are not incidental — they are often the primary location of distortion and, under God's hand, the primary material for formation.

---

### Distortion Persists Through Implicit Memory

Every person operates with two memory systems simultaneously:

- **Explicit memory** — consciously accessible, verbal, narrative. What the client says they believe.
- **Implicit memory** — encoded below conscious awareness in emotional responses, bodily reactions, and automatic patterns. What the client *functions from*.

A client can state a biblical truth about their identity and simultaneously function from a completely different belief — because the functional belief lives in implicit memory, not the conscious mind. This is not weakness of faith. It is how memory works.

> *Coaching implication:* Telling a client a true thing about their identity is necessary but rarely sufficient. The implicit layer requires a different approach — relational, embodied, and repeated over time. Insight does not automatically produce identity change. Formation does.

---

### Shame Is the Primary Disruptor

Shame is not primarily an emotion about behavior. It is an attack on *being* — on the self's sense of worth, visibility, and belonging. Shame's primary move is to hide the self from being known. Because it operates before conscious thought, it cannot be outrun by cognitive correction alone.

- Shame produces a shame-based identity — one organized around concealment rather than disclosure, self-protection rather than engagement
- Shame suppresses emotional access, distorts the God-image, forecloses the imagination, and fragments the story
- The antidote to shame is not correction but being *known through* it — the precise opposite of shame's hiding mechanism

> *Coaching implication:* A shame-based identity requires being known through it, not argued out of it. The coach's attunement — the quality of genuine, non-reactive presence — is not a soft skill. It is the primary mechanism by which the conditions for identity change are created.

---

### Integration vs. Fragmentation

A healthy identity is integrated — coherent across contexts, emotionally accessible, connected to one's own story, and capable of being known. A distorted identity moves toward one of two poles:

- **Rigid** — defended, over-controlled, closed. The person manages what is known about them and resists being moved.
- **Chaotic** — fragmented, inconsistent, unstable. The person has no reliable center from which to engage.

Both patterns are observable in session through voice, story, emotional access, and relational behavior. Neither is a clinical diagnosis — both are coaching-visible identity patterns.

## Part 4 — Diagnostic Layer: What to Look for in Session

### Story as the Primary Diagnostic Window

A person's identity is revealed more fully through how they tell their story than through what they say they believe. The mouth speaks from the overflow of the heart (Luke 6:45) — and the shape of the story reveals the shape of the heart.

Listen for:

| Story Element | What It Reveals |
|---|---|
| **What rises** — what the client remembers and weights as significant | What the heart has judged as defining |
| **What disappears** — what gets minimized, rushed past, or never reached | What shame has hidden or the false self has managed away |
| **What repeats** — recurring themes across different life domains | The organizing belief underneath multiple presenting issues |
| **How God appears** — the God of the client's story | The distorted God-image; functional theology vs. stated theology |
| **Who the client is in their own story** — present and agentive, or absent and acted upon | Whether the person has a self that can be known and that acts, or a self that only receives and endures |

---

### The False Self / True Self Axis

Every client is presenting from one of two identity positions — and often moves between them within a single session.

- **False self** — the identity constructed for survival around a wound. Practiced, defended, and often loud. Characterized by performance, hiding, or control. The false self is not the enemy — it was built for a reason. But it is not who the client is.
- **True self** — the person as God designed and is renewing. Quieter, less practiced. Surfaces in unguarded moments of genuine rest, unexpected courage, inexplicable generosity, or joy that the session didn't manufacture.

> *Coach diagnostic question:* *"Which self is speaking right now?"*

Naming when the true self surfaces — without over-explaining it — is one of the most formative moves available in session.

---

### Character Wound Types

Three observable false self types, each formed around a primary wound and organized by a core shame statement. These are recognizable in voice, story, relational posture, and how the client relates to the coach.

| Type | Primary Wound | Core Shame | False Self Pattern |
|---|---|---|---|
| **The Warrior** | Betrayal — someone who should have protected, didn't | *"I am weak"* | Control, aggression, refusal to yield, protecting through dominance |
| **The Hermit** | Terror — exposed to threat without protection | *"I am too much / unwanted"* | Withdrawal, invisibility, self-erasure, minimizing presence |
| **The False Noble** | Ambivalence — inconsistent or conditional care | *"I am not enough"* | Performance, impression management, achievement, managing others' perception |

> Full diagnostic content — including in-session behavioral markers, story patterns, emotional signatures, God-image distortions, and true self emergence signals for each type — is in [[Covenant Identity — Character Wound Diagnostic Tool]].

---

### Emotions as Theological Language

Emotional patterns in session are not side content — they are the most direct access point to functional identity beliefs, bypassing the false self's managed presentation.

| Emotional Pattern | Often Reveals |
|---|---|
| Contempt — of self or others | Shame being externalized or suppressed |
| Chronic despair | Functional belief that God is absent or uncaring |
| Persistent low-grade anger | Belief that a covenantal obligation — by God or others — has been failed |
| Pervasive fear or anxiety | Identity organized around threat; God not trusted as protector |
| Flat affect / emotional absence | Implicit identity suppressing emotional access; shame at the access point |
| Absence of joy or rest | Identity organized around striving, performance, or hiding |

---

### Self-Concept Diagnostic Questions

Four components of self-concept — each can be distorted, each is accessible through direct questioning.

**Self-image** — *How I see myself*
- "If you described yourself honestly — not how you want to be seen — what would you say?"
- "What words come up when you think about who you are?"

**Self-esteem** — *How I evaluate myself*
- "What determines whether you feel good about yourself?"
- "When are you hardest on yourself? What triggers that?"
- "What would have to be true for you to feel like you're enough?"

**Ideal self** — *Who I think I should be*
- "What expectations do you carry for yourself — and where did they come from?"
- "Is the person you're trying to become driven by grace or by something else?"

**Social self** — *Who I think others see me as*
- "Who are you when you think someone important is watching?"
- "Does how you act change depending on who's in the room? How?"

---

### Core Belief Surfacing

- "What would it mean about you if that happened?"
- "If that fear came true, what would that say about who you are?"
- "What do you believe is fundamentally true about yourself that you almost never say out loud?"
- "What belief would have to be false for you to respond to this differently?"

---

### The Gap Question — Primary Identity Diagnostic Move

Surfaces the distance between stated and functional identity — where the client says one thing and functions from another.

- "If what God says about you is true, how does that fit with how you actually experience yourself?"
- "You said you believe [X]. When does that belief break down for you?"
- "You said God loves you unconditionally. When does that stop feeling real?"

The gap is not a failure of faith to be corrected — it is the diagnostic location. It shows the coach exactly where the implicit layer diverges from the explicit, and where the formation work is needed.

## Part 5 — Scope Boundary

### What Coaching Addresses

- Identity patterns in normally functioning people — no active clinical disorder present
- Present and future orientation — building on and moving toward, not excavating pathology
- Awareness, reflection, and new practice — not psychological reconstruction
- The whole person as a unified subject of formation under God

### Refer When

- An active clinical disorder is present or suspected — depression, anxiety disorder, PTSD, dissociation, or any presentation requiring clinical assessment and treatment
- Sustained focus on past trauma exceeds what a coaching container can appropriately hold
- Somatic symptoms are clinically significant
- The coaching relationship is not providing sufficient containment for what is emerging
- The client needs something the coach is not trained to provide

Referral is not a coaching failure — it is a scope decision that serves the client.

## Sources and Boundary Notes

This document draws on four contributors: **Curt Thompson** (*Anatomy of the Soul*; *The Soul of Shame*), **Dan Allender** (*To Be Told*; *Redeeming Heartache* with Cathy Loerzel), **Eric L. Johnson** (*Foundations for Soul Care*), and **Gary Collins** (*Christian Coaching*).

**Thompson** builds on Dan Siegel's interpersonal neurobiology (IPNB). Siegel's observational findings regarding relational formation, memory systems, and neural integration are used here as mechanism descriptions. Siegel's framework telos — psychological integration as the goal of human flourishing — is held subordinate to Scripture's account of human flourishing as conformity to Christ and life in right relation to God.

**Collins** references Erikson's developmental stage theory and CBT schema research. The observational content of both is used: developmental history shapes identity patterns; core beliefs operate automatically below conscious awareness. Erikson's normative definition of healthy identity as autonomous self-construction and CBT's assumption that cognitive restructuring alone produces lasting change are not imported.

All psychological contributions in this document operate under Scripture as the governing plumbline. For the full integration rationale — including the theoretical justification for this approach and its distinction from classic psychological integration — see [[Covenant Identity — Integration Rationale]].
