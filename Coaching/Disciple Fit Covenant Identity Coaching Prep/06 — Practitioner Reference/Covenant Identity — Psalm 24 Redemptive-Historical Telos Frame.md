# Covenant Identity — Psalm 24 Redemptive-Historical Telos Frame

**What this is:** A practitioner reference for Psalm 24 as the telos anchor of the Psalm 22-23-24 triptych — the declaration layer, the sovereignty frame, and the horizon within which all formation work takes its meaning. Covers the three theological anchors the psalm provides, when and how to introduce it to the client, client-facing session language, and how Psalm 24 connects to the Interior Life as Telos nine qualities.

**When used:** Two registers. (1) The practitioner holds Psalm 24 as an internal orientation frame from session one onward — it is always running as background. (2) Psalm 24 content is introduced to the client in late-stage work (Phase 3–4), when the Psalm 22 lament has been inhabited, the Psalm 23 shepherd has been encountered in the darkness, and the client has enough interior stability to hold a larger frame without it functioning as bypassing.

**Who uses it:** Practitioner reference with embedded late-stage client language.

---

## The Text — Key Phrases with Practitioner Definitions

**"The earth is the LORD's, and the fullness thereof; the world, and they that dwell therein"** — That's sovereignty over everything. Not a category of events, not the areas where God has been invited. Everything. Including the darkness the client has been living in.

**"For he hath founded it upon the seas, and established it upon the floods"** — That's creation authority over chaos. The seas and floods in the ancient Near Eastern frame are forces of disorder. God subdued them at creation and governs them still. What the client experiences as overwhelming disorder has not exceeded the God who established order over chaos.

**"Who shall ascend into the hill of the LORD? or who shall stand in his holy place?"** — That's the qualification question. Not rhetorical — real. The standard is named; the question has an answer.

**"He that hath clean hands, and a pure heart; who hath not lifted up his soul unto vanity, nor sworn deceitfully"** — That's the standard the client cannot meet in their own condition. External acts (hands) and internal disposition (heart) must both be clean. This is the question the shame and condemnation wounds generate from the inside.

**"He shall receive the blessing from the LORD, and righteousness from the God of his salvation"** — That's declared standing, received as gift. The righteousness (*tzedaqah*) is given "from the God of his salvation" — not generated by the ascender. It is covenantal vindication, not achieved moral purity.

**"This is the generation of them that seek him, that seek thy face, O Jacob"** — That's the community defined by seeking, not arrived righteousness. The qualification condition is the face-seeking — not clean hands by self-effort. The client who cannot qualify by their own condition belongs in the generation of seekers.

**"Lift up your heads, O ye gates; and be ye lift up, ye everlasting doors; and the King of glory shall come in"** — That's the return, announced with certainty. Future tense spoken as settled fact. The gates are addressed twice — because the first command might seem incredible, and the second removes all doubt.

**"Who is this King of glory? The LORD strong and mighty, the LORD mighty in battle"** — That's the enemies arc resolved. The forces that have held authority in the client's story — shame, accusation, false identity, wound — are in the presence of the one who is mighty in battle. Their current authority is not their ultimate standing.

**"The LORD of hosts, he is the King of glory"** — That's the final answer to every competing claim. Not strong in some domains, not sovereign over some things. LORD of hosts — the entire assembly of heaven and earth.

## Three Theological Anchors

### Anchor 1 — "The Earth Is the LORD's": Sovereignty Over the Darkness

The most immediate practitioner use of Psalm 24 is the sovereignty frame — and it must be understood correctly or it collapses into dismissal.

The claim is not that the client's darkness was good, chosen, or required. It is that the darkness did not occur outside the LORD's ownership. Nothing in the client's story escaped his governance. The wound, the loss, the failure, the confusion — all of it was held within a sovereignty that was never interrupted.

This is the direct telos answer to the Psalm 22 experience. When the psalmist cries "my God, why hast thou forsaken me?" the implicit belief is: God has lost ground here. The darkness has exceeded him, or displaced him, or he has chosen not to show up. Psalm 24 places a frame around the entire Psalm 22 experience: the earth is the LORD's. Everything that happened in the Psalm 22 darkness happened within God's ownership. He was not absent from what he owns.

**What this is not:** It is not "God sent this for your good" or "everything happens for a reason" — both of which bypass the Psalm 22 reality. It is: the darkness was real, and nothing in it occurred outside the LORD's authority. The two things are both true simultaneously.

**For the client:** The sovereignty frame gives the suffering its correct proportion. It does not minimize it. It prevents it from having the final word. The wound is real and it does not define the shape of reality.

### Anchor 2 — "Who Shall Ascend?": The Qualification Question Answered in Christ

The qualification question in verses 3-4 is the interior question that the shame wound, the condemnation wound, and every other wound generates: *Do I have standing to ascend? Is there a version of me that qualifies to be in the presence of God?*

The question is legitimate. The standard is real. Clean hands and a pure heart are not lowered or redefined — they are the actual qualification. The gospel does not change the standard; it answers the question from a different direction.

The one who perfectly met this standard descended — into Psalm 22 ("my God, why hast thou forsaken me?" — cited in Matthew 27:46 as Christ's cry from the cross) — so that those who cannot qualify in their own condition could stand on the hill in him. The ascent of Christ at the resurrection and ascension is the background event that makes the client's ascent possible.

**For the declaration tracks:**
- The shame wound's false identity — "I am disqualified by what I am" — is answered here: the qualification is located in Christ's clean hands, not in the client's condition.
- The condemnation wound's false identity — "the record against me bars the ascent" — is answered here: the one who bore the record has ascended and is now at the right hand, interceding.

**Client-facing language (Phase 3-4 only):**
*"The question 'who shall stand in his holy place?' is the question your wound asks about you every day. Psalm 24 raises the standard — and then the gospel answers it. You stand on the hill not because you arrived clean but because the one who was clean descended to get you. That's what the robe and ring in the Prodigal Son story mean in this frame — you are given standing before you've produced it."*

### Anchor 3 — "The King of Glory Shall Come In": The Horizon That Gives the Valley Its Meaning

The antiphonal section (vv.7-10) is the most dramatically structured part of the psalm. The gates are addressed, the question is asked ("Who is this King of glory?"), the answer is given — and then the entire sequence repeats. The repetition is not redundancy; it is insistence. The coming of the King is certain enough to say twice.

For the client in Psalm 23 territory — in the valley, accompanied by the shepherd, formation in process — Psalm 24 provides the horizon. The valley is real. The formation is incomplete. The wound still has moments of authority. All of this is true. And: the King of Glory is coming. The suffering and formation work exist in the space between the cross (Psalm 22) and the return (Psalm 24). That space has a direction and an end.

**What this does for the client:**
- It gives the present suffering correct proportion without dismissing it. The valley is not the last word; the King's return is.
- It gives the formation work its ultimate framing. The client is not being formed for its own sake — they are being prepared for the King who is coming, as an ambassador of the reality he brings.
- It places the enemies in their true proportion. The forces that have exercised authority in the client's story — the wound, the shame, the accuser — are in the presence of the one who is "mighty in battle." Their authority is real and it is temporary. The defeat is already determined.

**The practitioner's background question:** *"Is this client living as though the King of Glory's return is real — as though the arc of their story is held and aimed — or are they living as though the darkness they came out of is still the defining frame?"* Psalm 24 is the frame that answers that question.

## How Psalm 24 Completes the Triptych

The client is a Psalm 23 person living between Psalm 22 and Psalm 24.

| Psalm | What It Names | The Client's Relationship to It |
|-------|--------------|--------------------------------|
| **22** | The wound, the darkness, the cross | Where the client came from; what the lament inhabited; what the shepherd entered |
| **23** | Formation in process, valley still real, shepherd present | Where the client is; the zone of active formation work |
| **24** | The return of the King; sovereignty over everything | Where the client is headed; the horizon that gives the valley its meaning |

The present moment of formation — the client's between-session practices, the declaration work, the ongoing wound management — is given its meaning by both ends:
- From Psalm 22: the lament was heard. The shepherd entered the darkness. What happened was real and was not abandoned.
- From Psalm 24: the King is coming. The darkness the client came through did not escape his ownership. The formation work is preparation for a reality that is already secured.

The client does not need to arrive at Psalm 24 to receive it. Psalm 24 orients them while they are still in Psalm 23. The declaration "the King of Glory shall come in" is received in the valley — it does not require exiting the valley to be true.

## When to Introduce — Timing and Readiness Markers

Psalm 24 content is held by the practitioner from the beginning and introduced to the client when readiness markers are present. Introducing it prematurely produces one of two failure modes.

**Failure Mode 1 — Bypassing:** The eschatological frame is used to avoid present pain rather than to locate present pain within the arc. "It will all be okay in the end" bypasses the valley rather than orienting the client within it. The client uses Psalm 24 to exit Psalm 23 work prematurely.

**Failure Mode 2 — Disqualification intensification:** "Who shall ascend into the hill of the LORD?" lands as further evidence for the shame or condemnation wound if the client does not yet have enough formation stability to hold the gospel answer. The question intensifies the wound rather than opening the declaration.

**Readiness markers — client is ready for Psalm 24 introduction when:**
- The Psalm 22 lament has been genuinely inhabited (the false covenant object named, the cry received as legitimate, the lament held as covenant address)
- The shepherd has been encountered inside the darkness — not just described as present, but received as present; the He→You shift has occurred (see Triptych Practitioner Orientation Frame)
- The client can hold present suffering and present provision simultaneously without collapsing one into the other
- Declaration work in Stage 3 is beginning to hold — covenant truth is abiding, not just being received
- Phase 3–4 is active

**Readiness markers — not yet:**
- Client is in acute desolation; the darkness is the total frame
- God-representation work has not yet surfaced and addressed the absent/distant or punitive image — Psalm 24's sovereignty declaration will land as distant omnipotence, not as personal holding
- The client's engagement with covenant truth is still primarily cognitive (they know it, they can recite it, they cannot feel it) — Psalm 24 material will add to the cognitive layer without reaching depth

## Session Language for Introducing Psalm 24

**Opening move (Phase 3-4, when readiness markers are present):**

> "I want to ask you something about the arc of your story — not where you are right now, but where it's going. Do you have a sense of what you're being formed toward?"

*(Listen. What the client names reveals their current telos orientation — whether it is primarily relief from the wound, or something larger.)*

**Introducing the sovereignty frame:**

> "There's a psalm I want to hold with you for a moment — not to read it as a Bible study, but to ask what it does to you. Psalm 24 opens with 'the earth is the LORD's, and the fullness thereof.' Everything. Not some things. Not the good things. Everything — including the darkness you've been through. When you hear that, is there a part of you that resists it? Or does something settle?"

*(The client's response to "everything" — including the darkness — surfaces the functional God-image and the degree to which sovereignty has been received or resisted.)*

**The qualification question:**

> "The psalm asks: 'Who shall ascend into the hill of the LORD?' — it's asking who has standing. What do you hear in that question for you personally? Not theologically — what does your interior say when you hear it?"

*(This surfaces the shame or condemnation wound's answer to the question. It is diagnostic before it is declaration.)*

**After the wound's answer is named — the gospel answer:**

> "Here's what I want to set next to that. The one who perfectly met that standard came down into your Psalm 22 — into what you described earlier — so that you could stand on the hill in him. You don't stand there because you arrived clean. You stand there in the one who was clean and who descended to get you. What does that do to what you just said about yourself?"

**The return:**

> "The psalm ends with the King of Glory coming in — and it says it twice, like it needs to be said again because the first time might seem too incredible. That coming is certain. Your valley, your formation work, the gap between who you are now and who you're becoming — all of it exists between the cross and that return. You are a Psalm 23 person living between Psalm 22 and Psalm 24. Does that frame feel like it fits your story?"

## Client-Facing Question Bank (Phase 3–4)

**Sovereignty questions:**
- "When you think about the hardest thing in your story — the thing you came through before this — does 'the earth is the LORD's' include that? Or does some part of you locate that outside his ownership?"
- "What would it mean for the darkness you came through to have occurred within God's governance rather than beyond it?"

**Ascent questions:**
- "What does your interior say when you hear 'who shall ascend into the hill of the LORD?' Is there a voice that answers before you can think theologically?"
- "If your standing on the hill is located in Christ's clean hands rather than your own condition — what does that do to the charge you've been living under?"

**Return questions:**
- "What does it mean that the King of Glory is coming — not as a theological statement, but as a statement about your story specifically?"
- "Your formation work is happening in the space between Psalm 22 and Psalm 24. What does it mean that the story you're in has that arc — that it ends with the King coming in?"

**Enemies arc:**
- "The things that have had authority in your story — the wound, the accusation, the fear — how do they look in the presence of the one who is 'mighty in battle'?"
- "Their authority has been real. Is it the last word?"

## Connection to Interior Life as Telos (Nine Qualities)

The Interior Life as Telos document (`Covenant Identity — The Interior Life as Telos.md`) identifies nine qualities of the sanctified inner life as the coaching telos: Settled, Undivided, Free, Moved Without Being Swept, Unhurried, Loved and Knowing It, Truthful Without Cruelty, Purposeful Without Striving, Available.

These nine qualities are the *experiential* content of what the Psalm 24 telos looks like from the inside — what it feels like to be formed toward the King's return. Psalm 24 provides the redemptive-historical arc and the sovereignty frame; the Interior Life as Telos document provides the interior texture of what formation toward that arc produces.

**The VIM integration:**
- **Vision** = Psalm 24 (King of Glory returning; the arc of all things) + Interior Life as Telos (what the client is being formed into experientially)
- These are two descriptions of the same telos from different angles: Psalm 24 from outside (the redemptive-historical arc), Interior Life as Telos from inside (the nine qualities of a formed interior life)

When Vision work in Phase 4 asks "what are you being formed toward?" — both frames should be available. The client who can hold only one may have an incomplete Vision. Psalm 24 without interior texture is eschatological abstraction. Interior Life as Telos without Psalm 24 is spiritual self-improvement. Together they describe what the formation is for.

## Watch Points

**The practitioner holds Psalm 24; the client walks toward it.** Do not make Psalm 24 the client's orientation frame prematurely. It is the practitioner's background orientation from session one. The client receives it when they are ready — and only then.

**Sovereignty is not explanation.** "The earth is the LORD's" does not answer the question "why did this happen?" It answers the question "was this outside God's reach?" Those are different questions. Do not allow the sovereignty frame to be used as an explanation of the wound or a justification of the suffering. It is a frame, not an answer to the theodicy question.

**"Who shall ascend?" requires the gospel answer immediately.** Never leave the qualification question in the room without the answer. The practitioner may ask the diagnostic version of it ("what does your interior say when you hear it?") to surface the wound's response — but always bring the gospel answer into the same session. Do not let the psalm raise the standard and leave the client holding the disqualification without the declaration.

**The King's return is orientation, not escape.** Psalm 24 is not an exit from present formation work. The client is Psalm 23 people who are oriented by Psalm 24, not Psalm 24 people who are waiting to skip Psalm 23. Watch for the client using eschatological language to bypass present valley work.

**"Mighty in battle" is the enemies arc — do not deploy too early.** The image of God as warrior who defeats the enemies is powerful for late-stage clients and potentially destabilizing for clients still in Psalm 22 territory, where the enemies appear to be winning. This language belongs to Phase 3-4 clients who have already experienced the table set in the presence of enemies (Psalm 23) and can now hold the final proportion.
