# Covenant Identity — Stage 4b Implicit Level Practitioner Reference

**What this is:** A practitioner reference for Stage 4b of the Diagnostic Lens Transition Logic — the Relational/Attentive Response stage. It equips the coach to work at the implicit level when explicit tools have stalled and the barrier is relational or somatic rather than cognitive. This document is for the coach's eyes only; no client-facing materials are derived from it.

**How to use it:** Read in conjunction with the Diagnostic Lens Transition Logic. Return to this document when a client demonstrates the Stage 4b entry profile — covenant truth has been received intellectually but cannot land at the level it needs to. Do not use the skill taxonomy as a checklist in session; internalize it through reflective practice and peer consultation outside session.

---

## I. Stage 4b — Entry Profile and Distinction from Stage 4a

The Diagnostic Lens Transition Logic names two distinct failure modes when covenant truth is blocked:

- **Stage 4a:** Cognitive distortion is the primary barrier. The client's thought patterns actively reframe or reject truth before it can be received. CBT tools are the appropriate response.
- **Stage 4b:** Implicit memory is the primary barrier. The client can articulate the covenant truth clearly — may even say it sounds right — but the truth cannot reach the level at which it needs to land. No amount of reframing moves it. The barrier is relational and somatic, not cognitive.

### Entry Signals for Stage 4b

- Client articulates covenant truth clearly but reports no change in felt experience ("I know it in my head but not my heart")
- Reframing, truth-application, and cognitive tools have been applied and the client remains stuck at the same place
- The block has a physical or relational quality — a heaviness, a numbness, a sense of distance — not a thought pattern to be corrected
- Client repeatedly returns to the same stuck place despite intellectual clarity and genuine desire to move
- "I know this but..." does not resolve with more information or more truth
- Covenant truth triggers a subtle pull-away, distancing, or shutdown — not argument or reframing

### Distinction from Stage 4a

At Stage 4a, the client *resists* the truth — there are arguments, reframes, minimizing language. At Stage 4b, the client *cannot receive* the truth despite agreeing with it. The block is pre-cognitive: it operates at the level of procedural relational memory, not conscious thought.

## II. What the Implicit Level Is

Implicit relational knowing (Lyons-Ruth, 1998; Stern et al., 1998) is procedural, non-declarative knowledge of how to be in relationship with another person. It is not stored in narrative memory. It operates below conscious processing. It is laid down through repeated relational experience — primarily early experience — and shapes how a person moves through relationships before they think about it.

This is where attachment patterns live. A client whose early relational experience taught them that being truly seen leads to being used, that expressing need leads to abandonment, or that trust precedes betrayal — will carry that knowledge in their body and relational reflexes, not in their thoughts. They may consciously reject it. It will still shape their experience.

**Why explicit tools cannot reach it:** Explicit tools — reframing, truth declaration, Lie Eliminator work — operate in declarative memory. They can produce clarity, even genuine conviction. But implicit relational knowing is reorganized only through *new relational experience*, not new information. The client needs to experience something different *with a real person* — not just understand something different about God or themselves.

This is not a failure of the client or the tools. It is a different category of barrier requiring a different category of response.

### Why Relational Experience Produces Structural Change — The Reconsolidation Mechanism

The claim that implicit relational knowing requires new relational experience — not new information — to reorganize is clinically grounded. The mechanism by which that reorganization occurs is memory reconsolidation (Nader & LeDoux, 2000; Ecker, Hulley & Hulley, 2012).

When an implicit belief is reactivated — emotionally live, not merely intellectually present — the synaptic encoding briefly destabilizes (the labile state). During this window, new experience introduced into the labile encoding can be incorporated into the re-stabilized trace. The original belief is not suppressed; it is replaced at the synaptic level.

Three conditions are required:

**1. Activation:** The implicit belief must be emotionally live in the session. Cognitive acknowledgment does not open the labile window.

**2. Prediction error:** The disconfirming experience must be genuinely surprising to the implicit belief — not logically contradictory but somatically unexpected. The implicit level registers: *this is not what I was wired to expect.* Generic empathic responses ("I hear you," "that makes sense") do not register as prediction error. Authentic accompanying presence when the client expected aloneness or judgment *does.*

**3. Juxtaposition:** The disconfirming relational experience must be held simultaneously with the activated belief — both present, both live, in the same moment. This is what a moment of meeting is at the neurological level: the implicit belief (*I am alone*) is active, and genuine accompaniment is simultaneously present. The implicit level registers the mismatch. The encoding rewrites.

**The failure mode this explains:** When Covenant Truth is introduced while the client is calm, reflective, and cognitively organized — the truth is received as a new proposition. The implicit belief was not active; the labile window was not open. The declaration adds to explicit memory. The implicit belief continues unchanged. "I know it in my head but not my heart" is the client accurately describing this outcome.

**The Stage 4b implication:** Stage 4b is not about delivering truth more effectively. It is about creating the relational conditions in which the implicit belief activates — the client feels the aloneness, shame, or fear, in the room, with the coach — and in which the coach's genuine non-abandoning presence is simultaneously there as a disconfirmation. When those conditions are present, the moment of meeting is both a relational event and a reconsolidation event. The implicit encoding rewrites.

## III. Theological Frame

### Spirit as Primary Agent — Still Holds

Nothing about Stage 4b transfers agency to the coach. The five-layer mechanism holds: the coaching relationship creates formation context (Layer 2), but the Spirit is the primary agent of transformation (Layer 1). The coach does not engineer implicit-level change. The coach creates the relational conditions in which the Spirit's work at this level is not blocked.

This maps onto Willard's VIM framework: the Stage 4b skill set is entirely in the *Means* layer. The coach's presence, attunement, and willingness to stay are conditions — not causes — of transformation.

### Moments of Meeting as Spirit-Available Context

*Strong inference, not established fact.* The Boston Change Process Study Group describes "moments of meeting" — genuine interpersonal contact between two subjectivities — as the mechanism of implicit relational reorganization. These moments cannot be scripted; they emerge from authentic presence.

If the Spirit works through genuine interpersonal encounter — the incarnational principle; "where two or three are gathered" (Mt 18:20 ESV); the "participation in the Spirit" Paul names in Phil 2:1 (ESV) — then moments of meeting are not merely psychological events. They are Spirit-available relational contexts: moments in which genuine human contact creates conditions for the Spirit to do what no tool can do.

This does not mean every now moment is a divine appointment. It means the category of genuine interpersonal meeting is theologically coherent as a site of Spirit-mediated formation. The coach's job is not to manufacture spiritual moments, but to not block the ones the Spirit is making available.

### Transformance — The Client Is Already Moving Toward Healing

Most therapeutic and formation models treat resistance as the primary force to work against. Fosha's inversion is decisive: the primary motivation is not resistance but **transformance** — the deep organismic drive toward healing, growth, and flourishing. Resistance is secondary — it is learned, fear-based, the result of past relational harm. It is not the default.

The client is always on the side of their own healing, even when they appear to resist. The question is not "how do I overcome this person's resistance?" but "where is the drive toward healing already moving in this person, and what is blocking it?" Find the glimmer — the small moment of genuine aliveness, authentic longing, warmth. Follow that. The momentum is already there.

*Theological mapping:* The Spirit is always already working toward formation. The Spirit moves in the direction of the imago Dei the client was made for, toward the covenant identity they already carry. The false self's resistance is fear-based and learned; the Spirit's momentum and the client's deepest longing are aligned. The coach's job is not to initiate transformation but to find where the Spirit is already moving and remove what blocks it.

*The Stage 4b implication:* When a client cannot receive covenant truth, the practitioner's frame is not "what is this person resisting?" as if the client is the obstacle. It is "where is the Spirit already moving in this person?" Find the transformance and stay close to it.

### The Coach's Own Formation — Clinical Necessity

Both Schore and Fosha make the same point from different directions: the practitioner's unworked implicit patterns become liabilities at this level. What has not been worked in the coach will shape what the coach can hold for the client.

A coach who has never sat with their own fear of abandonment will rescue a client from the edge of that fear. A coach whose own implicit relational knowing says "being truly seen is dangerous" will signal unavailability even when present. A coach who has not experienced genuine interpersonal safety at the implicit level cannot create it.

This is the clinical argument — not merely the virtue argument — for the coach doing the same formation work asked of the client. The *Secured in Father* encounter preparation, the Character Wound work, the Lie Eliminator — these are not optional self-improvement for the coach. They are formation of the coach's capacity to hold this level of work.

## IV. Skill Taxonomy

These four categories organize what the coach is doing at Stage 4b. They are not sequential steps. They operate simultaneously as the session unfolds.

> **Critical note on usage:** Do not treat this taxonomy as a checklist. Authentic presence cannot be performed. A coach who deploys "extend silence" as a technique has already broken the thing the silence was supposed to hold. These skills must be internalized through the coach's own formation and reflective practice — not applied as protocol. The goal is to have *become* someone who does these things naturally, not to remember to do them in session.

---

### Category 1 — Tracking: What to Attend To

The coach's primary attention at Stage 4b is on *where the client is right now* — not on content, not on what to say next, not on which tool to reach for.

| Skill | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| **Moment-to-moment state tracking** | Continuously reading which state the client is in (see State Model below); noticing the moment something shifts — not just at end of session |
| **Reading nonverbal channels as primary signal** | Prosody (tone, rhythm, pace, volume — especially *changes* in these), face, posture, breath, gesture. These channels carry more relational information than verbal content. |
| **Monitoring own somatic state** | Continuously. What are you feeling right now, in your body? That is diagnostic data about what the client is transmitting (Schore: countertransference as neurobiological signal — not noise to suppress, but data to read). |
| **Recognizing transformation markers** | The glimmer (a fleeting moment of authentic aliveness); physical stilling; tears that shift quality (from managed to authentic); spontaneous memory access; awe or wonder arising without prompting; a sense of rightness or clarity emerging unprompted |
| **Tracking for now moments** | When the normal coaching frame breaks — something unexpected and emotionally charged is live, and neither party knows what's supposed to happen next. These cannot be scripted. |
| **Noticing enactments** | The client's implicit relational pattern is being enacted *with you* right now. Have you been recruited into it? Common forms: being recruited into moving away, into rescuing, or into staying safely unavailable. |

---

### Category 2 — Being: Presence Skills

What the coach *is* at Stage 4b matters more than what the coach *does*. These are not techniques.

| Skill | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| **Remaining regulated while the client dysregulates** | Not performed calm — actual nervous system availability. Staying "online" while the client activates. Your regulation actively co-regulates the client (Schore). If you dysregulate, the co-regulation breaks. |
| **Bifocal awareness** | Two channels simultaneously: content (what the client is saying) and process (what state they are in, what is shifting, what is happening between you). |
| **Therapeutic reverie** | Hovering attention — not gripping any particular content, interpretation, or next move. Open and receptive. This is the state from which implicit perception is possible. It is entered by releasing the grip on agenda, not by trying harder to be present. |
| **Tolerating not knowing** | Sitting at a now moment without reaching for technique. The instinct is to do something. The skill is to stay. |
| **Undoing aloneness** | The client's implicit relational knowing may say: this much pain means being abandoned, or this much exposure means being used. Your job is not to fix the pain but to not leave. Accompanying into — not observing from distance. |

---

### Category 3 — Responding: What to Do

These are active moves — but they are light moves. Stage 4b response is characterized by restraint, not activity.

| Skill | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| **Slow down / extend silence** | Let something land. The implicit level processes slower than cognition. Silences longer than feel comfortable. Do not fill. |
| **Follow the affect, not the content** | When affect appears, stay there. "Stay with that" — not "so what does that mean?" The content will be accessible later; the affect may not return if you leave it now. |
| **Invite felt-sense attention** | When the client is in contact with the stuck place but cannot articulate it — redirect from content to the pre-verbal: *"As you sit with that — not what you think about it, what do you notice in your body right now?"* Then wait. The felt sense forms slowly; hold space against the pull to fill it with another question or reframe. Distinct from somatic intervention — this is an invitation to attend, not a directive to process body sensation. If something surfaces (a word, a quality, a physical location), stay with it the same way you would stay with affect: "Stay with that." |
| **Name the visible without interpreting** | "Something just shifted." "I notice you went quiet." "Something is happening right now." Observation — not analysis. Name what you can see without telling the client what it means. |
| **Authentic response at now moments** | Personal, specific, fitting — not scripted. What does *this* person, in *this* moment, with *this* history need from *you*? The authentic move cannot be planned. |
| **Meta-processing the relational experience** | Name and briefly stay with the experience of being in the coaching relationship. "What's it like to feel that here, with me right now?" "What happened for you just then?" This is the move most directly within coaching scope at Stage 4b. |
| **Allow transformation markers to land** | When you see a transformation marker — the glimmer, the still, the shift — do not immediately move to meaning. Witness. Do not rush past it. Let it be what it is before naming or exploring. |
| **Metatherapeutic processing** | After a State 3→4 transition — after a transformation marker has landed and been witnessed — ask: *"What is it like to have just gone through that?"* or *"What do you notice now?"* This is not a check-in; it is making the transformation explicitly conscious so the new implicit knowing links to narrative and identity and consolidates. Without this step, the shift may remain implicit and dissipate. Stay with the answer; the response often deepens State 3 or 4 rather than moving away from it. Distinct from meta-processing the relational experience (which names the coach-client dynamic) — this names the transformation itself. |

---

### Category 4 — Avoiding: What Not to Do

These are the moves that break implicit-level processing. Most of them feel like good coaching in the moment.

| Avoidance | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **Rescuing the client from affect** | Filling the silence with a question, reframe, or reassurance teaches the client: this is too much to stay with. You confirm their implicit expectation that affect equals abandonment or overwhelm. |
| **Shifting to content when affect appears** | The most common response and the most reliable way to break implicit processing. When affect emerges and the coach reaches for a content question, the window closes. |
| **Premature interpretation** | Telling the client what their experience means before the experience has completed. Switches the client into left-brain processing and exits implicit contact. |
| **Generic empathic responses** | "I hear you." "That makes sense." These fail the specificity requirement of a moment of meeting. The client does not feel *met* — they feel processed. |
| **Rushing to repair rupture** | When something tears in the relational fabric, the instinct is to fix it fast. If repair happens before the rupture has been felt and metabolized, the repair erases rather than transforms. Stay in it longer than feels comfortable. |
| **Over-explaining what just happened** | Naming and analyzing a moment of meeting kills it. The implicit level processes experience; it does not process explanations of experience. |
| **Retreating to technique at now moments** | Reaching for a tool when a now moment arrives announces: I am not actually available to be here with you. The client's implicit relational knowing registers this accurately. |

## V. The AEDP State Model — Tracking Tool

Use this as a tracking instrument, not a protocol. You are reading which state the client is in, not moving them through states. The client moves (or doesn't) on the Spirit's timing.

| State | What You're Seeing | What to Do |
|---|---|---|
| **Defensive processing** | Controlled presentation; sophisticated verbal content with flat or absent affect; subtle distancing; rapid topic shifts; intellectualization | Build safety; affirm what is positive and alive; gently note what is absent without naming it as a problem |
| **Core affective experience** | Shift in voice quality; facial softening or vulnerability; tears that change character; breath shifts; somatic activation; the client goes somewhere they weren't before | Slow down. Stay with. Do not interpret. Follow the affect. Say very little. |
| **Adaptive action tendencies (State 3)** | The emotion processes to completion. Specific markers: spontaneous self-compassion arising without prompting; a felt sense of rightness or relief; warmth where there was constriction; tears that shift from managed to authentic and releasing; quiet settling in the body; what was effortful becomes easy. The client is no longer fighting — something in them has gone with the truth. | Stay present. Allow the completion. Witness without intervening. Do not redirect to content. |
| **Transformational affects (State 4)** | The glimmer: a fleeting moment of authentic aliveness or vitality. Awe; wonder; gratitude; stilling; a sense of something having shifted below thought; tremulous quality to presence; the client may look lighter, more open, more themselves; sometimes spontaneous theological language or Scripture arising uninvited; sometimes surprise at what they feel. | Name carefully if at all. Do not rush to meaning. Stay in it. This is the moment for metatherapeutic processing (see Category 3). |

## VI. Scope Boundary

This section is load-bearing. Stage 4b requires precise knowledge of what is within and outside coaching scope.

### Within Scope

- Tracking (observational — requires no clinical license)
- Presence and regulated attunement (relational — supported by the coach's own formation)
- Extending silence rather than filling it
- Not rescuing the client from difficult affect
- Naming what you observe without interpreting ("Something just shifted")
- Meta-processing the coaching relationship ("What's it like to be here right now?")
- Recognizing now moments and responding authentically
- Recognizing transformation markers and staying with them

### At Scope Boundary — Proceed Carefully

- **Somatic naming:** naming what you observe in the client's body ("I notice your shoulders dropped just now") — observation only, not intervention. Do not direct the client to attend to body sensations as a therapeutic technique.
- **Naming the relational dynamic:** "I notice something between us just shifted" — observation only, not clinical interpretation. Name the observation; do not analyze the pattern.

### Out of Scope

- Directly processing trauma
- Working with body sensations as a primary therapeutic intervention (directing the client to "stay with the sensation and notice what happens")
- Processing early childhood relational trauma
- Attempting to reorganize implicit relational patterns through sustained relational work across many sessions — this is the work of attachment-informed psychotherapy, not coaching
- Anything requiring clinical licensure

## VII. Referral Signals

At Stage 4b, implicit material that has been blocked may become accessible. This is a formation opportunity — and a referral trigger. Know the difference.

**Refer when:**

1. **The implicit material is trauma-rooted and presents with trauma symptoms:** dissociation during session (glazed affect, apparent absence, fragmented speech), flashback-adjacent experiences, somatic activation that escalates rather than resolves, hypervigilance or shutdown that does not ease with your presence
2. **The client's history includes relational trauma of clinical complexity:** early childhood abuse, neglect, or abandonment; complex developmental trauma; attachment disorganization — particularly when these are presenting as the primary barrier rather than background context
3. **The affect is not moving and is escalating:** if a client is deeply activated, is not co-regulating with your presence, and is escalating rather than stabilizing over multiple sessions — this exceeds coaching scope
4. **Enactments are severe or persistent:** if the client's relational patterns are generating significant rupture in the coaching relationship that cannot be resolved through honest meta-processing — this may indicate clinical complexity requiring therapeutic intervention
5. **The client's functioning is impaired:** if the implicit-level material is surfacing significant functional impairment (sleep, relationships, work, basic self-care) that has not been addressed in intake — clinical referral is appropriate before continuing coaching

**Referral posture:** Referral at Stage 4b is not failure — it is diagnostic precision. The coach who knows when to refer is more effective, not less. Identify 2–3 trusted clinical referral relationships before working at this level.

## VIII. Failure Mode Guards

### Legalism

**Risk:** Turning the skill taxonomy into a checklist produces exactly what it is designed to prevent. A coach who is tracking through categories and checking boxes cannot also be genuinely present. Authentic presence cannot be performed.

**Guard:** These skills are formed, not applied. This document equips understanding; formation through peer consultation, reflective practice, and the coach's own ongoing formation work builds capacity. Use this taxonomy for reflection *after* sessions — not as a guide *during* sessions.

### Insight Without Formation

**Risk:** Reading this document and understanding the implicit level is not the same as being able to work at the implicit level.

**Guard:** Explicit development pathway required. Peer consultation with a trained supervisor or experienced coach-practitioner. Reflective review of session recordings or notes. The coach's own ongoing formation through *Secured in Father*, Lie Eliminator, and Character Wound work — not as background activity but as active developmental practice.

### Tool Dependence

**Risk:** The AEDP state model, once known, can become a protocol the coach tries to move clients through rather than a tracking instrument for reading what is already happening.

**Guard:** The state model is descriptive, not prescriptive. The client moves through states (or doesn't) on the Spirit's timing. The coach reads, follows, and holds — does not direct.
