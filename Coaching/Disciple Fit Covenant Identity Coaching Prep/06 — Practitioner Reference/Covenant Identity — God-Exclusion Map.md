# Covenant Identity — God-Exclusion Map

**What this is:** Practitioner reference and in-session execution guide for Layer 4 of the Affections Discovery Process. The God-Exclusion Map organizes the affections and trust data surfaced in Layers 1–3 into a domain-by-domain picture of where God has been functionally excluded from the client's actual reliance — replaced by other sources of security, worth, comfort, control, belonging, purpose, identity, and hope. Used to name functional exclusion concretely before transitioning to Layer 5 (Identity Revelation) and Stage 2 of the Diagnostic Lens (False Identity Named).

**Who it's for:** Coach (practitioner reference, in-session navigation). Not client-facing as written.

**Read after:** Affections Discovery — In-Session Protocol (which frames all five layers); Diagnostic Lens Transition Logic (which positions Layer 4 in the full diagnostic sequence).

---

## The Concept: Functional Exclusion vs. General Idolatry

The God-Exclusion Map is not a general idolatry audit. The distinction matters:

- **General idolatry** asks: what do I want too much?
- **Functional exclusion** asks: in which domains of life is God simply not in the operating picture — not as competitor to another desire, but as absentee? God has not been consciously rejected; He has been written out of the functional account entirely, leaving another source to carry the weight He alone can carry.

Jeremiah 2:13 is the governing image: *"My people have committed two evils: they have forsaken me, the fountain of living waters, and hewed out cisterns for themselves, broken cisterns that can hold no water."* The person has not chosen a rival god through deliberate worship. They have constructed functional systems — cisterns — that attempt to do what only the fountain can do. The cisterns leak. The anxiety, shame, anger, and collapse that bring a client to coaching are often the cisterns failing under the weight they were never designed to carry.

Isaiah 31:1 gives the domain-specific form of the same dynamic: *"Woe to those who go down to Egypt for help and rely on horses, who trust in chariots because they are many... but do not look to the Holy One of Israel or consult the LORD."* Egypt is not worshipped. Egypt is pragmatically relied upon. God is not rejected — He is simply not consulted for this particular domain. That is functional exclusion: domain-specific, pragmatic, and often invisible to the person because it feels like wisdom, not unfaithfulness.

**Coaching implication:** The client is not usually a deliberate idolater. They are a functional one. The God-Exclusion Map surfaces the domains where God is simply absent from the operational account — not opposed, just out of the picture. This distinction is pastorally important: it reduces shame and increases diagnostic precision. The coach is not exposing a deliberate offense; they are helping the person see where the cisterns are.

## Position in the Affections Discovery Sequence

| Layer                              | Function                                                                   |
| ---------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Layer 1 — Surface Inquiry          | What is presenting? What brought the client here?                          |
| Layer 2 — Pattern Surfacing        | What recurring emotional, relational, or behavioral patterns appear?       |
| Layer 3 — Dependency Probe / Trust | What does the client lean on? What do they fear losing?                    |
| **Layer 4 — God-Exclusion Map**    | **In which domains is God functionally absent from the reliance picture?** |
| Layer 5 — Identity Revelation      | What does the exclusion pattern reveal about the false identity structure? |

By Layer 4, the coach has:
- A presenting picture (Layer 1)
- Recurring patterns and emotional signatures (Layer 2)
- The functional sources the client leans on and fears losing (Layer 3)

Layer 4 organizes this data into domains and names the exclusion explicitly. It converts the affections picture into a structured map the coach holds and eventually names back to the client. The map is a coach-held framework during the session — not a worksheet the client fills out.

## The Eight Domains

These domains represent the primary arenas where God tends to be functionally excluded. Each has a description of what the domain is designed to carry (what only God can provide), and the most common functional substitutes.

### 1. Security
**What it carries:** The ground of safety — the assurance that I and those I love are protected from harm, loss, and catastrophe.
**Common functional sources:** Financial reserves and management, physical health control, relational stability, planning and preparation, risk avoidance, information-gathering.
**Exclusion looks like:** Anxiety spikes when financial, health, or relational margins shrink. Safety is functionally located in the margin, not in God. The margin is not a tool; it has become the ground.

### 2. Significance / Worth
**What it carries:** The settled sense that I matter — that my existence has weight, value, and purpose regardless of performance.
**Common functional sources:** Achievement, productivity, expertise, others' approval and praise, status markers (role, title, income, appearance, competence).
**Exclusion looks like:** Failure produces existential dread rather than simple disappointment. Worth rises and falls with output and reception. A season of low productivity is a season of low worth.

### 3. Belonging / Love
**What it carries:** The assurance of being truly known, wanted, and unconditionally received — not earned, not conditional on performance.
**Common functional sources:** Marriage, family, close friendship, community role, ministry position, being needed by specific people.
**Exclusion looks like:** Relational rejection or distance triggers identity-level destabilization. Belonging is functionally located in the relationship, not in covenant standing. When the relationship is threatened, the self is threatened.

### 4. Control / Agency
**What it carries:** The confidence that outcomes can be navigated, that the future is held, and that I am not at the mercy of chaos.
**Common functional sources:** Planning, perfectionism, management of others' behavior, information control, rigid routines, avoidance of uncertainty and risk.
**Exclusion looks like:** Loss of control — of circumstances, others, or self — produces disproportionate anxiety, anger, or collapse. The person manages rather than trusts. God is confessed as sovereign; control is functionally located in the plan.

### 5. Comfort / Relief
**What it carries:** The capacity to be soothed, restored, and carried through pain and distress — to find rest that actually holds.
**Common functional sources:** Food, entertainment and media, work and busyness, substances, pornography, shopping, sleep, exercise used as escape, chronic distraction.
**Exclusion looks like:** Distress triggers automatic movement toward the comfort source rather than toward God. The question "where do you go when you are in real pain?" surfaces this domain directly.

### 6. Purpose / Direction
**What it carries:** The sense that my life has a telos — a direction, calling, and reason for being that transcends immediate circumstance and cannot be revoked.
**Common functional sources:** Career and vocational identity, family role (parent, provider), ministry position, achievement goals, legacy projects.
**Exclusion looks like:** Loss of the role or project triggers purposelessness, drift, or disorientation. The functional telos was the role, not covenant identity in Christ. When the role ends, direction ends.

### 7. Identity / Self-Definition
**What it carries:** The stable, settled answer to "who am I?" — not dependent on what I do, what others say, or what I have.
**Common functional sources:** Professional role, reputation, relationship status, theological knowledge and expertise, moral performance record, parental identity, physical capability.
**Exclusion looks like:** Status threats — role removal, reputation damage, physical decline, relational loss — produce identity collapse. The person may know their identity is in Christ but does not inhabit it at the level of affect. The known truth and the felt truth are in two different layers.

### 8. Future / Hope
**What it carries:** The orientation toward what is coming — the confidence that the future is held, that there is something to move toward, and that it will be good.
**Common functional sources:** Retirement savings, career trajectory, children's success, relationship hopes, ministry plans, health management and longevity strategies.
**Exclusion looks like:** Uncertainty about the future produces consuming anxiety or despair. The future is experienced as an uncontrolled void rather than a held one. God's governance of the future is confessed but not inhabited at the felt-experience layer where the anxiety actually fires.

## In-Session Execution — Layer 4 Protocol

### What the coach holds entering Layer 4
Before opening Layer 4, the coach has a working hypothesis about which domains are active. Layers 2–3 typically surface 2–4 domains organically. Layer 4 confirms, deepens, and names them — it does not begin from scratch.

**Coach internal question before Layer 4:** *In which domains is God simply absent from this person's operating account? Where are the cisterns?*

The coach does not share this framework with the client. It is a coach-held map used to navigate and name.

### Opening move
Layer 4 does not open with an idolatry frame. It opens with a curiosity frame about where God actually shows up in the person's lived experience:

> *"I want to ask you something a little different. When you think about [domain — e.g., your financial situation / your marriage / what gives you a sense of direction] — in your actual day-to-day experience, not what you know to be true theologically — where does God enter that picture?"*

The contrast embedded in the question ("not what you know to be true theologically") creates permission to tell the truth about functional experience rather than performing correct belief. The goal is lived account, not doctrinal report.

### Mapping moves
For each domain that surfaces as active, the coach uses a weight question to confirm and locate the functional source:

> *"So what does [security / worth / belonging] actually rest on for you — when things are working, what's giving you that stability?"*

> *"When [the functional source] is threatened — when the income drops, when [person] pulls away, when the plan falls apart — what happens internally?"*

The second question surfaces the emotional weight of the functional source. A disproportionate emotional response relative to the actual threat indicates the source is carrying identity or survival weight it was never designed to carry.

### Naming the exclusion
When the map is sufficiently clear, the coach names it — without shame, without theological lecture, and with a confirming question:

> *"What I'm hearing is that in [domain], God isn't really in the operating picture right now — [functional source] is carrying that weight. Does that land?"*

The coach names the exclusion, not the failure. The person is not condemned for having cisterns — they are being helped to see where the cisterns are. The naming move is diagnostic, not corrective. It opens the door; it does not close it with judgment.

### Stay / move signals

**Stay in Layer 4** when:
- The client is still discovering which domains are active
- An exclusion has been surfaced but not yet confirmed or named
- The emotional weight of a domain has not yet been clarified

**Move to Layer 5** when:
- 2–4 domains have been mapped and named
- The client has confirmed the exclusion ("yeah, that's right" / "I hadn't seen it that way but yes")
- A pattern across domains is visible — that pattern sets up the false identity frame in Layer 5

## Question Bank

**Opening / orienting:**
- "When you're honest about your actual experience — not your theology, but what you actually live out — where does God show up practically in [domain]?"
- "Where does [security / worth / comfort] actually come from for you on a given week?"
- "When things are working and you feel OK, what's making that true?"

**Dependency and weight:**
- "When that source is threatened, what happens inside you?"
- "What would it mean — at the gut level, not the theological level — if you lost [functional source]?"
- "What's the thing you're most afraid of losing right now? What does that tell you about what's carrying your weight?"
- "Is there anything in your life that, if it were taken away, you're not sure you could survive?"

**Domain-specific probes:**
- *Security:* "What gives you a sense that things are going to be OK? What has to be in place for that feeling to be there?"
- *Significance:* "When do you feel like you actually matter? What has to be true for that to land?"
- *Belonging:* "Who has to see you and know you for you to feel like you're not alone? What happens when that person isn't available?"
- *Control:* "What happens in you when a situation is completely outside your ability to manage or predict?"
- *Comfort:* "Where do you go when you're in real pain — what's the first thing you reach for?"
- *Purpose:* "If [role / project / career] was taken away tomorrow, what would happen to your sense of direction?"
- *Identity:* "How would you know who you were if [role / reputation / relationship] was gone?"
- *Future:* "What do you find yourself counting on for tomorrow? What makes the future feel livable?"

**Naming / confirming:**
- "It sounds like in [domain], [functional source] is carrying a weight that's hard to put into words. Is that close?"
- "Where does God enter that picture — practically, not theoretically?"
- "Is there a version of [domain] where you actually trust God with it, or does that feel abstract right now?"
- "What would it look like for God to actually be in that picture — not as a theological answer, but in your real experience?"

## Diagnostic Signals

**Natural exclusion — God is simply absent:**
The person describes a domain without mentioning God at all. No resistance — God just doesn't appear. This is the most common form: not deliberate exclusion, just operational absence. The domain has its own logic and God doesn't fit it functionally.

**Emergency-only God:**
God appears only when the functional source fails. *"I pray when things get really bad."* God is the backup system, not the primary one. The functional source is still carrying the weight; God is the emergency lever pulled when the cistern is empty.

**Theological knowledge without functional inhabitation:**
The person can articulate correct theology about God's role in a domain, but their emotional response contradicts it. This is the functional theology gap — the belief is real; the formation is incomplete. The truth lives in the explicit memory layer; the functional source lives in the implicit layer where the emotional response actually fires. (See *Covenant Identity — Functional vs. Confessional Theology — 20 Diagnostic Contrasts* for a full domain-by-domain mapping of this gap.)

**Loss of functional source = identity crisis:**
When the removal of a functional source produces not just grief but identity collapse or existential dread, the source was carrying identity weight. This indicates the domain is load-bearing for a false identity structure — a primary target for Lie Eliminator and Identity Installer work in Stage 3.

**Domain concentration:**
When anxiety, control patterns, or avoidance behavior concentrate heavily in one domain, that domain is under high functional pressure. Often indicates a formative wound or attachment pattern connected to that domain. Cross-reference with the Character Wound Diagnostic to identify the wound type driving it.

**Shame-based approach to God in that domain:**
When the person approaches God about a domain with apology rather than access — *"I feel like I shouldn't even bring this to God because I've made such a mess of it"* — God has been functionally displaced and the person believes access requires self-repair first. The cistern has not just replaced the fountain; it has become the prerequisite for approaching it.

## Transition to Layer 5 — Identity Revelation

The God-Exclusion Map sets up Layer 5 by making visible the implicit identity claim embedded in each exclusion. The functional source reveals what the person believes is required for their existence to be viable — and that belief is the false identity structure driving the exclusion.

**Transition question:**

> *"As I listen to all of this — the weight that [financial security / approval / control / belonging] is carrying — I'm curious about something underneath it. What does it say about who you think you are that these things have to be in place for you to be OK?"*

This pivots from domain-specific exclusion to the false identity claim sustaining it. The exclusion is the symptom. The false identity is what the exclusion is protecting.

**Pattern examples:**

| Domain primary | Implicit identity claim |
|---|---|
| Security | "I am the one responsible for keeping everything safe. I cannot afford to be caught unprepared or weak." |
| Significance | "I am only as valuable as my last performance. If I stop producing, I stop mattering." |
| Belonging | "I am only real when someone specific sees and wants me. Without that witness, I don't exist." |
| Control | "I am the only competent agent in my situation. If I let go, everything falls apart." |
| Comfort | "I cannot bear pain without relief. I am not strong enough to be present to my own suffering." |
| Purpose | "I am what I do. Without the role, I have no direction and no reason." |
| Identity | "I am what others say I am, or what my record shows. My self is built on visible evidence." |
| Future | "The future is uncertain and I am alone in it. I must secure it myself or it will not hold." |

These identity statements are the entry point for Stage 2 (False Identity Named) and the primary targets for Covenant Truth application, Lie Eliminator entries, and Identity Installer work in Stage 3.

## Anticipated Critiques

### Critique 1 — Powlison-Adjacent Idolatry Diagnostic: Naming functional god-substitutes risks a sin-focused diagnostic that bypasses the attachment wound underneath

**Steelmanned form:** The God-Exclusion Map is fundamentally a sin-diagnostic: it surfaces where the client has turned from God to a substitute. In a model that also draws on Allender's wound typology and Thompson's attachment research, this creates a structural imbalance. The coach has a domain-specific map of what the client is functionally worshipping but may have no pathway for addressing *why* those cisterns were built in the first place. Idolatry-diagnostic frameworks (Powlison, Keller, Welch) locate the problem in the will — in what the heart trusts and wants. But the formation literature (Allender, Thompson) locates identity distortion primarily in relational wound — in how unmet need and early harm shaped the organizing structures of the self. A diagnostic that identifies the idolatrous substitutes without reaching the wound underneath produces conviction without care — and a client who understands their functional gods intellectually without having their dependency addressed at the attachment level where it was actually formed.

**Response:** The document's opening distinction between functional exclusion and general idolatry addresses the pastoral risk directly: the client is presented not as a deliberate idolater but as someone whose cisterns were pragmatically constructed under pressure — a distinction explicitly named as reducing shame and increasing diagnostic precision. More importantly, the wound dimension enters through the Affections Discovery In-Session Protocol (Layers 1–3, which surface the emotional wound picture) and the Character Wound Diagnostic Tool — both of which precede or accompany Layer 4 work. The God-Exclusion Map does not stand alone; it organizes data already gathered by the wound-sensitive prior layers. The critique is most valid when this document is used as a standalone diagnostic rather than in its documented position within a five-layer sequence.

**Remaining exposure:** The document does not explicitly require that the wound picture be present before Layer 4 is executed — it assumes the coach has it from prior layers. A practitioner who uses the God-Exclusion Map independently of the full Affections Discovery sequence will produce the critique's scenario: an exclusion map without the attachment wound that explains why the cisterns were built. This document could be more explicit that Layer 4 presupposes Layer 3's emotional and wound picture, not only the functional dependency inventory.
