## 1. ANA-MARIA RIZZUTO — *The Birth of the Living God* (1979)

### The God-Representation as Psychic Object

Rizzuto's foundational contribution is the concept of God as a psychological "illusory transitional object" — distinct from theological propositions about God. The God-representation is an internal working model of how a person *imagines* God to be, not an intellectual definition. This explains the central paradox: someone can profess theologically that God is loving while functionally relating to God as punishing, conditional, or distant. The believing mind and the relational experience operate on separate levels. Rizzuto argues that the God-representation is a major element in the fabric of one's view of self, others, and the world — it functions as a psychological organizing structure, not merely doctrine.

### Developmental Formation (Infancy Through Early Childhood)

Rizzuto grounds God-representation in early relational experience. Drawing from Winnicott, Fairbairn, and Erikson, she examines how the raw material for the God-representation emerges from primary caregivers — their emotional availability, consistency, warmth, responsiveness. The God-representation develops through the actual felt experience of being held, soothed, or neglected; being seen or dismissed; experiencing safety or danger. Unlike Freud's model (which tied God-image solely to the father), Rizzuto demonstrates that the God-representation draws from multiple sources: parental figures, siblings, religious instruction, cultural narratives, and personal experience woven together into a lived psychological object.

### The Static-Yet-Mature Problem

Rizzuto identifies a critical clinical reality: the God-representation can remain essentially unchanged even as a person matures intellectually and theologically. A person may achieve sophisticated theological understanding (seminary, reading, intellectual development) while their *lived* God-representation — what actually governs their emotional and behavioral relationship with God — remains frozen at the developmental stage it solidified in childhood. This is why theological education alone does not necessarily transform spiritual experience. The God-representation has its own psychological persistence independent of rational conviction.

### Transformation Mechanisms

Rizzuto argues that the God-representation changes through psychic work and new relational experience, not through intellectual assent. She describes God as an "object that one finds and creates throughout life," suggesting a dynamic process. Transformation requires engagement with the representational structure itself — becoming conscious of the implicit God-image one is actually operating from, recognizing its origins in early relational patterns, and allowing new relational experiences to gradually reshape it. The "living God" is not static; it can be reborn through conscious psychological engagement and lived relational experience.

### "The Living God" vs. The Theologian's God

Rizzuto's title captures this distinction: *The Birth of the Living God* refers to the psychic object that lives in the person's internal world, shaped by affect and relational memory. This is distinct from the theologian's propositional God — the conceptual framework that may be correct but emotionally inert. The theological God and the living God may be in direct conflict, and the living God (implicit, relational, neurologically encoded) will typically govern behavior and emotional response until it is directly addressed.

**Source quality note:** Rizzuto's work is foundational and widely cited, though some contemporary researchers argue her Winnicottian transitional object framework, while helpful, may not fully capture how sacred objects (which are culturally shared rather than idiosyncratic) shape God-image formation.

## 2. TODD W. HALL — Implicit vs. Explicit God-Image

### The Core Distinction

Hall's critical contribution is the empirical distinction between implicit and explicit spiritual functioning. Explicit knowing consists of conscious beliefs — theology, Scripture, doctrines you can articulate. Implicit knowing is gut-level expectations encoded in your nervous system long before language: the felt sense that God is safe or dangerous, present or absent, responsive or indifferent. These are two separate ways of knowing and processing emotional information.

This distinction explains why a person can *know* (explicitly) that God is unconditionally loving while their *implicit relational expectation* with God mirrors the anxious, conditional, or punishing patterns from their early attachment figures. The theological belief and the lived experience operate on separate neural pathways.

### The Empirical Research Basis

Hall's research with J.P. Moreland and colleagues (particularly the 2009 study *"Attachment to God and Implicit Spirituality"*) demonstrates that correspondence operates at implicit levels — attachment patterns with human caregivers do correspond to implicit God-image, but at the neurologically encoded, pre-conscious level, not at the level of explicit theological belief. Hall's studies show:
- Anxious attachment to humans correlates with anxious attachment to God (experienced as an inconsistent, conditional provider)
- Avoidant attachment to humans correlates with avoidant attachment to God (experienced as distant, unresponsive)
- Secure attachment to humans enables secure attachment to God

The empirical finding: **explicit belief alone does not change implicit God-image.** A person can have theologically correct beliefs about God and still carry an implicit relational template that operates at the attachment system level.

### The Relational Template

Hall argues that early attachment experiences create relational templates — implicit expectations about how relationships work, how safe others are, whether needs will be met. These templates become automatic, neurologically hardwired before conscious reflection is possible. When applied to God, the relational template shapes whether someone approaches God for comfort or avoids God in times of stress, whether they expect responsiveness or rejection.

### What Changes the Implicit God-Image

Hall emphasizes that transformation requires **new relational experiences**, not new information. "When a person grows up with experiences that wire the attachment system to expect certain relational patterns with important people and with God, those experiences have to be rewired through new relational experiences." The nervous system learns through felt experience with an attuned other — someone who is consistently responsive, emotionally present, and safe. Rewiring can occur through secure relationships with people and/or through deeply felt experiences of God's presence in prayer and community.

Hall's framework integrates attachment science with interpersonal neurobiology: real change happens when the attachment system encounters a different relational reality than what it was wired to expect.

**Source quality note:** Hall's work is empirically grounded in peer-reviewed research and represents current consensus in the attachment-spirituality field. His implicit/explicit distinction is increasingly adopted across psychology and spiritual formation literature.

## 3. DAVID BENNER — The Believed-In God vs. The Experienced God

### The Core Distinction

Benner identifies a fundamental blockage in spiritual formation: the gap between the believed-in God and the experienced God. Most people "call themselves Christian on the basis of belief more than experience." They believe (cognitively, propositionally) that God is loving and trustworthy, yet they do not *experience* God that way. Believing you are unconditionally loved is different from *experiencing* that unconditional love. This is not merely semantic — it points to a dissociation between doctrine and lived reality.

### Spiritual Formation is Blocked by the Gap

Benner argues that authentic spiritual formation cannot proceed when the experienced God does not match the believed-in God. A person may have correct theology while experiencing God as distant, punishing, or conditional. This creates internal conflict: the person is trying to grow spiritually using a theology that their nervous system does not actually trust. Until the experienced God shifts, growth is limited because the person is operating from defensive or anxious patterns in their actual relationship with God.

### How God is Actually Encountered

Central to Benner's approach is the priority of *encounter* over *doctrine*. God is met in prayer, in presence, in the relational space where a person opens themselves to be known and transformed. Benner emphasizes that prayer is not primarily communication *about* God but *communion with* God — a relational meeting in which God does the spiritual work of transformation. He stresses practices of opening, listening, and surrender rather than cognitive assent or doctrinal study.

Benner's synthesis: nothing can be transcended — including one's internalized God-image — until it is accepted as part of one's reality and story. The experienced God (however punishing or unreliable it may be) must be acknowledged before transformation can occur.

### What Enables Transformation

Benner's pathway involves:
1. *Acknowledgment* — recognizing the experienced God (not the believed-in God) without shame or denial
2. *Encounter* — opening oneself to actual relational experience with God through prayer, spiritual direction, and contemplative practice
3. *Surrender* — allowing God's presence and love to reshape the internal working model through lived experience, not doctrine

**Source quality note:** Benner's work bridges psychology, spirituality, and contemplative practice. The specific mechanisms by which experienced God-images shift in contemplative practice are described more narratively than empirically. His work complements rather than contradicts the empirical attachment research of Hall and Kirkpatrick.

## 4. LEE KIRKPATRICK — Attachment Theory and the Compensation vs. Correspondence Debate

### The Two Competing Hypotheses

Kirkpatrick proposes that when people think about and relate to God, their attachment system activates — the same cognitive, emotional, and behavioral machinery that governed their early relational experiences. Two models result:

**Correspondence Hypothesis:** Attachment patterns with humans correspond to attachment patterns with God. Secure to parents → secure God-image; anxious caregiving → anxious God-image; avoidant caregiving → avoidant God-image.

**Compensation Hypothesis:** God functions as a *substitute* attachment figure. People with insecure human attachment may find in God a level of felt security they never experienced with caregivers — God compensates for relational deficit.

The empirical evidence shows both operate: correspondence holds at the implicit level (nervous system expectations), while compensation may appear at the behavioral/motivational level (people with insecure attachment may be more actively involved in religious relationship and practice as they seek relief).

### God as an Attachment Figure

Kirkpatrick applies Bowlby's four attachment criteria to the God relationship:
1. *Proximity maintenance* — seeking closeness to God, desiring to sense his presence
2. *Safe haven* — approaching God in distress for comfort and reassurance
3. *Secure base* — God provides a foundation from which to explore life and make decisions
4. *Separation distress* — anxiety or distress when God feels distant (doubt, unanswered prayer, spiritual dryness)

For the God-relationship to function as a secure attachment, God must also be perceived as stronger and wiser (Bowlby's condition). Bowlby noted: "When an individual is confident that an attachment figure will be available whenever he desires it, that person will be much less prone to either intense or chronic fear" — this describes the psychological benefit of secure attachment to God when it can be established.

### Attachment Patterns Under Stress

When stress activates the attachment system:
- *Secure:* approaches God for comfort, experiences reassurance, returns to equilibrium
- *Anxious:* seeks constant reassurance, worries about God's responsiveness, experiences distress as inconsistency, views God as sometimes available and sometimes withholding
- *Avoidant:* distances from God in crisis, distrusts God's availability, maintains emotional independence, experiences God as distant and unresponsive

### Research Evidence: Insecure Attachment Predicts Specific God-Representations

Kirkpatrick and Shaver's foundational research, along with subsequent studies by Rowatt and others, established:

- *Anxious attachment:* positively associated with neuroticism, negative affect, depression, death anxiety, and viewing God as inconsistent in meeting needs. God-image: emotionally volatile, conditional provider.
- *Avoidant attachment:* inversely associated with secure attachment, positively associated with depression, characterized by distrust of God as an attachment figure. God-image: impersonal, distant, unresponsive.
- *Secure attachment to God:* positively associated with mental health, optimism, self-esteem, and inversely associated with depression, anxiety, and loneliness.

The correlation is stronger at the implicit/emotional level than at the explicit/theological level.

**Source quality note:** Kirkpatrick's work is empirically robust and widely replicated. The compensation hypothesis has received mixed empirical support and may depend on developmental timing and specific circumstances.

## Cross-Theorist Synthesis

### What all four share

1. The same core distinction — what is *believed* about God vs. what is *functionally operative* in relating to God. Rizzuto: living God vs. theologian's God. Hall: implicit vs. explicit. Benner: experienced vs. believed-in. Kirkpatrick: attachment patterns.

2. Early relational experience is formative at the implicit level — in all four frameworks, the raw material for how God is functionally experienced is laid down developmentally, before theological instruction can reach it.

3. Transformation requires more than intellectual assent — all four frameworks converge on new relational experience (Hall), lived encounter (Benner), or conscious engagement with the representational structure (Rizzuto) as the mechanism. Kirkpatrick implies the same through the compensation dynamic.

### Areas of potential tension / open questions

- Rizzuto's developmental framework predates the specificity of attachment category research (Kirkpatrick). Her transitional object framework and Kirkpatrick's attachment framework are largely compatible but use different language and operate at different levels of empirical precision.
- Hall's neurobiological pathway (rewiring through attuned relationship) and Benner's contemplative encounter pathway (surrender and presence) represent slightly different transformation mechanisms aiming at the same target. The relationship between these two pathways — whether they are sequential, parallel, or interchangeable — is not fully resolved in the literature.
- The compensation vs. correspondence debate (Kirkpatrick) remains partially unresolved empirically. Evidence suggests compensation may appear at the behavioral/motivational level while correspondence holds at the implicit/nervous system level — but this framing needs further research to confirm.
- None of the four theorists addresses in depth how theologically robust declaration work (distinct from generic affirmation) interacts with the implicit God-image. This is a gap relevant to Covenant Identity Coaching specifically.

### Practitioner implication summary

The four frameworks converge on a single actionable sequence:
1. Name the gap between what is believed and what is functionally experienced (Rizzuto/Hall/Benner)
2. Trace the relational origins of the implicit God-image without shame (Rizzuto/Kirkpatrick)
3. Create conditions for new relational experience that contradicts the implicit template (Hall/Benner)
4. Allow the lived experience to gradually reshape the representation — not through information but through encounter (all four)

## Applied Taxonomy: Five God-Representation Types in Covenant Identity Coaching

*This taxonomy is derived from the research synthesis above and the clinical patterns observed across the four contributor frameworks. Used in [[Covenant Identity — God-Representation Deepening Tool]] and [[Covenant Identity — God-Representation Between-Session Practices]].*

The four-contributor synthesis produces four primary God-rep types observable in coaching contexts, with a fifth added 2026-05-19 to address a presentation not cleanly captured by the original four.

**1. Punitive**
God as a demanding judge who monitors for failure. Love is conditional on performance. Prayer feels like confession-or-else; silence feels like disapproval; mistakes trigger shame spirals connected to God. Research basis: Kirkpatrick's anxious attachment pattern at the implicit level; Rizzuto's God-representation as organized around conditional parental presence; Hall's explicit/implicit divergence (client states God is gracious; nervous system braces for accusation).

**2. Absent/Distant**
God as real in theology but experientially uninvolved — indifferent, unresponsive to this particular person. Prayer feels one-directional; significant events feel unwitnessed. Research basis: Kirkpatrick's avoidant attachment pattern (God experienced as impersonal and unresponsive); Hall's correspondence hypothesis at the implicit level; Benner's believed-in vs. experienced God gap (the gap is widest here — no functional experience of encounter).

**3. Permissive/Safe**
God as entirely affirming, never confrontational, no demands and no edges. Spiritually comfortable and therefore spiritually static. Research basis: Kirkpatrick's compensation hypothesis — God functions as an idealized object rather than a real relational Person; Rizzuto's "created God" that serves psychological function without requiring transformation.

**4. Transactional**
God as ledger-keeper — gives when you perform, withholds when you don't. The relationship is contractual rather than covenantal. Research basis: Kirkpatrick's anxious attachment behavioral pattern (constantly seeking to secure God's responsiveness through sufficient religious behavior); Hall's implicit relational template organized around conditional provision.

**5. Overwhelming/Consuming** *(added 2026-05-19)*
God's presence is too much — consuming, intense, unsafe to approach fully. Full encounter feels like annihilation or being swallowed. The client may genuinely desire God but approaches only partially, holding something back. Not fully captured by the original four types. Research basis: Kirkpatrick's avoidant attachment under conditions of perceived threat from the attachment figure (rather than mere absence); Benner's acknowledgment that the experienced God can feel dangerous rather than absent or punishing — a distinct phenomenological presentation requiring its own coaching approach. Strongly correlates with the Hermit character wound type. Clinically distinct from Punitive (God watches and judges) — here God's holiness and presence itself is experienced as overwhelming, not God's disapproval specifically.
