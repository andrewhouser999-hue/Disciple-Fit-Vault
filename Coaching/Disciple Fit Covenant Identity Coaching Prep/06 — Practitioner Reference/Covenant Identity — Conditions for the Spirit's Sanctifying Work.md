**Purpose:** This document provides the biblical-exegetical foundation for the claim that practitioners and clients participate in sanctification by *creating the conditions under which the Holy Spirit ordinarily operates.* It is practitioner-facing only — not client-facing. Primary deployment: coach formation and framework anchoring; secondary: responding to theological objection when "conditions" language is pressed. Integrates with the Sanctification Module, Spiritual Disciplines Guide, and Formation Mechanism Guide.

---

## Governing Claim

The New Covenant pattern across both Testaments is consistent: **God is the agent of transformation; humans participate by adopting postures, attentions, and practices that Scripture commands.** These are not payments that purchase the Spirit's activity. They are, in Willard's framing, the riverbed through which the water flows — remove the riverbed and the water doesn't dry up, but it no longer goes where it needs to go.

**The pivotal text:**

> *"Work out your own salvation with fear and trembling, for it is God who works in you, both to will and to work, for his good pleasure."* — Philippians 2:12–13

Two things matter linguistically. First, shared root: κατεργάζεσθε ("work out," human present imperative) and ἐνεργῶν ("works in," divine present participle) both derive from ἐργ-. The human "working out" is the *site* where God's "working in" becomes operative — not a separate contribution running parallel. Second, the "for" (γάρ) makes God's working the *ground* of the command, not its supplement. Both verbs are present tense: ongoing, not a one-time transaction.

This text holds divine sovereignty and human responsibility in the same sentence without resolving the tension. Any framework that collapses that tension — "God will just do it" (passive quietism) or "I have to make this happen" (exhausted effort) — has moved away from what the verse actually says.

## I. Categories of Condition in Scripture

### A. Posture/Orientation — Sustained, Not Episodic

Conditions of posture are *continuous states of direction* — not acts performed and completed but orientations the person either inhabits or does not.

**Galatians 5:16, 25**
> *"Walk by the Spirit... let us keep in step with the Spirit."*

Two distinct terms: περιπατεῖτε (walk, live in step) and στοιχῶμεν (keep in step — a marching/formation term, staying aligned with a pace already set by Another). The fruit (καρπός — organic, not manufactured) is what the Spirit produces; walking is the condition under which that production occurs. Fruit is not achieved; it grows in someone who is walking.

**Romans 8:5–6**
> *"Those who live according to the Spirit set their minds on the things of the Spirit. For to set the mind on the flesh is death, but to set the mind on the Spirit is life and peace."*

φρόνημα is not primarily cognitive — it encompasses mindset, desire, and orientation. Closer to "what you are set toward / what you love" than "what you think about." This maps directly onto the affections diagnostic layer: the presenting question is not only "what does the client believe" but "toward what is their φρόνημα turned?"

**John 15:4–10**
> *"Abide in me, and I in you... If my words abide in you, ask whatever you wish, and it will be done for you... If you keep my commandments, you will abide in my love."*

Abiding (μένω) is relational and continuous. It has content: "if my words abide in you" (v.7), "keep my commandments" (v.10). This is not passive mysticism — abiding is a condition with identifiable substance, sustained over time.

### B. Attentional — What You Behold Shapes What You Become

Attentional conditions govern the *object of sustained gaze* — what the practitioner or client's attention and affection are habitually turned toward.

**2 Corinthians 3:16–18 — The Central Mechanism Text**
> *"When one turns to the Lord, the veil is removed... And we all, with unveiled face, beholding the glory of the Lord, are being transformed into the same image from one degree of glory to another. For this comes from the Lord who is the Spirit."*

The mechanism is explicit and sequential: **turning → unveiling → beholding → transformation.** The agent of transformation is named: "the Lord who is the Spirit." The governing verb is passive — *are being transformed* (μεταμορφούμεθα, same root as the Transfiguration). The human act (turning, beholding) creates the condition; transformation itself is received. This is the governing frame for the Formation Mechanism Guide and the Between-Session Library's attentional practices.

*Textual note:* κατοπτριζόμενοι is genuinely ambiguous in Greek — the middle voice can mean "beholding [as in a mirror]" or "reflecting [as a mirror]." Most modern translations render "beholding." This does not change the coaching application but warrants acknowledgment with theologically literate audiences.

**Romans 12:1–2**
> *"Do not be conformed to this world, but be transformed by the renewal of your mind."*

Both verbs are present passives. "Do not be conformed" (μὴ συσχηματίζεσθε) — the human act is *refusing* the mold the world presses into. "Be transformed" (μεταμορφοῦσθε) — the transformation happens *to* the mind, not *by* it. The human acts are presenting the body as worship (v.1) and refusing conformity. Renewal itself is received.

**Diagnostic implication:** Rom 12:2 and Eph 4:22–24 together identify two forces competing for formative influence over a client's attention: the world's conforming pressure and the Spirit's renewing work. A client without deliberate counter-attentional practices is not in a neutral posture — they are being actively conformed to something.

### C. Petitionary/Receptive — Asking as a Condition

**Luke 11:9–13**
> *"Ask, and it will be given to you... how much more will the heavenly Father give the Holy Spirit to those who ask him!"*

Set within the persistent-friend parable — the condition named is *persistence* (αἰτοῦσιν, present: keep asking). This is not a one-time petition but an ongoing receptive posture toward the Spirit's gift.

**Galatians 3:2, 5**
> *"Did you receive the Spirit by works of the law or by hearing with faith?... Does he who supplies the Spirit to you and works miracles among you do so by works of the law, or by hearing with faith?"*

Paul's term is ἐξ ἀκοῆς πίστεως — "hearing with faith," receptive trust. V.5 frames this as continuous: God *is* (present tense, ἐπιχορηγῶν — keeps supplying) the Spirit on the basis of ongoing hearing-with-faith, not a prior event only. The condition is receptive trust, not performance.

### D. Negative Conditions — Postures That Hinder

Scripture names specific postures that obstruct the Spirit's ordinary operation. These function as a *diagnostic layer* when a client presents with practices in place but without fruit.

**Ephesians 4:30** (context: vv. 25–32)
> *"Do not grieve the Holy Spirit of God."*

The context is relational sin: lying (v.25), unresolved anger (v.26–27), bitterness, wrath, slander, malice (v.31). Relational and affective sin — not only missed practices — is identified as the mechanism of grieving. Diagnostic implication: a client doing disciplines without fruit may have an unresolved relational blockage rather than a practice-gap.

**1 Thessalonians 5:19**
> *"Do not quench the Spirit."*

σβέννυτε = extinguish a fire. Paired with "do not despise prophecies" (v.20) — the quenching happens through *dismissiveness* toward the Spirit's communicative activity. Relevant for clients who intellectualize, deflect, or minimize what surfaces in session.

**Psalm 51:10, 17**
> *"Create in me a clean heart, O God... a broken and contrite heart, O God, you will not despise."*

בָּרָא (create) is the Genesis 1 creation verb — David is not asking for repair but for a new-creation act. The condition named is *contrition* — not as a work that earns renewal but as the posture that precedes and correlates with it. Contrition is the opposite of the defensive posture that blocks formation.

## II. Old Testament Roots — Divine Initiative, Human Response

These texts establish the pattern Paul inherits and applies in Phil 2:12–13.

**Ezekiel 36:26–27**
> *"I will give you a new heart... I will put my Spirit within you, and cause you to walk in my statutes."*

The Hebrew is causative (וְעָשִׂיתִי) — God's Spirit-gift is what produces the walking. Yet the Torah never stopped commanding Israel to walk in the statutes. Command and divine enablement sit side by side without embarrassment. This is the New Covenant's constitutive move: the Spirit is given as the agent who enables covenant compliance, not as a reward for achieving it.

**Deuteronomy 30:6**
> *"The LORD your God will circumcise your heart... so that you will love the LORD your God with all your heart and with all your soul."*

Divine act → enabled human response, within a document that simultaneously commands "choose life... love the LORD" (30:19–20). This is not incoherence — it is the covenant structure. God's enabling act comes first; human response is genuine and required; both statements are simultaneously true.

## III. Theological Weight and the Interpretive Fault Line

### Where Interpreters Split

**Reformed/monergist reading:** Phil 2:12–13's "for it is God who works in you" means the human "working out" is itself a gift — the evidence and fruit of a prior sovereign work, not an independent condition God responds to. On this reading, "we create conditions for the Spirit" risks inverting the causal order (God enables → humans respond, not humans posture → God responds). The concern is spiritual technique-ism: do X, guaranteed Y from the Spirit.

**Wesleyan "means of grace" reading:** There are real, divinely-appointed channels (prayer, Scripture, sacrament, fasting, fellowship) through which grace *ordinarily flows* — not meritorious, but genuinely conditional in a covenantal sense, because God has bound himself to work this way. This is the tradition Willard draws from directly. "Conditions" language carries no technique-ism risk as long as the causal order remains clear: God's prior initiative enables; human participation receives and positions.

**Eastern Orthodox (synergeia/theosis):** Ongoing human cooperation with grace at every moment is essential, not only at conversion. "Conditions" language fits naturally, though the theological scaffolding (theosis) differs from Protestant categories.

### Where This Model Lands

The Covenant Identity model operates within the Wesleyan/means-of-grace stream. The correct formulation is:

> *Conditions are not causes. Conditions are the riverbed through which grace flows. The Spirit is not manipulated by discipline — he is received by a person who has positioned themselves to receive.*

**Guard against:** any practitioner or client language that implies the Spirit is a guaranteed output of the right inputs. Phil 2:12–13's "fear and trembling" is itself a guard clause — this is a covenant posture to inhabit, not a formula to run.

## IV. Coaching Translation

**Primary mechanism — 2 Corinthians 3:18:** Transformation follows beholding. What a person's attention and affection are habitually fixed on is what they are being conformed to (Rom 12:2 = negative case; 2 Cor 3:18 = positive case). The affections diagnostic is not only about belief — it is about gaze: *what is this client turned toward, and is it veiled or unveiled before the Lord's glory?*

**Formation layer — Willard/VIM:** Spiritual disciplines are the riverbed; the Spirit is the water. A client who has been disciplined and feels no visible change is not necessarily failing — conditions do not guarantee timing, only positioning. The fruit is organic (καρπός), not manufactured.

**Negative diagnostic — Eph 4:30 + 1 Thess 5:19:** When a client presents with practices in place but without fruit, probe these before adding more practices:

| Obstruction | Diagnostic Signal | Coaching Move |
|---|---|---|
| Grieving (Eph 4:30) | Unresolved relational sin: bitterness, slander, unprocessed anger | Relational/affective work before formation work |
| Quenching (1 Thess 5:19) | Dismissiveness toward what surfaces in session; intellectualizing, minimizing | Slow down; attend to what is being avoided |
| Contrition absent (Ps 51) | Defensive posture; not broken over the gap | Lament layer; Stage 4b relational/attentive presence |

## V. Session Application

**Tension-holding (Phil 2:12–13):** Anchor text for clients who oscillate between passivity ("God will just do it") and exhaustion ("I have to make this happen"). Name the γάρ explicitly: *"God's working in you is the reason you can work out — not the reason you don't have to."*

**Attention diagnostic (2 Cor 3:18 + Rom 12:2):** *"What is your gaze fixed on this week, and what is it forming you toward?"* — between-session reflection; pairs with media-diet and mental rehearsal conversations.

**Negative diagnostic (Eph 4:30 + 1 Thess 5:19):** When a client reports disciplines without fruit, probe for relational/affective blockage before adding more practices.

**Divine initiative frame (Ezek 36:26–27 + Phil 2:12–13):** Theological container for clients who need a reason both to trust ("God is the one working in me") and to act ("and so I will work out"). Reinforces the new covenant backbone without requiring clients to navigate the monergism debate.
