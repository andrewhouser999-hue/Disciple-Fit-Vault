# Covenant Identity — Assigning Receptive Return — Practitioner Technique
*Practitioner Reference — how a coach actually words the between-session instruction Node 7 requires*

*Related: [[Covenant Identity — Consolidation Through Receptive Return — Mechanism]] (the mechanism this technique operationalizes — why posture, not repetition, consolidates implicit reorganization); [[Covenant Identity — What Moves Covenant Truth from Registration to Reception]] (Condition 3 — receptive posture; the Between-Session Dimension section this document elaborates); [[Covenant Identity — Matched Declaration Architecture]] (the declaration text this instruction is built around); [[Covenant Identity — Character Wound Diagnostic Tool]] (per-type false identity patterns behind the calibration notes below)*

**What this is:** A compressed, practitioner-facing walkthrough of how to actually word a between-session receptive-return assignment for a specific client — not the general distinction (receptive return vs. affirmation repetition, already established), but the concrete sentence-by-sentence instruction a practitioner hands a client at the end of a session where covenant truth has landed.

**When used:** Immediately after a Node 5-6 event in session — covenant truth has been spoken into an activated false structure and something has landed — when the practitioner is about to send the client home with a between-session practice. Also useful when a client reports faithfully "doing their declarations" with no felt change, and the practitioner needs to re-issue the instruction correctly rather than assume the mechanism has failed.

**Who uses it:** Practitioner only.

## Why "Read Your Declaration Daily" Doesn't Work

Naming the distinction between receptive return and affirmation repetition is not the same as knowing how to word an instruction that actually produces the former. A practitioner who understands the mechanism perfectly can still default to generic language under the time pressure of closing a session — "just keep coming back to this truth this week" — which the client will predictably execute as repetition, because repetition is the default mode for engaging with a written statement unless something explicitly redirects it.

**Generic (produces affirmation repetition):** *"Keep reading your declaration this week."*
**Wordable (produces receptive return):** A five-part instruction, given once, out loud, at the point of assignment — not left to the client to infer from a workbook note they may not read closely.

---

## The Five-Part Instruction Formula

Every receptive-return assignment has the same underlying shape, regardless of which specific declaration or wound type is involved. The formula exists so a practitioner can construct it correctly under session-ending time pressure rather than reaching for generic language.

### 1. Name the specific declaration — not the general truth

Use the exact claim produced by Condition 1 work, not a paraphrase or a general theological statement. The declaration should already exist in the client's own language from the session, not be freshly composed at the moment of assignment.

### 2. Instruct a single utterance, not repetition

Tell the client explicitly to speak the declaration *once*. This is a deliberate departure from how affirmations are normally taught ("say it until you believe it") and needs to be named as a departure, or the client will default to their prior assumption about how declarations work.

### 3. Instruct an explicit stop

After the single utterance, tell the client to stop speaking — not trail into elaboration, explanation, or a second and third repetition "to make sure it lands." The stop is the hinge between the affirmation-repetition mode and the receptive mode; without an explicit stop instruction, most clients will keep talking.

### 4. Instruct stillness oriented toward God as the active party

Direct the client into waiting — not passive blankness, but attention turned toward the Father as the one who acts on what was just spoken, mirroring the in-session receptive posture (Condition 3) the client already experienced when the truth first landed. Naming that connection explicitly — "this is the same stillness we sat in together earlier" — helps the client locate the posture rather than invent a new one.

### 5. Name the prohibition directly — don't manage, don't repeat

State outright what not to do: don't repeat the phrase again, don't start reasoning about whether it's true, don't self-monitor whether the stillness is "working." Naming the wrong pattern by name is what prevents the client from defaulting back to it the moment they're alone with the practice and unsure what they're supposed to be doing.

## Worked Example — Dismissiveness / Warrior Wound

**Session context:** Client has named the false identity ("I am safe when I do not need anyone; needing my wife means I could be controlled or disappointed the way I was before"), grieved its cost (Node 3 complete — weariness, "I've been alone the whole time I was protecting myself"), and received the covenant truth in a receptive posture (Nodes 4-6): the declaration *"I am already secure with my wife — I don't need distance to be safe."*

**The instruction, given verbatim at the end of session:**

*"Here's what I want you to do with this between now and next time. Each morning, bring this before the Father: 'I am already secure with my wife — I don't need distance to be safe.' Speak it once. Then stop — don't say it again, don't start explaining it to yourself. Go still, the same way we just sat together. Wait. You're not trying to convince yourself of anything. You're asking the Spirit to make this real in you, not just familiar to you. If you notice yourself repeating it, or reasoning about whether it's true, just notice that and return to stillness."*

Every one of the five parts is present: the specific declaration (not a paraphrase), a single utterance, an explicit stop, stillness oriented toward God as agent, and the named prohibition against repetition and self-management.

## Wound-Type Calibration

The five-part formula is constant; the risk each wound type brings to the practice is not. A practitioner who assigns the same instruction identically across wound types will miss the specific way each type tends to co-opt or misuse the practice.

| Wound Type | Risk in the Practice | Calibration Note |
|---|---|---|
| **Warrior** (betrayal → control/independence) | Turns the stillness itself into a performance to master — "doing receptive return correctly" becomes the new competence project, re-enacting the very self-sufficiency the declaration is meant to displace. | Say explicitly: *"There's no way to do this wrong. You're not being evaluated on the stillness."* Naming the absence of a performance standard removes the thing this wound type will otherwise supply on its own. |
| **Hermit** (terror → isolation/avoidance) | Private stillness alone can be indistinguishable from the wound's own default strategy — withdrawal dressed as a spiritual practice. Solitude does not disconfirm "proximity is threat" the way a relational encounter would. | Pair the private instruction with an explicit relational channel — see [[Covenant Identity — Accountability Companion Guide]] — rather than assigning solitude as the only vehicle. Name the pairing reason to the client: *"This truth needs somewhere relational to land too, not only a quiet room."* |
| **False Noble** (shame/ambivalence → performance/approval) | Stillness becomes a new arena for self-evaluation — "Am I feeling reverent enough? Is this landing the way it's supposed to?" — the same approval-anxiety the declaration is meant to address, now aimed at the practice itself. | Say explicitly: *"There's nothing to get right here, and no one grading whether it landed. If nothing feels different, that's not failure — just come back tomorrow."* Removing the evaluative frame is the calibration, not adding reassurance about the content. |

## Common Mistakes When Assigning This

**Leaving the instruction implicit in a workbook note.** The Lie Eliminator and Identity Installer workbooks both contain the receptive return / affirmation repetition distinction in their "Before You Begin" sections, but a client under emotional load at the end of a heavy session is unlikely to re-read and internalize written instructions later. The instruction needs to be spoken, out loud, at the point of assignment — the workbook note reinforces it; it does not substitute for it.

**Compressing the five parts into a single sentence.** *"Bring this to God each morning and sit with it"* sounds complete but omits the explicit stop and the named prohibition — the two parts most responsible for actually preventing the client's default drift back into repetition.

**Assigning it once and never re-checking.** Per the Consolidation mechanism, the diagnostic question when a client reports no change is *"walk me through what you're actually doing"* — not an assumption that the mechanism has failed. Practitioners should expect to re-issue or refine this instruction at least once per client, not treat the first assignment as sufficient.

**Treating the instruction as identical across wound types.** See the calibration table above — the same words can either support or undermine the practice depending on what the client's specific wound-driven default is likely to do with unstructured stillness.
