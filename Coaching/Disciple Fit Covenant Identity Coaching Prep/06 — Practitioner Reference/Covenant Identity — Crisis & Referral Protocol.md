# Covenant Identity — Crisis & Referral Protocol
*Reference document for all phases of the Covenant Identity Coaching engagement. Read before beginning your first client engagement. Return to it when any of the triggers below are present. The in-session crisis section is designed to be scannable under pressure — do not read it for the first time when you need it.*

---

## A Note on What This Document Is

This protocol exists because wounded people bring wounded material into coaching, and not all of it belongs in coaching. Working with Christians in the Gap between their declared and lived covenant identity means working with people who carry significant formation wounds — and some of those wounds require clinical care that coaching cannot and must not attempt to provide.

This document is not about fear or over-caution. It is about precision. The coach who tries to hold material that exceeds their scope does not serve the client — they delay appropriate care and take on risk they are not equipped to carry. The coach who refers well communicates to the client: *what you're carrying deserves more than I can give you here.* That is not rejection. That is care.

---

## 1. Scope of Practice — What This Coaching Is and Is Not

**Covenant Identity Coaching is:**
- A coaching engagement working in the Gap between the client's declared and lived covenant identity
- Appropriate for emotionally functional adults navigating formation issues, identity distortion, schema patterns, and spiritual formation blocks
- Non-clinical: the coach does not diagnose, treat, or prescribe
- Focused on the present and forward — excavating formation history to understand the present, not to process trauma clinically

**Covenant Identity Coaching is not:**
- Therapy, counseling, or psychiatric treatment
- A substitute for clinical care when clinical care is indicated
- Appropriate for clients in acute psychiatric crisis, active trauma response requiring clinical processing, or presentations requiring diagnosis and treatment
- Equipped to address suicidal ideation, self-harm, psychotic symptoms, active substance use disorders, eating disorders, or severe dissociative presentations beyond initial grounding and referral

**The diagnostic question before every engagement:**
*Is this person emotionally stable enough to do the forward-facing, gap-closing work of coaching — or do they need clinical stabilization first?*

Some clients need therapy before coaching. Some need therapy alongside coaching. Some need coaching only. Some move between. The intake form's responses to Q1 (presenting problem duration and failed attempts), Q7 (lament capacity), Q8 (failure meaning), and Q10 (shame content) are the primary pre-engagement screening data. Trust your gut when something presents as more than coaching can hold.

## 2. Referral Triggers — Non-Crisis Situations

These do not require immediate action but do require a referral conversation within 1-2 sessions of recognition.

**Clinical presentations requiring therapy, not coaching:**
- Active major depressive episode — persistent low mood, anhedonia, sleep/appetite disruption, hopelessness lasting more than two weeks
- Active anxiety disorder — panic attacks, severe generalized anxiety, OCD presentations, phobias significantly impairing daily functioning
- Trauma requiring clinical processing — PTSD symptoms (flashbacks, hypervigilance, avoidance, nightmares), complex developmental trauma requiring EMDR or trauma-focused therapy
- Eating disorder behaviors — restriction, bingeing, purging, significant body dysmorphia
- Active substance abuse — patterns that go beyond use into dependence or that are being used to manage emotional pain
- Bipolar presentation — cycling mood states, periods of elevated mood with decreased need for sleep, grandiosity, followed by depressive periods
- Psychotic spectrum symptoms — loosened associations, ideas of reference, paranoid ideation, breaks with consensus reality
- Severe dissociation — frequent or extended episodes of depersonalization/derealization, identity fragmentation beyond what IFS coaching can hold

**Scope creep signals within the coaching engagement:**
- The client consistently requires crisis-level support between sessions (frequent texts, urgent messages, emotional emergencies)
- Wound material is destabilizing the client's daily functioning rather than being processed within the window of tolerance
- The same wound material is surfacing repeatedly without any movement across multiple Phase 2 sessions — a signal that deeper clinical processing is needed before coaching can be effective
- The coach is consistently feeling out of their depth — not stretched, but genuinely over the skill level required to hold what is present
- The client is not improving and there is no clear reason why — when coaching isn't working, consider whether the presenting need is clinical

**Faith-specific referral situations:**
- Spiritual abuse history with active symptoms — coaching can work with formation wounds, but spiritual abuse that has produced trauma-level symptoms belongs in therapy first
- Religious scrupulosity (OCD-related religious obsession) — presenting as extreme guilt, compulsive confession, inability to receive forgiveness despite theological understanding; this is clinical, not a coaching issue
- The client whose theology requires them to refuse mental health care — this is itself a presenting issue that may need gentle exploration before any referral is possible; forcing the referral conversation prematurely shuts the door

## 3. Crisis Categories and Severity Assessment

The following categories require immediate protocol response (Section 4), not a deferred referral conversation.

### Suicidal Ideation

The most critical assessment in any helping relationship. Research is clear: asking directly about suicidal ideation does **not** increase risk. Avoiding the question does not protect the client — it protects the coach's discomfort.

**Use the Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS) framework for triage:**

| Level | Description | Response |
|---|---|---|
| **Passive ideation** | "I wish I were dead" / "I wouldn't mind if I didn't wake up" — no intent to act | Stay present. Explore. Do not minimize. Assess for escalation. Consider referral. |
| **Active ideation, no plan** | "I want to kill myself" — no method, no plan, no intent | Do not leave the client alone. Assess support network. Refer to therapist within 24 hours. Safety plan. |
| **Active ideation with plan, no intent** | Has thought through a method; no stated intent to act | Same as above, urgency elevated. Connect to crisis resources before session ends. |
| **Active ideation with plan and intent** | States intent to act; has means available or accessible | **Immediate intervention.** Do not end the session until safety is established. Call 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline) with client or direct client to call. If imminent danger, call 911. |

**The direct question to ask:**
*"I want to ask you something directly, and I want you to know it's safe to answer honestly. Are you having any thoughts of hurting yourself or ending your life?"*

If yes: *"Tell me more about what that looks like. Do you have a specific plan? Have you thought about how you would do it?"*

Do not treat any level of suicidal ideation as a coaching issue. It is not. Refer at every level. The difference is urgency.

### Self-Harm

Current or recent self-harm (cutting, burning, hitting, other) requires immediate referral to a mental health provider. Self-harm is often a regulation strategy for unbearable emotional pain — the wound material of Phase 2 can activate it. If a client discloses current self-harm:

- Respond with care, not alarm: *"Thank you for telling me. That takes trust. I want to make sure you're getting the right support for this."*
- Assess whether wounds require medical attention (if yes — end session, direct to ER or urgent care)
- Do not continue coaching until client is connected with a therapist who can hold the self-harm work
- Coaching may resume concurrently with therapy once the therapist has confirmed the client is stable enough for coaching

### Acute Trauma Response in Session

Phase 2 wound mapping involves approaching difficult formation material. Occasionally, this approach activates a trauma response that exceeds the coaching window of tolerance. Signs:

- Flooding: the client is no longer narrating the wound — they are reliving it; affect escalates rapidly, eyes go distant, they lose the ability to track the present moment
- Severe dissociation: the client appears to leave the room, goes entirely flat, cannot reorient to the present when directly addressed, may appear glazed or unreachable
- Somatic crisis: shaking, hyperventilation, inability to speak, physical symptoms that suggest a panic attack or freeze response that is not self-resolving

**In-session response:** Ground first. (See Section 4 — Grounding Protocol.) Do not pursue wound material. End the excavation. Stabilize the client in the present before the session ends. Do not leave a client in a dysregulated state. If the response recurs in subsequent sessions, the wound material requires clinical processing before coaching can work with it.

### Disclosure of Abuse

If a client discloses current domestic violence, physical abuse, sexual abuse, or child abuse:

**Current abuse (the client is in an unsafe situation now):** The coaching relationship cannot hold this alone. Provide domestic violence hotline information (1-800-799-7233 / thehotline.org). The client must have a safety plan. Do not pressure the client to leave — that can increase danger. Connect to resources and continue the relationship as a point of stability if appropriate.

**Reported child abuse:** Life coaches are generally not mandated reporters under state law, but this varies by jurisdiction. Know your state's requirements. Regardless of legal obligation, if a child is currently in danger, the ethics of the situation require action. Encourage the client to report. Consult if uncertain.

**Historical abuse:** This is common in Phase 2 wound mapping and does not require crisis protocol — it requires careful titration (Phase 2 tools) and may indicate need for concurrent therapy.

### Psychotic Presentation

If a client presents with symptoms of active psychosis — disorganized thinking, beliefs inconsistent with consensus reality (paranoid ideation, grandiosity, delusions), hallucinations, or severe loosened associations — coaching is not the appropriate container.

Respond with calm and without reinforcing the psychotic content: *"I want to make sure you're getting the right support. I'd like to pause our coaching work and help you connect with someone who can help more than I can right now."*

Direct to a family member, their physician, or a mental health crisis line. This is not a coaching situation.

## 4. In-Session Crisis Protocol

*This section is designed to be scannable. Read it before your first session. Know it before you need it.*

---

### Step 1 — Stay Regulated

Your nervous system is contagious. If you dysregulate, the client cannot regulate. Breathe. Slow your voice. Stay in your body. The client needs a regulated presence, not a distressed one.

---

### Step 2 — Name What You're Seeing

*"I want to pause for a moment. What you're sharing is significant, and I want to make sure I respond to it well."*

Do not pretend you didn't hear what was said. Do not redirect to coaching content. Name it.

---

### Step 3 — Ask Directly

For suicidal ideation: *"Are you having thoughts of ending your life or hurting yourself?"*

For self-harm: *"Are you hurting yourself?"*

For safety: *"Are you safe right now?"*

Direct questions are not harmful. Evasion is.

---

### Step 4 — Assess

Listen to the answer. Use the C-SSRS levels (Section 3) to triage:
- Is there a plan?
- Is there intent?
- Is there means?
- Is this immediate?

---

### Step 5 — Ground If Needed

If the client is in acute trauma response or dissociation before you can assess:

**5-4-3-2-1 grounding:** *"I want you to stay here with me. Name five things you can see. Four things you can touch. Three things you can hear. Two things you can smell. One thing you can taste."*

**Physical anchor:** *"Both feet on the floor. Feel the chair under you. You are here, in this room, with me."*

**Breathe with them:** Slow your own breath visibly. *"Follow my breath. In for four, hold for four, out for six."*

Do not return to wound material after a grounding intervention. The session's work is done.

---

### Step 6 — Connect to Resources

Do not end the session until the client has:
- Acknowledged they are not in immediate danger (or has been connected to emergency services if they are)
- A specific person they will call or be with after the session
- A specific resource they will contact (crisis line, therapist, ER)

*"Before we end today — who is your person? Who do you call when things are hard? I want you to call them today."*

**Have these numbers available:**
- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline:** Call or text 988 (U.S.)
- **Crisis Text Line:** Text HOME to 741741
- **National Domestic Violence Hotline:** 1-800-799-7233
- **Emergency:** 911

---

### Step 7 — Do Not End the Session in Crisis

Do not end a session while the client is still dysregulated, actively suicidal, or without a safety anchor. Extend the session if needed. If the session is remote (video/phone), do not disconnect until the client is regulated and connected to a resource or person.

---

### Step 8 — Document Immediately After

Within one hour of the session, write a factual record:
- Date and time
- What the client disclosed, verbatim where possible
- Your assessment (passive/active ideation, plan/no plan, intent/no intent)
- What action you took
- What resources you provided
- What the client agreed to do
- Whether you made a referral and to whom

Keep this record. It protects both you and the client.

## 5. How to Have the Referral Conversation

The referral conversation fails in two ways: it is avoided entirely (the coach keeps working beyond their scope and hopes it resolves), or it is delivered clumsily (the client feels rejected, shamed, or like a problem being handed off).

A well-delivered referral communicates: *what you're carrying deserves more than I can give you here. I'm not stepping away from you — I'm making sure you get what you actually need.*

**The structure:**

**1. Name what you're observing — specifically:**
*"I've been noticing over our last few sessions that [specific observation — e.g., the wound material we're working with is activating a level of distress that isn't settling between sessions / the depression you've described is affecting your daily functioning in ways that go beyond what coaching addresses]."*

**2. Name the limit honestly:**
*"What you're carrying is real and significant. And I want to be honest with you — it's beyond what coaching is designed to hold. This isn't a reflection of your willingness or your work. It's about what the work requires."*

**3. Name what you're recommending:**
*"I want you to connect with a [therapist / counselor / psychiatrist]. Specifically, someone who works with [trauma / depression / anxiety / etc.]. Not instead of our work — but as a foundation that makes our work possible."*

**4. Stay in the relationship:**
*"This doesn't mean we're done. Coaching and therapy can work together. Once you're connected with someone and they've confirmed you're stable enough for this kind of work, I'd like to continue. What you've built here doesn't disappear."*

**5. Offer practical help:**
*"Can I help you find someone? Do you know where to start? I want to make sure this actually happens, not just gets thought about."*

---

**When the client resists the referral:**

Some clients — especially in faith communities — carry stigma about therapy. Common resistances and responses:

*"Christians shouldn't need therapy — I just need to pray and trust God more."*
Response: *"I understand that instinct. I also believe God gave us medicine for bodies and skilled people for minds. A broken leg needs more than prayer. What you're describing may be similar. Seeking help is not a failure of faith — it may be the most faithful thing you can do."*

*"I've tried therapy before and it didn't help."*
Response: *"Tell me more about that. What didn't help? There are many kinds of therapy — what didn't work before may not be representative. And what you're working on now is different from what you may have worked on before."*

*"I can't afford it."*
Response: *"Let's look at what's available. Community mental health centers often have sliding scale fees. Some therapists offer reduced rates. And some things you cannot afford not to address."*

*"I don't want to start over with someone new."*
Response: *"You don't have to tell them everything. You start where you are. And you're not losing what you've built here — you're adding support, not replacing what we have."*

## 6. Concurrent Coaching and Therapy

Coaching and therapy can operate concurrently. When they do:

**The default posture:** Coaching works forward; therapy works through. Coaching addresses the Gap and the covenant identity formation work. Therapy processes the clinical material that is blocking the coaching work. These are complementary, not competing.

**With the client's consent:** If the client is willing, brief communication with their therapist (what coaching is addressing, what clinical material has surfaced) can prevent duplication of work and ensure both relationships are pulling in the same direction. Never share session content without explicit client consent.

**When to pause coaching until therapy is established:** If the wound material is too active to work with safely in the coaching frame — if every Phase 2 session is producing significant destabilization — pause coaching. Continue only when the therapist confirms the client is stable enough for the kind of formation excavation Phase 2 requires.

**When coaching should simply stop:** If the client is in active crisis, actively suicidal, in a severe depressive episode, or presenting with symptoms that require clinical stabilization — coaching stops. Not pauses. Stops. The relationship can resume in a future season when the client is stable. This is not abandonment. It is clarity about what each relationship is for.

## 7. Documentation Requirements

Maintain a brief record for any session where the following occur:
- Crisis material surfaces (suicidal ideation, self-harm disclosure, abuse disclosure, acute trauma response)
- A referral is made or recommended
- The scope of practice limit is reached and named

**What to document:**
- Date and time of session
- What was disclosed, in the client's approximate words
- Your assessment at the time
- Action taken (grounding, referral, resource provided, session extended)
- Client's response to your action
- What you agreed the client would do next
- Follow-up needed

**Why documentation matters:** If harm occurs and your response is questioned, documentation demonstrates that you identified the issue, responded appropriately, and took action to connect the client with appropriate care. Documentation also helps you track pattern across sessions — a client whose crisis disclosures are escalating is telling you something important.

**Storage:** Keep crisis documentation separately from Session Growth Notes. It should be accessible but not embedded in the general coaching record. Maintain confidentiality standards consistent with your coaching ethics agreement.

---

## 8. After a Crisis Session — Coach Care

A session where significant crisis material surfaced is not a routine session. Do not treat it like one.

**Immediately after:**
- Complete the documentation while it is fresh
- Do not book another client session back-to-back with a crisis session if avoidable
- Check in with yourself: What is your somatic state? What did the session activate in you?

**Within 24 hours:**
- Contact a supervisor, mentor coach, or trusted peer for debriefing — not to process the client's content, but to process your own response to it
- If you have ongoing supervision, bring this session to your next session

**If the session activated your own material:**
Wound mapping work touches the coach's own wounds. A client's suicidal ideation, abuse history, or spiritual wounding may land in places in you that need care. Name it. Get supervision. The coach who absorbs crisis material without processing it carries it into the next client.

**The theology of limitation:**
You are not the only resource this person has. You are not responsible for outcomes you could not control. You are responsible for recognizing your limits, responding with care, connecting the client to appropriate help, and documenting that you did so. The rest belongs to God. This is not a convenient theology — it is a true one. And it is the theology that allows a coach to keep showing up for wounded people without being destroyed by the weight of what they carry.

## 9. Know Before You Need It — Preparation Checklist

Before your first client engagement:

- [ ] Locate your nearest mental health crisis center and save the address
- [ ] Save 988, Crisis Text Line (741741), and the National DV Hotline in your phone
- [ ] Identify 3-5 therapists in your area you can refer to confidently — ideally including at least one who works with trauma, one who works with depression/anxiety, and one who is faith-integrated
- [ ] Know your state's mandated reporter laws as they apply to life coaches
- [ ] Complete QPR (Question, Persuade, Refer) training — free certification available at qprinstitute.com; takes approximately one hour; teaches you to ask about suicidal ideation competently and without escalating risk. qprinstitute.com for 1 hour training. 
- [ ] Include scope of practice language in your coaching agreement — clients should know before session 1 that coaching is not therapy and that you may recommend referral to clinical care

*QPR training is not optional. Complete it before your first engagement. One hour of training changes your capacity to respond to a suicidal client from anxious guessing to grounded competence.*
