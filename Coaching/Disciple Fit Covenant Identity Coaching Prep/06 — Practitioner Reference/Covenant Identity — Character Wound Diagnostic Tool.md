# Covenant Identity — Character Wound Diagnostic Tool
*Practitioner Reference — secondary diagnostic layer for identifying character wound type and deepening the false identity picture*

*Related: [[Covenant Identity — Whole-Person Identity Framework]]; [[Covenant Identity — Affections Discovery — In-Session Protocol]]; [[Covenant Identity — Diagnostic Lens Transition Logic]]*

---

## How to Use This Tool

**This is a secondary diagnostic layer — not a primary frame.** Use it after affections discovery has produced a specific false identity picture. Type identification deepens that picture; it does not replace or precede affections work. Used early, this tool produces confirmation bias — the coach types the client and then reads all subsequent data through that lens.

**Type identification is a provisional hypothesis, not a diagnosis.** Hold it lightly until multiple behavioral, story, and emotional signals confirm it across sessions. Behavioral markers in sessions 1-2 are often managed presentations — the type becomes clearest when the coaching relationship has enough trust to produce unguarded moments.

**Wound and sin are both present in every type.** Each pattern began as a survival response to harm — and has been cultivated through repeated choice until it operates automatically. The coach who reads identity distortion as exclusively wound-produced will miss the moral agency dimension. The coach who reads it as exclusively sinful will miss the compassion the wound requires. Both are true.

**God-image data takes time.** The God-image distortions listed below typically become visible in sessions 2-3, not session 1. They confirm type identification — they rarely initiate it. Hold them as confirmatory, not primary.

## The Three Types

---

## The Warrior

### Primary Wound and Core Shame

**Wound:** Betrayal — someone who should have protected, didn't. The person responsible for the Warrior's safety either failed them or actively harmed them. The lesson embedded: *no one can be trusted to protect me; I must be strong enough to protect myself.*

**Core shame:** *"I am weak."* The Warrior's false identity is built entirely against this shame statement. Strength, control, and self-sufficiency are not character flaws in isolation — they are the Warrior's defense against the one thing they cannot afford to be.

**Wound-sin note:** The survival response to betrayal — control, self-reliance, dominance — has been reinforced across decades of choice and reward. The Warrior has often been successful precisely because of these patterns. What began as protection has become identity. Both the wound and the cultivated disposition require the coach's attention.

---

### In-Session Behavioral Markers

- Controls the pace and direction of conversation — redirects when discussion approaches vulnerability
- Tests the coach's limits early — may probe, challenge, or lightly dominate to assess whether the coach can hold the field
- Language of strength, competence, and action — verbs over feelings; doing over experiencing
- Minimizes or intellectualizes emotional content — moves quickly from feeling to analysis
- Produces solutions before the coach finishes asking the question
- Warm and engaging surface with strategic distance underneath — relationally skilled but not relationally open
- Physical: upright, controlled presentation; intensity when challenged; rarely physically collapses

---

### Story Patterns

- Stories emphasize what the Warrior did, overcame, or managed — they are always the agent; passivity is absent or brief
- Others in the story are weak, unreliable, or incompetent — the Warrior alone can be counted on
- Betrayal events are described with controlled anger or flatly minimized — *"It is what it is"; "I dealt with it"*
- Genuine need or receiving help rarely appears — or is framed as a temporary exception they recovered from quickly
- God appears as someone who needed them to handle it — or who failed to show up when it mattered most

---

### Emotional Signature

- Chronic low-grade anger or contempt — often presented as realism, directness, or frustration rather than named as anger
- Contempt is the most diagnostic emotion — directed at weakness in self or others; a primary tell
- Grief is largely inaccessible — surfaces as hardness or impatience rather than sadness
- Fear is present beneath the surface but carries its own shame — the shame of being afraid is greater than the fear itself
- Pride functions as a lid on shame — *"I've handled worse"* keeps the shame of weakness from surfacing

---

### God-Image Distortion

- God as demanding commander — relationship is based on competence and performance, just a different category of performance than the False Noble's
- OR God as unreliable protector — theologically trusted in principle, but functionally the one who didn't show up at the critical moment
- Prayer tends toward reporting and requesting — not toward receiving, resting, or being held
- God is respected but not depended on — the Warrior admires God but manages their life without expecting God to come through

---

### False Self vs. Refined False Self

**Raw false self:** Overt control, pushback, dominance in session — the coach feels managed or challenged.

**Refined false self:** The mature, self-aware leader. The Warrior who has done work on themselves can name their control patterns, acknowledge their wound history, and present with composure and self-insight. The control is still operative — it has simply become more socially acceptable. The coach error is reading self-awareness as healing.

**Distinguishing marker:** The refined Warrior can describe their wound. What they cannot do is receive care without deflecting it back toward competence or reframing it as strength. Watch for the pivot — from receiving to performing, from vulnerability to insight. The pivot is the false self reasserting itself.

---

### True Self Emergence Signals

- Acknowledges need without immediately pivoting to how they are handling it
- Allows grief to surface without shutting it down — sits in it briefly before moving on
- Protects or advocates for another person from a posture of genuine care rather than control
- Receives something from the coach — an observation, an affirmation — without deflecting or immediately demonstrating competence
- Sits with uncertainty without generating a solution

*These are moments, not sustained states. When they appear, name them without over-explaining. Let the Warrior notice they just did something without the false self framework.*

*For individualized true self work — who this person is in Christ, not just the redeemed wound — bring to Stage 3 (Covenant Truth).*

---

### Coaching Implications

- The Warrior needs a coach who is neither intimidated nor dominant — someone who holds the field without competing for it. A coach the Warrior can run over gives them nothing to encounter. A coach who pushes back aggressively triggers the wound. The non-anxious, non-reactive presence is the formation field.
- Direct and honest feedback, not softened — but not escalated. The Warrior can receive directness; they cannot receive what they perceive as weakness in the coach.
- Invite grief before inviting change — the Warrior is more ready to change behavior than to grieve what betrayal cost them. The grief is the deeper work.
- The coaching relationship is the Warrior's primary formation context — being known without being controlled is the thing they have never experienced.

---

### Clinical Referral Triggers

Refer when:
- Chronic anger is destabilizing relationships — job loss, marriage threat, legal consequences
- Controlling behavior is affecting others' wellbeing — not just personal patterns
- History of violence or active aggression (verbal or physical) toward others
- No coaching move produces any movement across multiple sessions — rigidity at this level may indicate structural clinical work is needed

## The Hermit

### Primary Wound and Core Shame

**Wound:** Terror — exposed to threat or harm without protection. The person responsible for the Hermit's safety was absent, overwhelmed, or unable to provide safety. The lesson embedded: *the world is not safe; I am safer invisible than exposed.*

**Core shame:** *"I am too much / I am unwanted."* The Hermit's false identity is organized around minimizing presence — taking up less space, needing less, wanting less, being less visible — so that the threat of being too much and therefore unwanted never has to be tested again.

**Wound-sin note:** The withdrawal that began as survival has become a cultivated practice of self-erasure. The Hermit has often been rewarded for compliance, low maintenance, and not being difficult — which reinforces invisibility as identity. Secondary gains (safety, no exposure, no risk of rejection) actively maintain the pattern. Both the wound and the cultivated disposition are present.

---

### In-Session Behavioral Markers

- Minimizes and qualifies everything — *"I don't know," "maybe," "it's probably nothing," "I could be wrong"*
- Physically smaller — may fold in, reduce eye contact, speak quietly or trail off
- Defers to the coach readily — agrees without resistance, rarely contradicts or corrects
- Apologizes for taking up time or for responses being inadequate
- Difficult to locate their actual opinion — says what they sense the coach wants to hear
- Strong internal experience, minimal external expression — the interior life is richer than what surfaces
- Emotional presence is muted — not flat, but suppressed; the feeling is there, under pressure

---

### Story Patterns

- The Hermit is frequently absent from their own story — events happen; they survive, endure, or accommodate
- Others in the story are large; the Hermit is small, peripheral, managing
- The wound is consistently minimized — *"It wasn't that bad," "other people have it much worse"*
- Genuine desires are rarely named — the Hermit has often stopped knowing what they want, having suppressed wanting as a protection against disappointment
- God appears as abstract, generally loving but not personally present or specifically for them

---

### Emotional Signature

- Sadness and loneliness are the deepest register — rarely surfaced without invitation; often just below the surface
- Shame about shame — embarrassed by their own emotional experience, apologizes for having feelings
- Fear presented as uncertainty and minimizing — not named as fear, but functionally organizing everything
- Anger is deeply suppressed — its surfacing is a healing signal, not a problem
- Moments of surprising clarity or directness — these are true self breaks, not behavioral noise

---

### God-Image Distortion

- God as large and distant — present in principle, not personally near or specifically for them
- *"I know God loves me, but..."* — the universal love is intellectually held; the particular love is not felt
- Prayer is thin and apologetic — doesn't want to bother God; doesn't believe their needs warrant God's attention
- God is described as caring about others more specifically than about them
- The incarnation — God personally seeking, finding, and naming them — has not yet become experientially real

---

### False Self vs. Refined False Self

**Raw false self:** Full withdrawal, minimal engagement, unable to sustain presence in session — the coach must work to keep contact.

**Refined false self:** The humble, unassuming servant. Self-deprecation repackaged as godly humility; invisibility framed as spiritual virtue — *"I just want to serve; I don't need to be seen."* The coach error is reading genuine humility into what is actually self-erasure.

**Distinguishing marker:** Genuine humility can receive affirmation and delight in being seen without distress. The refined Hermit false self deflects affirmation compulsively — being seen is still threatening even when framed in spiritual language. Watch the response to being directly affirmed. Deflection, minimizing, or redirecting the affirmation to someone else are the false self markers.

---

### True Self Emergence Signals

- A moment of unguarded directness — an opinion stated without immediate qualification
- Anger that surfaces without instant apology or retraction
- A desire named without immediately dismissing it as selfish, unimportant, or too much
- Genuine laughter or delight — fully present, not self-monitored
- Receiving the coach's affirmation without deflecting — allowing being seen to land as good

*These moments are often brief and the Hermit may not notice them. Name them specifically and without pressure. The Hermit needs to discover their own presence is not a problem.*

*For individualized true self work — who this person is in Christ, specifically sought and named — bring to Stage 3 (Covenant Truth).*

---

### Coaching Implications

- The Hermit needs the coach to be curious about them specifically — not their problem, not their situation, but *them as a person*. Being sought is the formation experience the Hermit has not had.
- Do not let the Hermit disappear in session — gently and persistently bring them back to their own experience. *"I notice you moved away from yourself there. Come back — what were you actually feeling?"*
- Receive what they offer without immediately asking for more — the Hermit needs to discover that their small disclosures land before they can risk larger ones.
- Be the person in the room most convinced that this client matters. That posture is not a technique — it is the intervention.
- Do not rush their pace. The Hermit's slowness is not resistance; it is the cost of safety being built.

---

### Clinical Referral Triggers

Refer when:
- Withdrawal is severe enough to impair functioning — unable to maintain employment, relationships, or basic self-care
- Social isolation is entrenched — coaching is the only active relationship
- Depressive presentation — flat affect, hopelessness, inability to imagine a future
- Trauma history is unprocessed and actively destabilizing current functioning
- Any history of self-harm or passive suicidal ideation

## The False Noble

### Primary Wound and Core Shame

**Wound:** Ambivalence — inconsistent or conditional care; loved, but not for who they are. The love available was real but attached to performance, achievement, or being a certain way. The lesson embedded: *I am loved for what I produce, not for who I am; I must keep producing.*

**Core shame:** *"I am not enough."* The False Noble's false identity is organized around closing the gap between who they are and who they need to be in order to secure love. Achievement, growth, and impression management are not vanity — they are the structure that holds the shame of inadequacy at bay.

**Wound-sin note:** Performance as survival has become performance as identity — and has often been massively rewarded. The False Noble is frequently the most visibly successful person in the room. The pattern has been reinforced by institutions, relationships, and faith communities that reward output. Cultivated pride and self-justification operate alongside the wound. Both are present.

---

### In-Session Behavioral Markers

- Manages the coach's impression from the opening minutes — presents as self-aware, growth-oriented, relationally engaged
- Uses psychological and spiritual vocabulary fluently — sounds insightful about their own patterns
- Produces good answers — has internalized what a mature coaching client looks like and performs it
- Hard to get beneath the managed presentation — the surface is warm, capable, and relationally skilled
- Rarely discloses something without having pre-processed it into insight — raw, unpackaged experience is scarce
- The coach may feel unusually positive about this client early — this is a diagnostic signal, not a confirmation of health
- When not being perceived positively, the False Noble works harder — not less

---

### Story Patterns

- Stories often arrive with a redemptive arc already attached — experiences have been pre-processed into lessons before the session
- Achievement and growth appear frequently; failure is acknowledged but reframed quickly
- Relationships in the story are described partly in terms of how others perceive them
- The wound — inconsistent love — is minimized or spiritualized (*"It made me who I am"; "I've forgiven that"*)
- God appears as someone they are trying to please or impress — the relationship is described in terms of progress, not rest

---

### Emotional Signature

- Anxiety is the primary register — specifically the performance variety: fear of being found inadequate, exposed, or not enough
- Shame is present but processed rapidly — the False Noble rarely sits in shame long enough to feel it; it gets repackaged into insight or growth orientation
- Positive affect around growth and insight — this excitement is real, but also serves the performance
- Genuine confusion, lostness, or not-knowing rarely surfaces — these feel too exposed, too far from competent
- The absence of ordinary, unredeemed failure — every difficulty arrives with a lesson attached

---

### God-Image Distortion

- God as a standard-setter who is pleased by growth and quietly disappointed by stagnation
- Relationship with God described in terms of what they are pursuing, becoming, or doing — not in terms of being received
- Prayer is progress-oriented — reporting, asking for help to improve, committing to growth
- Grace is intellectually embraced and experientially inaccessible — *"I know it's not about performance, but..."*
- God's love is experienced as continuous on ongoing growth — not yet unconditionally present regardless of output

---

### False Self vs. Refined False Self

**Raw false self:** Obvious impression management — the client is visibly working to be perceived well, adjusting in real time to coach feedback.

**Refined false self:** The spiritually mature, growth-oriented disciple. Genuine theological fluency. Visible humility — sometimes including explicit acknowledgment of their performance patterns. The coach error is reading spiritual sophistication as spiritual maturity. Self-awareness that is itself packaged and presented is still performance.

**Distinguishing marker:** The refined False Noble can talk about their performance patterns insightfully. What they cannot do is be *unproductive* in session — truly present without growing, without insight, without the session moving somewhere. If genuine rest is impossible, if every silence must be filled with progress, the false self is still operative regardless of vocabulary. Watch for whether the client can simply be, without the session becoming a growth project.

---

### True Self Emergence Signals

- Expressing genuine confusion or not-knowing without immediately resolving it
- Receiving the coaching relationship as something other than an investment in becoming better
- A moment of play or levity that is not managed or strategic — unselfconscious lightness
- Acknowledging ordinary failure without attaching a lesson — just the failure, sitting in it unredeemed
- Receiving the coach's care without deflecting it back into growth language

*These moments often look like nothing to the client — "we didn't get much done today." They are frequently the most formative sessions for a False Noble.*

*For individualized true self work — the person received and loved as they are, not as they are becoming — bring to Stage 3 (Covenant Truth).*

---

### Coaching Implications

- The False Noble's primary formation need is to be received as they are — not as they are becoming. The coach who rewards insight and growth validates the false self. The coach who is equally present and warm regardless of session productivity disrupts it.
- Resist the pull to make every session productive — name it when you sense the session becoming a performance: *"I notice we're making good progress today. I wonder if we could just sit here for a minute without it going anywhere."*
- Do not reward insight — receive the person behind the packaging. *"That's a well-formed observation. What's underneath it before the lesson?"*
- Name the performance when you see it — as observation, not accusation. The False Noble can hear it because they already know it; they need someone who names it without shame and without being impressed by the self-awareness.
- The coach's consistent, non-contingent regard — unchanged whether the session is insightful or flat — is the intervention.

---

### Clinical Referral Triggers

Refer when:
- Anxiety is severe enough to impair functioning — panic attacks, inability to tolerate uncertainty, somatic symptoms
- Burnout or performance collapse — presenting with depressive features after sustained high-output periods
- Perfectionism entrenched enough that no session produces movement across multiple weeks
- Disordered eating or body-image distortion connected to the not-enough shame structure

## Warrior vs. False Noble — Early Session Distinguishing Tells

These two types are externally similar in early sessions: both high-functioning, both relationally capable, both achieving, both presenting with composure. The distinction is clearest when something they value is threatened or questioned.

| Situation | Warrior Response | False Noble Response |
|---|---|---|
| Coach offers gentle challenge | Pushes back, clarifies, may counter | Receives it quickly, produces insight, re-establishes competence |
| Session goes poorly | May express frustration; maintains control | Works harder; increases engagement and performance |
| Coach names a pattern | May resist or deflect immediately | Pre-empts the naming — *"I know I do this because..."* |
| Something the client values is criticized | Defends it directly | Absorbs and recalibrates to manage impression |
| Coach is unexpectedly warm | Receives briefly, redirects to task | Uses it as relational fuel; increases investment |

**The diagnostic key:** Under pressure, the Warrior pushes *out* — against. The False Noble moves *toward* — performs more skillfully. The Warrior tightens and resists; the False Noble produces more. This distinction usually becomes visible by session 2 when the coaching relationship has enough substance for the threat response to surface.

## Blended Presentations

Types are archetypes, not discrete categories. Real clients blend — typically with a primary type that organizes the default false self, and a secondary type that surfaces under increased pressure or in specific relational contexts.

**Warrior primary / False Noble secondary**
High-functioning leader who controls most contexts but performs for specific significant others — a spouse, mentor, parent figure, or God. Control is the default; performance emerges when the control strategy isn't available or isn't producing the security needed.

**False Noble primary / Hermit secondary**
The performing person who withdraws completely under sufficient exposure or failure. The performance holds until the shame threshold is crossed — then the Hermit response activates. May appear as a sudden personality shift under acute stress or public failure.

**Hermit primary / Warrior secondary**
The quiet, minimizing person who becomes surprisingly direct or aggressive when their safety or boundaries are violated. Withdrawal is the default; the Warrior response surfaces when the hiding strategy is threatened or fails to protect.

**Coach diagnostic move:** When a blend is suspected, ask what happens under the client's specific stressor — or watch what happens when the coaching itself applies pressure. The secondary type surfaces most clearly when the primary strategy is not working.

## How Type Informs Affections Questioning

Once a type is identified or suspected, these affections probes are most likely to reach the defended dependency. These complement the [[Covenant Identity — Affections Discovery — In-Session Protocol|Affections Discovery Protocol]] — they are not a replacement for it.

| Type | Affections most likely orbit | Priority probe |
|---|---|---|
| **Warrior** | Control, strength, self-reliance | *"What would you need in order to feel safe enough to not be in control?"* |
| **Hermit** | Safety, invisibility, not being too much | *"What would you need to believe about yourself to take up more space in your own life?"* |
| **False Noble** | Approval, achievement, being seen as growing or good | *"What would it mean to stop becoming for a season — to simply be, not improve?"* |

## Document Provenance

**Why this exists:** Affections discovery identifies *what* a client is trusting for security, significance, or identity — but not *why* the false identity has taken its particular shape, *what* to expect when you name it, or *how* the coaching relationship needs to be configured to work with this specific person. The character wound type fills that gap: it explains how the wound experience shaped the false identity, what the refined false self looks like in session (distinct from the obvious version), and what the true self emergence looks like when formation is actually occurring.

**Governing question:** How does the type of wound a client experienced shape the particular form their false identity takes, the way they present in session, what they need from the coaching relationship, and what signals genuine formation versus a more sophisticated performance of health?

**Design decisions:**

- *Secondary diagnostic layer — stated explicitly in the opening paragraph:* The tool's most important design statement is in the first line: "This is a secondary diagnostic layer — not a primary frame. Use it after affections discovery has produced a specific false identity picture." Reason: wound typing before affections work produces confirmation bias. The coach reads all subsequent client behavior through the typed lens rather than following the client's own material. The label forecloses the listening. It must deepen an already-established picture, not precede it.

- *False Self vs. Refined False Self section included per type:* The distinction between the obvious false self and the socially sophisticated version is a specific design addition not present in most typing frameworks. Reason: most coaching clients have already done some form of self-awareness work. They are likely to present the refined version — which looks entirely different from the raw version. The Warrior who can talk about their wound with composure, the Hermit whose invisibility is framed as humility, the False Noble whose insight is itself impressively packaged — these can all be misread as health. Each type entry includes the specific distinguishing marker that differentiates authentic movement from refined self-presentation.

- *True Self Emergence Signals per type:* Formation moments are often brief, unannounced, and easily missed — especially with refined false selves who can recover the managed presentation within seconds. The signals (what the true self actually looks like when it breaks through in each type) allow the coach to recognize and name the moment before the false self reasserts. This section was included because the absence of it in the original design meant formation moments went unnamed.

- *Warrior/False Noble distinguishing tells as a standalone table:* These two types are nearly identical in early sessions — both high-functioning, relationally skilled, composed. The tells (Warrior pushes back against challenge; False Noble performs harder in response to the same challenge) were added after it became clear that the distinction could not be made from the type descriptions alone without a direct comparison. The table gives the practitioner a single observable: how does this client respond when the coaching itself applies pressure?

- *Blended presentations included:* Real clients blend types. A tool with only pure archetypes produces a practitioner who forces single-type identification onto what is actually a primary/secondary structure. The three common blends (Warrior/False Noble; False Noble/Hermit; Hermit/Warrior) are included with their specific diagnostic move: what happens under the client's specific stressor is what activates the secondary type.

**Scope boundary:** These wound types are descriptive archetypes for coaching use — not formal psychological diagnoses, personality disorder categories, or assessment instruments. Clinical presentations that superficially resemble these types (narcissistic PD in Warrior/False Noble range; avoidant PD in Hermit range; borderline features in Fearful-Avoidant presentations) require clinical assessment, not coaching deepening. The referral triggers per type mark where coaching scope ends. The tool provides a working frame for formation work with functioning adults — it is not a clinical instrument.

**Client summary:** Practitioner-only reference. Not shared with clients.

**Contested ground:** The three wound types (Warrior, Hermit, False Noble) are derived from Allender's clinical-pastoral framework — they are not empirically validated diagnostic categories with psychometric validation or established inter-rater reliability. They are archetypes organized for coaching utility. The mapping between wound types and attachment classifications is a structural inference (Warrior ↔ dismissing-avoidant; Hermit ↔ fearful-avoidant; False Noble ↔ anxious-preoccupied) that is defensible and convergent with clinical observation but not formally tested as a validity claim. Any peer reviewing this tool from a clinical or research psychology background should understand these categories as working pastoral-clinical constructs, not as formal diagnostic criteria.
