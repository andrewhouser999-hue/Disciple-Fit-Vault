## Governing Framework

All components documented in this reference operate within the five-layer mechanism that governs Covenant Identity Coaching:

1. **Spirit as primary agent** — The coach, the tool, and the practitioner create conditions. The Spirit produces transformation. No component of *Secured in Father* is a transformation mechanism — each is a condition-creating mechanism. This distinction is load-bearing. A practitioner who understands each component's mechanism without holding this layer will inadvertently present tools as causes rather than conditions — generating tool dependence, one of the three failure modes this practice is designed to prevent.
2. **Coaching relationship creates formation context** — For self-coaching use (the primary design context of this document), this layer is the practitioner's own formed posture. For client use, the coaching relationship is the relational container within which the tool operates. The tool does not replace the container.
3. **Diagnostic work surfaces false identity** — The Self-Diagnostic and Pattern Descriptions in *Secured in Father* are a preparatory diagnostic layer: they name the active pattern before encounter. They do not constitute the full diagnostic work of the model, which requires the Affections Discovery Protocol and Character Wound Diagnostic.
4. **Covenant truth applied and received** — The Evidence Anchors and Encounter Prompts are the primary covenant-truth layer: Scripture as semantic anchor, episodic memory as felt grounding, encounter as the relational reception point.
5. **Between-session disciplines sustain and deepen the work** — *Secured in Father* is a between-session formation discipline. It occupies this layer. It is not designed as a primary in-session coaching intervention, and its components assume a self-regulated, prepared user.

**Scope note — solo practice ceiling:** The Component Evidence Map documents the mechanism each component is designed to activate. Practitioners must hold the honest ceiling alongside these claims: solo practice, however well-designed, primarily shifts explicit attachment representations (how the user thinks about God and their patterns) and somatic readiness (nervous system priming for encounter). Lasting reorganization of automatic attachment responses — the implicit layer — requires repeated felt experience of a responsive other. The mechanism of that reorganization in encounter with God is theologically grounded (Spirit-mediated attunement, union with Christ) and empirically untested as a distinct neurological pathway. The tools create conditions. The Spirit produces whatever implicit reorganization occurs. This ceiling is not a design flaw — it is an honest description of what formation disciplines accomplish and what only the Spirit's work in sustained encounter can accomplish.

## I. Component Evidence Map

Each component of *Secured in Father* is documented below with: empirical and/or theological basis, mechanism description, design-for-effect, and evidence level per the standard scale:

- **Established** — meta-analyses, RCTs, or strong clinical consensus
- **Supported** — solid studies, emerging consensus
- **Theoretical** — plausible, not yet well-studied
- **Expert opinion** — respected voice, limited empirical backing

### 1. Entry Ritual

**The component:** Sit upright. Feet flat. Hands open. Three slow breaths. Name aloud: *"I am here. He is here. Something in me does not yet fully believe it. I am bringing that too."*

**Empirical basis:**

- Porges, S. W. (2011). *The Polyvagal Theory: Neurophysiological Foundations of Emotions, Attachment, Communication, and Self-Regulation.* Norton. — The ventral vagal complex (VVC) governs social engagement. Vocalization, intentional breath, and upright posture activate the VVC. The VVC is the neurological state required for relational encounter — not passive relaxation, but active social readiness. Without VVC activation, the nervous system processes relational material through either sympathetic activation (anxiety/fight-flight) or dorsal vagal shutdown (dissociation/freeze).
- Van der Kolk, B. (2014). *The Body Keeps the Score.* Viking. — Attachment wounds and trauma are stored in the body, limbic system, and brainstem — below the level of language and prefrontal processing. Narrative and cognitive interventions alone cannot access these layers. Somatic engagement is not supplemental; it targets the correct neurological storage location.
- Levine, P. A. (2010). *In an Unspoken Voice: How the Body Releases Trauma and Restores Goodness.* North Atlantic Books. — Titrated somatic engagement (small, regulated contact with difficult material) reorganizes the nervous system's automatic responses without retraumatization.

**Mechanism:** Each element of the ritual targets a specific physiological function. Extended exhale in three-breath sequence activates the vagal brake (parasympathetic downregulation of arousal — Porges). Feet flat on floor is a somatic orienting technique (grounding — Levine). Hands open shifts postural cue from guarding/gripping to receiving. Speaking the statement aloud engages the social engagement system, not just the internal monologue system.

**Design for effect:** Shift the nervous system from sympathetic or dorsal vagal state toward ventral vagal social engagement before cognitive work begins. Without this shift, the pattern descriptions are processed as information. With this shift, they have access to the implicit memory system — the layer where attachment patterns actually live.

**Load-bearing:** Yes. Removing or shortening the ritual is not a stylistic simplification — it disconnects the document's cognitive layer from the implicit layer it is trying to reach. The ritual is not a warm-up. It is targeting the correct system.

**Evidence level:** Supported (polyvagal theory clinical applications — widely referenced and clinically influential; specific VVC anatomical claims remain contested in neuroscience literature); Established (body-based storage of attachment wounds — Van der Kolk, Levine); Supported (specific ritual elements as somatic mechanism).

### 2. Self-Diagnostic

**The component:** Three-pattern identification (Anxious/Preoccupied, Dismissing-Avoidant, Fearful-Avoidant) plus three response postures (insecurity found, secure attachment found, growth from past insecurity recognized).

**Empirical basis:**

- Ainsworth, M. D. S., Blehar, M. C., Waters, E., & Wall, S. (1978). *Patterns of Attachment: A Psychological Study of the Strange Situation.* Erlbaum. — Original three-pattern taxonomy: secure, anxious/ambivalent, avoidant. The taxonomy is the foundational classification behind all adult attachment research.
- Main, M., & Solomon, J. (1990). "Procedures for identifying infants as disorganized/disoriented during the Ainsworth Strange Situation." In M. T. Greenberg, D. Cicchetti, & E. M. Cummings (Eds.), *Attachment in the Preschool Years.* Chicago. — Disorganized/fearful-avoidant as fourth pattern. Mechanism: caregivers who are simultaneously a source of fear and a source of comfort produce contradictory attachment activation — approach and flight activate simultaneously, producing collapse or dissociation.
- Kirkpatrick, L. A. (1994). "The role of attachment in religious belief and behavior." In K. Bartholomew & D. Perlman (Eds.), *Advances in Personal Relationships, Vol. 5.* Jessica Kingsley. — Correspondence Hypothesis: adult attachment style with humans predicts the functional quality of relationship with God. Empirically supported across multiple study designs.
- Kirkpatrick, L. A. (2005). *Attachment, Evolution, and the Psychology of Religion.* Guilford. — Full treatment of the Correspondence Hypothesis and its limits. Compensation Hypothesis (some individuals show divergence between human and God-attachment) is addressed in the document's Contested Ground section.

**Mechanism:** Without naming the active pattern before engagement, the pattern governs how the document is read. An avoidant reader processes the content efficiently and cognitively, avoiding emotional contact. An anxious reader over-identifies with every section. A fearful-avoidant reader oscillates between engagement and withdrawal mid-section. Naming the pattern before entry interrupts the default processing mode.

**Design note — "not fixed categories":** The statement that patterns shift by season and circumstance is load-bearing. If users read the Self-Diagnostic as assigning a fixed identity, it functions contrary to the document's formation goals — creating a new label rather than a current lens. The language "which pattern is active today" enforces situational use.

**Design note — disorganized presentation within the Fearful-Avoidant category:** The Self-Diagnostic describes Fearful-Avoidant as conscious oscillation — "I want to be near Him and something in me pulls back at the same time." This captures the phenomenological experience of the fearful-avoidant adult, but misses the more disorganized presentation: freeze, blank, impossible, or numb approach with no interior movement in either direction. The distinction matters for self-recognition. Fearful-avoidant involves conscious ambivalence about approach; disorganized involves collapse of all approach strategies — the person doesn't experience it as wanting-and-fearing but as approach-being-unavailable. A person with a disorganized history may read the oscillation description and conclude the document doesn't apply, when in fact the *Disorganized* encounter prompts throughout are written precisely for them. The Self-Diagnostic now includes a partial-version note under Fearful-Avoidant that catches freeze/blank/impossible presentations and routes them to the *Disorganized* encounter prompts. Disorganized is not added as a fourth category (scope boundary; self-diagnostic unreliability in this population; referral threshold already addresses clinical-level presentations) — instead the existing Fearful-Avoidant category is extended to name the disorganized phenomenology as a variant presentation.

**Design note — three response postures:** Added to complete the Self-Diagnostic's instruction set. Without them, the diagnostic produces a named pattern but no guidance for what to do with it. The three postures cover the full range of outcomes: insecurity identified, secure attachment found (the document still applies — as formation deepening rather than repair), and growth from a past insecurity recognized (receive it; be open to future insecurity as invitation to a deeper layer of the same work).

**Design for effect:** The reader enters each section with a named lens rather than an unnamed default. The pattern is observable before it becomes operative.

**Evidence level:** Established (Ainsworth taxonomy, Main's disorganized category); Supported (Kirkpatrick's Correspondence Hypothesis).

### 3. Pattern Descriptions and Partial-Version Notes

**The component:** For each of the 10 dimensions, three primary pattern descriptions (Anxious/Preoccupied, Dismissing-Avoidant, Fearful-Avoidant) followed by italicized self-perception gap notes for each pattern.

**Empirical basis:**

- Main, M. (1985). *Adult Attachment Interview Protocol.* Unpublished manuscript. University of California, Berkeley. — The AAI assesses attachment patterns through narrative coherence, not content. Clinical portraits describe high-amplitude, fully-developed presentations. The research also reveals moderate and partial presentations that do not match clinical portraits.
- Hesse, E. (2008). "The Adult Attachment Interview: Protocol, method of analysis, and empirical studies." In J. Cassidy & P. R. Shaver (Eds.), *Handbook of Attachment: Theory, Research, and Clinical Applications* (2nd ed.). Guilford. — Comprehensive review of AAI findings, including moderate presentations across non-clinical populations. Moderate presentations are the norm, not the exception, in community samples.
- Siegel, D. J. (1999). *The Developing Mind: How Relationships and the Brain Interact to Shape Who We Are.* Guilford. — Window of tolerance. Material describing clinical-level presentations is unusable for people whose experience falls at the moderate edge of the window — they cannot locate themselves in the portrait.

**Mechanism:** Two-tier description (primary paragraph + italicized partial-version note) serves a coaching population where moderate presentations predominate. The italicized note names the disguise — what the pattern feels like from the inside when it is not operating at full amplitude. This is what allows self-recognition in people who would otherwise conclude the section does not apply.

**Critical distinction:** The partial-version note is not a softer version of the same description. It describes a fundamentally different phenomenological experience. The person with moderate dismissing-avoidant attachment does not feel like they are avoiding — the pattern feels like maturity, doctrinal groundedness, or healthy independence. Without naming this disguise, the person reads the primary description, does not recognize themselves, and skips the section. The section applies to them; the primary description simply does not fit their experience of it.

**Design for effect:** Self-recognition across the full range of pattern intensity. A coaching population (non-clinical) encounters this tool at the moderate end. Generic clinical description produces a false negative — "that's not me" — and the encounter work proceeds without the correct lens.

**Evidence level:** Established (AAI findings, window of tolerance); Strong inference (two-tier design as application to non-clinical population).

### 4. Defusion Statements

**The component:** *"I notice the pull to [pattern-specific behavior]. That pull is a pattern. It is not a prophecy about today."* — placed after pattern descriptions, before the encounter prompt.

**Empirical basis:**

- Hayes, S. C., Strosahl, K. D., & Wilson, K. G. (1999). *Acceptance and Commitment Therapy: An Experiential Approach to Behavior Change.* Guilford. — Cognitive defusion: creating distance between the observing self and a thought, urge, or pattern. The mechanism is not challenge (as in CBT's thought records) but observation — "I notice the thought that..." rather than "That thought is wrong." Defusion reduces the behavioral influence of difficult internal events without requiring the person to believe differently first.
- Levin, M. E., Hildebrandt, M. J., Lillis, J., & Hayes, S. C. (2012). "The impact of treatment components suggested by psychological flexibility theory on outcomes in acceptance and commitment therapy." *Behavior Therapy, 43*(4), 741–757. — Meta-analytic support for defusion as an active mechanism in ACT outcomes.
- Neff, K. D. (2011). *Self-Compassion: The Proven Power of Being Kind to Yourself.* William Morrow. — Spoken self-compassionate statements produce stronger physiological effects (heart rate variability, skin conductance response) than internally held equivalents. Vocalization is not incidental.
- Porges (2011) — Vocalization maintains VVC activation through difficult material, sustaining the social engagement state during the defusion statement.

**Mechanism:** The defusion statement is placed at the structural hinge — after the pattern is activated by recognition (pattern descriptions) and before the encounter prompt. At this moment, the pattern is maximally active. Without defusion, the pattern governs the encounter prompt. With defusion, the pattern becomes observable: the reader can see the pull without being inside it.

**Critical distinction — defusion vs. declaration:** The defusion statement is not a claim that the pattern's expectation is false. "I notice the pull to seek repair urgently and not be able to receive it" is not "That fear is unfounded." Both are true simultaneously. Defusion does not require the person to believe differently — it creates enough distance for the encounter to be entered from outside the pattern rather than from within it.

**Three-pattern structure is load-bearing:** Each pattern's pull is genuinely different. A single generic defusion statement ("I notice the pull to avoid this") would not serve an anxious reader who is not avoiding. The three-way differentiation is what allows the defusion to be accurate rather than generic.

**Design for effect:** The pattern moves from operative (governing automatic response) to observable (available for examination). The encounter prompt is then entered from outside the pattern.

**Evidence level:** Established (ACT defusion mechanism in therapist-delivered ACT — Levin et al. citation reflects therapist-delivered outcomes); Supported (self-directed ACT defusion applications — less robust evidence base than therapist-delivered); Supported (vocalization producing stronger physiological response — Neff, Porges).

### 5. Evidence Anchors

**The component:** A Scripture verse followed by a "name one moment" instruction — specifically, a moment of experiential encounter with God that contradicts the pattern's expectation.

**Empirical basis:**

- Ecker, B., Ticic, R., & Hulley, L. (2012). *Unlocking the Emotional Brain: Eliminating Symptoms at Their Roots Using Memory Reconsolidation.* Routledge. — Memory reconsolidation sequence: (1) activate the old emotional learning/implicit expectation, (2) introduce a genuinely contradictory felt experience, (3) allow the mismatch to register at the implicit level. The contradictory experience must be felt, not merely known, for reconsolidation to occur.
- Tulving, E. (1985). "Memory and consciousness." *Canadian Psychology, 26*(1), 1–12. — Episodic memory (specific autobiographical memories with temporal and contextual markers) is distinct from semantic memory (general knowledge, propositions). Only episodic memory activates the felt quality of an experience — the specific "what it was like."

**Mechanism:** The Scripture verse provides semantic/declarative grounding (God is safe, God is responsive, etc.). The "name one moment" instruction activates episodic memory of a specific past encounter that contradicts the pattern's implicit expectation. This is not positive thinking or affirmation. It is retrieving actual experiential data from long-term memory and bringing it into active contact with the implicit expectation the pattern is generating.

**Critical distinction — episodic vs. semantic:** "God is good" is semantic — it is a proposition. "The moment after my conversion when He opened my eyes and I knew I was received, not crushed" is episodic — it carries the felt quality of the experience. Only the episodic activates the felt contradiction required for reconsolidation. A person who affirms "God is my safe haven" without a specific felt memory is engaging their semantic system only. The pattern's implicit expectation remains uncontacted.

**Theological convergence note:** The design of the Evidence Anchor — retrieving specific past experience rather than affirming general propositions — finds a theologically convergent pattern in the biblical use of זָכַר (*zakar* — "remember"). In Psalms and Deuteronomy, *zakar* frequently involves specific past events rather than abstract affirmation: Psalm 77:11 — "I will remember the deeds of the LORD; yes, I will remember your wonders of old." Psalm 22:4–5 — "In you our fathers trusted; they trusted, and you delivered them." The biblical speaker reaches for specific past events when personal experience of God is absent. This parallel is theologically coherent and suggestive — it is not a claim that Tulving's episodic/semantic distinction maps onto the Hebrew word's full semantic range (*zakar* carries communal, covenantal, and divine dimensions that exceed the psychological category), and it is not a derivation of the Evidence Anchor's design from exegesis. The parallel establishes theological convergence, not warrant.

**Design for effect:** The episodic memory and the pattern's implicit expectation are simultaneously active — the condition required for reconsolidation. Whether reconsolidation occurs depends on the quality of felt contact in the specific session. The Evidence Anchor creates the necessary condition; it does not guarantee the outcome.

**Evidence level:** Established (episodic vs. semantic memory distinction — Tulving); Supported (memory reconsolidation as mechanism in therapist-facilitated clinical contexts — Ecker; application to solo self-practice is Theoretical — see Governing Framework Scope Note); Strong inference (*zakar* as theologically convergent pattern — suggestive parallel to episodic design, not exegetical warrant for the neuroscientific distinction).

### 6. Somatic Invitations

**The component:** A brief body-awareness instruction placed between the Evidence Anchor and the Encounter Prompt. Locating sensation without labeling or explaining it. Breath directed to the location. A short spoken statement.

**Empirical basis:**

- Levine (2010) — Titrated somatic engagement without flooding. The key distinction: asking "where do you feel it?" risks activating prefrontal labeling. Asking "where in your body — chest, throat, gut?" directs attention to location without inviting conceptual categorization, maintaining somatic contact rather than reactivating cognitive processing.
- Porges (2011) — Interoception (awareness of body state) is a VVC function. Maintaining interoceptive awareness sustains the social engagement state through the transition from cognitive work to encounter.
- Van der Kolk (2014) — Interoceptive awareness is prerequisite to processing implicit/body-stored material. A person who is disconnected from body sensation during encounter is processing in the explicit system only.

**Mechanism:** The Evidence Anchor does primarily cognitive and episodic work — naming, recalling, speaking Scripture. Without a somatic bridge, the transition to the Encounter Prompt is a purely cognitive transition. The Somatic Invitation re-grounds the body before entry into the encounter, maintaining access to the implicit layer through the transition.

**Design logic of specificity:** "Notice where distress lives in your body — not conceptually but in your body" is specific instruction, not generic mindfulness language. "Not conceptually" is a direct instruction to stay below the labeling function. This keeps the somatic contact at the right level.

**Spoken statement:** Speaking a brief statement ("This is what I'm bringing") while maintaining body awareness bridges the somatic and relational layers — the body awareness is named into the relational context (encounter with God), not left as pure sensation.

**Design for effect:** Maintains access to the implicit memory layer through the transition into the Encounter Prompt. Prevents the Encounter Prompt from being processed purely cognitively, which would reduce it to a reflection exercise rather than an encounter.

**Evidence level:** Established (somatic engagement and implicit layer access); Supported (interoception maintaining VVC state).

### 7. Encounter Prompts

**The component:** Three pattern-differentiated prompts (Anxious / Avoidant / Disorganized) for each of the 10 dimensions. Each prompt specifies a practice, a posture, and often a spoken element.

**Empirical basis:**

- Ecker et al. (2012) — The Encounter Prompt is the reconsolidation window. It is the structural point at which the old implicit expectation (activated by the pattern descriptions and partially shifted by defusion) and a new relational experience with God are simultaneously present. This is where lasting implicit change is possible — not guaranteed, but the necessary condition exists.
- Main (1990); Siegel (1999) — Earned security through repeated experience of a responsive other. The encounter with God is proposed as the primary relational corrective. The Siegel mechanism: new relational experience reorganizes implicit attachment representations through the orbitofrontal cortex, which integrates emotional and cognitive processing and is experience-dependent and malleable across the lifespan.

**Mechanism — why three patterns per prompt:** Each insecure pattern needs a fundamentally different kind of contradictory experience to interrupt the implicit expectation.

- *Anxious:* The implicit expectation is that proximity requires effort and that stillness equals abandonment. The corrective is stillness that is not abandonment. Therefore anxious prompts are consistently oriented toward restraint — bring once and stop, sit without re-presenting, practice settled silence. This is the specific experience that contradicts what the anxious pattern expects.
- *Avoidant:* The implicit expectation is that genuine need-expression will rupture the relationship or produce disappointment. The corrective is bringing actual need and discovering the relationship intact. Therefore avoidant prompts consistently orient toward full disclosure — bring the specific thing, resist efficiency, name the raw reality. The discovery that the relationship survives is the contradictory experience.
- *Disorganized:* The implicit expectation is a double bind — approach activates both longing and danger simultaneously. There is no clean corrective because the person cannot remain in full approach-mode long enough to receive it. Therefore disorganized prompts begin with honest naming of the bind itself (*"I want to be near You and I'm afraid at the same time — I'm bringing both"*). This externalizes the conflict rather than requiring resolution before entry.

**Load-bearing:** The three-pattern differentiation is not decoration. A single generic prayer prompt would be processed in the mode of the pattern rather than against it. An anxious reader given a general "bring your need and trust God" prompt will bring their need repeatedly (anxious escalation), not once with trust. The differentiation is what makes the encounter a corrective rather than a repetition.

**Theological warrant:** The encounter prompt rests on the claim that God functions as a genuinely responsive relational other — that the Spirit mediates real attunement in a way that operates on the implicit system, not only the explicit. This is theologically grounded in union with Christ, Spirit indwelling, the incarnation, and the Father's posture in Luke 15 (running before the son finished speaking). On the incarnation specifically: Hebrews 4:15 establishes that Jesus sympathizes with human weakness and understands temptation from the inside — the claim that God thereby engages the human attachment system is a theological application of the incarnation, not a direct exegetical reading of the text. The attachment lens is a modern application of incarnational theology, not an exegetical derivation. This claim is theologically defensible and empirically untested as a distinct neurological mechanism. The distinction must be held — theological warrant is not empirical confirmation.

**Design for effect:** The reconsolidation window — the session point where lasting implicit reorganization is possible. Whether it occurs depends on the quality of felt contact in the session. The prompt creates the condition. It cannot guarantee the experience.

**Evidence level:** Established (Siegel's earned security through relational experience); Supported (reconsolidation logic in therapist-facilitated clinical contexts — Ecker; contested for reliable replication even in clinical settings); Theoretical (solo spiritual practice triggering reconsolidation through God-encounter — untested; see Governing Framework Scope Note); Expert opinion (theological warrant — Ferguson, Billings, Nouwen).

### 8. Repair Protocol

**The component:** Five-step protocol for when the Encounter Prompt produces no felt movement — naming the absence, not interpreting silence, borrowing faith from testimony, committing to return, naming any actual rupture.

**Empirical basis:**

- Siegel, D. J. (1999). *The Developing Mind.* — Repair is the mechanism of secure attachment, not perfect attunement. Ainsworth and Siegel both establish that security is formed through the repeated experience that misattunement can be survived and repaired. A dry encounter is misattunement. The Repair Protocol provides the repair sequence.
- Main, M. (1990) — Earned security specifically involves the internalized expectation that return is possible after rupture. The Repair Protocol is designed to maintain and strengthen this expectation even when felt encounter is absent.
- Pennebaker, J. W. (1997). *Opening Up: The Healing Power of Expressing Emotions.* Guilford. — Sustained expressive engagement with difficult emotional experience (returning repeatedly, naming what is true) produces health benefits and emotional regulation improvement over time. The "keep returning" instruction is not motivational — it has an empirical basis.

**Mechanism of Step 3 — Borrow faith from testimony:** This is not a coping strategy. It is the Psalms' own epistemological move when personal experience fails. Psalm 22:4–5 — *"In you our fathers trusted; they trusted, and you delivered them. To you they cried and were rescued."* The speaker explicitly reaches for the testimony of others when personal experience of God is absent. This is a biblically established epistemological pattern, not an imported technique.

**Mechanism of Step 2 — Do not interpret the silence:** Each pattern offers its own interpretation of a dry encounter (anxious: "He has left"; avoidant: "This is why encounter doesn't work for me"; disorganized: panic/freeze). The pattern's interpretation is itself the implicit expectation — confirming the pattern's prediction. Declining to sign the interpretation keeps the loop open rather than closing it in the pattern's favor.

**Design for effect:** Prevents a dry encounter from functioning as implicit confirmation of the insecure attachment expectation. Without the Repair Protocol, a session that produces no felt movement confirms the pattern: "I came, and the relationship was unresponsive." The Repair Protocol reframes the dry encounter as consistent with secure attachment — responsive others are not perfectly attuned; repair is the mechanism; returning is the declaration that the relationship is not over.

**Evidence level:** Established (Siegel's repair findings); Supported (Pennebaker's returning/sustained engagement); Strong inference (Psalm 22:4–5 as biblical epistemological warrant for Step 3).

## II. Sequencing Rationale — 10 Dimensions

This section answers two distinct questions: **Why these 10 dimensions** (the selection principle — what criteria determined what was included and what was not), and **why this order** (the architectural logic governing the sequence). The two questions have different answers and cannot be collapsed.

---

### Why These 10 Dimensions

The document does not derive the 10 dimensions from a single source. They emerge from two overlapping frameworks placed in deliberate relationship.

**Bowlby's Four Criteria (organizational scaffold):**
Bowlby identified four defining behavioral criteria for attachment: proximity maintenance (seeking closeness to the attachment figure), safe haven (returning to the figure for comfort under threat), secure base (using the figure as ground for exploration), and separation protest (distress when the figure is unavailable or perceived as lost). These four criteria are the functional description of what attachment does. They tell you what a healthy attachment relationship provides — not what obstacles prevent it or what demands test it.

**The limitation of four criteria for this purpose:** Bowlby's four criteria describe behavior. They do not address the inner architecture of obstacles and distortions that prevent insecurely attached people from accessing what attachment provides. A document that organized itself around four behavioral criteria would tell a dismissing-avoidant person that they struggle with "safe haven" — but it would not name the internal experience that makes safe haven inaccessible, or what theological lie operates beneath it, or what distinct distortion each pattern runs in that dimension.

**The expansion logic:** The 10 dimensions elaborate the four criteria into a map of the specific distortion patterns that block access to each — and add dimensions that the four-criteria framework does not contain because it is behavioral, not theological.

| Dimension | Relationship to Bowlby | What it adds |
|---|---|---|
| §1 Safe | Prerequisite to proximity maintenance | Names the specific question: is approach to God dangerous? Addresses before proximity-seeking can occur |
| §2 Seen | Elaborates proximity maintenance | Names the depth question: safe approach ≠ safe exposure; distinguishes behavioral nearness from interior disclosure |
| §3 Shame | No direct Bowlby equivalent | Names the primary internal obstacle to all four criteria — shame operates beneath every other dimension and must be addressed directly, not subsumed |
| §4 Soothed | Safe haven function | Specifies the receiving of distress; focuses on the figure's capacity to hold rather than the person's proximity-seeking |
| §5 Secure Base | Secure base function | Direct correspondence — names the launch-and-return logic that Bowlby identified |
| §6 Contingent Response | Elaborates proximity maintenance | Names the reliability question: is the figure attuned? Does signal reach response? Bowlby implies contingency; this dimension makes it explicit |
| §7 Worth | No direct Bowlby equivalent | Names the identity declaration that underlies all four criteria — you cannot seek proximity, safe haven, or secure base if you believe you are not worth the figure's sustained engagement |
| §8 Lament | Elaborates separation protest | Names what healthy separation protest looks like (protest within the relationship, not against it); separates from the pathological presentation of the same protest |
| §9 Suffering | Extension of safe haven under acute conditions | Addresses the attachment system under existential threat — acute suffering is the sharpest test of whether safe haven holds when circumstances most demand it |
| §10 Repair | Directly addresses separation distress resolution | Names the mechanism of security itself: repair after rupture, not absence of rupture |

**Why not fewer dimensions?** Collapsing dimensions would conflate problems that require different interventions. Safe (§1) and Seen (§2) are not the same problem — a person can approach God safely while remaining completely hidden. Shame (§3) and Worth (§7) are not the same problem — shame is the acute experience of being unacceptable in the moment of approach; worth is the settled identity question about value. Lament (§8) and Suffering (§9) are not the same problem — lament is the relational protocol (can I protest?); suffering is the situational test (does the attachment hold under extreme circumstances?). Collapsing any of these pairs loses the specificity that makes pattern-differentiated intervention possible.

**Why not more dimensions?** The selection criterion is: does this dimension address a distinct obstacle pattern that is not addressed by any other dimension, and does it address a lie about God or about the self in relation to God that each insecure attachment pattern runs in a distinctive way? Dimensions that did not meet both criteria were not included. For example, gratitude, intercession, and listening prayer are all dimensions of a full prayer life — but they are not primarily structured by attachment pattern distortions. They can run within or alongside a secure attachment; they do not define the architecture of obstacles to encounter that this document addresses.

---

### Why This Order

The sequence is not arbitrary. Four architectural zones organize the 10 dimensions, and the progression across zones follows a strict logic: conditions for approach → active relational experience → identity consolidation → stress testing → return mechanism. The zones build on each other, and the zone sequence is load-bearing in ways the within-zone order is mostly not.

---

**Zone 1 — Threshold Conditions for Approach (§1–3)**

These three dimensions address prerequisites — what must be established before any of the attachment capacities can function. Without these three, the relational work in §4–10 is conducted in the dark.

*§1 Safe* comes first because it is the necessary precondition for every other dimension. A person who does not believe approach to God is safe will not engage any other dimension genuinely. The anxious reader scanning for danger signs cannot rest into being seen; the fearful-avoidant reader cannot receive soothing from a figure whose approach simultaneously feels threatening. Safety is the first question — not because it is the only question, but because nothing else can proceed without a basic yes.

*§2 Seen* comes second because being seen is the next question after safety. Approach can be physically safe and relational exposure still dangerous. A person can enter the room and remain completely hidden. The Seen dimension addresses the interior threshold — not just "am I safe to be near?" but "am I safe to be fully known?" This is the depth question that only surfaces once the first question (approach safety) has a preliminary answer.

*§3 Shame* comes third — not fourth, not seventh — because it is, as the document names it, "the primary emotional disruptor of every attachment pattern in its approach to God." Shame operates beneath every other dimension. If it is not addressed within the first cluster, every dimension from §4 onward is conducted with shame's lie functioning as background noise: *you are not fundamentally acceptable for this*. The placement at §3 addresses shame before any of the relational capacities — soothing, secure base, contingent response, worth — can be received. Without §3, a person reads §4 (He receives my distress) while internally holding the shame layer's conclusion that they are too much to be received.

**Zone 1 sequence constraint:** §1 must precede §2; §2 must precede §3. Safety before self-exposure; self-exposure before the shame that operates in response to it being named and addressed. The within-zone order is load-bearing.

---

**Zone 2 — Active Relational Capacities (§4–6)**

These three dimensions address the relational functions of the attachment relationship as they operate in active encounter. They address what the relationship provides and how it works.

*§4 Soothed* addresses safe haven — the receiving of distress. This is the first active relational experience addressed after the threshold conditions: once approach is established as safe (§1), self-exposure established as safe (§2), and shame addressed (§3), the question becomes: what happens when I bring my actual distress? The soothing dimension names the specific capacity that is most directly dependent on the threshold conditions. You cannot receive soothing from a God who is not safe, who does not see you, and in relation to whom shame says you are too much.

*§5 Secure Base* addresses the launch-and-return logic. Developmentally, the sequence safe haven → secure base is established: a child learns the base is a source of comfort before learning to venture from it. You do not launch from a base whose comfort you have not yet experienced. §5 follows §4 for this reason — the relational function of support-in-distress must be established before the relational function of support-for-venture can be accessed.

*§6 Contingent Response* addresses the reliability of the figure's attention and response. It is placed after §4 and §5 because it requires that the reader has already experienced (at least in the document's formational exercise) what soothing and venturing from a base feel like — and can now address the trust question underneath both: *does my signal reach response?* Contingent response is the question of attunement — not "is God safe?" (§1) or "does He see me?" (§2) but "does what I bring actually land?"

**Zone 2 sequence constraint:** §4 before §5 reflects developmental order and has a grounding argument. §6 after both is defensible on the grounds that the attunement question follows the establishment of specific relational experiences rather than preceding them. The within-zone order has more flexibility than Zone 1, but the developmental logic should not be overridden without argument.

---

**Zone 3 — Identity Consolidation (§7)**

*§7 Worth* stands alone at the center of the document's architecture. It is placed after the threshold conditions and active capacities have been addressed, and before the more demanding territory of lament, suffering, and repair.

Worth is not addressed in Zone 1 because it is not primarily a threshold condition. You do not need to hold settled worth to approach safely — you need to approach safely to discover you are worth it. Worth as an identity declaration requires that the relational experience of the first six dimensions has occurred: you have been safe (§1), seen (§2), released from shame's verdict (§3), soothed in distress (§4), given ground to venture from (§5), and confirmed as responsive (§6). Worth is the identity consolidation of all of this: given what has been established, here is who you are in this relationship.

Worth is not addressed in Zone 4 (§8–9) because lament and suffering require the identity declaration as a foundation, not as a conclusion. You cannot protest within a relationship (§8) — which is a declaration that the relationship is still worth fighting for — if you have not established that you are valued as you are. You cannot hold "He is for me" through suffering (§9) without the prior declaration that your worth is not contingent on circumstances.

Worth placed at §7 is not arbitrary. It is the capstone of the relational foundation (§1–6) and the ground of the demanding terrain (§8–10). Moving it before §3 (Shame) or after §8 (Lament) would structurally dissolve the consolidation function it serves.

---

**Zone 4 — Demanding Relational Terrain (§8–9)**

These two dimensions apply the relational foundation to circumstances that most stress the attachment system. They require everything established in §1–7.

*§8 Lament* comes before §9 (Suffering) because lament is the relational protocol that suffering requires. Lament establishes: you can protest within this relationship without losing it. That protocol must be in place before it is applied to the most acute conditions. Suffering (§9) is the circumstance; lament (§8) is the relational register that makes honest speech in that circumstance possible. A person who encounters §9 without §8 has no formed vocabulary for bringing raw protest into the relationship — suffering produces isolation rather than protest-within-nearness.

*§9 Suffering* comes last in Zone 4 (before Repair) because suffering is the sharpest test of everything that precedes it. It requires §1 (God is not dangerous in this), §2 (He sees this), §3 (shame is not the explanation for why this is happening), §4 (distress brought at its worst is still received), §5 (the base holds even here), §6 (signal still reaches response), §7 (worth is not contingent on circumstances), and §8 (honest protest is available). Suffering as a dimension tests all eight preceding dimensions simultaneously. If any of them are weak, suffering exposes the weakness. Placed at §9, it functions as a pressure-test of the whole architecture before the final synthesis.

---

**Zone 5 — Return Mechanism (§10)**

*§10 Repair* is the final dimension and cannot be anything other than final. It synthesizes the entire architecture: the mechanism of security is repair, not the absence of rupture. But repair requires:

- The relationship is safe to return to (§1)
- The figure will see what the rupture produced, including what the person brought to or withheld from it (§2)
- Shame does not make return impossible (§3)
- The figure receives the distress of rupture without being overwhelmed by it (§4)
- There is a base to return to (§5)
- The return signal will reach response (§6)
- The person is worth the return (§7)
- Lament is available if the rupture produced protest (§8)
- The relationship holds through suffering-level rupture (§9)

Repair as last is not the end of a story — it is the synthesis of a mechanism. Secure attachment is formed through repair. The document closes at repair not because everything before it was building to a conclusion, but because repair is the mechanism by which everything before it is maintained, deepened, and returned to when broken.

---

### Load-Bearing Sequence Constraints

These are the ordering claims that, if violated, break the document's architectural logic. They are not style choices or organizational preferences — they are structural.

| Constraint | Reason |
|---|---|
| §1 before all | Safety is the prerequisite condition. No other dimension is accessible without a basic threshold of approach safety. |
| §3 within Zone 1 | Shame addressed before relational capacities (§4–6). If shame is deferred, the capacities are received with shame's lie still operative beneath them. |
| §4 before §5 | Safe haven (soothing) before secure base (venturing). Developmental sequence: comfort received before exploration launched. |
| §7 before §8–9 | Worth established before lament and suffering test it. Identity must precede its testing — otherwise the test is addressed without the foundation. |
| §8 before §9 | Lament protocol before suffering circumstance. Suffering without lament established leaves the person without relational vocabulary for acute protest. |
| §10 last | Repair is the synthesis of the entire architecture. It requires all preceding dimensions as operative. There is no repair function without relationship established, tested, and held. |

### Flexible Elements Within the Sequence

| What is more flexible | Governing constraint |
|---|---|
| §2 and §3 internal order within Zone 1 | The current order (Seen, then Shame) has a developmental logic: you need to be seen before shame has something to react to. The reverse order (Shame, then Seen) is arguable — address shame first, then establish being seen. Either has a case; the current order reflects the phenomenological sequence (exposure → shame response). |
| §4, §5, §6 order within Zone 2 | The developmental argument favors the current order. The within-zone sequence is less load-bearing than the between-zone sequence. Swapping §5 and §6 loses some argument but does not collapse the architecture. |
| §8 and §9 order within Zone 4 | The current order (Lament protocol before Suffering circumstance) has the stronger argument. However, a case could be made that acute suffering is what generates the need for lament, and §9 could precede §8. Either requires an argument. Neither destroys the zone logic. |

---

### Summary: The Five-Zone Architecture

| Zone | Dimensions | Question addressed |
|---|---|---|
| 1 — Threshold Conditions | §1–3 | Can I come? What makes approach possible? |
| 2 — Active Relational Capacities | §4–6 | What does the relationship provide? How does it function? |
| 3 — Identity Consolidation | §7 | Who am I in this relationship? |
| 4 — Demanding Relational Terrain | §8–9 | Does this hold under pressure? Can I protest and still be held? |
| 5 — Return Mechanism | §10 | Can I always come back? |

The zones progress from enabling conditions to active relational experience to identity declaration to stress-testing to return. This is not a narrative arc — it is a developmental sequence that maps how secure attachment is actually formed, applied as a preparation architecture for encounter with God.

## III. Anticipated Critiques and Responses

Each critique below is stated in its strongest specific form — not as a summary of a position but as the actual argument a real critic from that tradition would make, with the specific sources and claims they would cite. "Where the critique has real force" names what the response does not fully resolve. Knowing the residual exposure is more useful for critic defense than pretending the response is complete.

---

### Critique 1 — Clinical Psychology: "The mechanisms you are invoking do not apply to solo spiritual practice"

**The critique:**
The document invokes memory reconsolidation (Ecker et al., 2012) as the mechanism behind its Evidence Anchors and Encounter Prompts. This claim requires scrutiny at three levels.

First, the foundational reconsolidation neuroscience. Nader, Schafe & LeDoux (2000, *Nature*, 406, 722-726) established that fear memories in rats become labile upon retrieval and require new protein synthesis to restabilize — the reconsolidation window. This is the peer-reviewed neuroscience basis. But the conditions required to open this window are highly specific: the original memory must be retrieved in a way that genuinely activates the original learning circuit (not merely recall of content), a prediction error must occur within a narrow temporal window relative to retrieval, and the contradictory information must be sufficiently surprising relative to the encoded expectation. Ecker's *Unlocking the Emotional Brain* is a practitioner-theorist text, not peer-reviewed neuroscience. It extrapolates from the rodent fear-conditioning research to complex human emotional memories without establishing that the same boundary conditions apply. That extrapolation is contested even in the clinical literature (see Scully & Bhagya, 2021, *Psychological Science*; Redondo et al., 2014, *Nature* — boundary conditions for reconsolidation in humans are significantly more complex than the animal model suggests).

Second, earned security. Siegel (1999, *The Developing Mind*, pp. 281-313) and Mikulincer & Shaver (2007, *Attachment in Adulthood*, pp. 456-472) are both explicit that changes in attachment security require repeated, contingent responses from a human relational other — not because of methodological bias, but because the mechanism is interpersonal: the orbitofrontal cortex reorganizes through right-hemisphere-to-right-hemisphere attunement with an attuned other. A document cannot be attuned. It does not detect and respond to the user's state. The question of whether God functions as a neurologically effective attuned other is not a question psychology can answer — but the document should not invoke Siegel's earned security findings as support for the claim that solo practice produces the same mechanism.

Third, somatic engagement risk. Levine's Somatic Experiencing is a clinical methodology with a multi-year certification track (the SEP designation). Rothschild (2000, *The Body Remembers*, pp. 89-110) specifically cautions that body-based interventions can activate trauma material faster than the nervous system can regulate without skilled therapeutic support present. The document's somatic invitations are immediately preceded by Pattern Descriptions deliberately designed to activate the attachment pattern — meaning the user is engaging somatic attention to activated material without a practitioner present to monitor for dissociation, flooding, or dysregulation.

**Where the critique has real force:** At all three levels. The reconsolidation claim as applied to solo spiritual practice is Theoretical, not Supported — and was incorrectly labeled in the initial document (now corrected). The earned security mechanism requires interpersonal attunement, which a document cannot provide. The somatic engagement of activated material without clinical oversight is a genuine risk for presentations the Contraindications section now addresses.

**The response:**
The document does not claim to produce attachment reorganization through the tools themselves. The governing statement in the parent document: *"You are not the mechanism. He is."* The tools are condition-creating; the Spirit produces whatever implicit reorganization occurs through encounter with God. The claim being made is theological (God as responsive relational other who mediates genuine attunement through the Spirit), not neurological. The Governing Framework Scope Note is explicit: *"The mechanism of that reorganization in encounter with God is theologically grounded and empirically untested as a distinct neurological pathway."*

The reconsolidation framework is referenced descriptively — the structure of activating the old expectation, introducing a felt contradiction, and allowing mismatch to register is the logic the document follows. Whether reconsolidation technically occurs in any given session is not a claim the document makes. The Evidence Anchor creates the condition for a felt contradiction; it does not certify the outcome.

On somatic engagement: the document addresses the activation-and-somatic-engagement sequence in Contraindications (Section V). Clients with complex PTSD, unprocessed trauma, and high-amplitude disorganized attachment are contraindicated for exactly the reason this critique names. The residual risk is a practitioner who fails to apply the contraindications — not a design flaw in the tool itself.

**Remaining exposure:** The critique correctly identifies that the somatic engagement of activated material without practitioner presence is a real risk for presentations at the edge of the contraindications. The Contraindications section names signals requiring referral, but a solo user (not client) encountering this document without practitioner guidance has no one to enforce the contraindications. If the document is distributed beyond a practitioner-mediated deployment context, this becomes significant.

### Critique 2 — Biblical Counseling (CCEF-Adjacent): "This framework displaces the biblical diagnosis with a developmental one"

**The critique:**
Powlison (2003, *Seeing with New Eyes*, pp. 15-32) argues that the fundamental diagnostic category of Scripture for human dysfunction is not developmental history but idolatry — the worship of created things in the place of God. Jeremiah 17:9 names the heart as deceitful above all things. Romans 1:18-23 locates the root of all human dysfunction in the suppression of truth and the exchange of the Creator for created things. The biblical counseling tradition's central claim is that these categories — not psychological development — are the structurally governing explanation for why people struggle with God.

When this document explains the obstacles to prayer through attachment patterns — anxious, avoidant, disorganized — it is substituting a developmental explanation for the biblical one. A person who struggles to approach God in prayer does not have an insecure attachment pattern as the root problem. They have a heart that worships created things over the Creator, and the particular form that takes in their prayer life is what the pattern descriptions name. The pattern is the surface; the idolatry is the root. Treating the surface as the diagnostic category displaces the root diagnosis.

More specifically: Kirkpatrick (2005, *Attachment, Evolution, and the Psychology of Religion*, pp. 72-100) frames the God-relationship explicitly as God occupying the attachment figure slot — the "ultimate attachment figure" who provides proximity maintenance, safe haven, secure base, and protest at separation. This is a substitution framework: God completes what inadequate caregivers failed to provide. The problem is that it positions the believer's unmet developmental needs as the structuring category for understanding what the God-relationship is supposed to deliver. The categories are Bowlby's, not Scripture's. The document's 10 dimensions (Safe, Seen, Soothed, Secure Base, Contingent Response, Worth, Lament, Suffering, Repair) are Bowlby's four criteria elaborated — they are not derived from biblical categories of covenant, sonship, or union with Christ. Welch's *Running Scared* (2007) demonstrates what a fully biblical treatment of fear in relationship with God looks like — without importing Bowlby's criteria to define what that relationship is supposed to provide.

**Where the critique has real force:** The critique correctly identifies that the 10 dimensions are organized around Bowlby's attachment criteria, not biblical categories. That is accurate — and it is a design choice that requires defense, not dismissal. The critique also correctly identifies that Kirkpatrick's framing can import a substitution logic if not carefully bounded.

**The response:**
The framework uses attachment theory in a specific direction: to describe how human dysfunction manifests as obstacles to approach — not to define what the God-relationship is or what it is supposed to provide. The distinction matters. Scripture's categories — covenant, adoption, union with Christ, the Father's posture — govern what the relationship is. Attachment research names the functional patterns by which insecure humans fail to receive it. The diagnostic center of the model remains biblical: Affections Discovery and Character Wound Diagnostic (informed by Powlison, Welch, and Allender) surface idolatry and disordered worship as the root. *Secured in Father* addresses the approach layer — the functional obstacles that prevent the already-given covenant reality from being received. Attachment language describes the obstacle; it does not define the relationship.

On Kirkpatrick: the Correspondence Hypothesis is used descriptively — it establishes that human attachment patterns predict functional access to the God-relationship, which is why the patterns matter for formation. The document does not adopt Kirkpatrick's substitution framing. The Theological Anchor explicitly frames the covenant relationship as already given and independent of attachment patterns: *"These are not qualities you are reaching toward or producing through effort. They are the posture of a Father who has already declared you His own."*

On the 10 dimensions: they are organized around Bowlby's criteria not because Bowlby defines the God-relationship, but because each criterion corresponds to a specific distortion pattern in approach to God that the attachment research documents and that Scripture addresses. The theological content of each dimension is derived from Scripture; the organizational frame is Bowlby's. These are different contributions.

**Remaining exposure:** The critique will not be fully satisfied by the above response, because the underlying disagreement is about whether importing Bowlby's organizational categories — even as scaffold — imports his anthropology structurally. The practitioner in a CCEF-shaped context will need to be prepared to argue that the scaffold does not govern the content, and to demonstrate that by pointing to where biblical counseling categories remain primary (Affections Discovery, Character Wound Diagnostic) and where attachment language is subordinate.

### Critique 3 — Confessional Reformed: "This adds to the appointed means and cultivates illegitimate religious experience"

**The critique:**
The Westminster Confession of Faith (21.1-3) defines the appointed elements of worship and the ordinary means of grace: the Word read and preached, the sacraments, and prayer. The Spirit works through these appointed means. R. Scott Clark (*Recovering the Reformed Confession*, 2008, pp. 87-123) identifies what he calls the Quest for Illegitimate Religious Experience (QIRE) — the pattern in evangelical spirituality of seeking direct, unmediated, felt encounter with God beyond the appointed means. The QIRE pattern says: "Word and sacrament are fine, but what I really need is a direct experiential encounter." This document's encounter prompts — which direct users to "wait for a knowing," "sit until something settles," "notice what your body does" as indices of spiritual encounter — are precisely this pattern.

More concretely: the document prescribes specific body postures (sitting upright, feet flat, hands open), breath practices (three slow breaths with extended exhale), somatic attention exercises ("notice where distress lives in your body"), and encounter-waiting postures ("stay until something settles"). None of these are appointed means. The Westminster Larger Catechism addresses what the Spirit does in and through prayer; it does not describe a mechanism by which somatic preparation unlocks encounter. The document's claim that the entry ritual "targets the correct neurological system" and that without it "the pattern descriptions are processed as information" adds a physiological precondition to encounter that God has not appointed and that has no scriptural basis.

Furthermore: the Repair Protocol's framing of dry encounters — sessions where no felt experience occurs — as misattunement that "does not close in the pattern's favor" assumes that the felt quality of encounter is the standard by which the Spirit's work is assessed. This trains users to locate the reality of encounter in felt experience, and then to interpret the absence of felt experience as a problem requiring technique. The confessional Reformed response is that the answer to a dry encounter is not technique — it is trust in the objective promise of God received by faith regardless of accompanying feeling.

**Where the critique has real force:** The critique correctly identifies that the document adds somatic preconditions to encounter that are not among the appointed means. It also correctly identifies a risk in the Repair Protocol: framing dry encounters in terms of technique (step 2: "do not interpret the silence"; step 4: "keep returning") can cultivate the habit of managing the encounter relationship rather than resting in the objective promise.

**The response:**
The document does not add new appointed means of grace. The entry ritual, somatic invitations, and encounter prompts are not worship elements — they are formation practices aimed at removing obstacles to receiving what the ordinary means already deliver. The Reformed tradition has a well-developed category of spiritual disciplines as means to that end: Calvin (*Institutes* III.2.8) describes faith as "a firm and certain knowledge of God's benevolence toward us" that must "penetrate into our heart" — the formational challenge is not intellectual assent but heart reception. Jonathan Edwards (*Religious Affections*, 1746) argued extensively that genuine spiritual transformation must reach the affections — not merely the understanding — and that formation practices aimed at the affections are not additions to the means of grace but preparations for receiving what the means deliver. Willard's VIM framework (Vision, Intention, Means) — which is broadly Reformed-compatible — frames disciplines as "putting ourselves in the place where God can work in us." This is precisely the document's claim.

On somatic engagement specifically: the body is engaged not as a spiritual means but because attachment wounds are stored below the level of language and rational processing (Van der Kolk, 2014). Attending to the body in preparation for prayer is attending to the whole person that the Shema calls to love God — heart, soul, strength, and mind. The somatic layer is not adding spiritual content; it is addressing the creature's embodied condition.

On the Repair Protocol: the critique is partially right. The document should not be used to cultivate technique-management of encounter as a substitute for resting in the objective promise. The Repair Protocol is designed specifically for the insecurely attached person whose pattern tells them the dry encounter proves the relationship is over — its purpose is to interrupt that conclusion, not to replace objective promise with subjective technique. The practitioner should frame the Repair Protocol explicitly within the objective covenant promise: the encounter may be dry; the covenant is not.

**Remaining exposure:** The Clark QIRE critique will not be fully resolved by the above response for a practitioner in that tradition. The honest position: this document operates in a continuationist pneumatological and broadly evangelical formation tradition. It is not designed for or optimized for deployment in a strictly confessional Reformed context. That scope should be named rather than defended away.

### Critique 4 — Cessationism: "You are training people to locate the Spirit's work in their subjective internal states"

**The critique:**
B.B. Warfield (*Counterfeit Miracles*, 1918, pp. 3-31) argued that direct, experiential Spirit-work beyond the Word was an apostolic-era attesting sign that ceased with the completion of the canon. The underlying concern — relevant here even where the document does not claim charismatic gifts — is about where the Spirit's work is located and how it is identified. MacArthur (*Strange Fire*, 2013, pp. 71-92) develops this into a specific critique of continuationist spirituality: when Christians are trained to seek and identify the Spirit's work through subjective internal states — feelings of peace, a sense of knowing, somatic settling — they are trained to treat their own internal experience as the criterion for whether the Spirit has acted. This is epistemically dangerous because: (a) it makes the subjective experience the test of the encounter's reality, (b) it produces false conclusions when the experience is absent ("the Spirit was not there"), and (c) it is vulnerable to demonic counterfeiting of genuine spiritual states (MacArthur, pp. 82-88).

Concretely, this document's encounter prompts repeatedly direct users toward subjective internal markers: "stay until something settles," "wait for a knowing," "notice what your body does with the word comfort," "notice what happens in me when I try to believe this." The Repair Protocol addresses dry encounters — sessions where no felt movement occurs — with a series of techniques for sustaining engagement. But the techniques do not resolve the underlying problem the cessationist identifies: the document has established subjective felt experience as the criterion for whether the encounter was real. The Repair Protocol's solution to absent felt experience is more technique, not return to the objective Word. This is precisely the pattern Warfield critiques: when experience fails, seek it harder rather than resting in objective promise.

**Where the critique has real force:** The critique correctly identifies that the encounter prompts use subjective internal states as markers of the Spirit's work. This is a genuine design commitment, not an oversight. It is appropriate for the pneumatological tradition within which the document operates — but that tradition should be named, and the cessationist context should be flagged as outside the document's design scope.

**The response:**
The encounter prompts do not require charismatic gifts, special revelation, or extra-biblical experience. What they require is that prayer be genuine communication with a responsive God — a claim the Westminster Standards affirm (WCF 21.3: prayer is "an offering up of our desires unto God"). Romans 8:16 — "The Spirit himself bears witness with our spirit that we are children of God" — is an ongoing pneumatological reality affirmed across cessationist and non-cessationist traditions. The felt dimension of the encounter prompts is not the criterion for whether God was present; it is the means by which the implicit layer — where the attachment pattern lives — is reached. The document distinguishes between the Spirit's work (not dependent on felt experience) and the human person's reception of that work (which is embodied and sometimes felt).

On the Repair Protocol: it does not tell users to seek felt experience harder. It tells them: name the absence honestly (do not perform a presence you do not feel), decline to interpret the silence as abandonment, borrow from testimony when personal experience is absent, and return. The "borrow from testimony" step (Psalm 22:4-5) is explicitly the move from subjective experience to objective testimony — precisely what the Warfield critique recommends.

The honest scope boundary: this document operates within a pneumatological tradition that affirms ongoing, responsive, sometimes-felt Spirit-work in the believer's formed experience. A practitioner in a hard cessationist context should name this tradition explicitly before introducing the encounter prompts, rather than assuming the framework is neutral.

**Remaining exposure:** The MacArthur critique of training users to locate the Spirit in subjective states is not fully resolved by the above. A practitioner who deploys this document in a cessationist community without the pneumatological framing established first will encounter this objection and will need to be prepared to argue it at the level of pneumatology, not just formation practice.

### Critique 5 — Coaching Ethics and Scope: "The clinical methods embedded here require clinical training to deploy safely"

**The critique:**
The ICF Core Competencies (2021 revision, Competency 2 — Embodies a Coaching Mindset) specify that coaches refer clients to other support professionals when the client's needs exceed the scope of the coaching relationship or require professional expertise the coach does not have. The specific methods embedded in this document — Somatic Experiencing (Levine), Polyvagal Theory (Porges), memory reconsolidation (Ecker) — are clinical modalities with their own scope-of-practice expectations. Somatic Experiencing has a multi-year certification track (SEP designation) precisely because somatic work with activated material requires trained clinical judgment. Rothschild (2000, *The Body Remembers*, pp. 89-110) identifies specific clinical criteria for when body-based interventions are contraindicated, including alexithymia, dissociative tendencies, and unprocessed trauma history. A coach without clinical training cannot reliably assess whether these criteria are present in a client.

The specific scope risk in this document is the activation-and-somatic sequence: the Pattern Descriptions are deliberately designed to activate the attachment pattern (which may be trauma-associated in some clients) immediately before the somatic invitations direct the user to attend to body sensation in the activated state. Rothschild specifically flags this sequence — activating distressing material and then directing somatic attention to the activation without a clinician present to monitor for dissociation or flooding — as requiring clinical expertise. The document addresses this in Contraindications, but the contraindications are in a practitioner reference document, not visible to the solo user who may encounter the parent document without practitioner mediation.

Furthermore: the Pattern Descriptions and Partial-Version Notes, while described as "observational and recognitional," are in practice offering the client a clinical-adjacent assessment of their attachment functioning. The precision and sophistication of these descriptions — specifically the partial-version notes distinguishing moderate from high-amplitude presentations — requires the practitioner to have sufficient clinical literacy to recognize when a client's self-identification as "moderate" is itself a manifestation of the dismissing-avoidant pattern masking a more significant presentation.

**Where the critique has real force:** The activation-and-somatic-engagement sequence is the real scope risk, and it is real regardless of how the tools are framed. Calling the somatic invitations "condition-creating rather than therapeutic" does not change the physiological reality that activated attachment material + directed somatic attention = clinically relevant territory for some presentations. The Contraindications section addresses this, but enforcement depends entirely on practitioner discernment.

**The response:**
The deployment context is determinative. In its designed deployment — between-session use by a client with an established coaching relationship, adequate reflective capacity, no clinical contraindications, and practitioner-mediated introduction — the practitioner's role is specifically to perform the contraindication assessment before the document is assigned. The practitioner is not present during the somatic work; they are present before it (introduction, orientation) and after it (debrief). This is a standard between-session formation practice deployment, not a clinical intervention.

The Pattern Descriptions are self-recognitional, not diagnostic. The practitioner is not assessing the client's attachment classification — the client is selecting a preparation posture for prayer. The self-selection is imprecise; that is not a problem for the tool's function. The function is: name a current pattern as a lens, not diagnose an attachment disorder. These are different activities with different precision requirements.

The scope boundary is the practitioner's responsibility to enforce, not the tool's design to prevent. A practitioner who assigns this document without performing the contraindication assessment — specifically assessing for trauma history, somatic reactivity, and dissociative tendency — is the scope violation. The tool is designed to be used after that assessment, not to perform it.

**Remaining exposure:** The critique correctly identifies that the activation-and-somatic sequence is the real risk, and the contraindications are in a practitioner reference document rather than visible to the solo user. If the parent document is distributed beyond a practitioner-mediated context — as a general resource, in a class, or in publication — the scope risk increases significantly. This is the same remaining exposure identified in Critique 1.

## IV. Outcome Markers

This section answers a practitioner question: *how do I know if this document is producing formation movement in myself or in a client?* The question is harder than it appears, because the document targets a layer — implicit attachment representations — that does not announce itself with obvious felt shifts, and because each pattern's progress marker looks different from the others. A practitioner who evaluates all three patterns through a single framework (e.g., "the person feels more at peace in prayer") will miss movement in one pattern and mistake habitual compliance for movement in another.

---

### What "Progress" Means in This System

This document operates on three layers simultaneously:

**Explicit representations** — how a person consciously describes God, themselves, and the relationship. ("I know God is safe." "I believe I am valued.") Explicit representations can shift through single sessions. They are the most accessible layer and the most likely to report as changed without underlying formation occurring.

**Behavioral patterns** — how a person actually prays, what they bring, how they respond to silence, whether they return after dry encounters. Behavioral markers take longer than explicit ones and are more reliable — they are less subject to the compliance effect (saying what sounds correct).

**Implicit representations** — automatic, below-awareness responses to God, encounter, and the attachment relationship. These are the layer where attachment patterns actually live, and where lasting reorganization requires repeated, felt experience of a responsive other. The document's honest ceiling (Governing Framework, Scope Note) applies here: explicit and behavioral progress are trackable; implicit reorganization is theologically grounded but its occurrence in any given session or user is not directly verifiable. Practitioner claims about implicit change should be provisional.

**Progress = narrowing of the gap.** In a person with insecure attachment, there is a gap between what they explicitly believe about God (safe, present, for them) and how they automatically respond to God in encounter (anxious, managed distance, oscillation). Progress is the narrowing of that gap — explicit belief and implicit response increasingly matching. The practitioner cannot observe implicit response directly; behavioral markers are the proxy.

---

### Cross-Pattern Markers — Movement Regardless of Pattern

These markers indicate that the document is working at the mechanism level, regardless of which pattern was active on a given day. They are the first indicators a practitioner should track.

| Marker | What to look for | What it indicates |
|---|---|---|
| **Self-Diagnostic shifts** | The person identifies a different pattern than previous sessions, or names "less of" their primary pattern today | The Self-Diagnostic is being used actively as a lens rather than as a fixed label; situational awareness is increasing |
| **Entry ritual tolerance increases** | Less internal resistance to the ritual; shorter time to settle; somatic engagement feels less effortful | The nervous system is learning the ritual as a genuine transition rather than treating it as a performance |
| **Defusion produces distance** | Person reports that the defusion statement created a recognizable moment of separation from the pull — "I noticed the pull, and it didn't just sweep me in" | The observing-self is developing capacity to separate from the pattern at the structural hinge; this is the ACT defusion mechanism working |
| **Evidence Anchor retrieval becomes richer** | More episodes accessible; person retrieves an episodic memory faster or with more sensory detail; episodes from different seasons begin to appear | The episodic memory layer is being engaged regularly — access is improving because the retrieval is being practiced |
| **Dry encounters held differently** | Person returns after a dry encounter without the pattern's standard interpretation (abandonment / "this doesn't work" / panic); names the absence without signing the pattern's reading of it | Repair Protocol integration — the person has internalized that dry encounters are not the pattern's predicted outcome |
| **Spontaneous between-session use** | Client mentions using the framework outside of assigned use — naming a pattern in a relational context, or using the defusion structure in a non-prayer moment | The formation is beginning to integrate beyond assigned practice; this is evidence of explicit-to-behavioral transfer |
| **God-description language shifts** | The language used to describe God in session debriefs changes incrementally — less qualified, less defended, more relational | Explicit God-image is shifting; note this as leading indicator, not confirmation of implicit change |

---

### Pattern-Specific Progress Markers

Each insecure pattern has a distinct trajectory of progress because each pattern's implicit expectation is different. What movement looks like in an anxious person is not what it looks like in an avoidant person. Applying the wrong template produces false negatives — concluding nothing is happening when movement is occurring — or false positives — reading habitual compliance as formation.

---

#### Anxious / Preoccupied

**Primary movement trajectory:** From escalation toward settledness. The core implicit expectation is that proximity requires effort and that stillness equals abandonment. Progress looks like the practiced capacity to sit without re-presenting, not the elimination of urgency. It is likely to be gradual and will initially feel to the anxious person like restriction rather than growth.

**Early markers (first weeks):**
- Can identify the pull to re-present without acting on it in a given session — even if the restraint is effortful
- The defusion statement produces a moment of recognition ("that's the pattern") before the escalation resumes
- Comfort lands briefly before the loop reopens — even brief landing is movement

**Mid-formation markers:**
- Can bring something once and end the session without re-presenting it; does not compulsively review whether it was received
- Silence in prayer produces less urgency (not necessarily comfort — the urgency diminishes before the comfort arrives)
- The loop between distress and re-presentation has a longer interval — the anxious person waits longer before checking
- Between-session: notices when prayer has shifted from communication to proximity-management — can name this without the self-naming producing shame

**More sustained markers:**
- Comfort begins to land and stay — not immediately re-questioned or re-presented for confirmation
- Self-Diagnostic shows seasonal variation rather than the same pattern every session (security accessed in some contexts)
- The encounter prompt "bring once and stop" feels less like deprivation — the restraint begins to carry its own quality of trust rather than loss

**Practitioner misread to avoid:** High session frequency or intensity is not a progress marker in this pattern — it may be the pattern running in the formation tool. Anxious clients will over-apply; more is not better. The meaningful signal is what they do *after* bringing something once — whether they can stop.

---

#### Dismissing-Avoidant

**Primary movement trajectory:** From managed distance toward genuine exposure. The core implicit expectation is that genuine need-expression will rupture the relationship or produce disappointment, so efficiency and self-sufficiency are the protection. Progress looks like things beginning to have a cost — the person who previously processed the document efficiently starts to notice that some sections are harder than others. Initial "progress" may actually look like resistance increasing as the pattern is named more precisely.

**Early markers (first weeks):**
- Acknowledges specific sections are harder to move through — not dismisses them as not applicable
- Entry ritual takes longer — not because the person is struggling but because something is actually being contacted; the habitual speed of processing is slowing
- Identifies the partial-version note as applicable before defaulting to "that's not really me" — even tentative self-recognition is movement

**Mid-formation markers:**
- Something costs something to say in the encounter prompt — the avoidant pattern's primary progress signal is that bringing actual interior material produces some friction, because the protection is dissolving enough to feel what was behind it
- Evidence Anchor activates something felt (not just a theological confirmation of what was already known) — the episodic memory retrieval reaches something affectively charged
- Can sit with a section's material past the efficient-processing moment — tolerates not yet moving on
- Between-session: something from a session appears unbidden in daily consciousness (a verse, an image from an encounter prompt) — this indicates that material processed in the document is not being efficiently categorized and filed away

**More sustained markers:**
- Closeness with God begins to feel like something — initially this may feel uncomfortable, not comfortable (the avoidant pattern has defended against closeness because it was threatening, not because it was unwanted; its return may initially produce anxiety)
- Self-Diagnostic begins to identify avoidant patterns in other relational contexts, not only with God — the lens has generalized
- Enters encounter prompt without converting it into a task, observation, or theological reflection — genuine relational engagement beginning

**Practitioner misread to avoid:** Doctrinal clarity and theological articulation are not progress markers in this pattern — they may be the pattern operating in the formation tool. The meaningful signal is what cannot be easily processed — discomfort, friction, something that won't resolve into a clean proposition. If every session produces tidy theological insights with no relational residue, the avoidant pattern may be running in the very tool designed to interrupt it.

---

#### Fearful-Avoidant / Disorganized

**Primary movement trajectory:** From enacted oscillation toward named oscillation. The core implicit expectation is a double bind — approach activates both longing and fear simultaneously, producing collapse, dissociation, or rapid cycling between engagement and withdrawal. Progress does not look like settling (that is the anxious trajectory). It looks like the bind becoming observable rather than operative — the person can say "I notice both at once" rather than simply being swept between the two poles.

**Critical practitioner note:** In this pattern, progress sometimes looks like regression. Increased access to grief, anger, or named fear before prayer is often an early marker that the document is reaching the correct layer — not evidence that it is making things worse. The pattern's default is to keep affective material inaccessible. When that defense softens, difficult material becomes accessible. This is movement.

**Early markers (first weeks):**
- Can remain in a section's material slightly longer before the pull to withdraw activates — any increase in dwell time is significant
- Names the bind aloud ("I want to come near and something pulls back") rather than simply enacting the approach-and-flight cycle without observing it
- Uses the disorganized encounter prompts (which begin with naming the bind rather than resolving it) without requiring resolution before entry

**Mid-formation markers:**
- Oscillation slows — the approach-and-flight cycle has longer periods at each pole; approach doesn't immediately trigger flight; withdrawal doesn't immediately trigger desperate approach
- Dry encounters are used with the Repair Protocol rather than producing collapse, panic, or shame-laden withdrawal from the practice
- Disorganized encounter prompts produce something — not necessarily comfort, but an observable internal event (body response, emotion, image) that can be named to God
- Between-session: the person returns to the practice without it feeling like evidence of failure that the last session was difficult

**More sustained markers:**
- Can hold approach long enough to receive something from an encounter prompt without the fear layer closing it down before it lands
- Shame about the oscillation itself begins to decrease — the pattern is held as diagnostic information rather than as a spiritual verdict
- Spontaneous use of the "name the bind first" structure in prayer outside the document — indicates the disorganized encounter approach has been internalized as a prayer mode

**Practitioner misread to avoid:** Consistency of engagement is not the primary marker in this pattern — the avoidant pattern shows high consistency (efficient engagement); the anxious pattern shows high frequency. Fearful-avoidant progress is not measured by consistent use but by the quality of what happens during engagement and by what happens to the oscillation over time. Inconsistent use is expected; irregular deep engagement is more significant than consistent surface engagement.

---

### Practitioner Observation Signals — What to Listen for in Session Debrief

Each pattern reports its experience of the document differently. These differences are themselves diagnostic and carry information about whether the pattern is being interrupted or is running in the formation tool.

| Observation | Anxious signal | Avoidant signal | Disorganized signal |
|---|---|---|---|
| **How the debrief report is offered** | Effortful, seeking confirmation that it was done correctly; apologetic for what didn't happen | Efficient, informational; leads with what was learned rather than what was experienced | Variable — may minimize, may report overwhelm; debrief quality inconsistent across sessions |
| **What gets emphasized** | What the session didn't produce — the absence of feeling, the uncertainty about whether it landed | What was understood — insights, theological clarity; relational or affective content minimized | Highly variable — may not cohere into a clear report |
| **What gets minimized** | The moments comfort landed, the moments the defusion worked — these are underreported | The moments something cost something to say; friction with the material | The moments approach was sustained before the pull to withdraw — underreported because not remarkable to the person |
| **Return rate and completion** | Over-applies; may complete multiple sections per week when the design is one; frequency is anxiety-management | Under-applies; may report "not getting to it" without distress; between-session use deprioritized when life is full | Inconsistent; some weeks very engaged, some not at all; the pattern itself explains the inconsistency |
| **Spontaneous God-description shifts** | Notices when God feels more accessible, reports it with relief that quickly re-converts to uncertainty | Rarely names relational shifts unprompted; may notice change intellectually before feeling it | Reports shifts in fragments — a moment, an image, an unusual quiet — without integrating them into a sustained description |

---

### What Progress Is Not

**Felt emotion is not equivalent to progress.** Avoidant clients may have emotionally activated sessions early in use — the pattern has not yet defended the material, and direct encounter with the sections can produce unexpected feeling before the pattern reasserts its distance. This can look like fast progress and then a plateau when the defense reinstates. Anxious clients may have quieter sessions as the practice begins to regulate the nervous system — which can look like diminished engagement but may be early settledness.

**Comfort with the entry ritual is not equivalent to progress.** If the entry ritual has become smooth and easy, examine whether it is still transitioning the nervous system or whether it has become a rote sequence the pattern has accommodated to. Formation tools can be domesticated. A ritual that no longer produces any somatic effect may need to be varied.

**Completing all 10 sections sequentially is not the primary goal.** Sequential completion satisfies the project-completion impulse that the avoidant pattern runs in formation tools. Deep, repeated engagement with the 2–3 sections that address the active pattern is more valuable than efficient passage through all 10. Progress is depth in the relevant sections, not coverage of all sections.

**Absence of dry encounters is not progress.** Dry encounters are normal across all patterns. What matters is how they are held — whether the Repair Protocol is used, whether the pattern's interpretation of silence is declined, whether return happens. A practitioner who frames "productive encounters" as success and "dry encounters" as failure has imported performance metrics into a formation practice.

**Relief from self-knowledge is not the same as formation.** When a person reads the partial-version note for their pattern and says "that's exactly me — I finally understand what I've been doing," the explicit layer has been reached. Understanding the pattern is not the same as the pattern's influence diminishing. Monitor whether self-knowledge produces formation practices (changed behavior) or becomes its own sufficient stopping point (insight without formation — one of the three failure modes this practice is designed to prevent).

---

### Temporal Expectations — What to Expect When

These are not prescriptive timelines. They are realistic expectations based on the mechanism the document is designed to activate and its known ceiling for solo between-session use.

**Early use (first 4–8 sessions):** Primarily explicit representation shifts. The person's conscious description of God, the patterns, and the relationship changes. Defusion begins to produce moments of recognition. Evidence Anchor retrieval improves. These are real gains — and they are the most accessible layer. Do not mistake them for the whole formation picture.

**Continued use (first 3 months):** Behavioral markers begin to appear — how the person prays between sessions starts to change, not only how they report the document's sessions. Self-Diagnostic shifts begin. The pattern's influence in non-prayer relational contexts may begin to be noticed. These are the mid-formation signals that the explicit shifts are beginning to consolidate into behavioral change.

**Sustained use (6+ months):** Implicit layer traces may begin to appear. Automatic responses in relational contexts — not only prayer — show variation from pattern defaults. The person reports noticing the pattern activating before being swept by it. This is the earned security trajectory Siegel describes; it is slow, non-linear, and will include setbacks. Setbacks are not evidence of failure — they are evidence that the implicit layer is the target, and the implicit layer reorganizes through repeated experience, not through single breakthroughs.

**Ceiling reminder:** Lasting implicit reorganization requires repeated experience of a responsive relational other. For the God-relationship specifically, the mechanism is theological (Spirit-mediated encounter, union with Christ) and the practitioner cannot certify when or whether implicit reorganization has occurred. What the practitioner can observe and track are the explicit and behavioral layers. The implicit layer is the Spirit's territory. The practitioner creates conditions and tracks what is trackable.

## V. Contraindications

Deploying *Secured in Father* in the wrong presenting context does not merely produce a dry encounter. In some contexts it can confirm the pattern's worst expectation, activate trauma material faster than the client can regulate, or create attachment injury with God rather than formation toward Him. The practitioner must know not only what this tool can do but when it must not be deployed — and what to do instead.

---

### Absolute Contraindications — Do Not Deploy

**1. Active crisis or acute clinical destabilization**
Suicidality, active self-harm, acute psychosis, acute trauma response (flashback, significant dissociation), or marked functional impairment in daily life require clinical referral before any coaching tool is deployed. *Secured in Father* is not a crisis tool. If any of these are present or suspected, stop and refer. Do not use the tool as a de-escalation technique — the somatic engagement it requires is contraindicated during acute destabilization.

**2. Disorganized/Fearful-Avoidant attachment with complex PTSD and unprocessed traumatic memory**
The somatic engagement required by this document can activate trauma material faster than a self-coaching or coaching context can contain. High-amplitude disorganized attachment combined with unprocessed traumatic memory is clinical territory. Clinical signals requiring referral before deployment: (a) attachment pattern rigid and present across all relational contexts, not situational or seasonal; (b) known trauma history with strong somatic reactivity — physical symptoms, dissociation, or nervous system dysregulation in emotional or spiritual contexts; (c) history of dissociation specifically in prayer or encounter with God; (d) significant functional impairment in more than one life domain. Referral: attachment-focused clinical therapy — EMDR, Somatic Experiencing, or IFS with a trauma-trained clinician. This tool may function as an appropriate adjunct *after* clinical stabilization is established, not before.

**3. Active substance abuse or addiction**
Somatic engagement assumes a baseline nervous system that can tolerate titrated contact with difficult material. Active substance abuse alters this baseline in ways that exceed coaching scope. Stabilization and appropriate clinical or recovery support must precede deployment.

---

### Conditional Contraindications — Requires Judgment, Modification, or Prior Work

**4. Spiritual abuse history**
*Secured in Father* assumes a fundamentally accessible relational context with God. Clients whose prior religious experience was characterized by fear, coercion, spiritual manipulation, or abuse of religious authority may experience the encounter framework as reactivating rather than formative. The Fearful-Avoidant encounter prompts are partially designed for the terrain of God-fear — but spiritual abuse is a distinct category from attachment-based fear of God, and the tool is not primarily designed for it.

*Deployment requires:* (a) an established coaching relationship in which the client has experienced the practitioner as safe and non-coercive; (b) an explicit, unhurried conversation about the client's religious history before introducing encounter-based work — not as intake protocol but as genuine pre-work; (c) starting with §8 Lament or §3 Shame rather than §1 Safe, since the lament sections specifically normalize protest and honest speech without requiring approach to feel safe first. When in doubt, defer until the client names readiness unprompted.

**5. No accessible episodic memory of God**
The Evidence Anchors require retrieval of a specific past encounter with God. For clients who are new to faith, in a sustained dry season, or who report no discernible God-encounters, the "name one moment" instruction will produce either nothing or the opposite of its intended effect: absence of episodic memory confirms the anxious pattern's fear ("I cannot find a single moment — which means what I feared is true"). The tool can still be used; the Evidence Anchor component requires modification.

*Modification:* Replace "name one moment from your own experience" with the testimony-borrowing approach already present in the Repair Protocol's Step 3 — "Others have found Him faithful in exactly this. I am standing on their testimony until mine returns." The Psalm 22:4–5 move (speaker reaches for communal testimony when personal experience is absent) provides the biblical framing for this adaptation.

**6. Early-stage faith with limited formation vocabulary**
The Pattern Descriptions and Partial-Version Notes assume sufficient self-reflective capacity and emotional vocabulary to recognize oneself in nuanced phenomenological descriptions. Clients with limited formation exposure or early-stage faith may not have the inner language to navigate the document independently.

*Modification:* Do not assign for solo between-session use. Facilitate the Self-Diagnostic and the first selected section verbally in a coaching session before assigning independent use. This changes the deployment mode from between-session self-coaching to in-session facilitation — which is a legitimate use, but the document must be held as a facilitation scaffold rather than a self-guided text.

**7. Active anger at God or acute spiritual rupture**
If the client is in a season of significant anger at God, sustained withdrawal from prayer, or loss of faith, deploying the full document sequentially is not appropriate. The tool has sections specifically designed for this territory.

*Routing:* Direct the client to §8 Lament (protest within surviving relationship) and §10 Repair (returning after rupture) specifically. Do not begin at §1. The Self-Diagnostic still applies — naming the active pattern before entering the lament or repair sections is still correct. The sequential architecture is suspended; targeted routing replaces it.

---

### What to Do Instead

| Presenting situation | Do this first |
|---|---|
| Active crisis (suicidality, acute trauma, acute psychosis) | Immediate clinical referral. No coaching tool deployed until stabilization and clinical clearance. |
| Complex PTSD with disorganized attachment | Attachment-focused clinical therapy (EMDR, SE, IFS). This tool as adjunct only after clinical stabilization. |
| Spiritual abuse history | Establish coaching safety. Explicit history conversation. Begin with §8 Lament or §3 Shame only. |
| No episodic memory of God | Adapt Evidence Anchors to testimony-borrowing (Repair Protocol Step 3 language). |
| Early-stage faith, limited vocabulary | Facilitate verbally in session first. Assign for solo use only after demonstrated in-session navigation. |
| Active anger at God / spiritual rupture | Route to §8 Lament and §10 Repair specifically. Suspend sequential use. |
| Active addiction | Stabilization and recovery support first. |

---

### Scope Restatement

*Secured in Father* is designed for: (a) self-coaching use by a formed practitioner with adequate self-regulatory capacity, or (b) between-session use by clients with an established coaching relationship, adequate reflective capacity, no clinical contraindications, and sufficient prior formation exposure to navigate the document independently. It is not a crisis tool, not a trauma processing tool, and not a substitute for clinical therapy when clinical therapy is indicated. When clinical work and coaching are running concurrently (the preferred arrangement for clients with significant trauma history), coordinate with the clinical provider before deploying this tool — specifically regarding the somatic engagement components and the encounter prompts.

## VI. Load-Bearing vs. Modifiable Elements

This section exists for two audiences: a practitioner adapting the tool for a specific client context, and a future author adapting the document for publication. For both: changing a load-bearing element breaks the mechanism, whether or not the resulting document looks similar. Changing a modifiable element is legitimate adaptation. The distinction is not about fidelity to a fixed text — it is about preserving the functional logic that makes the document do what it is designed to do.

Each load-bearing element is followed by a one-sentence explanation of what breaks if it is removed or changed.

---

### Load-Bearing Elements

**1. Somatic engagement before cognitive content (Entry Ritual)**
The entry ritual must engage the body *before* the pattern descriptions are read. Attachment wounds are stored below the level of language and prefrontal processing — cognitive engagement first addresses the wrong system.
- *What breaks if removed:* Pattern descriptions are processed as information rather than as material that can reach the implicit layer. The reader reads about their pattern without it reaching the system where the pattern lives.

**2. Spoken aloud requirement (Entry Ritual and Defusion Statements)**
Speaking aloud — not reading silently — is required for both the entry statement and the defusion statements. Vocalization engages the ventral vagal complex and produces stronger physiological response than internal processing.
- *What breaks if changed to silent reading:* VVC activation is reduced; the defusion statement is processed as internal monologue rather than as a relational act; physiological effect (Neff, Porges) is significantly diminished.

**3. Self-Diagnostic before the sections, not after**
The pattern must be named before the pattern descriptions are read. If the diagnostic is embedded within each section or placed at the end, the pattern has already governed how the content was read.
- *What breaks if moved:* The reader processes the section through the unexamined pattern rather than from a named, observable position. The defusion statement has nothing to defuse from.

**4. Two-tier pattern descriptions (primary + partial-version note)**
Each pattern requires both a primary description (high-amplitude, clinical presentation) and a partial-version note (moderate, disguised presentation). A coaching population encounters this tool predominantly at the moderate end.
- *What breaks if partial-version notes are removed:* The moderate presentation produces a false negative — "that's not me" — and the reader proceeds without the correct lens. The primary descriptions describe clinical presentations that the majority of coaching users will not recognize themselves in.

**5. Three-pattern differentiation (not two, not one)**
The three-pattern structure (Anxious/Preoccupied, Dismissing-Avoidant, Fearful-Avoidant) reflects the research taxonomy and addresses genuinely distinct phenomenological experiences. Collapsing to two patterns (anxious/avoidant) loses the disorganized presentation entirely, which has distinct encounter dynamics. A single generic pattern loses specificity.
- *What breaks if collapsed:* The disorganized/fearful-avoidant presentation — which requires a specific kind of encounter prompt (naming the bind rather than resolving it) — is not served by anxious or avoidant interventions. Clients in that pattern using anxious prompts will experience escalation; avoidant prompts will confirm withdrawal.

**6. Defusion placement at the structural hinge**
Defusion must occur after pattern activation (pattern descriptions) and before encounter (encounter prompt). This is the only moment when the pattern is maximally active and can be made observable before it governs the encounter.
- *What breaks if moved before pattern descriptions:* The defusion has nothing activated to defuse from — it is processed as a general instruction rather than as a real-time interruption of the active pattern.
- *What breaks if moved after the encounter prompt:* The pattern has already governed the encounter. Defusion arrives too late.

**7. ACT defusion structure (observation, not challenge)**
The defusion statement must use the observation structure — "I notice the pull to..." — not the CBT challenge structure — "That pull is wrong/unfounded." Defusion does not require the person to believe differently before it takes effect. Challenge does.
- *What breaks if changed to challenge:* The mechanism shifts from defusion (creating distance from the pattern while it is active) to cognitive restructuring (requiring belief change before the encounter). Many users cannot produce belief change on command; the encounter is then entered from inside the failed challenge, not from outside the pattern.

**8. Episodic memory instruction in Evidence Anchors**
The "name one moment" instruction must retrieve a specific autobiographical memory, not a semantic affirmation. Only episodic memory activates the felt quality of past experience that can contact the implicit expectation.
- *What breaks if replaced with semantic affirmation:* The user affirms "God is good" (already believed cognitively) without activating a felt contradiction to the pattern's implicit expectation. The implicit layer remains uncontacted. The Evidence Anchor becomes an affirmation exercise rather than a reconsolidation condition.

**9. Somatic bridge between Evidence Anchor and Encounter Prompt**
The somatic invitation must occur between the Evidence Anchor and the Encounter Prompt, not before both or after both. Its function is to maintain body engagement through the transition from cognitive/episodic work to encounter.
- *What breaks if removed or repositioned:* The transition from Evidence Anchor to Encounter Prompt is purely cognitive, and the Encounter Prompt is processed in the explicit system only. The implicit layer is lost before the encounter window opens.

**10. Pattern-differentiated Encounter Prompts**
Each insecure pattern requires a structurally different kind of encounter because each pattern's implicit expectation is different. Anxious prompts orient toward restraint; avoidant prompts orient toward full disclosure; disorganized prompts begin with honest naming of the bind. A single generic prayer prompt is processed in the mode of the pattern rather than against it.
- *What breaks if unified:* An anxious user given a general "bring your need and trust God" prompt brings the need repeatedly (escalation, not trust). An avoidant user given the same prompt manages the encounter efficiently and never brings actual need. The encounter confirms the pattern rather than contradicting it.

**11. Repair Protocol exists and addresses pattern-interpretation of dry encounters**
Without the Repair Protocol, a dry encounter (no felt movement) confirms the pattern's implicit prediction. Each pattern has a specific interpretation of silence: anxious reads it as abandonment; avoidant reads it as confirmation that encounter isn't for them; disorganized experiences panic or freeze. The Repair Protocol interrupts each interpretation before it closes the loop in the pattern's favor.
- *What breaks if removed:* A single dry session can produce implicit confirmation of the pattern ("I came, the relationship was unresponsive") — exactly the opposite of what the document is designed to produce.

**12. Spirit primacy statement**
"You are not the mechanism. He is." This statement or its functional equivalent must be present and visible before the formation work begins. Without it, the document reads as a self-improvement technique and creates tool dependence — one of the three failure modes this practice is designed to prevent.
- *What breaks if removed:* The practitioner and the tools are implicitly positioned as the transformation agents. The document trains a different relationship to the tools than the one required by the model's five-layer mechanism.

**13. Scope statement ("this is preparation, not the mechanism")**
The document's self-description as preparation for encounter — not encounter itself, not therapy, not the transformation mechanism — is load-bearing for scope integrity and theological honesty.
- *What breaks if removed:* The document can be read as claiming to produce what only the Spirit produces. This creates both clinical scope risk and theological distortion.

---

### Modifiable Elements

The following are implementation choices. They can be changed without breaking the mechanism, subject to the governing constraints noted.

| Element | What is modifiable | Governing constraint |
|---|---|---|
| Specific Scripture verses in Evidence Anchors | Any verse that anchors the dimension's truth can be used | Must remain anchored to the specific dimension (§1 Safety ≠ §7 Worth); must function as semantic anchor before the episodic instruction |
| Number of breaths in Entry Ritual | Three is a specific choice; two or four would function similarly | Extended exhale principle is load-bearing; the number is not |
| Specific wording of pattern descriptions | Language can be rewritten for audience, publication context, or cultural register | Both tiers (primary + partial-version) must be preserved; clinical/moderate distinction must remain |
| Specific wording of defusion statements | The language can be adapted | Observation structure ("I notice...") is load-bearing; challenge structure is not an acceptable substitute |
| Order of the 10 dimensions | Order may be adjustable with argument | Sequencing Rationale (Section II, pending) will establish which ordering constraints are load-bearing; do not reorder without that analysis |
| Self-Diagnostic descriptive language | "Anxious/Preoccupied," "Dismissing-Avoidant," "Fearful-Avoidant" can be translated to plain language for a client-facing publication | Three-pattern structure is load-bearing; reducing to two or one is not an acceptable modification |
| Length of somatic invitations | Can be condensed or expanded | "Notice location, not concept" instruction is load-bearing; the specificity that keeps attention below the labeling function must be preserved |
| Format (prose, workbook, guided audio) | The document can be reformatted for different delivery contexts | Structural sequence (Entry → Self-Diagnostic → Pattern Descriptions → Defusion → Evidence Anchor → Somatic Invitation → Encounter Prompt) is load-bearing regardless of format |
| Client-facing register | Language can be adapted for different audiences | Directness principle (non-vague, non-generic) is closer to load-bearing; vague generality defeats the mechanism at every stage |
| Repair Protocol step structure | Five steps can be reorganized | Three functional principles are load-bearing: (1) name the absence honestly, (2) do not sign the pattern's interpretation of silence, (3) the epistemological move to borrowed testimony when personal experience is absent |
| Nuance Notes free-write space | Form and placement can be adapted | Function — making space for observations that exceed the named patterns — should be preserved in some form; removing it entirely closes the document to the particular |

### Three Distinctions That Must Be Held in Any Adaptation

These are not components — they are governing postures that run through the entire document. Any adaptation that loses any of these three has changed what the document is, not merely how it looks.

**1. Preparation vs. mechanism.** The document prepares for encounter; it does not produce encounter. Every instruction, prompt, and protocol is condition-creating. The Spirit is the mechanism. An adaptation that positions the tools as the cause of transformation has changed the document's governing claim about what it is doing.

**2. Diagnostic vs. prescriptive.** The Self-Diagnostic names a current pattern as a lens. It does not prescribe a fixed identity or a path of correction. The language "which pattern is active today" is not incidental — it enforces the situational, non-labeling function of the diagnostic. An adaptation that turns the patterns into permanent categories or correction targets has changed what the Self-Diagnostic is doing.

**3. Covenant given vs. covenant sought.** The encounter prompts invite reception of what the covenant already declares true — not reaching toward something not yet secured. The Theological Anchor's statement ("the posture of a Father who has already declared you His own") governs all 10 dimensions. An adaptation that frames the encounter as securing the relationship, earning the Father's regard, or producing what the covenant does not yet provide has changed the document's theological foundation.

## VII. Integration Map

This section answers the practitioner's navigation question: *where does Secured in Father sit in the broader Covenant Identity system, and how does it connect to the other documents that operate alongside it?* Four documents are mapped below. Each connection has a different direction, occupies a different layer in the five-layer mechanism, and carries distinct misuse risks. The map is not a workflow — it is a set of relationship descriptions that allow a practitioner to deploy these documents in concert without conflating what each one is designed to do.

---

### System Position: Where Secured in Father Sits

In the five-layer mechanism:

| Layer | Function | Document |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Spirit as primary agent | Creates conditions; Spirit produces transformation | — (governing claim, not a document) |
| 2. Coaching relationship creates formation context | Attunement, relational honesty, practitioner posture | — (practitioner competency, not a document) |
| 3. Diagnostic work surfaces false identity | Affections, character wounds, false identity structures | *Affections Discovery — In-Session Protocol*; *Character Wound Diagnostic Tool* |
| 4. Covenant truth applied and received | Scripture as anchor; identity declared and received | *Lie Eliminator*; *Identity Installer* |
| 5. Between-session disciplines sustain the work | Formation practice; implicit layer conditions | ***Secured in Father — Attachment Formation & Encounter Preparation*** |

*Secured in Father* is a Layer 5 document: a between-session formation discipline designed to sustain and deepen what Layer 3 and Layer 4 work has surfaced and applied in session. It does not do Layer 3 diagnostic work (that is the Affections Discovery and Character Wound Tool's job), and it does not do Layer 4 covenant truth declaration work in the way the Lie Eliminator does — it provides the preparation conditions under which covenant truth (already declared in Layer 4) can be received at the implicit attachment level.

The practitioner's integration question at any point in the coaching arc is: *does the client have the diagnostic clarity (Layer 3) and covenant truth grounding (Layer 4) needed for Secured in Father to function as formation rather than as unfounded encounter-seeking?* If not, Secured in Father is premature. If yes, it is the right between-session tool for working the attachment approach layer.

---

### Connection 1 — [[Secured in Father — Entry Guide]]

**Relationship:** The Entry Guide is the pre-document routing layer for the parent document. It is a standalone tool used *before* opening *Secured in Father*, not alongside it or embedded within it.

**What it does:** Three-step routing process — (1) identify the active attachment pattern (Anxious, Dismissing, Fearful), (2) identify the presenting emotional state from six options, (3) route to a primary section and secondary section of *Secured in Father* using a routing table.

**Why it is separate:** The routing function requires decision-making cognitive mode. The parent document is a devotional-encounter tool requiring receptive mode. Embedding a routing table at the opening of the parent document would create a mode conflict — the practitioner or client would be navigating a decision matrix at the moment they need to transition into somatic and relational readiness for encounter. The Entry Guide handles the navigation; the parent document receives the user after navigation is complete.

**Information flow:** Entry Guide → Parent Document (one direction, linear, sequential). The Entry Guide does not read from the parent document; the parent document does not require the Entry Guide. The Entry Guide is an efficiency layer — users who know their pattern and section can bypass it.

**Integration for practitioners:** When assigning *Secured in Father* to a client, the practitioner's first decision is whether to assign the Entry Guide alongside it or to route the client directly. Considerations:

- *Assign Entry Guide first* when: the client is new to the document, has not yet identified a consistent active pattern, or presents with variable emotional states session to session. The Entry Guide routes at the session level; it preserves accuracy when the active pattern shifts.
- *Route directly* when: the client has a well-identified primary pattern and a section established as central to their current work. Direct routing removes a decision layer that the practitioner has already made on the client's behalf.

**Practitioner Design Note misuse risk:** The Design Notes document (this document) contains the architectural rationale behind the routing decisions the Entry Guide makes. If a practitioner uses the Design Notes to construct custom routes that override the Entry Guide's routing logic, they are applying a practitioner reference document as a client navigation tool — a container error. The Entry Guide's routing logic is designed for client navigation. The Design Notes are designed for practitioner understanding of why the routing logic works as it does.

---

### Connection 2 — [[Covenant Identity — Affections Discovery — In-Session Protocol]]

**Relationship:** Two documents working the same territory from different angles at different mechanism layers. The most important connection to hold with precision — and the most likely to be conflated.

**Affections Discovery:** Layer 3. In-session. Practitioner-facilitated. Diagnostic purpose: surfaces disordered affections and false covenant objects — what the client worships in place of God and where they seek identity outside of Christ. Produces a diagnostic picture of the idolatry layer. Five-stage protocol (Surface Inquiry, Pattern Surfacing, Dependency Probe, God-Exclusion Map, Identity Revelation). Requires practitioner presence to navigate.

**Secured in Father:** Layer 5. Between-session. Self-directed. Formation purpose: prepares the attachment approach layer for encounter with God. Works the implicit patterns that prevent covenant truth from being received. Does not diagnose — it prepares. The Self-Diagnostic is a current-session preparation lens, not a clinical assessment.

**What connects them:** Both surface material about how the client relates to God and where distortion operates. The specific false covenant objects Affections Discovery identifies predict which *Secured in Father* dimensions will carry the highest activation:

| Affections Discovery finding | Most activated Secured in Father dimensions |
|---|---|
| False covenant object: approval / performance | §7 Worth (primary), §3 Shame, §6 Contingent Response |
| False covenant object: safety / control | §1 Safe (primary), §5 Secure Base, §6 Contingent Response |
| False covenant object: self-sufficiency / competence | §5 Secure Base (primary), §7 Worth, §4 Soothed |
| False covenant object: relationships / approval from others | §2 Seen (primary), §6 Contingent Response, §3 Shame |
| God-exclusion pattern: managed distance | §1 Safe, §2 Seen, §5 Secure Base |
| God-exclusion pattern: escalating petition | §4 Soothed, §6 Contingent Response, §10 Repair |

This table is a working map, not a rigid prescription. Its purpose is practitioner routing — using the Affections Discovery diagnostic picture to direct the client to the *Secured in Father* sections most likely to be generative, rather than assigning the document for sequential use from §1 to §10.

**Information flow:**
- Affections Discovery → informs practitioner routing into Secured in Father (diagnostic to formation: one direction)
- Secured in Father → can surface material that feeds back into Affections Discovery (formation surfacing diagnostic material: the reverse direction, less explicit but real)

The reverse direction matters: a client who uses *Secured in Father* and reports persistent resistance in specific sections is generating diagnostic information about where false covenant objects are operating. This feedback should be brought back into the in-session Affections Discovery work, not treated as formation failure.

**Category error to prevent:** Using *Secured in Father* as a replacement for Affections Discovery because it appears to surface similar affections-layer material. The documents are not interchangeable:
- Affections Discovery requires practitioner presence because it navigates a five-stage clinical-adjacent protocol that can produce significant affective material requiring real-time attunement.
- *Secured in Father* is designed for self-directed use after the diagnostic foundation is established, not before. Assigning it before Affections Discovery has produced a diagnostic picture produces formation without diagnostic clarity — the client is working the attachment layer without knowing what false identity structure the attachment pattern is protecting.

**Sequencing guidance:** Affections Discovery (in-session, Stage 1 of Transition Logic) should precede *Secured in Father* assignment. The diagnostic picture informs the formation routing. This is not an absolute rule — a practitioner may assign *Secured in Father* for its Entry Ritual and Self-Diagnostic value before Stage 1 is complete — but the full document should not be deployed as a formation practice until the diagnostic work has produced at least a working hypothesis about the false identity structure in play.

### Connection 3 — [[Covenant Identity — Character Wound Diagnostic Tool]]

**Relationship:** Secondary diagnostic layer that can deepen a practitioner's understanding of which *Secured in Father* dimensions will be most activated and why. These are overlapping frameworks that describe adjacent territory from different angles — not parallel tools doing the same job.

**Character Wound Diagnostic Tool:** Identifies three wound types (Warrior, Hermit, False Noble) based on the specific relational wound that formed the false self. Each type carries a distinct core wound, behavioral profile, emotional signature, and God-image distortion. Used after Affections Discovery has surfaced the false identity structure.

**The relationship between wound type and attachment pattern:** Character wounds and attachment patterns are not the same construct. Attachment patterns describe how an insecurely attached person approaches relational figures (avoidance, escalation, oscillation). Character wounds describe what happened to produce the false self and what specific lie was internalized about the self in relation to others. The two frameworks often co-present, but they are not one-to-one:

| Wound type | Core lie | Most common attachment overlap | Not always this — exceptions |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warrior (betrayal / weakness) | "I cannot trust the one who should have held me" | Fearful-Avoidant most common (approach triggers danger); Dismissing features in those who defended by self-sufficiency | Some Warriors present as Anxious — their escalation is an attempt to force the betraying figure to prove trustworthiness |
| Hermit (terror / too much) | "My full self overwhelms and drives others away" | Fearful-Avoidant (presence itself is dangerous); some Anxious (desperate seeking of connection to manage the terror of being too much) | Rarely Dismissing — the Hermit's wound requires the presence of others to be activated |
| False Noble (ambivalence / not enough) | "I am not inherently worth full regard — I must perform adequacy" | Dismissing-Avoidant (self-sufficiency as protection); Anxious (performance as the attempt to produce worth) | Both Dismissing and Anxious can carry this wound — the wound is not pattern-specific |

**Practical use:** Character wound type can predict which *Secured in Father* dimensions will carry the highest activation for a given client, beyond what the attachment Self-Diagnostic alone would suggest.

| Wound type | Highest-activation Secured in Father dimensions | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Warrior | §1 Safe, §2 Seen, §8 Lament | The base betrayed trust; approach is the danger site; honest speech within the relationship was punished |
| Hermit | §3 Shame, §4 Soothed, §8 Lament | The self was experienced as too much; distress expressed drove others away; protest was dangerous |
| False Noble | §7 Worth, §5 Secure Base, §6 Contingent Response | Worth was contingent on performance; the base required earning; signals that produced inadequacy were the operative signal history |

**Information flow:** Character Wound Diagnostic (in-session, secondary diagnostic layer) → informs practitioner routing within *Secured in Father* (which dimensions to prioritize, which encounter prompts will encounter the most resistance, which Contraindications may be elevated for this wound type).

The flow is one direction: wound type deepens the practitioner's routing intelligence. *Secured in Father* does not diagnose wound type — that is the Character Wound Tool's job.

**Contraindication implication:** Warrior wound type with unresolved traumatic betrayal corresponds directly to the Fearful-Avoidant/disorganized attachment Contraindication in Section V (Absolute Contraindication §2). A Warrior wound presentation with high-amplitude disorganized attachment markers (approach triggers danger, significant somatic reactivity, dissociation history) elevates the contraindication to absolute. The wound type provides a diagnostic lens for assessing whether the contraindication applies.

**Category error to prevent:** Using *Secured in Father* to diagnose wound type — i.e., drawing clinical-level conclusions about a client's character wound from their responses to the pattern descriptions and encounter prompts. The pattern descriptions in *Secured in Father* are observational and recognitional preparation tools, not clinical diagnostic instruments. Wound type requires the full Character Wound Diagnostic Tool applied with practitioner presence.

### Connection 4 — [[Covenant Identity — Diagnostic Lens Transition Logic]]

**Relationship:** The Transition Logic governs the in-session staging sequence. *Secured in Father* is between-session. They operate in different mechanism layers but are linked through the deployment question: *what between-session formation practice is appropriate at each stage of the Transition Logic, and when does Secured in Father specifically fit?*

**The stage map:**

| Stage | In-session work | Between-session role for Secured in Father |
|---|---|---|
| **Stage 1 — Affections Discovery** | Surface disordered affections, false covenant objects | **Not yet appropriate** — assigning before diagnostic clarity produces formation without foundation; the attachment layer cannot be effectively worked without knowing what false identity structure the pattern is protecting |
| **Stage 2 — False Identity Named** | Name the specific false identity structure; connect affections to the operating lie | **Viable but limited** — the client has a named false identity; the practitioner can use this to route specifically into *Secured in Father* (which dimensions, which pattern) rather than assigning the document for general use. Self-Diagnostic and Entry Guide work at this stage; full deployment of encounter prompts may outpace the client's readiness |
| **Stage 3 — Covenant Truth Applied and Received** | Apply covenant truth to the false identity; identity declaration and reception in session | **Primary deployment context** — *Secured in Father* extends the Stage 3 reception work between sessions. The Evidence Anchors and Encounter Prompts are the between-session counterpart to in-session covenant truth application. The client who receives an identity declaration in session (Lie Eliminator, Identity Installer) and then uses *Secured in Father* between sessions is working the same covenant truth at the implicit attachment level outside the session. |
| **Stage 4a — CBT Tools (cognitive distortion blocks reception)** | Address explicit cognitive distortions that prevent covenant truth from landing | **Continues alongside, different system** — CBT tools (Stage 4a) work the explicit cognitive layer; *Secured in Father* works the attachment approach layer. These are not alternatives; they address different systems. A client in Stage 4a who is also using *Secured in Father* is receiving both explicit-layer and attachment-layer support simultaneously — appropriate, not redundant |
| **Stage 4b — Relational/Attentive Response (implicit memory blocks reception)** | Address implicit memory barriers to receiving covenant truth | **Most direct connection** — Stage 4b is the in-session response to implicit memory blocking. The between-session tool that most directly addresses implicit memory is *Secured in Father*: Entry Ritual (somatic engagement), Evidence Anchors (episodic memory activation), Encounter Prompts (felt contradictory experience). A client in Stage 4b should have *Secured in Father* assigned as the explicit between-session complement to the in-session Stage 4b work |

**Return signals and Secured in Father:** The Transition Logic specifies return signals — situations requiring the practitioner to step back to an earlier stage. Two return signals have direct implications for *Secured in Father*:

- If a client returns from between-session use of *Secured in Father* with significant destabilization (flooding, crisis-level distress, activated trauma material), the Absolute Contraindications in Section V apply retroactively. The practitioner should suspend the document, address the destabilization, and reassess whether the client's presentation at the Absolute Contraindication level (disorganized attachment + complex PTSD) was correctly screened. The return signal triggers a Transition Logic regression and a contraindication review simultaneously.
- If a client reports persistent inability to engage the Encounter Prompts across multiple sessions (not dry encounters, but inability to enter the encounter at all — the prompt produces nothing, not dryness), this may indicate that the implicit memory barrier is stronger than the between-session solo practice can address. This is a Stage 4b signal: the in-session Relational/Attentive Response work may need to precede continued assignment of *Secured in Father*. The document is not failing — the mechanism requires more in-session support before the between-session practice can function at the reconsolidation level.

**Information flow:** The Transition Logic stages determine when *Secured in Father* is deployed (Stage 3 primary, Stage 4b direct complement, Stage 1 not yet). *Secured in Father* debrief material (what happened between sessions) informs whether the in-session stage should advance, hold, or return. The flow is bidirectional: stage governs deployment; between-session experience informs stage assessment.

**Category error to prevent:** Using *Secured in Father* to replace Stage 3 in-session covenant truth work because the between-session tool covers similar territory. The in-session Stage 3 work provides the relational container, the practitioner attunement, and the precision of named covenant truth applied to a specific false identity structure. *Secured in Father* works the reception of that truth at the attachment level — it requires the in-session work as its foundation, not as an optional parallel. Assigning the document as a substitute for Stage 3 rather than a complement to it collapses the distinction between Layer 4 (in-session covenant truth) and Layer 5 (between-session formation discipline that sustains Layer 4 work).
