# Covenant Identity — God-Representation Deepening Tool
*Practitioner Reference — Phase 1 Follow-Through and Full-Engagement Use*

---

## Purpose and Use Context

This tool is used after Phase 1 has surfaced the client's functional God-representation. It is not a Phase 1 document — it is the practitioner's guide for what to do with what Phase 1 reveals.

Phase 1 identifies the Gap between the client's declared covenant identity and their lived experience of it. Central to that Gap is almost always a distorted functional God-representation: the client's lived, implicit sense of who God actually is to them, which typically contradicts what they confess theologically.

This tool addresses:
- The theoretical frame explaining why the living God and the theological God can coexist in conflict
- The six primary God-representation types: how they form, how they present, and which schemas they correlate with
- The three practitioner errors that undermine the work
- How to work with the surfaced representation over time across all four phases
- Type-specific coaching approaches and a full question library
- What transformation looks like and how to recognize when the representation is shifting

## The Core Theoretical Frame

### The Lived God Is Not the Believed God

Ana-Maria Rizzuto established in *The Birth of the Living God* (1979) that every person carries two Gods: the *theologian's God* (propositional, doctrinal, what is believed) and the *living God* (the psychic object shaped by early relational experience). These two coexist without canceling each other. A person can hold sophisticated, accurate theology about God's unconditional love while their nervous system expects punishment, abandonment, or conditionality in the actual relationship with God. The representation is a major organizing structure in how a person views self, others, and the world — it is not merely doctrine.

Rizzuto grounded the God-representation in early relational experience, drawing from Winnicott and Fairbairn. The raw material for the God-representation emerges from primary caregivers — their emotional availability, consistency, warmth, responsiveness. The God-representation is formed not from instruction about God but from the *felt experience of being held, soothed, seen, or neglected*. Unlike Freud's model (which tied God-image solely to the father), Rizzuto demonstrated that the God-representation draws from multiple relational sources woven into a lived psychological object.

The critical clinical reality Rizzuto identifies: the God-representation can remain essentially *unchanged* even as a person matures intellectually and theologically. A person may achieve sophisticated theological understanding — seminary, years of Scripture study, intellectual development — while their *lived* God-representation remains frozen at the developmental stage it solidified in childhood. This is why theological education alone does not necessarily transform spiritual experience. The living God has its own psychological persistence, independent of rational conviction.

---

Todd Hall's empirical research (Biola, 2009+) provides the neurological architecture for Rizzuto's framework. Hall distinguishes **explicit God-image** (conscious, articulable beliefs about God) from **implicit God-image** (gut-level relational expectations encoded in the attachment system before language was available). These operate on separate neural pathways. The implicit God-image governs emotional response, behavioral choices under stress, and the quality of prayer — not the explicit one.

Hall's studies show that explicit theological belief alone does not change implicit God-image. The mechanism of transformation, Hall argues, is not new information but *new relational experience* — neurological rewiring that occurs when the attachment system encounters a different relational reality than what it was wired to expect through attuned relationship, prayer encounter, and community.

---

David Benner makes the same distinction experientially: the **believed-in God** vs. the **experienced God**. Benner argues that spiritual formation is blocked when these two diverge. A client cannot grow into their covenant identity while being relationally governed by a God they experience (not merely believe) to be punishing, absent, or conditional. Benner's pathway to transformation involves acknowledgment of the experienced God without shame or denial — before encounter can occur. "Nothing can be transcended until it is accepted as part of one's reality and story."

---

Lee Kirkpatrick extends this via attachment theory (Bowlby): God functions as an **attachment figure** — the human system automatically applies its internal working model, built from early caregiver experience, to the God-relationship. God meets the four Bowlby criteria for an attachment figure: the client seeks *proximity* to God, uses God as a *safe haven* in distress, relies on God as a *secure base* for exploration, and experiences *separation distress* when God feels distant.

Under stress, the attachment system activates, and the implicit God-image governs the response — not the explicit theological belief. Kirkpatrick's research distinguishes two models: the *correspondence hypothesis* (attachment patterns with humans correspond to patterns with God) and the *compensation hypothesis* (people with insecure human attachment may seek in God what caregivers couldn't provide). Evidence suggests correspondence operates primarily at the implicit/nervous-system level; compensation may appear at the motivational/behavioral level.

---

**The practitioner implication:** You are not dealing with a theological problem. You are dealing with a psychic structure built from relational experience, encoded in the nervous system, and functionally operative regardless of what the client confesses. Theological correction addresses the wrong layer. The living God must be worked with directly.

## The Six Primary God-Representation Types

Most clients present a primary type with secondary features from one other type. The types are not diagnostic labels — they are formation patterns worth tracking and naming.

---

### 1. The Punishing God

**Relational origin:** Primary caregivers who were critical, shaming, performance-monitoring, or expressed love conditionally based on behavior. Discipline that was harsh, arbitrary, or disproportionate. Religious environments that emphasized judgment, divine disapproval, or the weight of sin as an ongoing relational liability.

**Presentation in coaching:** The client approaches God with chronic guilt that feels unresolved even after confession. Prayer feels performative — an attempt to stay on God's good side rather than genuine encounter. The client repeatedly speaks of needing to "get right with God." There is an underlying assumption that God's current disposition toward them depends on recent behavior. Mistakes, failures, and sin produce disproportionate shame and spiritual distance that the client initiates (they withdraw, not God).

**Attachment pattern (Kirkpatrick):** Anxious — seeking constant reassurance, alternating between fear and relief, hypervigilant about divine evaluation.

**Schema correlation (Young):** Defectiveness/Shame — most commonly co-present.

---

### 2. The Absent/Distant God

**Relational origin:** Primary caregivers who were emotionally unavailable, physically absent, preoccupied, or unresponsive. The relational template formed: needs go unmet, presence cannot be relied on, seeking proximity is futile. Religious environments where God was described abstractly, impersonally, or as primarily transcendent and unreachable.

**Presentation in coaching:** The client has correct theology but describes their spiritual life as dry, rote, or one-directional. Prayer feels like talking to a wall. "I know God is there, but I don't feel it." There is low expectation that God will meet them in their specific experience. The client does spiritual disciplines out of obligation rather than relational anticipation. They describe God as real but inaccessible to them personally. There may be a resigned flatness in how they speak of God — not anger, just absence of expectation.

**Attachment pattern (Kirkpatrick):** Avoidant — emotionally self-sufficient, low reliance on God for comfort, low proximity-seeking under stress.

**Schema correlation (Young):** Emotional Deprivation — most commonly co-present.

---

### 3. The Permissive/Safe God

**Relational origin:** Primary caregivers who were consistently affirming but unable to offer honest, boundaried love — who could not say no, could not name problems, or required the child to be fine. Religious environments emphasizing God's unconditional acceptance without the corresponding reality of God's holiness, His invitation to transformation, or His honest engagement with the person's actual condition. In a significant number of cases, this image is *secondary and defensive*: a constructed Permissive God that sits over a more threatening primary image (most often Punitive), functioning as a buffer against having to contend with the demanding one. Moving the permissive image too quickly in these clients can surface the image underneath.

**Presentation in coaching:** The client's spiritual life is pleasant, comfortable, and shallow. Scripture's harder texts are consistently softened or bypassed. There is no experience of God as particularly responsive to who the client actually is — no felt weight of holy invitation, no sense that God is actively engaged with the specific condition of this person's soul. God's love is experienced as broad, warm, and undirected: unconditional approval rather than transforming covenant love. The client typically does not experience anything as wrong. This makes the Permissive/Safe type the most difficult to surface — the presenting issue is not suffering but the absence of real encounter.

**Secondary presentation:** In clients where the Permissive image is defensive, pressing toward specificity in God's engagement will produce low-grade anxiety rather than curiosity or relief. If this pattern appears, ease off — the session note should record the secondary-layer hypothesis. Do not attempt to move the image without first acknowledging what it is protecting.

**Attachment pattern (Kirkpatrick):** Secure-appearing but relationally low-demand. God is expected to be present and benign, and that expectation is typically met — but the relationship does not activate deeply in either direction. This differs from earned security, which reflects genuine encounter, disruption, and repair. Here there is no vivid history of being specifically known, pursued, or challenged.

**Schema correlation (Young):** Entitlement/Grandiosity (in presentations where the permissive image validates self-sufficiency and low-demand spiritual life); Emotional Inhibition (in presentations where God's blanket approval reinforces surface-level relating); Subjugation (in presentations where the permissive image conceals the more threatening one underneath and the client has learned not to make demands of God).

---

### 4. The Contingent/Transactional God

**Relational origin:** Primary caregivers whose love, attention, or approval was conditional — available when the child performed, achieved, or complied; withdrawn when the child failed or disappointed. The relational template formed: relationship is earned, not given. Religious environments that emphasized right behavior, spiritual performance, or "if you do X, God will do Y" frameworks.

**Presentation in coaching:** The client frames their spiritual life around what they are or aren't doing. Prayer includes bargaining. Obedience is motivated by securing God's blessing or protection, not by love or gratitude. The client's sense of God's closeness fluctuates in direct proportion to their spiritual performance — close when doing well, distant when not. They are often outwardly productive and religious but internally exhausted and insecure about their standing with God. Grace is intellectually known but experientially foreign.

**Attachment pattern (Kirkpatrick):** Anxious/Ambivalent — approval-seeking, performance-driven, underlying insecurity about whether needs will be met.

**Schema correlation (Young):** Unrelenting Standards — most commonly co-present.

---

### 5. The Capricious/Unsafe God

**Relational origin:** Primary caregivers who were unpredictable, volatile, or frightening — alternating between warmth and hostility without discernible pattern. Environments marked by chaos, instability, abuse, or sudden loss. The relational template formed: relationships are fundamentally unpredictable and potentially dangerous; trust is risk that invites harm.

**Presentation in coaching:** The client maintains careful distance from deep intimacy with God. Trust is extended tentatively and revoked quickly after perceived disappointment. The client may speak of specific past experiences where "God let me down" in vivid, concrete terms. There is an underlying conviction that full trust is dangerous — something bad follows closeness. The client oscillates between moments of connection and deliberate spiritual self-protection. Faith feels like risk management. Declarations and prayer encounter are approached cautiously, with part of the client watching for evidence that this, too, will be unsafe.

**Attachment pattern (Kirkpatrick):** Disorganized — no consistent strategy for the attachment relationship; approach-avoidance conflict in the God-relationship.

**Schema correlation (Young):** Abandonment/Instability — most commonly co-present.

---

### 6. The Overwhelming/Consuming God

**Relational origin:** Primary caregivers whose presence was intrusive, enmeshing, or who could not tolerate the child's separateness or developing autonomy. Caregivers who needed to be close in a way that swallowed rather than held. Religious environments with intense experiential or charismatic dynamics where "the presence of God" was overwhelming and the self was expected to dissolve into it. Histories of spiritual overwhelm, loss of self in intense devotional settings, or coercive spiritual environments.

**Presentation in coaching:** The client genuinely desires intimacy with God — unlike the Absent/Distant type, this is not low expectation. But full encounter feels dangerous. Something is held back; access is carefully metered. Extended worship, intense prayer, or certain types of spiritual community feel destabilizing — not because God is absent, but because His presence feels like something that could consume the self. The client may describe vivid past encounters followed immediately by protective withdrawal. They can articulate a desire for more and simultaneously be relieved when the encounter ends. Faith is real; its container is small by necessity. May verbalize fear of "losing myself in God" or express that full surrender feels threatening rather than restful.

**Attachment pattern (Kirkpatrick):** Avoidant under conditions of perceived threat from the attachment figure — distinct from the Absent/Distant avoidant pattern (which reflects low proximity-seeking due to low expectation of return). Here the threat is not absence but presence itself, which is experienced as dangerous to the self's integrity.

**Schema correlation (Young):** Enmeshment/Undeveloped Self — most commonly co-present. Sometimes co-present with Defectiveness/Shame when the underlying fear is that full exposure to God would confirm unworthiness.

## The Three Practitioner Errors

These are the most common coach responses to a surfaced God-representation that undermine rather than advance the work.

### Error 1: Theological Correction

When the client describes God as punishing or absent, the coach's first instinct is often to provide the correct theological view: "But God is loving and present — here's what Scripture says." This addresses the wrong layer. The client almost certainly *knows* the correct theology already. The living God is not a theological error to be corrected — it is a psychic structure to be worked with. Theological correction skips past the representation entirely and implicitly communicates that the client's experience is wrong, which typically produces shame rather than movement.

**The alternative:** Receive the client's experience of God without correction or reframe. Acknowledge the living God as the real relational reality the client is operating from. Stay with curiosity before introducing any content.

---

### Error 2: Forcing Premature Resolution

Phase 1 surfaces the representation. Phase 2 and Phase 3 work with its origins and begin the installation work. The coach who moves too quickly from "you experience God as punishing" to "now let's declare the truth about God" collapses the developmental sequence and bypasses the relational work that must precede transformation.

The representation does not change through declaration alone. It changes through: (a) making the implicit explicit — becoming conscious of the living God, (b) tracing its relational origins without shame, and (c) encountering God as genuinely different than the representation predicts, through felt relational experience, not information. Declarations offered before this relational groundwork is done tend to sit on top of the unchanged living God rather than reaching it.

**The alternative:** Hold the representation with patience. It is accurate data about the client's formation history. Treating it as an obstacle to eliminate quickly communicates that the client's experience is a problem. Treating it as meaningful developmental material communicates dignity and creates safety.

---

### Error 3: Treating the Theological God and the Living God as the Same Conversation

Because the client uses the same word ("God") for both their living God and their theological God, coaches often fail to distinguish which layer is operative in a given moment. The client may shift between explicit theology ("I know God loves me") and implicit experience ("but I never really feel that") within the same sentence. Treating these as equivalent creates confusion and misses the real terrain.

**The alternative:** Learn to track which layer is speaking. Explicit theology tends to arrive as assertion: *"I know..."* Implicit experience tends to arrive as felt-sense, contradiction, or flatness: *"I know, but..."* The word "but" is often the seam between the two. Name the distinction when it appears — not as a clinical analysis, but as a soft, curious observation. "You know it, and something else is happening underneath that knowing. Can we stay with what's underneath for a moment?"

---

### Error 4: Prescribing Solitary Contemplative Practice Without Considering Attachment Type

The most common between-session prescription in faith formation contexts is "spend more time in prayer and contemplation." For most clients, this is reasonable. For the Absent/Distant God type — who corresponds to avoidant attachment — it may be counterproductive. Research on meditation and attachment insecurity shows that individuals with high avoidant attachment report *increased loneliness* with more meditation frequency. Solitary contemplative practice can reinforce the very pattern that produced the absent God-representation rather than healing it.

The avoidant strategy is emotional self-sufficiency and low reliance on others. More time alone in silent practice may deepen that strategy rather than disrupt it. Relational grounding appears to be a *precondition* for contemplative practice to produce connection — not an outcome of it.

The Contingent/Transactional type faces a different version of this error: spiritual disciplines assigned without reframing can be absorbed into the performance frame. The client who is already exhausted by earning God's favor will apply the same structure to between-session practices, turning them into a spiritual to-do list. The practice becomes another arena for the transaction.

**The alternative:** Differentiate between-session prescriptions by God-representation type. The Between-Session Support Materials document carries type-specific practice guidance. Review it before assigning any between-session practice. For the Absent/Distant type: prioritize active, directed address over receptive silence in early phases. Contemplative practice for this type follows relational grounding — it does not precede it.

## The Deepening Process: Working with the Representation Over Time

The God-representation is active throughout the entire engagement, not only in Phase 1. It should be tracked through all four phases and named explicitly in the Growth Report.

### Stage 1 — Surfacing and Naming (Phase 1)

The Phase 1 session tools guide this process. The coach's job here is to create enough safety for the client's living God to be spoken aloud. This requires receiving it without correction or alarm. Questions that surface how the client *expects* God to respond (to shame, to silence, to need) reveal the living God more reliably than direct "describe your view of God" questions.

**Coach task:** Listen for the psychic object beneath the theological language. Note the affect that accompanies God-talk — flatness, anxiety, guardedness, warmth, grief. Identify the primary representation type. Note secondary features.

**Coach constraint:** Do not correct, reassure, or move prematurely to covenant identity language.

---

### Stage 2 — Tracing the Relational Origins (Phase 2)

The God-representation is built from relational material. Phase 2 (Wound Mapping) excavates the formation experiences that constructed it. The connection between caregiving patterns and God-representation type is reliable (see schema correlation table below) — the coach holds this awareness without imposing it.

The client often makes the connection themselves when given space. If the client says "my father was never emotionally available," the coach holds the awareness that this is likely also the raw material for an absent God — and waits for the client to move toward that connection rather than drawing it explicitly.

**Coach task:** Make space for the relational history to surface in connection with the God-representation. Hold the developmental awareness without functionally practicing as a therapist.

---

### Stage 3 — Creating New Relational Experience (Phase 3)

This is where Hall's neurological rewiring framework is most applicable. The implicit God-image changes through new relational experience — not new information. In Phase 3, this occurs through:

1. **Declaration as relational encounter.** Declaration is not primarily about speaking correct propositions. It is about allowing the client to meet the God the declaration describes and noticing what happens somatically and emotionally when they do. The resistance that arises in declaration is the implicit God-image in real time — it is diagnostic data, not failure.

2. **Lament as honest address.** Lament gives the client permission to speak to God *from* the living God — the punishing, absent, or contingent God the client actually experiences. The client who can say *"You feel absent to me"* to God is in contact with their living God in a way that creates the condition for encounter. Brueggemann's orientation-disorientation-new orientation arc is the theological structure for what Hall describes as neurological rewiring: the attachment system must encounter something genuinely different than it expects.

3. **Prayer posture tracking.** The client's prayer life between sessions is a live laboratory for God-representation work. Ask what they expected when they brought something to God, and what happened. Treat their prayer encounters as relational data worth examining session to session.

**Coach task:** Create space for the client to encounter God as different than their representation predicts. This is not engineering spiritual experience. It is naming what the client notices when they encounter God directly and treating that as real relational data about a representation in motion.

**The theological premise of this work:** Hall's rewiring framework requires an attuned other — a relational presence that responds differently than the implicit template predicts. The Covenant Identity model operates on the premise that the Holy Spirit is precisely that Other. The *paraklētos* ("one called alongside," John 14:16) is not a theological background assumption — the Spirit is the active agent of the transformation being pursued. Prayer that is genuinely receptive and relational, rather than cognitive and performative, constitutes encounter with a real Person who is specifically described as the Spirit of adoption bearing witness *with* the client's spirit (Romans 8:15-16). This means between-session practices — directed lament, embodied declaration, expectant prayer — are not self-directed in the secular sense. They are encounters with an attuned Other who is present and active. Hall's ceiling on self-directed practice does not fully apply to prayer understood in these terms.

**The precondition for between-session practice:** Between-session work extends what the coaching relationship initiates — it does not replace it or initiate transformation from scratch. The relational container established in the coaching relationship is the prerequisite that makes the client's between-session practice effective rather than circling. Assign practices from within that secure base, and differentiate them by God-representation type. See Between-Session Support Materials for type-specific guidance.

---

### Stage 4 — Marking the Shift (Phase 4)

Transformation of the God-representation often becomes visible before the client can articulate it. The coach who has been tracking the living God throughout the engagement will observe shifts in language, posture, and prayer before the client names them. Name what you observe. Growth amnesia will erase it without documentation.

**Indicators that the God-representation is shifting:**
- God-language moves from third-person description to second-person address
- Prayer reports move from obligatory or flat to relational and expectant
- The client distinguishes between a past God-experience and a present one without prompting
- Lament shifts to gratitude or trust without the coach facilitating the move
- The client reports encountering God differently than they expected
- Declarations no longer produce the same somatic resistance they produced earlier in the engagement
- The client refers to God in terms of presence or welcome rather than evaluation

**The Growth Report should name this.** If the God-representation has shifted, it is one of the most significant transformations in the engagement. Name it in before/after language drawn directly from session notes.

## Type-Specific Coaching Approaches

### Working with the Punishing God

**Core task:** Distinguish God's holiness from God's present disposition toward the client. The client has collapsed these — they experience God as chronically displeased. The theological truth (justification, adoption, the finished work of Christ) is likely cognitively known; the living God has not received it.

**Approaches:**
- Follow the guilt. Ask what the client expects God will do with it. Let the psychic structure surface fully before offering any reframe.
- Invite lament from the shame. "Is there something you'd want to say to God about what it's like to live under his evaluation?" Anger at the punishing God is not spiritual failure — it is honest contact with the living God and creates the conditions for encounter.
- Use adoption and justification language not as correction but as wonder and invitation: "What would it mean to you — not theologically, but in your body — if that is how God actually sees you right now?"
- Declaration with this type is most generative when it names the gap explicitly: "The declared truth is X. When I say it, I notice [anxiety / flatness / disbelief]. That's what we're working with."

**Avoid:** Reassurance loops ("But God loves you!"), Scripture as answer to unexplored experience, minimizing the client's guilt response as "just feelings."

---

### Working with the Absent/Distant God

**Core task:** Rebuild expectation. The absent God produces low anticipation — the client has stopped seeking because seeking has historically not worked. The work is not to generate warmth the client doesn't have, but to take the grief beneath the flatness seriously as an alive edge.

**Approaches:**
- Ask about moments when the absence was most painful. The grief underneath the flatness indicates relational longing that has been disappointed. Grief means they want contact. That is workable.
- Introduce "the God who pursues" as experiential question rather than doctrinal statement: "What would it be like if God had been actively seeking you during the time you experienced him as absent?"
- Simple between-session prayer assignment: one honest sentence — not asking for anything, not performing — just naming the absence. "I don't feel you." Treating that honesty as relational contact is itself a reorientation.
- Track prayer quality across sessions. Movement from flat to expectant, from one-directional to conversational, is measurable transformation. Name it.

**Avoid:** Manufacturing experiences of God's presence, dismissing spiritual dryness as a perception error, expecting the client to generate warmth that isn't organically present yet.

---

### Working with the Permissive/Safe God

**Core task:** Name the comfort and name the cost — without creating fear as the corrective. The client is not suffering around this representation; the entry is curiosity rather than alarm. The goal is not to replace the permissive image with a threatening one but to introduce the fuller, more real God: holy and tender held together, whose love is pursuing and transforming, not merely affirming.

**Approaches:**
- Begin with the distinction between broad approval and specific engagement: *"Is there a difference between knowing God loves everyone and knowing God is responding to you in particular?"* This is an opening question, not a challenge. Let the client sit with it.
- Invite God's honest engagement without framing it as confrontation: *"What would it mean for God to have a specific response to something in your life — not good or bad, just specific to you?"*
- Ask what God seems to have no opinion about: *"Is there something you'd actually want God to weigh in on — where it matters to you whether He responds?"* This surfaces longing underneath the comfortable surface.
- Use Gospel encounter texts that show Jesus loving specifically and saying something that cost the person something (Mark 10:17–22; John 21:15–17). Frame these not as "God will confront you" but as "God is specifically engaged with who you are and where you're headed."
- If the permissive image is defensive, the coaching will feel unusually stable and flat until the underlying layer surfaces. Watch for low-grade anxiety when pressing toward specificity in God's engagement. If it appears, name it lightly — *"Something shifted when I asked that. What was that?"* — and slow down. Note the secondary-layer hypothesis in session notes.

**Avoid:** Manufacturing distress the client doesn't have. Exchanging the permissive image for a punitive one as the corrective — that is not transformation, it is substituting one distortion for another. Dismissing the client's comfort as denial — the work is to deepen and enrich, not displace.

---

### Working with the Contingent/Transactional God

**Core task:** Disrupt the transaction. The client relates to God through performance and expects God to reciprocate through blessing or protection. Grace — unconditional, non-earned, non-transactional — is cognitively known but experientially foreign. The work is to make grace felt, not just believed.

**Approaches:**
- Name the exhaustion first. "What is the cost of maintaining that relationship?" The transactional God is exhausting; the client often hasn't allowed themselves to name this.
- Ask what it would mean if nothing they do or don't do changes God's posture toward them right now. This typically surfaces the Contingent God at full intensity — protest, disbelief, or relief. All three are workable.
- Experiment framing: ask the client to bring God something they failed at — not to confess it, but simply to observe God's response. What did they expect? What happened?
- Identity vote framing: "What would a child of God who knows they're fully accepted do in this situation?" — not as motivational pressure, but as exploration of what a non-transactional relationship with God would feel like in practice.

**Avoid:** Replacing behavioral performance with spiritual/emotional performance ("at least feel grateful"), suggesting grace is simple or easy without honoring how counterintuitive it is for this type.

---

### Working with the Capricious/Unsafe God

**Core task:** Slow pacing and safety. The capricious God produces approach-avoidance conflict — the client wants closeness but trusts it minimally. Do not push for intimacy on a timeline. The first priority is establishing the coaching relationship itself as consistently safe, predictable, and honest. That consistency becomes a relational template the client can begin to generalize.

**Approaches:**
- Never surprise the client. Name what is coming. Be consistent in approach, tone, and follow-through. The coach's own relational predictability is therapeutic data for this type — Kirkpatrick's correspondence hypothesis works in both directions.
- Explicitly give the client permission to go slowly in their relationship with God. "You don't have to trust God more than is actually real for you right now." This often produces visible relief, which is itself a small safe-haven experience.
- Introduce small, bounded experiments: "One moment this week — not sustained prayer, just one brief acknowledgment of God's presence. Notice what you expect to happen next."
- When the client reports a moment of connection followed by withdrawal or something difficult, take it seriously. "So when you got close, something happened that made it feel risky again. Let's stay there." Do not explain it away.

**Avoid:** Pushing for declarations requiring trust the client doesn't yet have, framing spiritual progress as contingent on emotional vulnerability, dismissing the client's self-protection as lack of faith.

---

### Working with the Overwhelming/Consuming God

**Core task:** Establish encounter safety before encounter depth. The client's goal is not to avoid God — it is to gradually expand the container in which they can receive God without self-dissolution. Progress looks like small encounters that hold, not large encounters that overwhelm.

**Approaches:**
- Assign short, bounded practices with explicit endings. One verse, five minutes of prayer, a brief meditation with a clear stop. The client needs to know the encounter has a container — and that they retain agency over when it closes.
- Frame the theology of encounter explicitly: *"Full union with God does not dissolve you. Covenantal union makes you more fully yourself, not less. He is not coming to consume what He made — He is coming to complete it."* (Jer 31:33; John 17:22–23)
- Do not interpret approach-avoidance as avoidance of God per se. Name it as the old protection pattern doing what it learned to do — protect the self from being swallowed. It is not faithlessness; it is a relational survival strategy applied to the wrong relationship.
- In session: offer regulated, predictable presence rather than intense personal attention. Keep proximity consistent; do not shift toward sudden warmth or intensity when the client seems to open. Predictability is the therapeutic data.
- Phase-specific questions: *"When you feel like you're getting close to God — what do you expect comes next?"* (Phase 1) | *"What did you learn about closeness in your family — what happened when you were too visible, too present?"* (Phase 2) | *"What would it mean for you — not in theory — if being fully seen by God was safe?"* (Phase 3)

**Avoid:** Ignatian Gospel imagination until encounter safety is established (full imaginative immersion is contraindicated early). Extended group prayer or worship formats where the client cannot self-regulate. Framing full spiritual surrender as the goal before the safety condition is met — doing so replicates the threat rather than interrupting it.

## Question Library

### Surfacing the Living God (Phase 1)
- "When you've sinned in a way that really troubled you, what did you expect when you went to God with it?"
- "How does it feel to come to God when you're struggling — anticipatory? Heavy? Uncertain?"
- "If you could describe the relational temperature between you and God right now — not what you believe, but what you actually sense — what would you say?"
- "Have you ever felt genuinely close to God? What was that like? What changed?"
- "When you pray, who do you feel like you're addressing?"
- "Do you expect God to show up for you personally? Where does that expectation come from?"
- "Is there a version of God you were taught growing up that you've never fully left behind?"

### Tracing the Relational Origins (Phase 2)
- "Who in your early life did you most need to earn love from?"
- "Was there someone whose emotional availability you could never quite rely on? What did you learn to do with that?"
- "What was discipline like in your home? What did you carry away from that?"
- "Is there a relationship that shaped what you expect from God more than any other?"
- "When you imagine God looking at you, whose eyes does he have?"

### Working with the Living God (Phase 3)
- "When you say that declaration, what does your body expect God to think of you for saying it?"
- "If you said to God directly: 'You feel distant to me right now' — what do you imagine God does with that?"
- "What would it mean to you — not in theory, in your body — if the way God sees you right now is exactly what that verse says?"
- "When the part of you that's performing shows up, what is it afraid would happen if it stopped?"
- "Is there something you want to say to God that you've been holding back? What's prevented you from saying it?"
- "What do you actually expect God to do with your pain?"
- "Has there been a moment in these sessions where God felt different than your expectation? What was that like?"

### Tracking Transformation (Phase 4)
- "How has the way you approach God changed over these sessions?"
- "When you're struggling now, what do you expect from God? Compare that to where you started."
- "What's different about how you pray now than when we began?"
- "Where do you feel the most significant shift in how you experience God — not believe, experience?"
- "What would you want someone to know about who God actually is to you now?"

## Integration Notes

### What feeds this tool
- **Phase 1 Session Tools** — the God-representation is first surfaced and named there; this tool is the practitioner's guide for what to do with what is surfaced
- **Intake Addendum Part B** — fields A5, A8, and A10 carry early relational data and the present-story statement, both of which contain God-representation material
- **Session Growth Notes** — God-representation shifts should be tracked in the Spiritual Formation Markers field each session

### What this tool feeds
- **Phase 2 (Wound Mapping)** — the God-representation type points directly to the relational formation material Phase 2 will excavate
- **Phase 3 (Identity Installation)** — declaration resistance, lament work, and lament-to-trust movement all engage the living God directly; this tool provides the conceptual structure for that engagement
- **Between-Session Support Materials** — the type-specific between-session practice guidance for God-representation work is housed there; this tool identifies which type is operative, which informs which between-session prescriptions are appropriate and which are contraindicated
- **Growth Report** — the God-representation shift, named in before/after language drawn from session notes, is among the most significant outcomes of the engagement and warrants explicit narration
