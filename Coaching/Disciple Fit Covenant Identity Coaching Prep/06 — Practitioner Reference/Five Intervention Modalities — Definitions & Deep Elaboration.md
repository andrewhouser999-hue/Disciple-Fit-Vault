# Five Intervention Modalities — Definitions & Deep Elaboration

---

## Quick Definitions

**1. Targeted Declaration Work**
Deliberate, repeated verbal or written assertion of a specific truth to counteract an identified lie. Unlike generic affirmation, it is *targeted* — aimed at a named belief, wound, or agreement. The declaration is theologically grounded and personally specific. Mechanism: repetition rewires automatic appraisal (neuroplasticity + schema restructuring).

**2. Schema Interruption Practice**
A schema is a deep, automatic belief about self, others, or the world — formed early, triggered fast, felt as fact. Schema interruption is a real-time practice of catching the schema activating and inserting a pause, a counter-response, or a reframe before the automatic behavioral sequence completes. Drawn from Schema Therapy (Young).

**3. Narrative Re-authoring**
From Narrative Therapy (White & Epston). The person is not the problem — the story they've internalized is. Re-authoring externalizes the dominant problem-saturated narrative and deliberately constructs an alternative story using *exception events* (times the problem didn't win) as evidence. The client becomes the author, not the subject.

**4. Lament-to-Trust Movement**
A biblically-structured emotional arc: naming pain honestly before God (lament — Psalms, Job, Jeremiah) as the path *into* trust rather than around it. It is not toxic positivity or suppression. It treats honest grief as faith-compatible and holds that full expression of anguish is what clears the interior space for trust to land.

**5. Big Assumption Experiment**
From Immunity to Change (Kegan & Lahey). Once a competing commitment and its underlying Big Assumption are identified (e.g., *"If I stop controlling everything, disaster will follow"*), the client designs a small, low-risk behavioral test to gather real data about whether the assumption is actually true. It converts an untested belief into an empirical question.

## Deep Elaborations

---

### 1. Targeted Declaration Work

#### What separates it from affirmation

Generic affirmations ("I am enough," "I am worthy") operate on the surface. Targeted declarations are surgical — they name the specific lie, address it directly, and deploy the specific counter-truth. This requires accurate diagnosis first. You cannot target what you haven't identified. The declaration is downstream of lie-detection work, not a shortcut past it.

#### The neurological mechanism

The brain is a prediction machine. It builds habitual grooves through repetition — whatever fires together, wires together (Hebbian learning). A lie that has been rehearsed internally for 20 years has thick neural infrastructure. A declaration spoken once doesn't compete with that. What changes it is *directed repetition over time* — consistently activating the counter-pathway until it begins to run automatically. This is not a feeling-based process. The declaration should be practiced before it feels true, not after.

Speaking declarations aloud matters more than thinking them. Vocalization recruits additional neural networks — auditory cortex, motor cortex, embodied proprioception — making the truth more multi-dimensionally encoded.

#### The biblical distinction

This is not New Age affirmation, which draws on the self as source. Declarations are alignment with external revealed truth. The source is God's word about reality, not the person's preference about themselves. This distinction is load-bearing: it grounds the practice in something that doesn't fluctuate with mood, circumstance, or self-perception. The person isn't generating truth — they're rehearsing it until it lands.

#### In session

The declaration isn't assigned like homework and left alone. A skilled coach observes what the client actually does with it: do they read it flatly? Rush through it? Tear up? Laugh nervously? The body's response to a declaration tells you how much the lie still owns the territory. That response is diagnostic data for the next session.

### 2. Schema Interruption Practice

#### What a schema actually is

Jeffrey Young extended Beck's cognitive therapy to address deep, chronic, pervasive patterns rather than situational distorted thoughts. A schema is not a thought — it is an *emotional-cognitive-behavioral system* built around an unmet core need in early development (safety, stable attachment, autonomy, esteem, freedom to express emotion). It functions like a lens that distorts incoming data to confirm itself.

Young identified 18 maladaptive schemas organized into 5 domains. A few examples relevant to your client population:
- **Defectiveness/Shame** — "I am fundamentally flawed; if people really knew me, they'd reject me"
- **Abandonment** — "People I depend on will leave or be inconsistent"
- **Failure** — "I am incompetent; I will inevitably fail compared to peers"
- **Emotional Deprivation** — "My emotional needs will never be met by others"
- **Subjugation** — "I must suppress my desires and emotions to avoid punishment or abandonment"

#### Why schemas are hard to shift with simple cognitive techniques

Standard CBT challenges individual thoughts. Schemas operate deeper — they feel like *facts about reality*, not beliefs. When activated, they flood the person with emotion so fast that rational counter-argument arrives too late. The person isn't choosing the schema; it's running them.

There are three common schema responses:
1. **Surrender** — fully going along with it ("of course I'm a failure, of course she'll leave")
2. **Avoidance** — numbing, distraction, withdrawing from schema-activating situations
3. **Overcompensation** — rigidly doing the opposite (the person with a defectiveness schema who performs relentlessly)

All three *maintain* the schema while giving the illusion of managing it.

#### What interruption targets

The gap between schema activation and behavioral response. Activation happens fast — often in under a second, usually felt somatically before it's consciously labeled. Interruption practice trains a person to:
1. Recognize their activation signals (tightness in chest, urge to shrink, sudden numbness, etc.)
2. Name what's running ("that's my abandonment schema — this is old data")
3. Insert a pause before the automatic response

That pause is where choice lives. The goal is not to eliminate schema activation — that takes years of deep work — but to stop it from automatically running the behavioral sequence.

#### In your framework

Schemas are the psychological infrastructure that makes lies *plausible*. A person with a defectiveness schema doesn't just encounter the lie "I am worthless" — they live in an emotional climate where it feels self-evidently true. Lie elimination work that doesn't address the schema operating underneath it will be constantly undermined. The schema keeps generating fresh evidence for the lie.

### 3. Narrative Re-authoring

#### The theoretical foundation

Michael White and David Epston, drawing on Foucault and Jerome Bruner: human beings don't experience life directly — they experience it through *story*. The stories we tell about ourselves determine which events we notice, which we discount, which we remember, and what we think they mean. The problem is never just the event; it's the dominant narrative that has formed around it.

Foucault's influence: dominant narratives often carry cultural or relational power — they were authored by someone else (a parent, a traumatic event, a community's verdict) and internalized as objective truth. Re-authoring recovers the person's own authorship.

#### Externalization

The foundational move in narrative work. The problem is *separated* from the person's identity: not "I am an anxious person" but "anxiety has been telling me..." — not "I am a failure" but "failure has been a story I've been living under."

This is not denial or blame-shifting. It's a precision move: it creates enough distance between the person and the problem that they can examine it, speak back to it, and make choices about it. You cannot fight something you believe you *are*.

#### Unique outcomes (exception events)

The dominant narrative is maintained by selective attention — the person keeps finding evidence for it. Re-authoring searches for *exceptions*: moments when the problem didn't win, when the person acted contrary to the story, when something different happened.

These exceptions are often already present in the client's experience — they've just been discounted ("that doesn't count," "I got lucky," "it won't last"). The coach's job is to find them, surface them, and *thicken* them by asking questions that give them weight and meaning:
- "What does this moment tell you about who you actually are?"
- "Who else has witnessed this in you?"
- "What would you call this quality?"
- "How long has this been true of you, even if you haven't named it?"

#### The biblical dimension

This is where narrative re-authoring becomes distinctly powerful in a faith context. Scripture is full of radical narrative revision: Abram → Abraham, Jacob (deceiver) → Israel (one who wrestles with God), Simon → Peter. God himself is the re-author who names people into a new story before the evidence is visible. The gospel is itself a cosmic re-authoring — humanity's story of exile and death rewritten through incarnation and resurrection.

The danger to guard against: re-authoring divorced from truth can become therapeutic fiction — a new story that feels better but isn't more accurate. Grounding the alternative narrative in Scripture and the specific work of God in the client's life prevents this. The new story has to be *true*, not just preferred.

### 4. Lament-to-Trust Movement

#### The theology of lament

Roughly 40% of the Psalms are lament psalms. Job is an extended lament. Jeremiah is called the weeping prophet. Lamentations is an entire book of grief. The pattern is not incidental — it's structural to the biblical witness. Honest complaint before God is a form of covenant engagement, not a breach of it.

The structure of lament psalms is almost always the same:
1. **Address** — direct cry to God ("O Lord," "My God")
2. **Complaint** — raw description of suffering ("I am poured out like water")
3. **Confession of trust** — often a sudden turn ("Yet you are holy")
4. **Petition** — specific request for intervention
5. **Vow/praise** — commitment to testify

The *turn* is the pivotal move. It doesn't erase the complaint — it holds the complaint inside a framework of trust. Psalm 22 opens "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" and ends with "He has not despised or scorned the suffering of the afflicted one; he has not hidden his face from him but has listened to his cry for help." The full arc is in one psalm.

#### Walter Brueggemann's framework

Brueggemann describes three postures: Orientation (settled, ordered life) → Disorientation (suffering, loss, chaos) → Reorientation (new, deeper relationship with God). Lament belongs to disorientation. The critical insight: *you cannot skip from disorientation to reorientation*. You must pass through lament. Attempting to jump directly to praise produces what Brueggemann calls "cheap grace" — a brittle, performative faith that has not been tested by honest engagement with darkness.

#### Spiritual bypassing

Ken Wilber's term for using spiritual language and practice to avoid emotional reality. It looks like faith but functions as avoidance: "I just trust God" used to suppress grief rather than carry it into God's presence. Spiritual bypassing produces people who appear spiritually stable but are emotionally frozen — and who eventually either crack or go numb altogether.

Lament is the anti-bypass move. It insists that emotional reality and spiritual engagement *must* occupy the same space.

#### The somatic dimension

Grief and lament have a physiological arc. The body processes emotion through completion cycles — the wave rises, peaks, and falls. When lament is suppressed (by theology that treats it as faithlessness, by performance pressure, by shame about emotion), the wave is interrupted mid-cycle. The body stores what was not completed. This shows up as numbness, chronic low-level anxiety, physical tension, spiritual flatness.

Facilitating lament to completion is partly a somatic act — creating a safe enough container that the body can finish what it started.

#### Why it leads to trust, not away from it

The act of crying out to God *assumes* that God exists, that He hears, and that He is somehow accountable to the relationship. Lament is an argument made to Someone. It contains trust structurally, even when it doesn't feel like it. The person who rages at God in grief is more relationally connected to God than the person who has gone silent in numb resignation.

### 5. Big Assumption Experiment

#### The full Immunity to Change architecture

Kegan and Lahey's research identified why intelligent, motivated people consistently fail to make changes they genuinely want to make. The answer: they are running an *immune system* against the change — a hidden competing commitment that makes the status quo feel necessary for survival.

The full four-column map:

| Column 1 | Column 2 | Column 3 | Column 4 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Visible commitment | Behaviors that work against it | Hidden competing commitment | Big Assumption |
| "I want to delegate more" | "I keep taking tasks back" | "I'm committed to nothing going wrong" | "If I let go, something will fail and I'll be blamed" |

Column 3 is the key discovery. The client isn't failing — they're *succeeding* at a different goal than the stated one. The competing commitment is not sabotage; it is protection. It feels necessary.

#### What a Big Assumption is and why it's sticky

The Big Assumption is the belief that makes Column 3 feel mandatory. It usually has the form: *"If X, then catastrophic Y."* It functions as a non-negotiable premise about reality — and because it's never been consciously identified or tested, it has never been falsified. It operates at the level of immune reflex.

The reason argument doesn't work against Big Assumptions: you cannot argue someone out of a belief they've never consciously held. Telling someone their assumption is wrong just triggers defensiveness. The immune system fights back.

#### The experiment logic

The experiment doesn't try to prove the assumption false — that's too high a bar and creates pressure. It tries to gather *data*. The question shifts from "Is your assumption wrong?" to "What would a small test tell you about whether it's actually true?"

A good experiment is:
- **Small and specific** — not "delegate more" but "leave one specific task to Sarah this week without checking in"
- **Time-limited** — a defined window with a defined observation point
- **Low-stakes** — designed to be survivable if the assumption turns out to have partial validity
- **Data-generating** — what did actually happen? What does that suggest about the assumption?

One experiment rarely demolishes a Big Assumption. But one piece of disconfirming data creates a crack. The client learns that the assumption is *not* a law of physics — it's a hypothesis that can be tested. That shift alone is significant.

#### The theological convergence

Big Assumptions in your client population are almost always also theological claims. "If I stop performing, I will be rejected" is simultaneously a psychological prediction and a statement about whether grace is real. "If I express my needs, people will leave" encodes a belief about whether God's design for relationship can be trusted.

The Lie Eliminator and the Big Assumption often describe the same belief from two angles: the lie is the theological framing, the Big Assumption is the operational behavioral logic. Working both simultaneously is more effective than working either alone — the experiment generates behavioral evidence; the declaration addresses the belief's spiritual root.

## Document Provenance

**What this document is:** The practitioner-facing definition and mechanism reference for the five explicit-layer intervention modalities deployed in Covenant Identity Coaching. It serves two functions: (1) giving coaches enough theoretical depth to deploy each modality with precision rather than as a technique; (2) establishing the scope boundaries for coaching-appropriate vs. clinical use of each modality.

**Position in the system:** This document sits at the explicit intervention layer — downstream of the diagnostic sequence (Affections Discovery → Wound Identification → Lie/Schema Surfacing) and upstream of the session tools and worksheets that deploy these modalities with clients. It is practitioner reference, not client-facing.

**Theoretical sources:**
- Jeffrey Young (*Schema Therapy*, Young, Klosko & Weishaar, 2003) — Schema Interruption mechanism; 18-schema taxonomy; schema response modes
- Michael White & David Epston (*Narrative Means to Therapeutic Ends*, 1990) — Narrative Re-authoring; externalization; unique outcomes; Foucault influence
- Robert Kegan & Lisa Lahey (*Immunity to Change*, Harvard Business Review Press, 2009) — Big Assumption Experiment; four-column competing commitments architecture
- Walter Brueggemann (*The Message of the Psalms*, 1984) — Lament-to-Trust movement; orientation/disorientation/reorientation structure
- Claus Westermann (*Praise and Lament in the Psalms*, 1981) — Structural lament analysis; five-movement arc
- Curt Thompson (*Anatomy of the Soul*, 2010) — Neurological basis for declaration work; implicit encoding; vocalization effect
- Donald Hebb (1949) — Hebbian learning principle: neural pathway formation through repetition
- Ken Wilber — Spiritual bypassing concept (used in Section 4, Lament)

**Deployment context:** All five modalities were developed in clinical or organizational contexts. Their deployment in coaching operates under different conditions (non-clinical population, coaching scope, different practitioner training). See Anticipated Critiques for implications.

## Anticipated Critiques

### Critique 1 — Deployment Conditions: Clinical modalities in coaching context change the evidence base

**Steelmanned form:** Schema Therapy (Young) requires trained therapists working with personality disorders; Narrative Therapy (White & Epston) was developed for entrenched clinical presentations; Immunity to Change (Kegan & Lahey) was developed for organizational leadership development, not personal identity formation. None were developed for or validated in life coaching contexts. Deploying these modalities in coaching changes the client population (non-clinical), the practitioner's training and scope, the depth and duration of the container, and what the practitioner can do when something goes wrong. The clinical evidence that these modalities work does not describe the actual coaching deployment conditions. A coach using these tools is borrowing evidence from a context that doesn't match their own.

**Response:** The critique correctly identifies the evidence gap — and this document makes no claim that clinical evidence transfers directly to coaching. The modalities are used because their mechanism descriptions are coherent with the coaching context, and because the theoretical basis for their efficacy is not exclusively context-specific: schemas operate in all populations, narrative shapes identity across all settings, Big Assumptions drive behavior in organizational and personal contexts alike, and lament is a biblical practice rather than a clinical protocol. The deployment in this model is scope-bounded: Schema Interruption is used for schema-awareness and pause-gap training, not schema restructuring; Narrative Re-authoring is used for exception-event identification, not traumatic narrative processing; Big Assumption Experiment generates bounded behavioral data within a coaching engagement, not deep immune system change. These scope distinctions are the difference between appropriate and inappropriate coaching use of each modality. See the [[Covenant Identity — Crisis & Referral Protocol]] for the scope boundary and escalation signals.

**Remaining exposure:** Without coaching-specific empirical validation, the mechanism-level effectiveness claims for these modalities in coaching contexts remain theoretical. Practitioners should hold them as theoretically grounded and clinically plausible, not as empirically validated coaching protocols. The scope distinctions described above are essential — practitioners who collapse coaching-appropriate use into clinical-scope use will both harm clients and misrepresent their competency.

## Load-Bearing vs. Modifiable

**Load-bearing** — changing these changes what the model is:

- *The five modalities as intervention coverage* — the five are selected because they address the explicit change layer at five points the diagnostic sequence produces: identified lie (Declaration), schema infrastructure (Interruption), distorted story (Re-authoring), blocked emotional access (Lament), behavioral avoidance of an untested assumption (Experiment). Remove any one and a corresponding intervention gap opens.
- *Declaration as alignment with external truth, not affirmation* — the distinction between God's revealed word as source and the self as source is theologically and mechanistically load-bearing. Generic affirmation has a different mechanism and different theological basis; if this distinction blurs, the practice loses both its biblical grounding and its efficacy claim.
- *Schema interruption as coaching scope (awareness/pause), not clinical scope (restructuring)* — the scope boundary is not a preference. Practitioners who use schema interruption as schema restructuring are exceeding scope. This distinction determines what the practitioner is authorized to do and what the client is being asked to undertake.
- *Lament as covenant engagement, not emotional processing technique* — the biblical framework is not decorative. Lament is described here as a form of honest address to God within a covenant relationship. Reframe it as a breathing exercise or emotional regulation technique and it loses its theological grounding.

**Modifiable** — can change without changing what the model is:

- *Specific source attribution* — Young, White & Epston, Kegan & Lahey, Brueggemann are the current primary sources. If more precise or better-validated accounts of any of these mechanisms emerged, the attribution could be updated without changing the modality.
- *Number of modalities* — five is not a theoretically derived number. A sixth modality addressing an identified explicit-layer gap could be added without restructuring the existing five.
- *Elaboration depth per modality* — the deep elaboration sections are practitioner reference material; they can be revised, expanded, or reorganized without changing the model's use of the modality.
