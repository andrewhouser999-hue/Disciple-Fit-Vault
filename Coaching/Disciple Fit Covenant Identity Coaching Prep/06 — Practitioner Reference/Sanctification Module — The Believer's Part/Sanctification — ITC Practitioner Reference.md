# IMMUNITY TO CHANGE — PRACTITIONER REFERENCE
## Covenant Identity Coaching — Disciple Fit
### Coach-Facing | Full Protocol

---

## I. WHAT THE IMMUNITY TO CHANGE MAP IS

Robert Kegan and Lisa Laskow Lahey developed the Immunity to Change framework at Harvard to address a specific and persistent problem: people do not change even when they genuinely want to, have clear goals, understand what is needed, and possess the will to try. The reason is not lack of information, motivation, or character. The reason is structural.

The system is *immune* to change — actively working against the stated goal in order to protect something the person values even more, without knowing they are doing it. The competing commitment is hidden not because the person is deceptive but because it operates below conscious awareness, encoded as an assumption so fundamental it functions like a law of nature.

The four-column map surfaces the structure:

| Column 1 | Column 2 | Column 3 | Column 4 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Improvement Goal | Undermining Behaviors | Hidden Competing Commitment | Big Assumption |
| What I genuinely want | What I actually do that works against it | What those behaviors are protecting | The belief that makes the protection feel necessary |

**The diagnostic power:** Column 4 — the big assumption — is almost always rooted in formative experience. It is a conclusion written in an earlier context, usually relational, that has not been updated. It operates as a law: *"If I do X, then Y will happen"* — where Y was real at the time the law was written and is now applied to every situation that resembles the original.

---

## II. WHY THIS TOOL WORKS FOR THIS POPULATION

For the 35–55, moderate-literacy, high-ACE population this practice serves, the ITC Map addresses three specific conditions that other tools do not reach:

**1. The vocabulary-without-framework gap.**
These clients know what they should want spiritually. They can name the goal. What they cannot explain is why knowing and wanting have not produced change. The ITC Map gives a structural answer — not "you lack faith" or "you need more discipline" but "here is the specific mechanism by which your system is preventing the change you say you want." That answer is relieving rather than shaming.

**2. The insight-without-formation block.**
Many clients have accumulated theological insight that has not produced formation. The ITC Map reveals why: the insight has not addressed the competing commitment or the assumption underneath it. The block is not cognitive — it is structural and formative. The map surfaces the structure so the formation work can address the right layer.

**3. The ACE-driven implicit barrier.**
In ACE-prevalent populations, Column 4 assumptions are frequently relational and wound-rooted — conclusions written by early experiences of harm, abandonment, or shame. These assumptions operate below conscious access and are not correctable by argument or cognitive reframing. The ITC Map gives them language — which is the first step toward the formation work that can actually address them.

**The ITC Map is not the formation work.** It is a diagnostic instrument. It surfaces what has been operating below awareness. What the coach does with that material — how it routes into Affections Discovery, the Character Wound Diagnostic, the Lie Eliminator, and the formation disciplines — is the formation work.

## III. THE FULL FOUR-COLUMN PROTOCOL

### Column 1 — Improvement Goal

**Purpose:** Identify what the client genuinely wants — specifically enough to be diagnostic.

**Run this in session.**

**The elicitation question:**
> *"What do you most want to change, grow in, or become — in your inner life specifically? Not a general answer. The specific thing underneath that."*

**What to listen for:**
A genuine goal has specificity and some emotional weight. Vague goals ("be more Christlike," "grow spiritually") are not yet Column 1 material — they are the category the goal lives in. Press gently for specificity.

**Coach prompts if the client is general:**
- *"If that happened — if you were more Christlike — what specifically would be different? What would you notice?"*
- *"What do you most wish were different about your inner life when no one is watching?"*
- *"If you could name the one thing you keep coming back to — the thing that stays stuck — what is it?"*

**When the goal is found:** The client's affect will often shift slightly. The goal that is real usually carries either a quiet ache or a small activation. Note it.

**Write the goal in Column 1. Keep it to one or two sentences.**

---

### Column 2 — Undermining Behaviors

**Purpose:** Surface what the client actually does — or does not do — that works against Column 1.

**Run this in session.**

**The elicitation question:**
> *"Looking at that goal — what do you actually do, or not do, that works against it? Not what you should do differently. What you observe yourself doing."*

**What to listen for:**
Concrete behavioral patterns — not character assessments. "I avoid deep prayer" not "I'm lazy." "I talk about others' spiritual needs more than my own" not "I'm deflective." The more behavioral and concrete Column 2 is, the more diagnostic Column 3 becomes.

**Coach prompts if the client produces character assessments instead of behaviors:**
- *"What does that look like on a Tuesday? What specifically happens — or doesn't happen?"*
- *"If I watched a video of your week, what would I see you doing that works against Column 1?"*

**Common Column 2 patterns in this population:**

| Goal (Column 1) | Common Undermining Behaviors (Column 2) |
|---|---|
| Deeper prayer life | Pray briefly, stay surface, avoid silence |
| Genuine inner transformation | Faithfully perform practices without interior engagement |
| Honest relationship with God | Maintain theological accuracy while avoiding personal exposure |
| Freedom from performance | Continue reporting spiritual progress in terms of what others see |
| Inner peace | Fill silence with activity; avoid solitude |

**Write 3–5 specific behaviors in Column 2.**

---

### Column 3 — Hidden Competing Commitment

**Purpose:** Identify what the Column 2 behaviors are protecting.

**Assign this as between-session work after Columns 1 and 2 are complete.**

**The framing before the client leaves:**
> *"Here is what I want you to sit with before we meet again. Look at your Column 2 behaviors. They are not random — they are protecting something. If your Column 1 goal fully happened, what would become uncomfortable, threatened, or at risk? What would you have to face, give up, or no longer be able to avoid? Don't edit what comes. Write it down and bring it."*

**What to listen for when the client returns:**
A genuine Column 3 has the quality of something the client did not expect to find. It often surprises them. It may produce mild embarrassment, recognition, or a quiet "I didn't want to write that but it's true."

**Signs that Column 3 is still a defense:**
- Spiritually acceptable competing commitment: *"I'm committed to not being prideful about my growth."*
- Abstract rather than personal: *"I'm committed to not disrupting my family."*
- Immediately reframes into what the client *should* feel rather than what they actually feel

**Coach response to defended Column 3:**
> *"That may be part of it. I want to go one layer deeper. If you imagine Column 1 fully real — transformed at the level you said you wanted — what is the thing that makes your stomach drop slightly? What are you quietly relieved to avoid as long as Column 1 stays a goal rather than a reality?"*

**Common Column 3 patterns in this population:**

- Commitment to not being fully known (if I change visibly, people will expect more, look more closely, see what I am actually like)
- Commitment to maintaining current relational identity (I am known as the person who struggles with this; changing would mean becoming someone my relationships are not organized around)
- Commitment to not having more demanded of me (if I grow, God will ask more than I can give)
- Commitment to staying in control (transformation requires surrendering outcomes I am not willing to surrender)
- Commitment to not feeling what is underneath (genuine inner engagement would open what has been closed for protection)

---

### Column 4 — The Big Assumption

**Purpose:** Surface the belief that makes Column 3 feel not like a choice but like a law.

**Assign as between-session work along with Column 3, or in a subsequent session depending on pacing.**

**The framing:**
> *"Column 3 is held in place by a belief — something you assume to be true that makes the protection feel necessary. This belief usually sounds like: 'If I [achieve Column 1], then [something bad/threatening/painful] will happen.' It's a law you've been living by, often without knowing it. See if you can name it."*

**Sentence stems for the client:**
- *"I assume that if I [achieve Column 1], then ___"*
- *"I believe that if I were truly [Column 1], ___"*
- *"What I know from experience is that when I [move toward Column 1], ___"*

**What to listen for when the client returns:**

The genuine Column 4 assumption has three qualities:
1. It feels *true* to the client — not a distortion they can immediately identify as false, but something that feels like an accurate reading of reality
2. It can be traced to a specific type of experience — a pattern of experience that wrote the law
3. It produces a particular quality of recognition — a quiet *"yes, that's actually what I believe"* rather than *"I suppose that might be true"*

**Note:** The assumption is not wrong because it is false. It was often an accurate reading of the context in which it was formed. It is now being applied to a context where it may no longer be accurate — but it does not feel that way to the system operating under it.

## IV. READING WHAT THE CLIENT BRINGS BACK

### What Column 3 tells you

| Column 3 content | What it signals |
|---|---|
| Fear of being known more fully | Insecure attachment; feeds Secured in Father work |
| Fear of relational displacement | Formation organized around relational identity, not covenant identity |
| Fear of God's increased demands | God-image distortion — demanding, not safe; feeds Affections Discovery Layer 4 |
| Fear of losing control | Trust deficit; Warrior wound type signal |
| Fear of feeling what is underneath | Mode 4 — the wall is protective; inner access work before formation practices advance |
| Fear of isolation if genuinely holy | False Noble wound type — belonging organized around shared struggle |

### What Column 4 tells you

Column 4 is the primary diagnostic output of the ITC Map. It surfaces the operating law — and in this population, that law almost always connects to one of the following:

**A false covenant object** — something being functionally trusted for safety, worth, or belonging that competes with God. Column 4 reveals what the client is actually living under as a governing reality. This is the direct diagnostic input for Affections Discovery Layer 5 (Identity Revelation).

**A character wound** — the type of assumption produced by Column 4 signals wound type:
- *"If I show weakness/need, I will be betrayed or abandoned"* → Warrior
- *"If I engage fully, it will be too much for me or for others"* → Hermit
- *"If the real me is seen, I will not be acceptable"* → False Noble

**A God-image distortion** — assumptions that include God as the anticipated source of harm: *"If I am fully transformed, God will ask more than I can survive"* or *"If I open this up, God will be disappointed in what He finds."* These are not primarily theological errors; they are relational wounds projected onto God. They are addressed through Secured in Father, not through doctrinal correction.

**An ACE-rooted relational law** — in ACE-prevalent populations, Column 4 frequently reveals a conclusion formed in the original wound context: *"I know that when I am fully seen, I am hurt."* This is not metaphor. It is memory operating as law. This is the signal that the formation work requires the coaching relationship's relational safety layer (Layer 2) before any further practice assignment.

## V. ROUTING ITC FINDINGS INTO THE COVENANT IDENTITY FRAMEWORK

### Column 3 → Affections Discovery
The competing commitment in Column 3 is a functional affection — what the client loves or fears more than Column 1 in practice. Route Column 3 findings into [[Covenant Identity — Affections Discovery — In-Session Protocol]] Layer 3 (Dependency Probe) and Layer 4 (God-Exclusion Map). Ask: what is the Column 3 commitment trusting for safety or worth?

### Column 4 → Affections Discovery Layer 5 (Identity Revelation)
The big assumption is the belief the false self is organized around. It answers: what does the false self believe about what happens when the true self is exposed? This is the direct entry point into Layer 5 of Affections Discovery and into Stage 2 of the [[Covenant Identity — Diagnostic Lens Transition Logic]] (False Identity Named).

### Column 4 → Character Wound Diagnostic
The content and structure of the assumption signals wound type. Use the assumption to confirm or refine the wound type reading from [[Covenant Identity — Character Wound Diagnostic Tool]]. The assumption is often the most precise single indicator of wound type available — more precise than behavioral observation alone.

### Column 4 → Lie Eliminator
The big assumption is frequently a specific lie that warrants a Lie Eliminator entry. The assumption is the lie as the client has internalized it; the Lie Eliminator entry names the corresponding truth, declaration, and Scripture Arsenal. Do not route to Lie Eliminator until the assumption has been received and sat with — premature counter-declaration before the assumption has been genuinely surfaced produces the insight-without-formation failure mode.

### Column 3 → Diagnostic Lens Transition Logic
When Column 3 reveals a competing commitment organized around a false covenant object or God-image distortion, it signals readiness to move from Stage 1 (Affections Discovery) to Stage 2 (False Identity Named) in the Diagnostic Lens. The ITC Map has done the surfacing work; the Transition Logic governs what comes next.

### Column 4 → Secured in Father
When Column 4 assumptions are relational — organized around fear of exposure, rejection, abandonment, or harm — route to [[Secured in Father — Practitioner Design Notes]]. These are attachment-layer assumptions that are not correctable by doctrinal argument; they require the formation of secure attachment to God through the practices Secured in Father is designed to support.

## VI. IN-SESSION FACILITATION — COLUMNS 1 AND 2

**Do not cold-introduce the ITC Map.** It needs a frame that makes sense of why you are doing it.

**Say:**
> *"I want to try something with you that I think will surface something important. Before I describe it, let me tell you what it's for. You've said you want [Column 1 goal] — and you mean it. What I want to look at is the specific reason that wanting hasn't yet produced the change. Not a character problem. A structural one. It takes about fifteen minutes and what surfaces tends to be more honest than most people expect."*

**Then elicit Column 1, then Column 2, in sequence.** Do not move to Column 3 in session unless the client produces it naturally and you have enough time to work with it well. Columns 3 and 4 are between-session work.

**After Columns 1 and 2 are written:**

**Say:**
> *"Look at what's in Column 2. Those behaviors are not random — they're doing something. They're protecting something. Before we meet again, I want you to sit with one question: if Column 1 fully happened — if you were actually [goal] — what would become uncomfortable, threatened, or at risk? Don't edit it. Write what actually comes. Column 3 is what you find. Column 4 is the belief underneath that. We'll work with both when you come back."*

---

## VII. RETURN SESSION — WORKING WITH COLUMNS 3 AND 4

**Opening the return session:**

> *"What did you find?"*

Then let them read or describe what they wrote. Do not interpret immediately. Receive it first.

### Working with Column 3

If the client produced a genuine Column 3:

> *"Say more about that. What makes [Column 3 content] feel like something worth protecting?"*

If the client produced a defended Column 3:

> *"That may be part of it — and I want to go one layer deeper. If Column 1 were fully real, what is the thing you would quietly be relieved to no longer have to avoid?"*

### Working with Column 4

**Do not immediately counter the assumption.** The assumption feels true. Arguing against it before it has been fully received and sat with produces the insight-without-formation failure mode.

Instead:

> *"Where did that come from? Not theologically — experientially. When did you learn that?"*

Let the client trace the assumption to its origin if they can. This is diagnostic: it confirms whether the assumption is wound-rooted (ACE, relational) or constructed from a different source. The answer governs what formation work comes next.

**After the assumption is traced:**

> *"So this belief — [Column 4 assumption] — has been operating like a law. Not a choice you make, but something that runs underneath your choices. The formation work we're doing together is about what happens to that law when the Spirit gets to it. It doesn't get argued away. It gets displaced — over time, through the means we've been working with, as the covenant truth becomes more real than the assumed one. That's a longer arc than a single insight. But now we know what we're working with."*

## VIII. COMMON OBSTACLES AND HOW TO HANDLE THEM

**Client cannot identify undermining behaviors (Column 2 is blank or very thin):**
> *"If someone who knew you well — someone who cared about you — looked at your week, what would they notice you consistently not doing that would serve this goal?"*
Third-person perspective often lowers defenses enough to produce Column 2 content.

---

**Client produces a spiritually acceptable Column 3:**
Receive it without challenge, then press:
> *"That's worth holding. And I want to ask about the thing underneath that — the more uncomfortable one. What would you quietly be protecting if you named it without the spiritual framing?"*

---

**Column 4 is extremely defended — client cannot access it:**
Hold it as an open question rather than a task:
> *"That's okay. Sometimes the assumption is too close to see directly. Carry the question — not as homework, but as something to notice. When you find yourself pulling away from Column 1, ask yourself: what am I protecting right now? We'll keep returning to it."*

---

**Client uses the ITC Map as self-improvement analysis:**
The client is describing themselves in the third person, with no affect, producing a well-organized map that carries no weight.

> *"You've organized this really clearly. What I want to ask is — does it land? Not whether it's accurate, but whether it hits anywhere when you hold it."*

---

**Coach rushes to Column 4:**
Column 4 is only as diagnostic as the work that preceded it. A Column 4 assumption reached in fifteen minutes is almost always a surface version. Give Columns 1–3 the time they need — over multiple sessions if necessary — before Column 4 is approached. The depth of Column 4 is directly proportional to the depth of the work in Columns 1–3.

---

## IX. SCOPE BOUNDARY NOTES

The ITC Map is coach-appropriate and non-clinical in its standard operation. The following signals indicate the material that surfaces requires clinical referral:

**Refer when Column 4 reveals:**
- Active ongoing harm — the assumption is not historical but current
- Abuse by a spiritual authority — do not use spiritual formation tools as the primary response; refer first
- A level of shame or self-condemnation that produces flooding or shutdown in session
- Dissociative response when approaching the assumption — client cannot re-regulate

**Continue coaching when:**
- The assumption is historical — written in a past context and being applied to the present
- The client can engage the material with some affect regulation
- The assumption produces recognition and discomfort without flooding
