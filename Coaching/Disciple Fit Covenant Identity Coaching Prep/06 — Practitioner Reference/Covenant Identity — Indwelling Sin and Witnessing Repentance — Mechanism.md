# Covenant Identity — Indwelling Sin and Witnessing Repentance — Mechanism

*Practitioner-only mechanism document. No client-facing form exists yet and none should be built until this has been used in live sessions — the risk of a client self-administering sin/shame content without a coach's real-time discernment is too high (see Failure Modes). This document refines Theory of Change Node 3 (Felt Inadequacy) — it is not a new diagnostic stage and does not sit ahead of or alongside the affections-first entry sequence.*

---

## What This Clarifies

CIC's diagnostic architecture is built to read the implicit layer — disordered affections, false covenant objects, functional theology, wound-driven false identity. All of that machinery is sound. What it has lacked is a theological account of what is actually operating at that layer in Scripture's own terms: **sin**, and what engages it: **repentance**. Instead, the system has consistently translated implicit material into psychological vocabulary (schema, false belief, functional theology) and addressed it with a psychological mechanism (truth replacing distortion). That mechanism isn't wrong, but it is incomplete — it can produce insight and even relief without ever naming the moral-relational reality Scripture insists is present, which leaves both coach and client without the categories to handle it when a client names something as sin themselves, which they will.

The felt cost of this gap is not abstract. The Felt Inadequacy mechanism doc already flags an open failure mode: "grief felt but not turned Godward." This document names why that happens and what turns it.

---

## Three Layers of Sin, One Behavior

Take a concrete case: a client in insecure-attachment work whose porn use surfaces mid-session. Scripture holds three true claims about that single behavior at once, and they are not the same claim.

**1. Legal/definitional** — a transgression of God's law. *"Sin is lawlessness."* (1 John 3:4, ESV). Jesus locates this at the level of the heart's intent, not just the external act: *"everyone who looks at a woman with lustful intent has already committed adultery with her in his heart."* (Matt 5:28, ESV). This is the **act level** — bounded, nameable, confessable.

**2. Relational/covenantal** — misplaced trust, not merely broken rule. Scripture's dominant metaphor for sin in the prophets isn't legal violation, it's **adultery** — going to the wrong source for something real. *"They have forsaken me, the fountain of living waters, and hewed out cisterns for themselves, broken cisterns that can hold no water."* (Jer 2:13, ESV). This is not a figure of speech about disobedience in general. It describes someone going to get a genuine need met from a source that cannot deliver it — which is a functionally exact description of what compulsive porn use *is*, behaviorally: an attempted regulation strategy for loneliness, unprocessed shame, or unsafe intimacy, aimed at a cistern that cannot hold what it's being asked to hold. This is the **relational level**, and it is where the attachment work belongs.

**3. Ontological/power** — an indwelling reality operating beneath conscious will. *"So now it is no longer I who do it, but sin that dwells within me... For I do not do the good I want, but the evil I do not want is what I keep on doing."* (Rom 7:17, 20, 19, ESV). Paul commands against it in the same breath he describes its automaticity: *"Let not sin therefore reign in your mortal body."* (Rom 6:12, ESV) — description of mechanism does not cancel moral agency. This is the **state level** — what CIC has been calling "disordered affection" or "false covenant object" without naming it theologically.

All three are true of the same behavior simultaneously. The client's error — and it is the default human error, not a special failure — is collapsing all three into layer 1 and stopping there. That produces guilt-as-event. It does not produce the deeper turning, because it never reaches the level where the actual reordering needs to happen.

## Wound and Sin Are Not the Same Claim

The Character Wound Diagnostic Tool already carries this tension correctly, per-type: *"Wound and sin are both present in every type... The coach who reads identity distortion as exclusively wound-produced will miss the moral agency dimension. The coach who reads it as exclusively sinful will miss the compassion the wound requires. Both are true."* This document extends that note; it does not revise it.

**The wound is etiology** — it explains why this particular self-protective shape formed, often around real injury the client did not cause (betrayal, abandonment, humiliation). **Sin, at the relational and ontological levels above, is what that shape now is in relation to God** — a functioning misdirection of trust, active now regardless of how it began. These are different questions. A person can be genuinely wounded and the pattern that formed around the wound can still, in its present operation, be a place of active distrust toward God that needs to be named as such. Neither claim cancels the other, and naming one is not a verdict on the other.

Each wound type's characteristic self-protection has a corresponding scriptural anchor for how it functions as sin, not merely as adaptation:

| Wound type | Self-protective shape | Sin expression | Anchor |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Warrior** | Control, self-reliance, dominance | Practical atheism — functioning as though provision and safety depend on the self, not God | *"Trust in the Lord with all your heart, and do not lean on your own understanding."* (Prov 3:5, ESV) |
| **Hermit** | Withdrawal, concealment, invisibility | Unbelief expressed as hiding — distrust that God's presence is safe to be seen by | *"I heard the sound of you in the garden, and I was afraid... and I hid myself."* (Gen 3:10, ESV) |
| **False Noble** | Performance, achievement, impression management | Self-justification — securing one's own standing rather than receiving it | *"I thank you that I am not like other men... The tax collector... would not even lift up his eyes to heaven, but beat his breast, saying, 'God, be merciful to me, a sinner!'... this man went down to his house justified."* (Luke 18:11, 13-14, ESV) |

**Guardrail this table exists to serve, not undercut:** these anchors describe what the pattern does when it is actively operating as distrust of God right now. They are not a retroactive verdict on how the wound was acquired, and they are never to be spoken to a client as an explanation of their injury. They are diagnostic language for the coach's own clarity — naming what the self-protection is currently doing theologically, so the coach knows what repentance, when it comes, is actually turning from.

## What Repentance Is

*Metanoia* (Gk.) — a change of *nous*, the perceiving mind itself. Not remorse. Hebrew *shuv* — to turn, to return — carries the same directional, relational weight; not an emotional register but a movement.

### Two levels, not one

**Propositional/act-level repentance** — naming the specific instance, confessing it. *"If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just to forgive us our sins and to cleanse us from all unrighteousness."* (1 John 1:9, ESV). This is quick, bounded, met with assurance, and should never be skipped or spiritually bypassed — but it is not where the deeper work happens, and dwelling here is itself a way of avoiding the state level.

**Implicit/state-level repentance — witnessing** — a turning of what is actually trusted, not just what is behaviorally resolved. This requires being able to *see* the pattern operating before it can be turned from; you cannot turn from what you have not yet witnessed. Two texts model this precisely:

*"Search me, O God, and know my heart! Try me and know my thoughts! And see if there be any grievous way in me, and lead me in the way everlasting!"* (Ps 139:23-24, ESV) — a **prayer**, addressed to God, asking to be shown. Witnessing is invited and Spirit-led, not self-generated and not coach-directed.

*"But when he came to himself, he said... 'I will arise and go to my father, and I will say to him, "Father, I have sinned against heaven and before you."'"* (Luke 15:17-19, ESV) — note the order. Recognition ("came to himself") precedes verbal confession ("I will say"). The prodigal witnesses his actual state before he speaks it. This is the sequence: witnessing, then confession — not the reverse.

### Why the same grief produces opposite outcomes

Judas and Peter both betrayed Christ within the same hours; both felt severe, genuine anguish afterward.

Judas: *"he changed his mind and... said, 'I have sinned by betraying innocent blood.'"* (Matt 27:3-4, ESV) — real self-accusation, ending in Matt 27:5 with self-destruction.

Peter: *"he went out and wept bitterly."* (Luke 22:62, ESV) — comparable anguish, ending in John 21:15-17 with restoration and recommission.

Paul names the variable directly: *"godly grief produces a repentance that leads to salvation without regret, whereas worldly grief produces death."* (2 Cor 7:10, ESV). The determining factor is not how intensely the person feels their guilt. It is whether the grief turns **toward** God and relationship, or collapses **into** the self. Worldly grief's native behavior is Genesis 3's behavior — hiding. Godly grief's native behavior is Psalm 139's behavior — inviting further exposure. They can look identical in the room in the first thirty seconds. They are not the same movement.

**Strong inference, offered as corroboration, not proof:** this tracks closely with Tangney and Dearing's empirical distinction between guilt ("I did a bad thing," which preserves a stable self capable of acting differently) and shame ("I am a bad thing," which collapses the self and is associated with *higher* relapse in compulsive behavior, not lower, because its native response is concealment rather than engagement). The fit is close enough to be clinically useful. It is not the same claim as the biblical text and should not be presented to a client as though Paul were describing a psychology study.

## Where This Sits in the Diagnostic Sequence

This is not a new stage. It is a refinement of **Theory of Change Node 3 (Felt Inadequacy)**, which already requires that the false covenant object be felt as failing before covenant truth can displace it. What Node 3 has not previously specified is *what kind* of felt failure completes the condition. "This isn't working for me" (self-interest frame) is not automatically the same event as "this is unbelief toward my Father" (relational frame) — and only the second is repentance. A client can complete Node 3 in the first sense and never reach the second, which is exactly the gap the existing "grief felt but not turned Godward" failure mode was already flagging without a named cause.

Practically: this document does not change the Diagnostic Lens Transition Logic's Stage 2 → Stage 3 sequence. It specifies what "felt cost" needs to become, inside that existing transition, for covenant truth to land as grace received rather than as self-help information.

## The In-Session Diagnostic Move

When behavior/sin language surfaces mid-session, the coach's task is to catch, in real time, which level the client has moved to.

**Bounded, act-specific language** ("I did X on Tuesday") → meet briefly with confession and assurance (1 John 1:9). Do not dwell. Return to the thread. This is not where the work is.

**Global, identity-level language** ("I'm disgusting," "I'll never be free," "I'm just like my dad") → this is worldly grief, not repentance, and it is now obstructing the session rather than deepening it. Do not argue theology or reassure it away. Name the shift and redirect to the affections entry:

*"That moved from what you did to who you are — those aren't the same claim, and only one of them is where God is actually taking you. What was going on underneath, right before it happened?"*

That question, not a verdict on the behavior, is the actual work. The behavior becomes usable diagnostic material — what broken cistern is this, what need is it failing to meet — only once it is released from the global moral-verdict frame the client has put it in.

**Repentance cannot be manufactured by increasing moral pressure on the act.** That is the client's own instinct, and it is backwards — it produces worldly grief, not godly grief. It becomes available only downstream of the vacancy being felt in relational terms, not upstream of it, forced by shame.

## Worked Example — Insecure Attachment and Compulsive Porn Use

**Presentation:** Client is mid-engagement, working an anxious-attachment pattern — activation around a partner's unavailability, fear of abandonment. Porn use surfaces: escalating over recent weeks, coinciding with a stretch of relational distance from the partner. Client says, unprompted: *"I know this is sin. I don't know why I keep doing this. I'm disgusting."*

**What just happened diagnostically:** the client has moved from bounded language (a behavior, a pattern, a timeframe) to global language ("I'm disgusting") in one sentence. The shame has arrived before the diagnostic work has. If the coach either agrees ("yes, this needs to be repented of") or reassures ("don't be so hard on yourself"), both responses accept the client's frame — that the conversation is now about a verdict on the act — and the actual material (what is this behavior doing, attachment-wise) never gets reached.

**Coach's move:** *"That moved from what you did to who you are — I want to come back to that, but first: what was happening in the relationship right before this started escalating?"* This does not dismiss the moral reality of the behavior. It refuses to let the global-identity version of it run the session, and it returns to the affections-first entry the model is built on.

**What surfaces, following that thread:** the escalation tracks precisely with the partner's unavailability — the client is using porn as a self-soothing substitute for a felt-unsafe attachment need, in a week where the actual attachment figure felt unreachable. This is Jeremiah 2:13 exactly: a broken cistern, reached for because the fountain felt (rightly or wrongly) unavailable.

**Sin language, now correctly placed:** the behavior is still sin — at the act level (confessable, 1 John 1:9, handled briefly and without dwelling) and at the relational level (a misdirected trust, reaching for a cistern instead of the fountain, which is where the real work is). What it is *not*, and what the client's shame spiral was treating it as, is a global verdict on identity that forecloses the attachment work. The coach does not need to litigate whether it's "really sin" — it is, at more than one level — the coach needs to keep the client from resolving all three levels into the one that stops the session.

**Repentance, when it comes:** not "I will never do this again" (a vow, self-generated, frequently the very willpower move that relapses) but a witnessed recognition — *"I go to this instead of bringing my fear of being left to God, or to my partner directly"* — followed by a turn toward the actual source, not just away from the behavior. That is Jeremiah 2:13's positive half, rarely quoted: the problem was never merely the broken cistern, it was forsaking the fountain. Repentance is turning back to the fountain, not just capping the cistern.

**Referral note:** if the pattern is escalating, compulsive, or resistant to this kind of engagement over several sessions, check against the existing referral criteria in the Crisis & Referral Protocol and the Character Wound Tool's clinical triggers before continuing to treat it as an affections-diagnostic case regardless of severity.

## Failure Modes and Guardrails

**Wound ≠ sin, and this document does not get to resolve that tension for the sake of tidiness.** The Character Wound Tool's existing caution stands: reading a pattern as purely sin erases the compassion the injury requires. A client whose pattern originated in real victimization (abuse, betrayal) must never hear "your wound is your sin." What can be named, carefully, is that the *self-protective shape*, in its *present operation*, may now function as distrust toward God — a claim about current functioning, not a claim about the injury's origin or the client's culpability for having been hurt.

**Witnessing must remain Spirit-invited, not coach-directed.** Psalm 139:23-24 is a prayer *to* God, not an inventory the coach runs on the client. The moment a coach becomes the one who exposes the client's sin rather than the one who creates conditions for the Spirit to do so, this has drifted from formation into correction — the exact failure mode this practice is built to avoid.

**Do not manufacture repentance by intensifying shame.** This is the client's own instinct in the worked example above, and it is backwards. Worldly grief is not a lesser version of godly grief that just needs more pressure — it is a different movement entirely, and pressure moves it further from God, not closer.

**Per-type misuse to watch for**, matching the pattern already established for other tools in this system:
- **Warrior** may receive this content as a new problem to fix through effort — confessing quickly and efficiently in order to move past discomfort rather than actually witnessing the pattern.
- **Hermit** may use "indwelling sin" language to confirm an existing belief that they are irredeemably flawed, collapsing every instance into global condemnation exactly as in the worked example.
- **False Noble** may use the three-layer distinction evaluatively — assessing whether they are repenting "correctly" — which is the performance pattern re-asserting itself inside the very framework meant to interrupt it.

## Interpretive Debate, Named Honestly

Romans 7:14-25's referent is a genuine, long-standing exegetical dispute — whether Paul describes his present experience as a believer, or uses a rhetorical "I" for pre-conversion or hypothetical experience under law. Augustine held both positions at different points in his life; Reformed and Wesleyan-holiness traditions differ substantially on how much "indwelling sin" persists, and in what form, after regeneration. This document takes the position (consistent with the model's existing landing in "Conditions for the Spirit's Sanctifying Work") that some real form of this struggle characterizes ongoing sanctification, without resolving how total or residual it is — that question does not need to be settled for the mechanism above to be usable in session.
