# Covenant Identity — Prodigal Son Diagnostic Narrative Framework

**What this is:** A practitioner guide for using Luke 15:11-32 as the primary client-facing self-locating diagnostic entry. The parable surfaces affections structure, false identity architecture, and functional God-image in one brief narrative encounter — before the client has the vocabulary or the trust to produce those diagnostics directly.

**When used:** Entry — Phase 1, Session 1 or Discovery Call. Before affections diagnostic work begins. The client's self-location in the parable gives the practitioner an initial diagnostic read that informs everything from question selection to God-representation work to the wound type most likely in play.

**Who uses it:** The practitioner. The introduction is client-facing; the diagnostic interpretation is not.

---

## Why This Parable

The Affections Discovery Process requires the client to report what they are organized around — what they love, what they fear, what they trust. Most clients at entry do not have access to that language. They know something is wrong; they don't know what their soul is doing.

The Prodigal Son parable provides a narrative container that does the diagnostic work sideways. The client is not asked "what are your disordered affections?" They are asked "where do you find yourself in this story?" The self-location reveals what could not be reported directly.

Three distinct client presentations appear in the parable — and the triptych framework misses one of them entirely. Psalm 22 captures desolation and abandonment; it does not capture the client who has never cracked open, who is performing faithfully and quietly resentful. The older brother lives in that gap. This parable is the only entry tool in the CIC system that catches all three.

**Secondary function:** The father's response in the parable is the most direct available test of the client's functional God-image. How the client receives the father's running — with relief, suspicion, disbelief, or ambivalence — tells the practitioner more about the God-representation than a direct question about prayer would.

---

## How to Introduce It

This is a narrative encounter, not a Bible study. The practitioner summarizes the parable in plain language — 60 to 90 seconds — and asks one question. No theological framing first. No setup that signals what the "right" answer is.

**Client-facing introduction:**

> "I want to tell you a short story — takes about a minute. I'll ask you one question after."
>
> *(Summarize in plain language:)*
> "A father has two sons. The younger one asks for his inheritance early — essentially tells his father, 'Give me what's mine, I'm not waiting.' He takes it and leaves. He spends it all, ends up broke and working a job he's humiliated by, feeding pigs. He realizes his father's servants eat better than he does. So he decides to go home — not as a son, but just to ask for a job. He prepares what he's going to say: 'I've sinned against you. I'm not worthy to be called your son. Just hire me.' Before he gets close enough to speak, his father sees him and runs. He doesn't wait. He's on him — robe, ring, sandals, feast.
>
> Meanwhile, the older brother comes in from the field, hears the music, and won't go in. 'I've served you my whole life. Never broke a rule. And you never threw me a party.' The father comes out to him too."
>
> *(Single question:)*
> **"Where do you find yourself in that story?"**

Then wait. Do not prompt. Do not offer the options. The client's unprompted response — what they name, how quickly they name it, what they leave out — is the first diagnostic data.

## Three Diagnostic Tracks

### Track 1 — The Far Country (The Prodigal)

**Client self-location:** "I've been living for something other than what I was made for."

**What the far country surfaces:**
The younger son's departure is an affections event, not primarily a behavioral one. He demands the inheritance before it is his — he is organizing his life around what he believes will satisfy him outside the father's house. Whatever the far country is for the client, it functioned as a promise: *this will give me what I need.* When the far country fails — when the inheritance runs out — the disordered affection is exposed. The collapse reveals what the client was actually trusting.

The practitioner's diagnostic question at this point is not "what did you do wrong?" It is: **what did you expect the far country to give you?** Security, significance, belonging, control, comfort, approval, purpose? That expectation is the affections structure. It points directly to Layer 1-2 of the Affections Discovery Process and, in Layer 4, to the false covenant object that functioned as shepherd.

**The hired-servant identity:**
When the son "comes to himself" and decides to return, he has already named his false identity: *"Make me as one of thy hired servants."* He is not proposing a ruse — he genuinely believes this is what he now is. The far country has rewritten his self-understanding. He cannot conceive of himself as a son anymore; he can only conceive of performing his way back into the household.

This statement is the most clinically direct moment in the parable for Stage 2 work. The hired-servant framing is the presenting false identity in narrative form. It contains the organizing belief: *I must earn the place I lost. Worth is now conditional on performance.* The client who identifies with this is almost certainly operating from a Performance / Slave Identity wound category.

**What the "coming to himself" moment is:**
This is the beginning of affections reorientation — awareness that the far country has not delivered what it promised. It is not yet repentance in the full sense; it is a realization that the disordered affection has failed. The client who is "coming to themselves" knows something has to change but does not yet know what. This is a crucial moment to hold carefully: premature theological framing of this moment as spiritual breakthrough will bypass the diagnostic work that needs to happen about *what the far country was* and *why the client went after it*.

---

### Track 2 — The Journey Back (The Returning Son)

**Client self-location:** "I know something has to change, but I'm not sure who I'll be received as."

**What this track surfaces:**
The returning son is mid-journey — he has left the far country but has not yet arrived at the father. This client has named that something is wrong; they have not yet received grace. They are organizing their return around the prepared speech: *I have sinned. I am not worthy. Hire me.* The content of that speech is the working false identity — and the assumptions embedded in it reveal the functional God-image.

**Key diagnostic: what does the client fear the father will say?**
The practitioner does not ask about theology. They ask: *"If you were writing your own version of the son's speech — what would you say? What would you have to do, or say, or prove, before you felt received?"* The answer reveals the implicit conditions the client is placing on the father's reception — which is the functional God-image. A client who can't imagine the father running before the speech is finished is operating from a transactional, conditional, or punitive God-image even if they affirm God's grace theologically.

**The father's interruption:**
The father sees the son while he is "yet a great way off" and runs before the son speaks. When the son begins his speech, the father does not let him finish it. The robe, ring, and sandals are given before the feast — before the son has produced anything, proven anything, or finished his prepared case. Covenant identity is declared before performance is required. This is the indicative/imperative grammar in narrative form.

**What this moment reveals:**
The client's response to the father running before the speech is complete is one of the most important diagnostic events in the entry session. Does the client find it:
- *Believable and personal?* — relatively secure God-image; covenant truth will land at a personal level
- *True for others, not for them?* — this is the implicit/explicit split; they know the theology; they can't feel it as true for themselves; Stage 3-4b work will be primary
- *Suspicious?* — transactional God-image; the running triggers the question "what does he want in return?"
- *Overwhelming or too much?* — overwhelming/consuming God-image or shame-based; they cannot receive what is being offered; the gift itself is threatening

See the God-Representation section below.

---

### Track 3 — Outside the Feast (The Older Brother)

**Client self-location:** "I've done everything right. I'm not sure it's worth it."

**What this track surfaces:**
The older brother has never left home. By external measure he is faithful — "these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment." But the practitioner hears what is underneath that sentence: the older brother has been relating to the father as a hired servant the whole time without knowing it. He calls his obedience "service." He has been keeping a ledger. And when the ledger is not honored in the way he expected, the grievance surfaces.

This is the moralistic/performance identity track. The client is not in the far country and not in desolation. They are in the right geography — in the father's house, doing the right things — but operating from law, not grace. Their identity is structured around their compliance record, not their sonship. The difference only becomes visible when grace is extended to someone who doesn't deserve it.

**The hidden ledger:**
"Thou never gavest me a kid, that I might make merry with my friends." The performance identity produces a hidden accounting of what has been given and what is owed. The older brother has been running the calculation in the background. The arrival of the younger brother's grace event makes the ledger visible — and the balance doesn't add up.

**Why the triptych misses this client:**
Psalm 22 begins with the cry of desolation. The older brother has no such cry — or rather, the cry is displaced into grievance. He is not in the valley; he is outside the feast. The practitioner using only the triptych will locate this client as "not in Psalm 22 territory" and reach for Psalm 23 provision work — but the provision block is not desolation; it is moralism. The provision the older brother cannot receive is not "rest" or "presence" — it is grace itself. The Psalm 23 provisions feel unearned to this client, or worse, unfair.

**Diagnostic note — the most defended track:**
Clients who identify with the older brother are often the most defended in entry. The performance structure has protected them for a long time and has produced external results they are proud of. The diagnostic work needs to go below the compliance record to surface what is operating underneath it: What has the obedience been for? What has the client been trying to secure through faithfulness? What does "thou never gavest me a kid" reveal about what the client was expecting in return? Those questions surface the affections structure underneath the performance.

**The father comes out to him too:**
This detail is clinically significant and often missed. The father does not stay at the feast and wait for the older brother to comply. He comes out — the same initiative he showed the younger brother. The older brother has not been abandoned; the father has been pursuing him through his compliance and he has not been able to receive it. The question for the practitioner: *"What would it take for you to go in?"*

## The Father as Diagnostic Lens

In all three tracks, the father's response is the primary window into the client's functional God-image. The practitioner listens not just for which son the client identifies with, but for how the client receives the father's behavior.

| How the Client Receives the Father's Running | What It Likely Indicates |
|---------------------------------------------|--------------------------|
| Relief — "That's what I needed to hear" | Relatively secure or anxious-attachment God-image; covenant truth may land emotionally |
| "That's true for people in the story, not for me" | Explicit/implicit split; theological knowledge and experiential absence are operating simultaneously; Stage 4b work likely needed |
| Suspicion — "What does he want in return?" | Transactional God-image; covenant relationship is being processed through a performance/exchange framework |
| Discomfort — "That's too much, it's embarrassing" | Shame-based or overwhelming/consuming God-image; the gift itself is threatening; cannot receive without collapsing or withdrawing |
| Grief — "I didn't know it could be like that" | Often indicates a long history of conditional reception; the running father is a contrast to the client's lived experience; this is a significant opening |
| Flatness — "That's a nice story" | Possible dissociation from affect, or deeply defended Track 3 presentation; the performance identity filters the father's emotion as sentimentality |

The God-Representation Deepening Tool is the primary next instrument once the client's God-image pattern is visible through this lens.

## Question Bank by Track

### Track 1 — The Far Country

*Affections diagnostic:*
- "If the 'far country' in your story had a name — what would it be? What were you going after?"
- "What did you expect it to give you that you felt you weren't getting?"
- "What was the first sign it wasn't delivering what you thought it would?"
- "When it ran out — what did you tell yourself you now were?"

*Far-country collapse diagnostic:*
- "There's a moment when the son 'comes to himself.' Have you had a moment like that? What did it feel like?"
- "When you came to yourself, what did you think returning would cost you?"

### Track 2 — The Journey Back

*God-image diagnostic:*
- "If you were writing your own version of the son's speech — what would you say you needed to prove before you felt received?"
- "What are you afraid the father will say when you arrive?"
- "The father runs before the son finishes his speech. What does that do to you — do you find that believable? For you specifically?"

*False identity probe:*
- "The son can't imagine going back as a son — only as a hired servant. Does any part of that feel familiar? That you'd have to earn your way in?"
- "What would it mean to receive the robe and the ring before you've finished your speech — before you've proven anything?"

### Track 3 — Outside the Feast

*Performance structure probe:*
- "Is there any part of your story where you've been doing everything right — and quietly wondering if it's worth it?"
- "If you kept a ledger of what you've given and what you've received — what would the balance say?"
- "The older brother says, 'I've never broken a rule.' What's he been expecting in return for that?"
- "Why do you think he never asked for the kid? What stopped him?"

*Grace reception probe:*
- "The younger brother gets the robe, the ring, the feast — without earning it back. What does that bring up in you?"
- "The father comes out to the older brother too — same initiative. What would it take for you to go in?"

## Transition Signals to Stage 1 Affections Work

**From Track 1:** When the client can name what the far country was and what they expected it to give them, the affections picture is beginning to form. The named "far country" object — approval, relationship, achievement, control, substance, status — becomes the primary presenting false covenant object for Layer 4 of the Affections Discovery (God-Exclusion Map). Move to Layer 1 to begin the full affections excavation.

**From Track 2:** The content of the client's prepared speech reveals the false identity statement in narrative form — use it to seed the Lie Eliminator entry before Stage 2 formal false identity work. The functional God-image revealed through the question "what does the father running do to you?" opens the God-Representation Deepening Tool alongside or before Stage 1 deepening.

**From Track 3:** The hidden ledger and the grievance structure are the surface presentation; the affections structure is beneath them. This track requires more patience before the affections picture becomes specific — the performance identity is well-defended. Move to Layer 1-2 of Affections Discovery, but expect the deepest diagnostic material to emerge in Layer 3-4 (the displacement and exclusion of God as primary object) rather than Layers 1-2. The question "what have you been trying to secure through faithfulness?" is the entry to what the compliance structure is actually organized around.

## Watch Points

**The client's self-location is diagnostic data, not necessarily diagnostic accuracy.** A client who names Track 2 (the returning son) may be presenting Track 3 behavioral patterns — performing penitence the way the older brother performed obedience. The client's self-report and the presenting pattern are both data; don't let one override the other.

**Split location is itself a diagnostic signal.** When a client says "I'm the prodigal in my work life but the older brother in my faith life" — that domain split reveals where the affections are most disordered in each domain. Track it by domain.

**Track 3 clients often don't recognize themselves initially.** The older brother identity is the most defended because it has delivered results and receives social affirmation. A client who dismisses Track 3 ("that's not really me, I know grace") may be the most in need of it. Don't push — but track the ledger language and the grievance pattern as they emerge across sessions.

**Don't interpret the father's response to the client before they have responded to it.** The practitioner's job is to ask "what does that do to you?" and listen, not to explain what the father's running means theologically. The client's uncoached response to the father is the God-image data. Theological framing can come later; the raw response comes first.

**This is entry, not resolution.** The parable opens the diagnostic picture; it does not complete it. The practitioner holds what emerges here lightly — as an initial read to be confirmed and refined through the full Affections Discovery Process.
