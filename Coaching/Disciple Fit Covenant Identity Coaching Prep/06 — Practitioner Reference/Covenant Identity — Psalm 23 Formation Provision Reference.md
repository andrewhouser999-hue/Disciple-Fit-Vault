# Covenant Identity — Psalm 23 Formation Provision Reference

**What this is:** A diagnostic and formational reference for the sixteen provisions of Psalm 23. For each provision: what the shepherd is doing, what blocks a client from receiving it, what the block reveals about the underlying wound or false identity, and the wound type most likely to block it. Includes a between-session question per provision and a client-facing section.

**When used:** Two functions. (1) *Diagnostic* — when a specific Psalm 23 provision surfaces as blocked, this document tells the practitioner what the block most likely reveals about the wound. (2) *Formational* — in Phases 2–3, to assign between-session practice targeted at a specific blocked provision. Both functions run simultaneously: the blocked provision is a diagnostic signal and the practice of receiving it is the formation work.

**Who uses it:** Practitioner reference. Client-facing section at the end marked for direct use.

---

## How to Read This Document

Every provision of Psalm 23 is being actively offered by the shepherd. The client's inability to receive a provision is not a failure of the shepherd's provision — it is a diagnostic signal revealing where the wound is active. The practitioner's question for each provision is not "why isn't the shepherd providing this?" but "what is preventing this client from receiving what is already being offered?"

The blocked provision and the wound beneath it correspond. Rest is blocked by the performance structure (the sheep cannot lie down while still performing). Presence is blocked by the abandonment wound (the sheep cannot receive "thou art with me" because the implicit encoding says "no one stays"). The block is not arbitrary — it is specific and it points.

**The grace foundation ("for his name's sake")** governs all provisions. The shepherd leads in paths of righteousness not because the sheep have earned it but because of the integrity of his own name and character. Nothing the client has done or failed to do is the basis for any of these provisions. When a client cannot receive a provision, the first diagnostic question is always: "Do they believe this provision is conditional on their performance or their condition?" If yes, "for his name's sake" is the foundational declaration that must be received before the specific provision can land.

## The Seven Core Provisions — Full Treatment

These are the provisions with the highest diagnostic density and the most direct connection to the wound types. Full treatment: what the shepherd is doing, what blocks reception, what the block reveals, wound type, and between-session question.

---

### 1 — Rest
*"He makes me lie down in green pastures." (v.2)*

**What the shepherd is doing:** Creating conditions for rest. The shepherd is not simply offering rest — he *makes* the sheep lie down. For a sheep to lie down, three conditions must be satisfied by the shepherd: safety from predators, freedom from hunger, and absence of internal flock conflict. The sheep does not choose rest independently; the shepherd must satisfy the conditions that make rest possible.

**What blocks reception:** The performance identity and hypervigilance. A client operating from the Warrior wound cannot rest because rest feels like losing ground — stopping production threatens the position that production maintains. The hypervigilant client is scanning for threat even in the green pasture; the nervous system has not received the shepherd's safety signal. The client may be in a season of relative external calm and still be unable to rest because the internal alarm has not been shut off by the shepherd's presence.

**What the block reveals:** The inability to rest surfaces the performance structure or the fear structure. The diagnostic question is: *"What would you lose if you stopped?"* The answer names what is functioning as the false covenant object that the rest would threaten — what the client believes they must keep producing in order to maintain their position or their safety.

**Wound type most likely blocking:** Warrior (primary). False Noble (secondary — the performance masquerade cannot afford to be seen resting, which would signal lack of effort or dedication).

**Between-session question:** *"Where did the shepherd make conditions for you to stop this week — and did you lie down, or did you stay standing?"*

### 2 — Refreshment
*"He leads me beside still waters." (v.2)*

**What the shepherd is doing:** Leading to water the sheep can actually receive. Sheep will not drink from turbulent water — they need still water. The shepherd does not simply point toward water; he leads to the specific kind of water the sheep's nature requires. This is attentiveness to what the sheep can receive in their current state, calibrated to their actual capacity for reception.

**What blocks reception:** Self-sufficiency and the inability to be led. The Hermit pattern appears here: the client knows they need refreshment but either refuses to go to the source (self-sufficiency — I will find what I need on my own) or has stopped believing the source is still there (withdrawal after disappointment). The turbulence in the client's environment can also make still water seem inaccessible — but the shepherd's role is to lead to it regardless of the surrounding conditions.

**What the block reveals:** Reception failure at its most basic level. The inability to be refreshed reveals either the belief that refreshment is not available to this client, or that receiving it requires the client to be led — and being led requires trust that the shepherd knows where the still waters are and will bring them there. The client who has been led to turbulent water in the past (betrayal by authority figures, spiritual environments that depleted rather than refreshed) will resist being led again.

**Wound type most likely blocking:** Hermit (isolation cuts off the still waters; self-sufficiency resists being led). Performance (cannot justify the stop; refreshment must be earned).

**Between-session question:** *"Where did you allow yourself to be led to still water this week — and where did you try to find it on your own, or decide it wasn't worth going to?"*

### 3 — Healing
*"He restores my soul." (v.3)*

**What the shepherd is doing:** Reconstituting the depleted self. The Hebrew *nephesh* (soul) carries the fullness of the animating inner life — not a spiritual sub-component but the whole self. Restoration suggests a return to original function, a reconstitution of what the wound has depleted or broken. The shepherd does this — the sheep does not restore itself.

**What blocks reception:** Shame — the belief that restoration is not available to this specific client because of what they are or what they have done. Also: the disbelief that healing is possible at depth. Many clients have experienced symptom management (behavior modified, crisis resolved) without soul restoration and have concluded that real restoration is not what is on offer. They have received surface repair and do not know there is something deeper.

**What the block reveals:** The shame wound's specific variant — "I am too depleted or too broken to be restored" — or the condemnation wound's variant — "my depletion is the consequence I deserve, not a condition the shepherd is addressing." The client's inability to receive healing at depth almost always carries a moral charge: they blame themselves for being depleted, which makes the shepherd's restoration feel either undeserved or inapplicable to their specific case.

**Wound type most likely blocking:** Shame (primary — disqualification from restoration; "too broken for this"). Condemnation (secondary — depletion as deserved consequence).

**Between-session question:** *"Is there a place in you that you have believed is beyond restoration — that the shepherd is not currently working on? What would it mean if he is?"*

### 4 — Guidance
*"He leads me in paths of righteousness for his name's sake." (v.3)*

**What the shepherd is doing:** Active leadership in specific directions — walking ahead, not pointing from a distance. "Paths of righteousness" (*ma'gelei-tzedek*) are paths of rightness, of covenant alignment, of the way that fits the sheep's nature and telos. The shepherd knows these paths; the sheep does not navigate to them independently.

**What blocks reception:** The control wound and the betrayal-of-authority wound. The client who has been led destructively by those in positions of spiritual, parental, or relational authority has a leadership wound — the shepherd's guidance carries the weight of past guidance that was harmful. The client's response is to navigate their own path rather than trust a guide, not from independence but from self-protection after betrayal.

**What the block reveals:** The betrayal wound underneath what presents as self-sufficiency or control. The client who cannot receive guidance is not primarily autonomous — they are primarily wounded by the misuse of guidance authority. The diagnostic question: *"What happened the last time you trusted someone else's direction?"* The answer names the wound.

**The grace note:** "For his name's sake" is the modifier attached to guidance — and it is the grace foundation for all provisions (see above). The shepherd leads not because the sheep's record merits guidance but because of the integrity of the shepherd's own character. The performance identity client may block guidance because they believe their access to it is conditional on their output record. "For his name's sake" directly counters that: the guidance is not contingent.

**Wound type most likely blocking:** Hermit (betrayal wound with authority figures — trusts no one's direction). False Noble (spiritual performance means claiming one already knows the path; receiving guidance admits dependence).

**Between-session question:** *"Where did you navigate on your own this week when the shepherd was offering to lead — and what was it that made his direction feel unsafe or unnecessary?"*

### 5 — Presence
*"For you are with me." (v.4)*

**What the shepherd is doing:** Being present inside the valley. Not beside it, not beyond it, not waiting at the exit. The provision is the shepherd's location — inside the same darkness the sheep is walking through.

This is also the He→You transition point in the psalm. The shepherd has been described in the third person throughout verses 1-3 (He, He, He). At verse 4 — in the deepest moment, the valley of the shadow of death — the language shifts to second-person direct address: *"for you are with me."* Distance collapses at the moment of greatest threat. The shepherd who was described becomes the shepherd who is addressed. See Triptych Practitioner Orientation Frame for full treatment of this signal.

**What blocks reception:** The abandonment wound (primary) — the orphan spirit's organizing belief is "no one stays." The implicit encoding of absence is so strong that the declaration "you are with me" is received as a theological statement rather than a present reality. The God-image operating as absent or distant prevents the presence from registering even when the client affirms it intellectually.

**What the block reveals:** The depth of the abandonment wound at the implicit level. A client who can describe every other provision the shepherd gives — who can speak of rest, healing, guidance — but cannot receive "you are with me" is showing the practitioner where the implicit abandonment encoding is most resistant. The explicit/implicit gap is widest here: theological belief and experiential reality are furthest apart.

**Wound type most likely blocking:** Abandonment/orphan spirit (primary). All other blocks are partial. This provision is foundational — when it is blocked, every other provision can be known but not personally received.

**Between-session question:** *"Was there a moment this week when the shepherd was present inside what you were walking through — not described as present but actually present to you? If not, what prevented you from receiving his presence in that moment?"*

### 6 — Honor
*"You prepare a table before me in the presence of my enemies." (v.5)*

**What the shepherd is doing:** Hosting a feast in the exact location of the threat. The enemies are not removed before the table is set — the provision is given while the exposure continues. This is honor received in the place of greatest vulnerability, visibility granted in the place of greatest risk of exposure.

**What blocks reception:** Shame — the inability to occupy the seat at the table when the enemies are watching. The shame wound's deepest conviction is that to be seen is to be exposed, and exposure leads to rejection. The prospect of being honored publicly while enemies witness it is unbearable to a client in deep shame — it intensifies the sense of risk rather than reducing it. Receiving honor in a place of vulnerability requires believing the shepherd's presence outweighs the enemies' witness. The shame wound does not believe this.

**What the block reveals:** The degree to which shame governs the client's relationship to visibility. The diagnostic question: *"What happens in you when you imagine being honored — seen and given a seat — in the place where you feel most exposed?"* The answer names the shame structure and its depth.

**Wound type most likely blocking:** Shame (primary). False Noble (secondary — honor received by grace while enemies watch feels threatening because it wasn't earned; the performance identity needs to be seen earning its seat, not receiving it as gift while others watch).

**Between-session question:** *"Is there a place in your life where the shepherd is setting a table — offering you honor or visibility — that you have been refusing to sit down at? What would it cost you to sit?"*

### 7 — Assurance
*"Surely goodness and mercy shall follow me all the days of my life." (v.6)*

**What the shepherd is doing:** Establishing a trajectory, not only a present moment. "Shall follow me" — the provision pursues the sheep. Goodness and *hesed* (covenant faithfulness, loyal love) are on the sheep's heels throughout life, not waiting at designated provision stations that the sheep must navigate to. The assurance is in the word "surely" and in "all the days" — both remove conditionality from the trajectory.

**What blocks reception:** The anxiety structure and the conditional God-image. The transactional God-image cannot receive "all the days" — the conditional qualifier the client applies ("as long as I...," "unless I...") interrupts the trajectory before it can land. The anxiety structure produces the compulsive need to verify that the goodness and mercy will still be there — it cannot rest in the declaration because resting would mean trusting without verification.

**What the block reveals:** Whether the client's functional God-image is covenantal or conditional. The conditional God-image (God's provision is contingent on the client's performance or current state) cannot receive "surely" — the word does not compute in a conditional framework. The diagnostic question: *"Do you believe goodness and mercy are currently following you — not will follow you if you perform well, but are actively pursuing you today?"* The client's answer reveals whether the trajectory is being received or held at arm's length.

**Wound type most likely blocking:** Performance (trajectory contingent on record; "surely" is conditioned by "as long as I keep producing"). Abandonment (trajectory too good to be personally true; "shall follow me" sounds like a general promise, not one with my name on it).

**Between-session question:** *"Is there evidence from this week that goodness and mercy were following you — that the provision was pursuing you rather than waiting for you to reach it?"*

## The Remaining Nine Provisions — Condensed Reference

| Provision | What the Shepherd Is Doing | Primary Block | Diagnostic Signal | Wound Type | Between-Session Question |
|-----------|---------------------------|---------------|-------------------|------------|--------------------------|
| **Relationship** — "The LORD is my shepherd" (v.1) | Establishing personal relational identity — not employer, manager, or function | Transactional or absent/distant God-image; the shepherd is generic, not personal | God-representation is the filter through which all other provisions are received; if this one isn't personal, none of the others land personally | Abandonment ("not specifically for me"), Performance ("for good sheep, not just any sheep") | *"Is the shepherd currently your shepherd — or do you relate to him as a shepherd in general who may or may not be attending to you specifically?"* |
| **Supply** — "I shall not want" (v.1) | Declaring sufficiency — the sheep under this shepherd's care will not lack what they genuinely need | Scarcity belief; anxiety about provision; the performance identity earns its supply | What the client is hoarding, controlling, or anxiously securing; the inability to name a need without immediately qualifying whether they deserve it met | Abandonment (no one provides), Performance (earn supply), Hermit (self-supply as protection) | *"What need did you carry this week without bringing it to the shepherd — and what made you hold it rather than receive provision?"* |
| **Grace foundation** — "For his name's sake" (v.3) | Establishing the unconditional ground of all provisions — none of them contingent on the sheep's record | The performance identity's belief that access to the provisions is earned; the client affirms grace theologically and applies conditionality functionally | Whether the client believes they currently qualify to receive the provisions; the diagnostic: "If you had performed poorly this week — would these provisions still apply to you?" | Performance (primary for all tracks); underpins every other block | *"Did you access any of the shepherd's provisions this week as though they were available regardless of your record — or did your performance level determine whether you felt entitled to them?"* |
| **Trial** — "Even though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death" (v.4) | Not removing the valley — naming it and remaining in it alongside the sheep | The belief that the valley itself is evidence of the shepherd's absence; reading darkness as abandonment | Whether the client has made the He→You transition; can they receive provisions while still in the valley, or are they waiting to exit it before believing the shepherd is present? | All wound types; primarily Abandonment (valley = forsaken) | *"Is there a valley you are currently walking through where you are waiting to exit before expecting the shepherd to show up — or have you received his presence inside it?"* |
| **Courage** — "I will fear no evil" (v.4) | Making courage possible through presence — the sheep's declaration is derivative of "for you are with me," not prior to it | Attempting to generate courage before presence has been received; willpower applied to fear | Whether Presence (provision 5) has been received; if not, courage is being attempted through resolve rather than derived from accompaniment | Hermit (terror structure; courage without received presence = exhausting performance) | *"Is the courage you're carrying this week generated by your own resolve, or derived from the shepherd's presence inside what you're afraid of?"* |
| **Protection** — "Your rod and your staff, they comfort me" (v.4) | Active defense (rod) and rescue/guidance (staff) — authority wielded for the sheep's protection, not against it | Authority wound — the rod and staff register as threat rather than comfort because past authority figures used their authority against the sheep | The client's felt response to the shepherd's authority; "does the rod and staff feel like comfort or threat to you?" surfaces the authority wound directly | Hermit (authority betrayal), Warrior (self-protection as alternative to received protection) | *"Is there a place this week where the shepherd's authority — his governance of your situation — felt like protection, or where it felt like threat?"* |
| **Consecration** — "You anoint my head with oil" (v.5) | Marking the sheep as belonging, as healed, and as set apart for purpose — hospitality, healing, and consecration in one act | Performance identity — consecration is received, not earned; purpose given by the shepherd, not produced by the sheep | Whether the client can receive purpose as given rather than earned; "if you were already set apart — already marked as belonging — what would change about how you approach your calling?" | Performance (must earn purpose), Shame (being distinguished feels threatening) | *"Is there a purpose or calling you are currently treating as something you need to earn or prove yourself into — rather than something the shepherd has already marked you for?"* |
| **Abundance** — "My cup overflows" (v.5) | Giving beyond what is needed — excess provision, grace that exceeds the requirement and spills over | Scarcity belief; the performance identity's ledger model (the amount should be proportional to the record) | What the client is rationing; what they believe is available only in limited supply; their response to the image of excess — does it seem implausible, undeserved, or wasteful? | Performance (abundance not proportional to record), Abandonment (overflow for others, not for me), Shame (overflow feels undeserved) | *"Where did grace exceed what you expected or felt you'd earned this week — and were you able to receive it as overflow, or did you reduce it to what felt proportional?"* |
| **Eternity** — "And I shall dwell in the house of the LORD forever" (v.6) | Establishing permanent belonging — not as visitor, servant, or temporary resident but as permanent dweller; "forever" removes all conditionality | Orphan spirit (cannot imagine permanent belonging; "I will eventually be asked to leave") and performance identity (forever feels contingent on sustained performance) | The deepest layer of the abandonment or performance wound; this provision is typically not receivable until significant formation work has occurred because the implicit belief in impermanence is the most deeply encoded | Abandonment (no such thing as permanent, especially for me), Performance (forever conditional on sustained record) | *"Is there a part of you that is waiting for the invitation to stay to be revoked — that is living as a temporary guest rather than a permanent dweller? Where does that show up?"* |

## Wound-Type Diagnostic Summary

When a specific wound type is confirmed through Stage 1 work, this table identifies which provisions are most commonly blocked and in what order they tend to open.

| Wound Type | Provisions Most Commonly Blocked | Provisions That Open First | Provisions That Open Last |
|------------|----------------------------------|---------------------------|--------------------------|
| **Warrior** (performance/betrayal) | Rest, Supply, Guidance (conditionality), Abundance, Assurance | Presence (when performance armor cracks) | Rest (the last to open; lies down only when the shepherd's safety is fully received) |
| **Hermit** (terror/withdrawal) | Refreshment, Protection, Presence, Relationship (personal) | Healing (often the first crack — brokenness allows reception) | Honor (visibility is the last frontier; requires the shame dimension to move first) |
| **False Noble** (shame/performance masquerade) | Honor, Consecration, Rest, Guidance | Healing (often disguised as "I need to recover") | Honor (cannot be seen at the table without the performance justification; opens in late Phase 3) |

## The Provision Sequence in Formation

The provisions do not have to be received in textual order — but there is a logic to which ones open first and which ones are last.

**Foundational provisions (must move first before others open):**
- **Relationship** — if the shepherd is not personal, no other provision is received personally
- **Presence** — if "thou art with me" is not received, the other provisions are known but not felt

**Mid-formation provisions:**
- **Rest, Refreshment, Healing, Guidance** — these are the primary formation zone; most Phase 2–3 work addresses blockages here

**Late-formation provisions:**
- **Honor** — requires that the shame wound has moved; the client must be able to be seen at the table
- **Assurance and Eternity** — require that the conditional God-image has been substantially replaced; "all the days" and "forever" cannot land until the conditionality structure is broken

## Client-Facing Use

### The Weekly Formation Question

This question is offered to the client as a between-session practice beginning in Phase 2:

> *"Which provision of the shepherd do you most need to receive this week — and which one feels most blocked?"*

The client is not asked to receive all sixteen. They are asked to identify one provision that is genuinely needed and one that is genuinely blocked. These are often different — what the client needs most is frequently what is most blocked.

Follow-up in the next session:
> *"Which provision did you identify? What happened when you tried to receive it?"*

### The Diagnostic Pair

The provision the client most wants to receive and the provision they are least able to receive are both diagnostic data.

- *Most wanted* — what the wound has most deprived them of; what the false covenant object was trying to provide
- *Most blocked* — where the wound is currently active; what the implicit encoding is resisting most

When these two are the same provision — when what is most wanted is also most blocked — the practitioner has identified the primary formation target for the current season.

### Client Language for Each Provision

When introducing a provision in session, use the "That is..." language from the Phrase Reference document to ground it concretely before working with the block:

> "The shepherd restores your soul — that's healing. Not symptom management. Not coping strategies. The whole self, reconstituted. Is that available to you right now — or does some part of you believe it's beyond what's on offer for you specifically?"

## Watch Points

**Do not diagnose the block as a character failure.** The client who cannot rest is not lazy or disobedient. They cannot rest because the wound has made rest feel unsafe. The block is diagnostic information, not a spiritual failure.

**The blocked provision is not always the wound the client names.** A client who presents with the performance wound may be most blocked at Presence, not at Rest — the abandonment structure is underneath the performance structure and it is what makes rest impossible (lying down requires trusting someone is watching). Follow the provision that is most blocked, not the provision that most obviously matches the wound type.

**Presence is the load-bearing provision.** If "you are with me" is not being received, the other provisions can be known but will not be personally felt. The practitioner should attend to Presence above all others. When Presence opens, the other provisions frequently begin to open alongside it.

**Between-session practice should be provision-specific.** Assigning "spend time with God this week" is not targeted enough. The between-session practice should name the specific provision: "This week, practice receiving rest — the shepherd's made-safe green pasture. When you have a moment to lie down, practice asking: what safety has the shepherd established that I am not yet receiving?"

**The "for his name's sake" diagnostic is primary.** Before working any specific blocked provision, verify whether the client believes the provision is available to them regardless of their performance this week. If not, address the grace foundation before the specific provision — otherwise the formation work will be received as another conditional requirement rather than as an unconditional offer.
