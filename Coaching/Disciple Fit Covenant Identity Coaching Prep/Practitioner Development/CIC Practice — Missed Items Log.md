## Why Test Before Study, Not After

The instinct to read and re-read the CIC source material before running a practice test feels responsible, but the learning-science evidence points the other way: attempting recall — even badly, even mostly wrong — builds more durable memory than reading the material first and testing afterward. This section is why; the Practice Protocol section below is what to actually do about it.

- **Testing itself is the more effective study method (the testing effect).** Roediger & Karpicke (2006) found that retrieval practice — trying to recall an answer, whether or not you succeed — produces significantly better long-term retention than repeated re-reading of the same material. This holds even when the recall attempt fails: the effort of searching memory for an answer, not the act of reading the correct answer, is what does the encoding work. Practically, this means running `/cic-l1` cold, with low confidence and a high miss rate, is not a wasted or premature attempt — it is the most effective single thing to do at this stage, more effective than reading source documents first.
- **Struggling to recall is a feature, not a problem to eliminate first (desirable difficulty).** Bjork (1994) named this class of finding "desirable difficulties" — conditions that make performance harder and slower in the moment but produce stronger, longer-lasting learning. Trying to answer a CIC question from memory and getting it wrong is exactly this kind of difficulty. Removing the struggle by studying the answer in advance removes the mechanism that builds the memory.
- **Spacing repeated attempts apart beats massing them together (the spacing effect).** Cepeda, Pashler, Vul, Wixted & Rohrer's (2006) meta-analysis of the spacing effect — one of the most consistently replicated findings in memory research — shows that the same number of study or practice repetitions produces measurably better long-term retention when spread across days rather than crammed into one sitting. Running `/cic-l1` three times in an afternoon is a weaker use of that effort than running it once every two to three days.
- **Mixing question types beats drilling one at a time (interleaving).** Rohrer & Taylor (2007) found that interleaving different problem or question types produces better discrimination and transfer than blocking practice by type (all of Domain A, then all of Domain B). The `/cic-l1` prompt already rotates across knowledge domains within a single run rather than grouping by domain — that design choice is correct and should not be overridden by, for example, re-reading all of Model Architecture before moving on to Diagnostic Sequence.
- **Explaining *why* an answer matters, not just *what* it is, deepens the encoding (elaborative interrogation).** The `/cic-l1` answer key already appends a third line to every answer — "why this matters operationally for a CIC practitioner" — alongside the correct answer and its source. That is elaborative interrogation built into the format. Its value depends on actually engaging it: reading past that line the way one skims a definition defeats the purpose. Saying or writing, in one sentence, how the answer would change something inside an actual session is what makes the elaboration do its job.
- **Generating an answer before seeing it beats recognizing the answer once shown (the generation effect).** Answer every question — including a guess you are not confident in — before looking at the key. Reading the question and moving straight to the answer key skips the retrieval step; at that point the exercise tests recognition ("yes, that looks right") rather than recall, which is a weaker form of memory and the one least likely to transfer into a live session.

## Practice Protocol

1. **Start `/cic-l1` cold.** No pre-reading of source documents beforehand. Answer every question as best you can, including guesses, before checking the answer key.
2. **Space repeat runs two to three days apart**, not back-to-back in the same sitting. Between runs, do not deliberately re-read the corpus "to prepare for next time" — that reintroduces the open-ended pre-study pattern this protocol is designed to avoid.
3. **Advance to `/cic-l2` once L1 answers come without hesitation on most items.** This is a rough readiness signal, not a percentage to optimize toward. L2 tests mechanism-level *why* questions, which draw on a different retrieval skill than L1's recognition/recall — worth testing on its own rather than folding more L1 reps into the same level indefinitely.
4. **Use a missed item as the trigger for reading, not the other way around.** When a specific domain keeps coming up wrong across two or more runs — this log exists to make that pattern visible — that is the signal to open the one source document covering that domain (via the [[Covenant Identity Coaching Prep — File Index|File Index]] or the Document Reading Order) and read it: targeted lookup after a failed retrieval attempt, not broad reading before the first attempt.
5. **Hold the line at the stop condition already set, not a fuller-mastery target.** [[Covenant Identity — Pre-Practice Readiness Action Plan]] already defines the finish line for structure internalization: a clean, unaided `/cic-l5` run. Treat reaching that as done. Continuing to drill L1–L6 past that point in pursuit of comprehensive command of the full document set re-opens the exact open-ended trap that plan was written to close.

- **Q:** Define "affections" in CIC. What are they *not*?
  **Answer given:** "Not relying on God."
  **Why this misses:** This describes the *Dependency Probe* — Affections Discovery layer 3 (what the client is trusting for security, significance, or identity) — not the definition of affections itself. The question asked what affections *are*, and no positive definition was offered.
  **Correct answer:** Affections are what the client prioritizes, trusts, and depends on — the functional center of life, the organizing loves. They are explicitly **not emotions**; a client can report feeling calm while their affections (what they actually organize their time, energy, and anxiety around) are pointed at something other than God.

  **Why it matters operationally:** Mistaking affections for feelings leads a coach to process emotion in-session instead of surfacing what the client is functionally trusting — which is the actual Stage 1 target. A coach chasing feeling-language will miss the affections data entirely.
