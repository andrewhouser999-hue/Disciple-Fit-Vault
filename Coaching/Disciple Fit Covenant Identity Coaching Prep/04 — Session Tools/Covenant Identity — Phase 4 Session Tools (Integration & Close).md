# Covenant Identity — Phase 4 Session Tools
## Integration & Close
*Practitioner reference. Sessions 8–10 (range varies with engagement length). Typically 2-3 sessions. Read in conjunction with Phase 3 Session Tools and the Growth Report Template.*

---

## Phase Handoff Note
*What Phase 3 produces. What Phase 4 requires.*

Phase 3 ends with evidence — at least one covenant identity layer beginning to be embodied, a schema interruption capacity that has been practiced and demonstrated, a present-story statement that has moved from the B2 baseline, identity votes made and debriefed. Phase 4 takes that evidence and builds it into a structure that holds after the engagement ends.

Phase 4 requires that Phase 3 is actually finished. This is not a formality. Phase 4 cannot consolidate what Phase 3 has not built. If Phase 3 installation work is incomplete — the declaration has not yet produced somatic shift, the schema interruption is purely cognitive, the present-story statement has not moved — extend Phase 3. Phase 4 entered prematurely produces a polished close of an unfinished engagement.

**What Phase 4 inherits from Phase 3:**
- The specific covenant identity layer work that was done — which layers moved, which remain primarily cognitive
- The schema interruption practice the client has developed — what it looks like for this client, where it works and where it still breaks down
- The re-authored present-story statement, if it was reached
- The identity votes and what the client discovered in them
- The state of the lament arc, if present — whether it has moved toward new orientation or remains in disorientation

**What Phase 4 does with it:**
Phase 4 does not add new formation. It stabilizes, names, and seals what Phase 3 built. It builds the forward structure that will hold the work when the coaching container is gone. And it closes the engagement with the weight and honesty it deserves.

**What readiness for Phase 4 looks like:**
The client has living evidence — in their body, their choices, their language — that the covenant identity is becoming operational. They can describe specific moments where they acted from the new story. At least one declaration has landed somatically. The coach can look at the Session Growth Notes and find specific, irrefutable evidence of movement from the intake baseline.

---

## 1. Phase Job Description

Phase 4 has three jobs: consolidate, sustain, and close.

**Consolidate** — Gather the evidence of what actually changed. Not what the client hopes changed, not what the coach observed — what the record shows. The Session Growth Notes across all sessions contain this evidence. The Growth Report synthesizes it. Phase 4 brings it before the client explicitly, in their own words, so they cannot argue with it.

**Sustain** — Build the forward structure that will hold the work when the coaching container is gone. The schema will activate again. The old story will return. The competing commitment will reassert itself under pressure. Phase 4 does not pretend otherwise. It gives the client a named protocol for when it happens — what to do, who to call, what the earliest warning signal is — and builds the relational ecology and daily practice that make the new story more likely to hold.

**Close** — End the engagement with the honesty and weight it deserves. The coaching relationship is real. It has held significant material. Ending it is not nothing. Phase 4 names the grief, names the gratitude, names what remains, and closes with the Growth Report — the client's own words, read back to them, as a record they carry out of the engagement.

**What Phase 4 is not:**
It is not a victory lap. Generic celebration at the close of an engagement — "look how far you've come!" — cheapens the work. The Growth Report does the work of naming growth; Phase 4 does not need to supplement it with coach enthusiasm. It is also not new excavation. When the client brings new material in Phase 4 — new memories, new wounds, new schema content — the temptation is to follow it. Resist. Phase 4 is not the time to open new terrain. If the new material is urgent and cannot be held, name it honestly and decide together whether it warrants extension or referral.

Phase 4 is not the beginning of an ongoing coaching relationship. The ethics of non-dependency require that Phase 4 actively builds the client's independence from the coach. When Phase 4 is complete, the client should be capable of continuing the work without requiring the container. They may choose to return for a new engagement at a future season — that is different from dependency.

## 2. Session Structure

Phase 4 typically spans two to three sessions. The structure below describes the two primary session types.

---

### Phase 4 Session 1 — Consolidation & Forward Structure (60–75 minutes)

**Opening — Naming the Transition (10 minutes)**

Do not begin Phase 4 as if it were another Phase 3 session. Name the transition explicitly.

*"We are in the final phase of our work together. I want to use our remaining sessions well — and that means being intentional about what we're doing. Today I want to look at what has actually changed, and begin building the structure that holds it after we're done."*

This naming matters. Clients who sense the ending is coming but don't hear it named often begin either pulling back (protective withdrawal from the relationship before it ends) or adding new material (keeping the engagement alive). Naming the transition creates a clean container for Phase 4's actual work.

**Consolidation — What the Evidence Shows (20–25 minutes)**

This is not a summary or a review. It is a specific examination of evidence.

Pull from the Session Growth Notes across all sessions — specifically Section 4 (language evolution) and Section 3 (identity votes). Read specific before/after language pairs back to the client. Not the coach's interpretation — the client's own words, then and now.

*"At intake you said [A1 or B2 verbatim]. In session [#] you said [verbatim from Growth Notes]. That's not my observation — those are your own words."*

Then ask: *"What do you make of that difference? Not what you think I want to hear — what do you actually make of it?"*

The consolidation work is not celebration. It is the client receiving the record of their own growth — which most clients struggle to receive without deflecting. The deflection is itself data: the schema is still operating at the point of self-recognition. Name it when you see it.

*"You just minimized what you heard. Why?"*

**Forward Structure Design (20–25 minutes)**

Phase 3 built the schema interruption capacity, the declaration work, the narrative re-authoring, the identity votes. Phase 4's job is to build the conditions that make it possible to continue this work after the engagement ends, in the client's actual life, without a coach.

Three dimensions of forward structure:

**1. Practice design** — What specific practices will the client maintain? These are not aspirational habits. They are small, named, sustainable practices directly connected to the formation work. Ask: *"What is the one practice — daily or weekly — that most directly keeps the schema from running on autopilot? Not the ideal practice — the one you'll actually do."*

**2. Relational ecology** — Who in the client's life can witness and reinforce the new story? The covenant identity needs relationship to sustain it. The client is not wired to hold their own formation alone. Ask: *"Who in your life sees the person you're becoming? Who can you call when the old story comes back hard? Is there someone who needs to know what happened here — not as a testimonial, but because they are part of your forward?"*

**3. Setback protocol** — Design this explicitly. The client will hit the old story again — not because the work failed, but because formation is a lifelong process and the schema is wired deeply. Phase 4's most neglected and most important contribution is a named protocol for when it happens. Ask: *"When the schema activates again — and it will — what is your plan? Name it step by step."*

A basic setback protocol has three elements: (1) the client can recognize the earliest warning signal in their body; (2) they can name the lie that fires; (3) they know one specific action to take and one specific person to call. That is enough. It does not need to be elaborate — it needs to be named and owned.

**Landing (10 minutes)**

Close with the Letter to Beginning Self prompt. Introduce it as the final exercise of the engagement.

*"Before our last session, I want you to write a letter — to yourself at intake, before we started this work. The version of you who was [brief description in their own language from B2]. Tell that person what was coming. What you discovered. What surprised you. What you want them to know about who they are. You can bring it to our final session to read aloud, or simply have written it."*

Do not over-explain. Give the prompt and let it land.

---

### Phase 4 Final Session — Growth Report (60–90 minutes)

The final session is the Growth Report session. Everything else in Phase 4 has been preparing the client and the coach for this.

**Preparation (before the session):**

The Growth Report is prepared by the coach, fully, before this session. It should be complete — all sections populated, all verbatim language drawn from the Intake Addendum and Session Growth Notes. Read through the Growth Report Template for the preparation requirements.

Bring two printed copies. One for the client to hold while the coach reads. One for the coach.

**Opening — Setting the Tone (5 minutes)**

Do not open with small talk. The session has a weight. Honor it from the start.

*"Today I'm going to read you something. It's a record of this engagement — built entirely from your own words, from the intake form and from our sessions. I haven't paraphrased or improved what you said. Your words are the evidence. I'm going to read it aloud. I want you to listen. There will be silence. That silence is part of it."*

Give the client their copy. Ask them to hold it rather than read along.

**Reading the Growth Report (30–45 minutes)**

Read slowly. Do not rush any section.

The most significant moments will be in Section I (Where You Were) and Section IX (The Gap Closed). When the client hears their own intake language — the words they used when they arrived — read back to them after all of this work, the weight of the distance traveled is often visceral. Let it land. Do not fill the silence.

When you see something happen in the client's body — contraction, tears, a long exhale, a stillness — pause. *"I notice something just happened. What is it?"*

Then continue. Do not stay too long in any one moment. The full reading is the experience — not a debrief of each section as you go.

**After the Reading — Open Space (10–15 minutes)**

When the reading is complete, close the report. Sit for a moment in silence. Then:

*"What do you want to say about what you just heard?"*

Do not guide this. The client may cry, speak, sit, or need a moment. Wait for whatever comes.

After the client has spoken — whatever form that takes — ask: *"What is most significant to you in what you heard?"*

This is not a discussion of the report's contents. It is the client receiving their own story. The coach's job here is presence, not facilitation.

**Letter to Beginning Self (10–15 minutes, if written)**

If the client has written the letter, ask if they want to read it aloud. Do not require it — make space for it. If they read it, listen without comment. Receive it. When they finish: *"Thank you for letting me hear that."*

If the letter was not written, offer: *"What would you say to that person — the one who filled out the intake form — if you could say one thing right now?"* Let the answer be whatever it is.

**Closing — The End of the Engagement (5–10 minutes)**

This close is not a session summary or a review of growth. It is the close of a real relationship, held with honesty.

Name what is real: *"This has been significant work. It has been my privilege to be present for this season of your story."*

Name what remains: *"The work of becoming who you already are in Christ is the work of a lifetime. What happened here is not the ceiling. It is proof that the floor is real — and that you can stand on it."*

Name the ending honestly: *"This engagement is complete. That is a real thing to mark. What do you want to say before we close?"*

Give the client the printed Growth Report. Tell them: *"Keep this. Read it when you forget. Read it when you think you haven't changed. Read it when you're back in the old story and need to remember that you've already walked out of it once."*

## 3. Primary Question Library

### Consolidation
- *"What has actually changed — not what you hope has changed, but what the evidence in your own words shows?"*
  — Forces the client to name specific evidence rather than general impressions.
- *"If someone who knew you well at intake met you today, what would they notice that is different?"*
  — External perspective. Often surfaces evidence the client has not named because they have adapted to the change and no longer sees it.
- *"Which of the nine covenant identity layers has moved the most during this engagement? Describe what that movement feels like from the inside."*
  — Connects the consolidation to the specific architecture of the model.
- *"What do you know about yourself now that you didn't know when we started?"*
  — Captures formation knowledge — not just behavioral change but the client's deepened self-understanding.
- *"What did this engagement cost you? Not financially — in terms of what it required of you."*
  — Honors the weight of the work. Clients who minimize what the engagement cost them are also minimizing what it produced.
- *"You just minimized what you heard. Why?"*
  — Named directly when the client deflects growth evidence. The deflection is schema material at the point of self-recognition.

### Setback Protocol Design
- *"When the old story comes back — and it will — what is your protocol? Name it step by step."*
  — Direct. The "when not if" framing normalizes the setback without minimizing the work.
- *"What is the earliest warning signal you've learned to recognize? The body signal, before the thought fires."*
  — Grounds the protocol in the somatic work from Phase 3.
- *"What is the specific lie that most reliably fires in your hardest moments? And what is the specific covenant truth that addresses it?"*
  — The core of the protocol. Not general — the exact lie and the exact counter-statement.
- *"What is one action you take and one person you call when the schema is running hard?"*
  — Names the two practical elements of the protocol: action and relationship.
- *"What does it mean for the work to have 'failed' when the schema activates again? Is that failure?"*
  — Addresses the narrative the client will likely impose on setbacks. Setback ≠ failure of the coaching or the formation. It is the ongoing nature of sanctification.

### Relational Ecology
- *"Who in your life sees the person you are becoming? Who witnesses the new story?"*
  — The relational dimension of sustainability. Formation requires witness.
- *"Is there someone in your life who needs to know what happened here — not as a testimonial, but because they are part of your forward?"*
  — Opens the question of whether the growth needs to be communicated to key relationships.
- *"Which relationships in your life consistently activate the old story? Not bad people — but relationships where the old pattern runs easily."*
  — Names relational ecology risk factors without shaming the relationships.
- *"What does your community of formation look like going forward — not ideally, but actually?"*
  — Forces specificity. "I'll be in community" is not a plan. "I'll be in [specific group] meeting [specific frequency] with [specific commitment]" is a plan.

### Forward Structure / Practice Design
- *"What is the one practice that most directly keeps the schema from running on autopilot? The one you'll actually do, not the ideal one."*
  — Sustainability requires realism. The ideal practice that isn't sustained is worse than a modest practice that is.
- *"What structure — routine, rhythm, commitment — would make the new story more likely in [specific area where the old story still has traction]?"*
  — Connects the structure design to the specific terrain that remains unresolved.
- *"What does your daily life look like, concretely, in six months — if you hold what was built here?"*
  — Vision work that is specific rather than aspirational. The specificity is the formation.

### Growth Report Session
- *"What do you want to say about what you just heard?"*
  — Open space. Do not guide. The first response after the reading belongs entirely to the client.
- *"What is most significant to you in what you heard?"*
  — After the initial response has been received. Invites the client to locate the most weighty evidence for themselves.
- *"There was a moment when I read [specific section] and something happened in you. What was that?"*
  — Names a specific somatic or affective response the coach observed during the reading. Keeps the client in contact with the weight of their own story.
- *"What do you want to say to yourself — to the person who started this work?"*
  — Opens the Letter to Beginning Self territory if the letter was not written.

### Ending the Engagement
- *"What are you taking with you that you didn't bring in?"*
  — The central question of the close. Not a summary — an accounting.
- *"What do you still need — and where will you get it?"*
  — Honest acknowledgment that the engagement did not resolve everything. Names what remains and where the client will go for it.
- *"What do you want me to know about what this has meant?"*
  — Gives the client space to speak about the relationship, not just the work.
- *"What would it mean to carry this forward without me?"*
  — The non-dependency question. The client's answer reveals whether they have internalized the work or are still looking to the coach as the container.
- *"What do you want to say before we close?"*
  — The final question of every final session. Do not rush. Wait for what comes.

## 4. Coach Observation Guide

### The Emotional Climate of Phase 4

Phase 4 has a distinctive emotional quality unlike any other phase. Multiple things are present simultaneously — often in the same session:

**Grief** — the relationship is ending. Both coach and client may feel this. The grief is appropriate. The coaching relationship has held real material, real exposure, real change. Ending it is a loss. The question is not whether to feel it but how to hold it without letting it distort the work. A coach who over-extends the engagement to manage their own grief about its ending is not serving the client.

**Pride** — the client sees their own work reflected in the Growth Report and in the consolidation conversation. For some clients, receiving this without deflection is the final formation work of the engagement. Watch for the schema activating at precisely this point — Defectiveness/Shame clients often have the most difficulty receiving the record of their own growth. They will minimize, qualify, or attribute it to the coach or to God in ways that release them from having to own it.

**Fear** — what happens when the container is gone? Clients who have benefited significantly from the coaching container often face real anxiety at Phase 4. This is not a reason to extend the engagement — it is the exact moment where the forward structure and setback protocol do their work. The fear is appropriate information. Name it and use it for the protocol design.

**Relief** — some clients arrive at Phase 4 with relief, sometimes mixed with guilt about feeling relieved. The work has been hard. The engagement has asked a great deal. Relief is not ingratitude — it is the body knowing that a concentrated season of formation work is completing.

Hold all of these without collapsing into any of them. Phase 4's tone is neither celebratory nor heavy — it is honest.

### Reading the Room During the Growth Report

The Growth Report reading is the most relationally intense moment of the engagement. Prepare for these responses:

**Tears at Section I (Where You Were):** The most common response when the client hears their own intake language read back. They had forgotten how far back the beginning was. Do not fill the silence. Let the tears land. When the client is ready to continue, continue.

**Minimizing during the reading:** *"I don't know why I said it that way"* or *"I've always known that, really."* This is the schema at the point of growth reception. Do not argue. Note it. If it continues through multiple sections, pause: *"I've noticed you're moving quickly past the evidence of your own change. What is that about?"*

**Surprise at coach observations (Section VII):** Clients frequently do not know that the coach was tracking the somatic and behavioral evidence they logged in the coach observations. The specificity of *"you arrived at session 1 with your shoulders like this — by session 6 that had changed"* is often more moving than any theological content. This is why the coach observations section of the Session Growth Notes matters.

**Flatness or dissociation during the reading:** A client who is flat through the entire Growth Report reading is either deeply defended (schema protecting against the full reception of the work) or not yet done with Phase 3 (the installation was not complete enough to bear the weight of the report). If flatness is present through Section IX, name it gently after the reading: *"I noticed you moved through that without much reaction. What was happening inside while I was reading?"*

### The Non-Dependency Indicator

The primary Phase 4 coach observation is whether the client is moving toward independence or toward dependency. Markers of genuine independence:

- The client speaks about the work in first person and past tense: *"I learned that..."* *"I discovered..."* — not *"you showed me..."* or *"this coaching taught me..."*
- The client's forward structure is self-generated, not coach-assigned
- When asked *"what would you do if the old story came back hard?"* the client has an answer that references their own resources — not *"I'd call you"*
- The client is curious about their own formation rather than dependent on the coach's read of it

Dependency markers that warrant naming:
- The client asks to extend the engagement with no new specific work to address — the request is relational
- The client speaks about what they will *miss* about the coaching more than what they are taking with them
- The forward structure design keeps circling back to the coach as a resource
- The client becomes destabilized at the mention of the engagement ending

If dependency is present, name it directly and compassionately: *"I notice you're describing your forward in terms of what you'll do without me, rather than what you'll do. Those are different orientations. What do you think is underneath that?"*

## 5. Common Pitfalls

**1. Reopening excavation in Phase 4.**
New material will come in Phase 4 sessions — new memories, new schema content, new wounds the client is ready to name now that they weren't ready to name before. The temptation is to follow it. Resist. Phase 4 is not excavation. If the new material is urgent and cannot be held, name it explicitly: *"What you're bringing matters. It is also new terrain at the close of our engagement. We need to decide together whether this warrants extending the work or whether this is something you carry forward to a future engagement or to [referral resource]."* Do not open Phase 2 work in a Phase 4 session and then close the engagement two sessions later.

**2. Making the Growth Report session a debrief rather than an event.**
The Growth Report is not a document to discuss. It is a document to be received. Read it. Do not pre-summarize sections. Do not stop to check in after each section. Do not soften the harder sections with commentary. Read it aloud, slowly, in full. The experience of hearing their own words — the before language and the after language, the milestones, the coach observations — is what the client needs. The debrief comes after the reading, not during it.

**3. Skipping the setback protocol because the client is doing well.**
The client who is thriving at Phase 4 does not need the setback protocol less — they need it exactly as much as the client who is struggling. The protocol is not crisis planning; it is formation infrastructure. Every client leaving a Covenant Identity Coaching engagement should be able to name their earliest somatic warning signal, the specific lie that fires in their hardest moments, and one person to call. Without it, the first significant activation after the engagement ends will feel like the work failed.

**4. Extending the engagement out of relational attachment rather than clinical necessity.**
Both coach and client can develop genuine attachment to the coaching relationship — the work is deep and the relationship is real. The coach must distinguish between two reasons to extend: (1) Phase 3 installation work is genuinely incomplete (the extension serves the work), and (2) neither party wants the relationship to end (the extension serves the relationship, not the client's formation). The second is not a legitimate clinical reason to extend. Name it and close.

**5. Failing to name the grief of ending.**
Coaches who move briskly through closure — "well, you've done great work, here's your report, keep in touch!" — leave the client carrying unprocessed grief about the ending of something real. The coaching relationship held significant material. Ending it is not routine. Name the loss explicitly: *"This has been a real relationship, not just a professional engagement. Ending it is something to acknowledge."* Let the client respond to that before moving on.

**6. Making the consolidation section a praise session.**
When the coach spends the consolidation conversation affirming the client's growth — *"you've come so far, you should be really proud"* — the evidence loses its power. The Growth Report's power is in the specificity of the client's own words, not the coach's enthusiasm. Let the evidence speak. Ask the client what they make of it. Receive the deflection when it comes. The coach's job is to keep the evidence in front of the client until they receive it — not to celebrate it for them.

**7. Leaving the relational ecology unaddressed.**
The client is returning to a relational environment that formed them. If key relationships in that environment consistently activate the primary schema — a parent who reinforces the Defectiveness lie, a marriage that runs the Emotional Deprivation pattern, a church culture that inverts the indicative/imperative at every turn — the work built in the coaching engagement will be under sustained pressure. Phase 4 must name the relational ecology honestly. Not to condemn those relationships, but to ensure the client is not walking back into them without awareness.

## 6. Integration Notes

**What feeds into Phase 4:**
- **Session Growth Notes (all sessions):** The complete evidence base. Review every session's Growth Notes before preparing the Growth Report. Section 4 (language evolution across all sessions) and Section 2 (growth moments) are the primary source material for the report's specific sections.
- **Intake Addendum Part A and Part B:** The before-baseline. Every verbatim quote in the Growth Report's Section I (Where You Were) comes from here. The B2 present-story statement is the before-anchor for the narrative comparison.
- **Summary Baseline table:** Track how the working hypothesis at the start of the engagement compares to what was actually found and worked. The Growth Report's narrative arc (Section II) is built partly from this comparison.
- **Growth Report Template:** The complete preparation guide is embedded in the template. Phase 4 does not replace the template — it operationalizes the session in which the report is presented.

**What Phase 4 produces:**
- **Growth Report (presented and given to client):** The primary deliverable of the entire engagement system. The client leaves with a physical document — their story, in their own words, sealed by the coach's signature.
- **Letter to Beginning Self:** Written by the client. If read aloud in the final session, it is one of the most significant formation moments of the engagement. If written but not shared, it is still formative. If not yet written, the prompt has been offered and the client carries it.
- **The client's forward structure:** Documented informally in the final session. Could be written by the client and kept — not a formal deliverable, but a named set of practices, relational commitments, and a setback protocol.

## 7. Transition Marker / Engagement Close

**The engagement is complete when:**
- The Growth Report has been presented, read aloud in full, and received by the client
- The client has a named setback protocol — they can describe their earliest somatic warning signal, the specific lie that fires, and one action and one person to call
- The client has identified at least one person in their life who can witness and reinforce the new story
- The forward structure has been designed with enough specificity to be sustainable — named practices, not aspirations
- The grief of ending has been named by the coach and received by the client
- The Letter to Beginning Self has been written, or the invitation has been clearly given
- The client can state in their own words what they are taking with them — not what the coach gave them, but what they built

**The engagement is not complete when:**
- Phase 3 installation work remains unfinished — extend Phase 3 rather than closing with incomplete work
- The client has no named plan for when the old story returns
- The Growth Report has not been presented — do not close the engagement without it
- The client is in active crisis or at a destabilizing threshold — close is not appropriate until stability is re-established

**What to do when the close is complicated:**

Sometimes the engagement cannot close cleanly. The client surfaces urgent new material in Phase 4. A significant life event destabilizes the final sessions. The formation work is genuinely incomplete and both coach and client know it.

In these cases: name it directly. *"I want to be honest with you — I don't think we're at a clean close yet. Here is what I observe: [specific]. I want to offer you [one or two additional sessions / a referral to / a future engagement]. What do you want to do with that?"*

Do not manufacture a clean close where one does not exist. The Growth Report has a section called "What Continues" precisely because the work is never entirely complete. The coaching engagement is a chapter, not the whole story.

*The engagement closes when the client can stand on the floor that was built — not when the floor is finished. It is never finished. The close honors what was built and releases the client to continue building without the coach holding the container.*
