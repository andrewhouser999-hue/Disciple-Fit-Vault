# Covenant Identity — Session Prep Template
*Complete before every session. Target time: 5-10 minutes. Copy the template block below for each session — keep completed blocks in the client's Session Growth Notes file or a separate prep log.*

*This is not a planning document. It is a readiness check. The coach who arrives unprepared is already behind before the session begins. The coach who arrives carrying unprocessed material from their own life has already introduced noise into the room. Five minutes before the session changes the quality of what happens in it.*

---

## Why This Exists

Every session in this model is built on two things: a well-prepared coach and a clearly held client. The Session Growth Notes track the client across sessions. This template tracks the coach's readiness and the session's specific terrain before the work begins.

The four failure modes this template prevents:
1. **Arriving without the thread** — not knowing where last session left off, what the client committed to, or what the current phase requires
2. **Carrying noise into the room** — the coach's own stress, fatigue, or unprocessed material bleeding into the session
3. **Generic presence** — meeting the client as a category (shame-schema client, Phase 2 wound work) rather than as a specific person at a specific moment
4. **Agenda attachment** — arriving with a plan rather than an intention; directing the session rather than following the client

---

## How to Use

1. Pull the client's **Session Growth Notes** (most recent session) and **Intake Addendum**
2. Work through the five parts in order — do not skip Part 1 to get to the client material
3. Total time: 5-10 minutes
4. When complete, close the prep document and arrive

## Coach Notes on Each Part

**Part 1 — Why it comes first:**
The coach who skips Part 1 and goes straight to the client file is prioritizing preparation over presence. The problem is that an unprepared inner state will show up in the session regardless of how well the coach knows the client's material. Name what you're carrying. A named distraction can be set aside. An unnamed one cannot.

**Part 2 — The one-sentence summary and the commitment:**
These two fields from the Session Growth Notes are the connective tissue between sessions. The one-sentence summary is the session's headline — it tells the coach what the most significant thing was. The commitment is the accountability thread — how the client carried the session's work into their week. Do not improvise these from memory. Look them up.

*Timing clarification for this template:* Parts 1–5 are completed before the session. The One-Sentence Summary (Part 2's source field in Session Growth Notes) is completed immediately after the session ends — before the full Growth Notes documentation. This means the summary field in Part 2 of this template is populated from the *previous* session's post-session writing. If the One-Sentence Summary was not written immediately after the last session, write it now from memory before completing the rest of Part 2 — the prep sequence depends on it being accurate, not reconstructed.

**Part 3 — Phase-specific review:**
The review fields shift because what matters in each phase is different. Phase 1 preparation is about the client's baseline language — knowing what they said at intake so the session can go beneath the surface quickly. Phase 2 preparation is about the map — knowing the schema hypothesis and the Immunity to Change state so excavation is targeted, not exploratory. Phase 3 preparation is about the installation thread — knowing which tool, which layer, which identity vote so the session builds rather than restarts. Phase 4 preparation is about the close — knowing what remains so nothing is left unaddressed.

**Part 4 — Intention vs. plan:**
An intention is a direction. A plan is a script. Sessions go off plan constantly — and they should. The client's most significant material rarely arrives on the agenda. Holding an intention means the coach knows what they are working toward without needing to arrive there by a particular route. The question "what I want to resist doing today" is equally important as the intention itself — it names the coach's most likely failure mode and puts it on notice.

**Part 5 — Who is this person:**
This question exists because it is easy to arrive at a session thinking about the client's schema, the last session's breakthrough, or the phase's requirements — and forget that you are meeting a human being who bears the image of God, whom God sees and chose, in whom God's presence lives. The covenant identity architecture applies to the client. Holding one layer in mind before the session begins changes the quality of presence the coach brings into the room.
