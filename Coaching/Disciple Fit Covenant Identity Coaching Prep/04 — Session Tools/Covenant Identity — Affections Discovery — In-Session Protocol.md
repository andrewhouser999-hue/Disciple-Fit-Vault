# Covenant Identity — Affections Discovery — In-Session Protocol
*Coach-Facing | In-Session Tool | Stage 1 of the Diagnostic Sequence*

*Related: [[Affections Discovery Process — Affections, Trust & Priorities]]; [[Covenant Identity — Diagnostic Lens Transition Logic]]; [[Covenant Identity — Whole-Person Identity Framework]]; [[Covenant Identity — God-Exclusion Map]]*

---

## Purpose and Position

This tool structures the in-session execution of affections discovery — Stage 1 of the diagnostic sequence. Its goal is to surface what the client is functionally trusting, prioritizing, and relying on beneath their presenting story — and to build a picture specific enough to name the false identity that trust structure reveals.

> **Affections Discovery → False Identity Named → Covenant Truth Spoken → CBT Tools (if distortion blocks reception)**

Affections discovery is not a session segment — it is a continuous diagnostic stance held across every session. This protocol is most intensively applied in early sessions and whenever coaching enters a new presenting domain.

**Coach-internal organizing question throughout:**
*"What is this person depending on instead of God — and what does that reveal about who they believe they are?"*

For the full theological foundation, question bank, and intake/discovery call integration, see [[Affections Discovery Process — Affections, Trust & Priorities]]. This document is the in-session execution layer.

## Before You Ask — What the Room Is Already Telling You

Before a question is asked, affections are already visible in how the client arrives, speaks, and holds themselves. This layer of observation confirms or challenges what the client says they value. Read it before entering the question sequence.

### What the Story Is Already Revealing

Listen to how the client tells their story in the first minutes of session:

| Story Pattern | What It Suggests About Affections |
|---|---|
| **What rises first** — what they lead with, return to, weight as most significant | The object the affections orbit — often the functional god |
| **What disappears** — what gets minimized, rushed past, or never quite lands | A dependency they haven't named; what shame has hidden |
| **What repeats** — the same theme surfacing across different domains | A structural affections pattern, not a situational one |
| **Who is absent** — God functionally missing from how they describe what helps or matters | God-exclusion visible before questions surface it |
| **Who the client is in their own story** — active agent or passive recipient | Initial read on control/performance vs. shame-hiding affections |

### What the Body and Emotion Are Already Revealing

The body communicates what is held implicitly — often before the client has words for it. Track these in real time:

| Signal | What to Consider |
|---|---|
| Emotional flatness when discussing a significant topic | Implicit affections suppressed; shame blocking emotional access |
| Agitation, constriction, or withdrawal when a domain is approached | Proximity to a defended dependency — the false identity being approached |
| Disproportionate emotional weight relative to apparent significance | Affections concentrated here — the object being protected |
| Voice flattens or quickens moving past something | Avoidance — the topic the affections are hiding from |
| Animation, energy, or relief when discussing a person, outcome, or domain | Possible affections object — what gives life when present, dread when threatened |

*These are coaching observations — notice and hold. Do not interpret aloud until the pattern confirms across multiple signals.*

## The Five Layers — In-Session

Work through the layers in sequence. The client's responses — not the sequence — determine pacing and depth. Select 1–3 questions per layer. Move when the layer has opened enough to inform the next. Do not exhaust a layer — open it and follow.

---

### Layer 1 — Surface Inquiry
*What does the client say they want and value?*

**Coach posture:** Listen without leading. This layer surfaces the presenting picture — rarely the diagnostic target. Note what is emphasized, minimized, and absent.

**Questions:**
- "What matters most to you right now?"
- "What would feel like winning in your life at this point?"
- "What are you most hoping changes?"

**What to notice:**
- Ratio of external vs. internal attribution — does life happen to them, or do they act in it?
- Whether stated priorities match the behaviors and patterns described elsewhere
- Absence of God in priority language — observe, don't address yet

**Stay or move:** Move to Layer 2 when you have the stated picture. Treat this as the presenting surface. The diagnostic target is underneath it.

---

### Layer 2 — Pattern Surfacing
*What does the client's actual life — time, energy, attention, anxiety — reflect they value?*

**Coach posture:** Surface the gap between stated and functional values without confrontation. The client is usually unaware of the gap. Behavioral questions — not belief questions — open this layer without triggering defensiveness.

**Questions:**
- "If I watched how you spent your time and energy this past week — not what you intended, but what actually happened — what would I conclude you care most about?"
- "What do you find yourself thinking about most when you're not focused on anything in particular?"
- "What would feel most threatening to lose right now — not just inconvenient, but actually threatening?"
- "Where does most of your emotional energy go in a typical week?"

**What to notice:**
- What generates the most anxiety when threatened — anxiety locates the functional god
- What the client emotionally orbits between sessions and within session
- Where perfectionism or avoidance is concentrated — the defended dependency lives there
- The gap between Layer 1 (stated) and what this layer surfaces (functional)

**Stay or move:** Move to Layer 3 when the gap between stated and functional priorities is visible — or when the anxiety object is becoming clear. If the client is still in stated values, one more behavioral question will usually shift it.

---

### Layer 3 — Dependency Probe (Trust)
*What is the client trusting and relying on for security, significance, or identity?*

**Coach posture:** Follow the dependency chain. What does this person need in order to feel okay? This is the most diagnostic layer. Use laddering when an answer surfaces — follow the belief chain downward until the core assumption appears.

**Questions:**
- "What would need to be true for you to feel okay — really okay — about yourself?"
- "When you're anxious or struggling, who or what do you reach for first?"
- "What gives you the most relief when things feel unstable?"
- "Is there something you believe you have to hold onto — or hold together — in order for things to be okay?"
- *(Laddering — follow any answer downward):* "And if that were true, what would that mean about you?"

**What to notice:**
- Conditional worth language — *"I'm okay when... / I'm not okay when..."* — identifies the dependency structure directly
- Sources of security that exclude God
- What the client cannot imagine losing — the thing they can't lose without losing themselves
- The laddering move's landing point — what belief sits at the bottom of the chain

**Stay or move:** Stay in Layer 3 until you can name specifically what the client is trusting for security, significance, or identity. Vague answers ("I just want to feel better") need more laddering. Move to Layer 4 when the trust object is specific.

---

### Layer 4 — God-Exclusion Map
*Where is God functionally absent from what the client is depending on?*
*(Hold as coach-internal first — surface directly only when the relationship can hold it without triggering defensiveness)*

**Coach posture:** This layer often operates as an internal diagnostic frame more than a direct question. Locate where God is functionally absent or displaced. The question "Where is God in this picture?" is most powerful when the relationship has established enough trust to carry it — asked too early it closes the client down.

**Questions (use selectively):**
- "Where is God in this picture you're describing?"
- "What would trusting God here look like — specifically, not in general?"
- "Is there any part of this you find yourself handling without much thought of God? What is that area?"

**What to notice:**
- God absent from what helps, provides relief, or gives meaning — the client managing without reference to God
- Self-sufficiency language — holding, controlling, or providing without God in the frame
- God described as distant, demanding, or functionally irrelevant to this specific domain
- Whether the client's stated God-theology matches their functional experience of God in this area

**Stay or move:** This layer often runs parallel to Layer 3 rather than strictly after it. When you have located the God-exclusion zone specifically — hold it. Move to Layer 5 when both the trust object and the God-exclusion are clear enough to inform the naming.

> **Full Layer 4 reference:** For the eight-domain framework (security, significance, belonging, control, comfort, purpose, identity, future), complete question bank, diagnostic signals, and transition logic to Layer 5, see [[Covenant Identity — God-Exclusion Map]].

---

### Layer 5 — Identity Revelation
*What false identity do the affections reveal?*

**Coach posture:** Name the functional identity specifically — not generally. The naming is an observation, not an accusation. Offer it tentatively and hold the question open. Let the client confirm, correct, or sharpen it.

**Questions:**
- "What does this pattern say you believe about yourself?"
- "What do you believe you need to be okay — at the deepest level?"
- "Who does your life say you are when no one's watching?"
- *(Reflection):* "What I'm noticing is that a lot of what you're describing circles around needing [X] to feel okay. Does that land?"
- "Is there a belief about yourself underneath all of this — something you haven't quite said out loud yet?"

**Naming examples:**
- Affections orbiting approval → *"I am only safe when I am accepted."*
- Affections orbiting control → *"I need to be in control in order to feel safe."*
- Affections orbiting achievement → *"I am only significant when I am performing at a high level."*

**What to notice:**
- The client naming their own pattern before you do — wait for it; receiving it from themselves lands differently than receiving it from the coach
- Behavioral consistency across multiple life domains — same dependency in multiple contexts signals structural false identity, not situational
- The body's response when the naming lands — relaxation or resistance both carry diagnostic information

**Transition:** When you can name the false identity specifically and the client can see it — even with resistance or partial acknowledgment — move to Stage 2 of the diagnostic sequence (False Identity Named).

## In-Session Diagnostic Signals — Quick Reference

| Signal | What It Suggests |
|---|---|
| Conditional worth statements (*"I'm okay when..."*) | Performance or approval dependency — significance tied to output or relational standing |
| Anxiety concentrated in one domain | Locates the functional god — the object being trusted for security |
| God described as distant in a specific area | God-exclusion is live here; functional theology diverges from stated theology |
| Can state biblical identity truth but cannot feel it as operative | Covenant identity gap present — implicit memory diverges from explicit belief |
| Self-sufficiency or control language | Control or provision functional identity — *"I must manage this myself"* |
| Worth tied to what others think or need | Approval or belonging functional identity |
| Same dependency appearing across multiple life domains | Structural false identity — root-level work indicated |
| Avoidance of a specific topic or domain | Secondary gain or competing commitment maintaining the false identity |
| Emotional flatness when approaching a significant area | Shame suppressing access; the false identity protecting itself |

## Transition — When Affections Discovery Is Complete

Move to Stage 2 (False Identity Named) when all three of the following are true:

1. **The trust object is specific** — you can name what the client is depending on for security, significance, or identity
2. **The God-exclusion is locatable** — you know where God is functionally absent in their dependence structure
3. **The false identity statement is forming** — the pattern is consistent enough to be named specifically, whether or not it has been said aloud yet

**Do not move prematurely.** Affections discovery is not complete when you have a general sense of dysfunction. It is complete when you have a picture specific enough to name the false identity without generalizing it.

**Transition move:**
*"What I'm noticing is that a lot of what you're describing circles back to needing [specific X] to feel okay. Does that land?"*

If yes — or if the client corrects it toward something more specific — move to Stage 2.

**If the picture isn't forming:** Return to Layer 2 or Layer 3. More behavioral specificity or more laddering will usually surface what is blocked. The affections picture rarely fails to emerge — it fails to be recognized when the questions are staying at the stated level.

## Connection to the Identity Framework

Affections work surfaces distortion across all five dimensions of the whole-person framework simultaneously. Track what each layer is revealing in each dimension — this informs the depth and scope of the false identity when named.

| Dimension | What Affections Work Often Surfaces |
|---|---|
| **Cognitive** | Core beliefs maintaining the false identity; conditional worth statements |
| **Emotional** | Which emotional patterns correspond to which dependency — anxiety, contempt, despair, flat affect |
| **Relational** | Who the client is trusting for significance or security outside of God; social self performing for approval |
| **Spiritual** | The distorted God-image; where God is functionally absent or displaced in the dependence structure |
| **Physical** | Somatic signals of defended dependency — tension, withdrawal, agitation when the trust object is approached |

**Design decisions:**

- *Pre-question observation layer placed first — "Before You Ask — What the Room Is Already Telling You":* The tool opens with what the coach observes before any question is asked. Reason: affections are already visible in the story structure, the body, and the emotional weight before the verbal exchange begins. A practitioner who starts with questions misses diagnostic information that is already present. Somatic and narrative signals often confirm or contradict what the client says they value — reading them first calibrates the question sequence that follows.

- *Somatic observation is coach-internal — explicit instruction "do not interpret aloud until the pattern confirms across multiple signals":* The body observation layer carries a specific restraint: hold what you observe; do not interpret aloud until the pattern is confirmed. Reason: interpreting somatic observations prematurely can feel invasive or presumptuous and close the client down. The observation informs the coach's internal read; it enters the verbal dialogue only when confirmed across multiple signals and when the relational trust can hold it.

- *Five layers with explicit "Stay or move" criteria at each transition:* Rather than leaving pacing to practitioner judgment, each layer ends with a specific criterion for when to move. Reason: without this, practitioners over-process Layer 1 (stated values — easy to engage) and fail to reach Layer 3 (dependency probe — where the diagnostic target is). The stay/move criteria protect against premature movement and prevent stalling.

- *Layer 4 (God-Exclusion) is parallel rather than strictly sequential — described as "coach-internal first":* The God-exclusion layer is positioned as running alongside Layer 3 rather than as a discrete subsequent step. Reason: asking "where is God in this picture?" early in the discovery sequence often triggers defensiveness or performance — the client gives the expected theological answer rather than their functional one. Mapping the God-exclusion internally while Layers 2-3 progress, and introducing the question only when the relationship can hold it, produces more accurate diagnostic information.

- *Transition to Stage 2 requires three explicit conditions, not a timing judgment:* "Move when all three are true: trust object is specific, God-exclusion is locatable, false identity statement is forming." Reason: without explicit conditions, practitioners move prematurely because they have a general sense of dysfunction and feel pressure to advance. The three conditions prevent premature transition that produces a generic false identity statement rather than a specific one.

**Client summary:** Practitioner-only reference. Not shared with clients.

**Contested ground:** The somatic observation layer (body signals read as implicit memory) draws from IPNB (Thompson, Siegel) and Allender's somatic-aware narrative reading. These are Supported but have not been validated specifically in a coaching context. Clinical somatic therapies (Somatic Experiencing, EMDR, clinical IFS) train practitioners extensively in somatic work; the coaching adaptation here is more limited — observe and hold, do not therapeutically process. The scope distinction is real and requires practitioner awareness: the coach reads somatic signals as diagnostic information about affections, not as material to therapeutically process. When somatic signals indicate significant trauma activation, that is a referral signal, not a coaching deepening.
