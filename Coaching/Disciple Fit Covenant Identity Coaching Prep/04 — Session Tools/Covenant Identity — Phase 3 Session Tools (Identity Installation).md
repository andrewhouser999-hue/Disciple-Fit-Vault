# Covenant Identity — Phase 3 Session Tools
## Identity Installation
*Practitioner reference. Not a script — a map. Sessions 4–8 (range varies with Phase 2 length). Read in conjunction with Phase 1 and Phase 2 Session Tools, Session Growth Notes, and the Summary Baseline table from the Intake Addendum.*

---

## Phase Handoff Note
*What Phase 2 produces. What Phase 3 requires.*

Phase 2 ends with a map — not healing, not resolution, but specific named knowledge: the primary schema and its maintenance behavior, the competing commitment and its Big Assumption, the covenant identity layer most severed and the wound content underneath it, the narrative the client has been living from and where it came from.

Phase 3 requires that map. Generic identity installation work without it is not Phase 3 — it is standard Christian encouragement. Phase 3 is precise because Phase 2 was precise. Before any Phase 3 session, the coach should be able to state in two sentences what specific layer they are working, what specific lie they are addressing, and what specific wound material they are holding as context.

**What Phase 3 inherits from Phase 2:**
- The Summary Baseline table — the working hypothesis row is the Phase 3 entry point
- The language evolution pairs from Phase 2 sessions — these are the specific before-language that Phase 3 work will move
- The named competing commitment and Big Assumption — Phase 3 will test, not argue with, the Big Assumption
- The lament work begun in Phase 2 — if lament opened in Phase 2, Phase 3 stays with it in its arc

**What unresolved Phase 2 material Phase 3 holds:**
Any wound material that was named but not yet fully received. The schema that was identified but not yet interrupted. The covenant layer the client went blank on in the B3 self-assessment. Phase 3 will return to all of it — not as excavation but as the site of installation.

**What readiness for Phase 3 looks like:**
The client can name the Gap in their own words. The wound material has been surfaced without the client being consumed by it. The coach and client both know, with specificity, what is maintaining the Gap. The alliance is strong enough to hold challenge, disappointment, and the discomfort of change.

## 1. Phase Job Description

Phase 3 is the delivery. Phase 1 built the container. Phase 2 built the map. Phase 3 uses the map.

This is the therapeutic center of the engagement — the sessions where the Gap actually begins to close. Not through understanding alone (that was Phase 1), not through excavation (Phase 2), but through sustained engagement: the whole person — mind, body, will, emotion, spirit — making direct contact with a specific covenant truth at the precise point of its absence.

The word *installation* is intentional. What is being installed is not a new belief to be performed but a new operating reality — the covenant identity beginning to function as the actual ground the client stands on rather than a theological position they hold. Dallas Willard's renovation of the heart language applies here: the formation goes deeper than cognition. It reaches the will, the body, the relational instincts, the automatic responses. That renovation does not happen quickly, and it does not happen through effort alone. It happens through repeated encounters with truth at the site of the wound — and through the accumulated evidence of living differently.

**What Phase 3 produces:**
- At least one covenant identity layer moving from cognitively held or rejected to partially felt or embodied
- Schema interruption capacity — the client beginning to catch the schema activating before it runs them, in some contexts, some of the time
- A present-story statement that has shifted from the B2 baseline — not performed optimism, but an honest account that the covenant identity has made possible
- Identity votes: deliberate acts from the new story, between sessions, tracked and debriefed
- Movement in the lament arc, where present
- The Big Assumption tested — not defeated, but observed through deliberate experiment

**What Phase 3 is not:**
It is not affirmation practice. Reading declarations until they feel better is not installation — it is spiritual self-talk. Phase 3 requires following the resistance in the declaration, not celebrating the affirmation. It is not behavior change programming. Identity votes are experiments in living from the covenant identity, not a to-do list. It is not the resolution of the wound — the wound may be significantly present through Phase 3 and into Phase 4. Resolution is not the goal. Reorientation is.

**What happens if Phase 3 is rushed or generic:**
The engagement produces insight without formation. The client understands themselves better, has good language for their patterns, and returns to living the same way. The map was built but never used. This is the most demoralizing outcome in this model — the client invested in the work, the coach did the diagnostic work, and the installation never happened because Phase 3 was not precise enough to close the Gap at its specific location.

## 2. Session Structure

Phase 3 sessions run 60–75 minutes. The structure is consistent across sessions; the tools used in the primary work section rotate based on what the client needs and what the map indicates.

---

### Phase 3 Session (60–75 minutes)

**Opening — Identity Vote Debrief and Follow-Up (10–12 minutes)**

Phase 3 sessions open differently than Phase 1 and Phase 2. The commitment carried from the previous session is almost always an identity vote or a Big Assumption experiment. The debrief is not administrative — it is often the most formative moment of the session.

Do not ask *"did you do it?"* Ask: *"What happened? Not the outcome — what was it like inside you when you did it?"*

**Read the Client**
*Read all three carefully.*
1. The client who followed through on an identity vote and is reporting what they experienced is generating formation data in real time. 
2. The client who did not follow through has Immunity to Change data. 
3. The client who followed through but minimized the significance of it is showing the coach that the schema is still framing the experience. 

If the identity vote produced something unexpected — a different response from others than the client anticipated, or a different experience of themselves — slow down and stay. *This is often the session's most significant material, and it arrives in the first ten minutes.*

**Primary Installation Work (35–40 minutes)**

Choose one primary tool per session. The error is using too many tools shallowly. One tool, pursued with depth, will produce more formation than three tools sampled. The choice of tool is guided by the Phase 2 map and what emerged in the previous session's debrief.

The five primary installation tools are described in detail in the sections below:
1. Targeted Declaration Work
2. Schema Interruption Practice
3. Narrative Re-authoring
4. Lament-to-Trust Movement
5. Big Assumption Experiment

**When the client pulls the session in a different direction:** Follow them, briefly, to understand what they are bringing. Then make a conscious choice: is this new material related to the Phase 2 map (in which case it may be the highest-yield thread in this session), or is it avoidance of the installation work (in which case name it gently and return)? *"What you're bringing sounds significant. I want to stay with it. And I also notice we've been moving away from [Phase 3 work] for a few sessions now. Are those things connected?"*

**Landing (12–15 minutes)**

Phase 3 landings are longer than Phase 1 or Phase 2 because the installation work requires more integration time. Do not rush.

Ask: *"What do you want to hold from today — not summarize, hold? What is the thing you most want to carry into the week?"*

Then design the identity vote for the coming week. The vote should be:
- Specific (a particular situation, not a general intention)
- Small enough to be realistic
- Directly connected to the layer being worked in Phase 3
- Framed as an experiment, not an obligation: *"What would it look like to act from [covenant layer] in that specific situation? Not perfectly — just once, and see what you discover."*

Close with: *"What do you want to say before we end?"* This is not a routine close. In Phase 3 it often produces the most honest sentence of the session — something the client held back during the heavier work.

### The Five Primary Installation Tools

---

#### Tool 1: Targeted Declaration Work

The declaration is not the goal. It is the probe.

Declaration work in most Christian contexts means reading or speaking a statement of truth until the feeling catches up. That is repetition, not installation. What makes declaration work formative rather than performative is following the resistance — the place where the declaration meets the wound belief — and staying there rather than moving through it.

**How to use it:**

Select the specific covenant layer being targeted — the one Phase 2 identified as most severed, or the one the client marked as rejected or blank in the B3 self-assessment. Do not work a layer the client has already partially embodied; work the hardest one.

Read the declaration slowly. *"I want to read something to you. I want you to track what happens in your body as you hear it. Don't evaluate it — just notice."*

Read: *"[Specific layer — e.g., 'You are loved the way the Father loves the Son.']"*

Then: *"What happened? Not what you think about that — what happened in you?"*

The responses cluster into four types:
- **Deflection** — the client moves immediately to theology, qualification, or explanation. The declaration hasn't landed — it's been managed. *"Before the explanation — what did your body do first?"*
- **Resistance** — the client names a specific objection, a competing belief, or the wound belief directly. This is the most useful response. Stay here. *"Say more about that. What is the thing that fires when you hear that?"*
- **Affect** — the client tears up, goes quiet, or shows grief. The declaration has found the wound. Do not fill the silence. Let it land. Then: *"What is happening right now?"*
- **Partial reception** — the client says something like *"I know that's true, but..."* This is movement. The "but" is the wound belief. Follow it.

Over multiple sessions, the declaration will begin to land differently. Track this in the language evolution table in the Session Growth Notes — the client's response in session 4 vs. session 7 is some of the most powerful Growth Report material.

**Key discipline:** Never move to the next session's declaration work because the current one *seemed* to land. A single moment of reception is not embodiment. Stay with the same layer across multiple sessions until the transition marker below is met.

---

#### Tool 2: Schema Interruption Practice

Phase 2 named the schema. Phase 3 practices interrupting it.

The schema is not eliminated — early maladaptive schemas are deeply wired and do not disappear through insight. What changes is the client's relationship to the schema: from being run by it without awareness to having a moment of conscious pause between activation and response. That pause is where choice becomes possible. Over time, and with accumulated identity votes, the schema's grip loosens.

**How to use it:**

Begin with the specific schema identified in Phase 2 (Defectiveness/Shame, Unrelenting Standards, Emotional Deprivation, or Abandonment/Instability). Identify with the client the specific recent situation where the schema activated.

*"Walk me through what happened — not the event, but the internal sequence. What did you feel first? What did you tell yourself? What did you do?"*

This produces the activation sequence: trigger → somatic response → automatic thought (the schema lie) → behavioral response (surrender/avoidance/overcompensation).

Then work three points of intervention:

**Point 1 — The somatic signal.** *"What does your body do first? Before the thought, before the behavior — what is the earliest physical signal that the schema is activating?"* The body's signal is the earliest intervention point. When the client can recognize the physical sensation as the schema, they have a moment of awareness before the thought fires.

**Point 2 — The lie.** *"What is the specific thing you tell yourself in that moment? Not the general shame content — the exact sentence."* Name it precisely. Then name the covenant truth that addresses it specifically. Not a general declaration — the exact counter-statement to the exact lie. This is why the Phase 2 map matters: generic truth does not interrupt specific lies.

**Point 3 — The choice.** *"In that moment — with the body signal recognized and the lie named — what would it look like to choose differently? Not perfectly. Not permanently. Just once, in that situation."* This is the identity vote. Design it in the session and assign it for the week.

Over Phase 3, the client builds an increasingly specific and internalized schema interruption practice. By the end of Phase 3 they should be able to name the sequence, catch the signal, identify the lie, and make a different choice in some contexts, some of the time.

#### Tool 3: Narrative Re-authoring

Phase 2 externalized the story — the client began to see the narrative they had been living as a story, not as reality. Phase 3 authors the new story.

This is not positive reframing. The new story is not optimistic — it is honest. It is the story that the covenant identity makes possible: the same facts of the client's life, narrated by a different narrator, with the wound placed accurately within the redemptive arc rather than as the defining chapter.

**How to use it:**

Return to the B2 present-story statement verbatim. Read it to the client. *"You said this at the beginning of our work: [exact words]. I want to ask you — could you say that again today? Does it still fit?"*

The client's response reveals movement. Three types:
- **Same story, slightly qualified** — minimal movement; Phase 3 declaration and schema work needs more time before narrative work will hold
- **Same story, but aware it's a story** — meaningful movement; the client can see themselves from outside the narrative even if they haven't authored a new one
- **Something has shifted** — the client begins to revise the sentence, hesitates, says something like *"it doesn't feel as completely true anymore"*; this is the opening for re-authoring

When the opening exists, do not rush to write a new present-story statement. Work toward it through questions. The re-authored story must be the client's own language — not the coach's framing.

*"What evidence from your own life — things you've minimized or overlooked — doesn't fit the old story?"* (Narrative therapy's sparkling moment: the counterevidence the problem-saturated story has suppressed.)

*"If the covenant identity were narrating the same events, what would it say about them?"*

*"Who are you in the story — not who the old story says you are, but who is actually there in those events?"*

When the client can answer these from their own material, invite the re-authoring: *"Finish this sentence again — without the old story's words: The story I'm now telling about who I am is..."*

Record the new present-story statement verbatim. This is one of the most powerful Growth Report before/after pairs in the entire engagement.

#### Tool 4: Lament-to-Trust Movement

Not every client will have entered lament in Phase 2. Those who have — who have brought wound material to God honestly, without the cleaned-up version — are now somewhere in Brueggemann's arc. Phase 3's job with these clients is not to resolve the arc but to stay present in it and track the movement.

Brueggemann's three movements: **orientation** (the world makes sense, God is good, life is ordered) → **disorientation** (something has broken — loss, wound, unanswered prayer, suffering) → **new orientation** (a harder, more honest trust that has come through the disorientation rather than around it).

Most clients who have been in the wound for years are living in chronic disorientation without knowing that is a real and biblical place to be. The lament psalms normalize it. New orientation is not the return to pre-wound naivety — it is the recovery of trust that has been tested and has held.

**How to use it:**

*"Where are you in the arc right now? Not where you want to be — where are you actually?"*

Do not hurry toward new orientation. The coach who moves the client toward resolution before it has been earned is not serving the arc — they are managing their own discomfort with the disorientation. The lament psalms stay in the disorientation for entire poems before they turn. Honor that.

If the client is deep in disorientation: *"What is it like to know that this is a real and named place in Scripture? That the psalmists lived here and God did not withdraw from them for it?"*

If movement toward new orientation is beginning: *"What do you notice changing — not in your circumstances, but in you? What is beginning to feel different, even slightly?"*

If the client has arrived at something like new orientation: *"Describe it. Not in theological language — in your own words. What is the trust that has come through this? What does it feel like compared to where you started?"*

The lament arc, when it completes, is often the most significant formation of the entire engagement. It is also fragile — do not declare it finished prematurely. New orientation usually arrives tentatively, then holds.

---

#### Tool 5: Big Assumption Experiment

Kegan and Lahey's Immunity to Change process identified the Big Assumption in Phase 2 — the belief the client's entire protective system is organized around. The Big Assumption is not defeated by arguing against it; it is addressed by testing it.

The experiment is not *"act as if the assumption is false."* That is too threatening for the protective system to allow. The experiment is: *"Design a small, specific action that will generate data about whether the assumption is actually true."*

**How to design the experiment:**

State the Big Assumption clearly. *"The assumption you identified was: [exact words from Phase 2]. That assumption has been running your system. We're going to test it — not refute it, not prove it wrong, just observe it."*

Design a small experiment together:
- It must be specific enough to generate observable data
- It must be small enough to be tolerable for the protective system
- It must directly test the assumption, not something adjacent to it
- It should be framed as *observation*, not *performance*: *"You're not trying to prove anything. You're collecting data. What do you notice?"*

**Example:** If the Big Assumption is *"If I let people see my real struggle, they will decide I'm too much and pull away"* — the experiment is not *"tell your closest friend everything."* That is too large. The experiment might be: *"In one conversation this week, share one true thing about how you're doing — something you would normally edit. Observe what happens."*

Debrief at the next session: *"What did you observe? Did the assumption hold? What actually happened — not what you predicted, what happened?"*

Many clients discover, in their first experiment, that the Big Assumption overstated the danger. This is formative — not because the assumption was wrong in every context (it may not have been), but because the client now has lived experience that the assumption is not universal law. That evidence begins to loosen the competing commitment.

Multiple experiments over Phase 3, with careful debrief each session, produce the most durable change in the Immunity to Change structure.

## 3. Primary Question Library

### Declaration Work
- *"Read that slowly. What happens in your body as you hear it?"*
  — Somatic tracking. The response — contraction, softening, blankness, affect — is more diagnostic than what the client says about the declaration.
- *"Before the explanation — what did your body do first?"*
  — Redirects when the client manages the declaration with theology or analysis.
- *"What is the thing that fires when you hear that? What is on the other side of the resistance?"*
  — Surfaces the specific wound belief the declaration is meeting. This is the most important question in declaration work.
- *"Say it in the first person, out loud. Not to me — as if it were true."*
  — First person spoken declaration is more activating than reading or hearing it. Watch the body.
- *"What would have to be true about your past for that statement to feel safe to receive?"*
  — Surfaces the wound material underlying the resistance without requiring direct excavation.
- *"What has changed since session 1 in how that lands? Describe the difference."*
  — Tracks movement across sessions. Useful from session 6 onward.

### Schema Interruption
- *"Walk me through the sequence — not the event, but the internal sequence: what did you feel first, what did you tell yourself, and what did you do?"*
  — Builds the activation map: trigger → somatic signal → automatic thought → behavior.
- *"What does your body do first? Before the thought fires — what is the earliest physical signal?"*
  — Identifies the earliest intervention point. The body signals before the thought forms.
- *"What is the exact sentence you say to yourself in that moment? Not the general shame content — the specific words."*
  — Precision matters. Generic lie → generic truth = no interruption. Specific lie → specific truth = real interruption.
- *"What is the covenant truth that addresses that specific lie — not in general, but in that moment?"*
  — Forces the installation work to be precise rather than categorical.
- *"What would catching it look like — in that specific situation? What would you say or do differently?"*
  — Designs the identity vote emerging from the schema interruption work.
- *"The schema ran you in that situation. That is not failure — it is data. What did you learn about where it's still strong?"*
  — Reframes schema activation as information rather than evidence of the client's defectiveness.

### Narrative Re-authoring
- *"You said at intake: [B2 verbatim]. Could you say that again today? Does it still fit?"*
  — Tracks narrative movement from the documented baseline.
- *"What evidence from your own life — things you've minimized or overlooked — doesn't fit the old story?"*
  — Surfaces the sparkling moments Narrative Therapy is looking for: the counterevidence the problem-saturated story has suppressed.
- *"Is that story yours? Or was it handed to you and you inherited it?"*
  — The externalization question. Reconnects Phase 2 origin work to Phase 3 re-authoring.
- *"If the covenant identity were the narrator of the same events, what would it say?"*
  — Does not require the client to feel different — only to imagine a different narrator.
- *"Who is actually present in those events — not who the old story says you are, but who is there?"*
  — Invites the client to locate themselves as an agent in their own history rather than only as a subject of it.
- *"Finish this sentence again, without the old story's words: The story I'm now telling about who I am is..."*
  — The re-authored present-story statement. Record verbatim.

### Lament-to-Trust Movement
- *"Where are you in the arc — still in the disorientation, or do you sense movement?"*
  — Non-leading. Does not assume either direction.
- *"What does it do for you to know that the Psalms stay in disorientation without resolving it — that lament is not a stage to pass through quickly?"*
  — Theological permission to remain in the disorientation without it feeling like spiritual failure.
- *"What has God done in the middle of this that you almost didn't notice?"*
  — Not forced gratitude — a genuine question about whether the client has perceived any divine movement in the disorientation.
- *"What would you say to God right now if you weren't editing yourself?"*
  — Continues the lament work if it is still active.
- *"New orientation is not a return to how things were before. What is the trust that could come through this — harder and more honest than what you had at the beginning?"*
  — Opens the possibility of new orientation without forcing it.

### Big Assumption Experiments
- *"The assumption we named was [exact words]. Has anything happened since then that tested it — even without your planning it?"*
  — Life often generates data before the experiment does.
- *"What is the smallest action that would let you observe whether this assumption is actually true in your specific life?"*
  — Designs the experiment. Emphasizes observation over performance.
- *"You're not trying to prove it wrong. You're watching it. What did you notice?"*
  — Debrief frame. Keeps the client in observer mode rather than evaluation mode.
- *"The assumption overstated the danger in that situation. What does that mean to you?"*
  — Follows up when the experiment produces disconfirming data. Does not declare victory — opens the question.
- *"Where is the assumption still accurate — where is the danger real? And where is it overstating?"*
  — Honors the protective function of the assumption. The assumption was survival intelligence; it is not entirely false. Precision matters.

### Identity Votes — Design and Debrief
- *"What would it look like to act from [specific covenant layer] in that situation this week? Not perfectly — once, as an experiment."*
  — Designs the vote. Framed as experiment, not obligation.
- *"What was it like inside you when you did it? Not the outcome — what was the internal experience?"*
  — The debrief question. The formation is in the internal experience, not the external result.
- *"The old story would have had you do [old behavior]. You chose differently. What made the difference?"*
  — Connects the identity vote to the Phase 3 work. Externalizes the new choice as evidence of movement.
- *"What did you discover about yourself when you acted from the new story?"*
  — Gathers formation data. The client often discovers something they did not expect — usually that the feared consequence did not materialize, or that they felt different than they expected.

## 4. Coach Observation Guide

### The Quality of Phase 3 Sessions

Phase 3 has a different texture than the earlier phases. The excavation is done. The room is quieter. The client is no longer primarily surfacing and excavating — they are working with specific material at specific points. The sessions can feel less dramatic than Phase 2 and more formative.

Watch for the temptation to reopen excavation when Phase 3 feels slow. Returning to Phase 2 work in Phase 3 sessions is usually schema avoidance — the client (and sometimes the coach) is more comfortable with discovering problems than doing the sustained work of installation. Trust the map. Stay in Phase 3.

### Reading the Declaration Response

The declaration's landing is the primary formation indicator in Phase 3. Track it across sessions, not just within a session. What you are looking for:

- **Early Phase 3 (sessions 4-5):** The declaration meets significant resistance. The wound belief fires quickly. The body contracts or the client moves immediately to management (theology, explanation, qualification). This is expected and normal.
- **Mid Phase 3 (sessions 5-7):** The resistance is still present but the client can name it more specifically. There is a moment of genuine contact — even briefly — before the management reflex. The body response may soften.
- **Late Phase 3 (sessions 7-8):** The declaration lands differently. There may be tears or long pauses where there used to be immediate deflection. The client begins to speak about the layer in first-person, felt language rather than third-person theological language. This is the installation marker.

If the declaration is producing no movement after three sessions of sustained work, examine: Is this the right layer? Is the Phase 2 map accurate? Is there deeper wound material underneath that hasn't been surfaced?

### Schema Activation in Phase 3

The schema will activate in Phase 3 sessions — sometimes in direct response to the installation work (the schema defending against the covenant truth) and sometimes in response to life material the client brings. Both are useful.

The significant milestone in Phase 3 is the client catching the schema activating before the coach does. This is not the same as the client naming it after the fact — the client says *"I felt it starting and I actually paused this time"* rather than *"I ran the whole pattern again before I noticed."* When this happens — note it explicitly. It is an identity vote.

**Schema defending against installation:** The schema will intensify when the declaration work is targeting its specific lie. Increased schema activation during Phase 3 is often a sign that the installation is touching the right place. Normalize it: *"The schema activating here is not evidence the work isn't working. It's evidence you're working the right seam."*

### Somatic Markers of Installation

Installation has not happened until it has reached the body. Track these markers:

- **Breathing changes** when a declaration lands — slower, deeper, a noticeable exhale
- **Posture opens** — the client who has spent Phase 1 and Phase 2 slightly contracted begins to sit differently
- **Voice quality shifts** — the client begins to speak about themselves in a warmer, less guarded register
- **Eye contact holds** where it used to break — the client can stay present with their own goodness without deflecting
- **Tears without shame** — the client cries in response to being seen or to a declaration landing, without apologizing for the tears or moving immediately to self-management

When you see these, name them. Not as praise — as observation. *"Something just happened when you heard that. What was it?"*

### The Client Who Is Performing Phase 3

Some clients enter Phase 3 and begin producing what looks like installation — they speak in more positive terms about themselves, they report identity votes, they engage enthusiastically with declarations. Watch carefully.

Performed Phase 3 looks like: the client reporting all the things they are doing differently, affirming declarations readily, speaking positively about themselves in a way that feels slightly elevated — as if they are performing their own transformation. The Unrelenting Standards schema often drives this. The client has applied the same performance frame to their spiritual formation that they applied to everything else.

Test it somatically. *"You said that easily and quickly. Say it again — more slowly. What happens this time?"* If the declaration still slides off without meeting resistance or affect, the installation is cognitive and performed, not embodied.

## 5. Common Pitfalls

**1. Declaration work becoming affirmation practice.**
The coach has the client read a declaration, the client reads it, the coach affirms the response, and they move on. Nothing has happened. The declaration is a probe — its job is to find the resistance, not produce the affirmation. If a client reads a declaration without any notable response, the work is not done. Follow the question: *"What is on the other side of that? What is the thing that doesn't quite agree?"*

**2. Using Phase 3 tools without the Phase 2 map.**
Working declaration practice without knowing which specific layer is most severed, or running schema interruption practice without knowing the specific lie that fires, produces generic work. Generic work produces generic results. Phase 3 is precise. If you cannot name the specific layer and the specific lie you are addressing in this session, stop. Locate the map. Then work.

**3. Abandoning the schema when Phase 3 is going well.**
Sessions in Phase 3 can go very well. The client is making identity votes, language is shifting, they are engaging enthusiastically. Coaches drift away from the schema material under the pressure of positive momentum. The schema is still there — it will activate again under pressure, under failure, under relational threat. Phase 3 must address the schema explicitly, across multiple sessions, not just celebrate the sessions where it was quiet.

**4. Treating identity votes as a behavior change list.**
The vote is not the goal. The debrief is. What the client discovers about themselves when they act from the new story — what they expected to happen and what actually happened, what felt different, what the schema predicted and what was true — this is the formation. A list of completed votes with no debrief is behavior modification, not identity installation.

**5. Rushing the lament arc toward new orientation.**
The coach who is uncomfortable with disorientation will push the client toward resolution before it has been earned. *"God is working through this"* in session 5 of a lament that has been building for years is not insight — it is the coach's discomfort speaking. Stay in the disorientation with the client. Track the movement. The new orientation will come. It cannot be argued or encouraged into existence.

**6. Failing to track somatic installation.**
A client who can articulate the covenant layer fluently has produced cognitive installation. Cognitive installation does not sustain under pressure. The formation has not reached the operating system until it has reached the body. Ask about the body in every Phase 3 session. The somatic shift — however subtle — is the marker that the work is going deeper than cognition.

**7. Letting the Big Assumption experiment become a behavior change assignment.**
The experiment must be framed as observation, not performance. A client who turns the Big Assumption experiment into an obligation to prove the assumption wrong will either perform it anxiously or avoid it. The frame is: *"You're watching, not proving. What do you notice?"* The data comes from the observation, not from the performance of the counter-assumption.

## 7. Transition Marker

**Phase 3 is complete when:**
- At least one covenant identity layer has moved from its Phase 1 self-assessment position (rejected, blank, or cognitively held) to partially felt or embodied — evidenced by both the client's verbal report and observable somatic shift in the declaration work
- The client demonstrates schema interruption capacity in at least some contexts — catching the activation, naming the lie, and making a different choice, even imperfectly and inconsistently
- The present-story statement has shifted meaningfully from the B2 baseline — the client can give a re-authored statement that they inhabit honestly, not one they are performing
- The client has made three or more identity votes reported from between sessions, and the debrief of those votes shows genuine formation data — the client is discovering something about themselves in the act, not just completing an assignment
- The Big Assumption has been tested at least once through a designed experiment and the debrief has been completed
- If lament is present, movement toward new orientation has begun — not arrived, but begun

**Phase 3 is not complete when:**
- Declaration work has produced cognitive engagement only — the client understands the layers and affirms them, but no somatic shift has occurred
- The schema is named but not interrupted — the client can explain their schema fluently but it still runs them in daily life without pause
- The present-story statement has not moved from the B2 baseline
- Identity votes are being completed as assignments with no debrief of what the client discovered
- The Big Assumption experiment has not been designed and carried out

**If Phase 3 is not complete by session 8:** Extend. The installation is the engagement. Phase 4 consolidates what Phase 3 built — if Phase 3 hasn't built it, Phase 4 has nothing to consolidate. One or two additional sessions in Phase 3 is not a failure. It is the work being done at the depth required.

*Phase 3 does not end when the coach is satisfied with the client's progress. It ends when the client has lived evidence — in their body, in their choices, in their language — that the covenant identity is becoming operational, not just understood.*
