# Covenant Identity — Phase 1 Session Tools
## Covenant Orientation
*Practitioner reference. Not a script — a map. Sessions 1-2 (sometimes extending to session 3). Read in conjunction with the Intake Addendum and Exceptional Coaching Practice reference.*

---

## 1. Phase Job Description

Phase 1 has one primary job: establish the ground.

Not movement. Not breakthrough. Ground. The theological and relational foundation on which every subsequent session will stand. If Phase 2 is excavation, Phase 1 is setting the container strong enough to hold what excavation unearths. Rush Phase 1 and you will pay for it in Phase 2 — the client will hit wound material without the theological frame that makes it safe, and you will spend sessions 3 and 4 doing the work that should have been done in sessions 1 and 2.

**What Phase 1 produces:**
- A fully populated Intake Addendum Part B (felt-sense, present-story statement, covenant identity self-assessment, coach synthesis statement)
- A working hypothesis: which covenant identity layer is most severed, what God-representation is functionally operative (not confessed), what schema material is most likely maintaining the Gap
- A named Gap — the client has heard it described in plain language and received it as something other than evidence of their failure
- A coaching alliance strong enough to bear the exposure Phase 2 requires

**What Phase 1 is not:**
It is not a welcome session. It is not intake form review. It is not content delivery or theological instruction. The client who leaves session 1 feeling warmly received but not genuinely seen has not had Phase 1 — they have had a pleasant conversation. Phase 1 begins real work immediately. The warmth and the work are not in tension.

**What happens if Phase 1 is skipped or rushed:**
The client enters wound mapping without the indicative frame — without understanding that what they are about to surface is not evidence of who they are, but evidence of what they have believed. Wound material surfaced against a backdrop of shame rather than covenant security is not therapeutic — it is re-traumatizing. The theological container is not optional.

## 2. Session Structure

Phase 1 spans sessions 1-2. Extend to session 3 if the transition markers below are not yet met.

---

### Session 1 (60-75 minutes)

**Opening — Designed Alliance (10 minutes)**

Do not begin with the agenda. Begin with the alliance. This conversation is not a formality — it is the first diagnostic data.

Ask: *"Before we start, I want to ask what you need from me to do your best work here. What conditions have to be in place? And what has gotten in the way in past attempts at this kind of work?"*

Read the response carefully. The client who says "I just need someone to listen" likely has an Emotional Deprivation schema and a history of not being received. The client who says "I need you to keep me accountable" is already operating from the performance frame — they are expecting the coach to be the enforcer of change rather than a partner in it. The client who can't answer the question at all has likely never been asked what they need. All of these are data. None of them are problems to solve in session 1.

**Intake Review and Verbatim Capture (20 minutes)**

Do not summarize the intake form back to the client. Instead, select 3-4 responses that carry the most emotional weight — typically A1 (presenting problem), A8 (failure meaning), A5 (God description), A10 (shame content) — and read them back verbatim, slowly.

*"You wrote: [exact words]. Tell me more about what you meant when you said that."*

Two things happen here: the client hears their own language, often for the first time with full attention on it, and the coach gathers the texture underneath the written response. What was compressed in the form expands in conversation. What was performed in writing softens when spoken.

Watch for the client who responds to hearing their own words with surprise, grief, or minimizing ("I don't know why I wrote it that way"). These are the moments to slow down, not move through.

**Present-Story Statement and Felt-Sense (15 minutes)**

These are not exercises. They are the foundational "before" data that will anchor every growth document in this system. Do not skip them.

Present-story prompt: *"I want you to finish this sentence out loud without editing yourself: The story I'm currently telling about who I am is..."*

Wait for the full completion. If the client edits mid-sentence, ask them to start again. The unedited version is the data.

Felt-sense prompt: *"When you think about [the presenting problem from A1] — what does it feel like in your body? Where does it live? Describe the physical sensation, not what you think about it."*

Clients who cannot answer the felt-sense question are disconnected from their somatic experience — note this in the Session Growth Notes. It is relevant diagnostic information and it will matter in Phase 2.

**Covenant Identity Self-Assessment (15 minutes)**

Frame it carefully before administering: *"I'm going to read ten statements slowly. After each one, I want to know — not what you believe theologically, but what actually lands in your gut as true. Some of these will land. Some won't. Some will feel dangerous. That's all useful information. There are no right answers here."*

Read each of the ten layers slowly. Pause after each. Watch the body as much as you listen to the response. A client who says "yes" while their shoulders drop and their gaze averts has given you more information than the yes. A client who goes blank at "I am loved the way the Father loves the Son" is showing you the primary work.

Note: affirmed, hesitant, or rejected/blank for each layer. Do not process the results yet. Simply receive them and move on. The self-assessment will be returned to in session 2.

**Landing (10 minutes)**

Do not summarize the session for the client. Offer a brief, grounded reflection of what you heard — one or two observations, no more. Then ask: *"What do you want to carry from today? What are you sitting with that you didn't expect to be sitting with?"*

Close with a specific, small commitment for the week — something connected to what surfaced, not an assigned exercise. The commitment should be theirs, not yours.

---

### Session 2 (60-75 minutes)

**Follow-Up (10 minutes)**

How the client followed through on last session's commitment is diagnostic. Non-follow-through is not failure — it is often the first Immunity to Change data of the engagement. Note it. Stay curious. Do not lecture.

**God-Representation Conversation (25 minutes)**

This is the centerpiece of session 2 and the most frequently skipped conversation in Phase 1. Skipping it because it feels intrusive or because the client gave a satisfactory theological answer in session 1 is the single most common Phase 1 error.

Begin with A5 verbatim: *"You described your relationship with God as [exact words]. I want to stay with that. Not the theological version — the lived experience. When did God first feel that way to you?"*

What you are surfacing is the functional God-image — the representation formed in childhood and early experience that operates independently of doctrinal belief. Ana-Maria Rizzuto's research established that every person carries a God-representation built from early relational experience with primary caregivers. This representation is not erased by conversion or theological education. It coexists with the believed-in God and often governs the emotional experience of the relationship.

The client who confesses a good and present God but functionally experiences God as distant, disappointed, or unpredictable is not theologically confused — they are living from their God-representation, not their theology. The Gap between these two is the terrain.

Follow-up questions when the God-representation has been surfaced:
- *"Is the God you pray to the same God you read about? Or do they feel like different people?"*
- *"When you fail — not when you reflect on failure later, but in the moment — what does God's face look like?"*
- *"What would God have to do specifically for you to feel secure with him right now?"*

**Naming the Gap (15 minutes)**

Name it directly. Do not assume the client has already understood it from session 1's framing.

*"There is a Gap between what is declared true about you in Christ and what feels true in your lived experience. That Gap is real. It is not small. And it is not evidence of weak faith or insufficient effort. It is the terrain we're working in together. The work is not to try harder to believe. The work is to understand what is maintaining the Gap — and to let those things yield, one by one."*

Then ask: *"Which of the ten layers we talked about last session — if it became fully operational, if it actually felt true in your gut — would change the most in your daily life?"*

This question identifies the primary covenant identity layer gap for the engagement. It is also the first moment the client begins orienting toward what is possible rather than cataloguing what is wrong.

**Working Hypothesis Naming (10 minutes)**

Share your working hypothesis. Not as a verdict — as a hypothesis offered for the client's response.

*"Based on everything I've heard in our first two sessions, here's what I'm working with as a hypothesis. Tell me if this is close or if I'm missing something: [primary schema hypothesis] is likely what's maintaining the Gap. And I think [specific covenant layer] is where the most significant distance is. Does that land?"*

The client's response to this — agreement, correction, surprise, minimizing — is itself more data. Receive it and adjust. Document the working hypothesis in the Summary Baseline table.

**Transition Framing (10 minutes)**

Name what comes next without clinical language:

*"The next part of our work involves going back before going forward. Not to live there — to understand what formed the Gap. Some of that material may be difficult. None of it is evidence of who you are. It is evidence of what you have believed and what you have protected yourself with. Are you ready to go there?"*

If the client hesitates — note it and honor it. The hesitation is not a problem. It is the first honest data about their readiness for Phase 2.

## 3. Primary Question Library

### Opening / Alliance
- *"What do you need from me to do your best work here?"*
  — Surfaces attachment style and theory of change. Listen for whether the client expects to be managed, listened to, challenged, or fixed.
- *"What has gotten in the way every other time you've tried to address this?"*
  — Surfaces the Immunity to Change structure before you've named it as such. The pattern of failed attempts is the competing commitment data.
- *"What are you most afraid I'll decide about you after today?"*
  — Direct shame content probe. Often surfaces A10 material more honestly than the intake form did. Use only if the alliance is already warm enough to hold it.
- *"What would it mean for this to actually work? Specifically — what would be different?"*
  — Forces the client out of vague change language into concrete vision. Also surfaces whether the client is motivated by growth or by relief from pain.

### Surfacing the Gap
- *"You've believed these things about yourself in Christ for how long? What's kept them from landing in your gut?"*
  — Directly names the Gap without shame. Assumes belief and looks for the maintenance structure.
- *"Describe the last time a covenant truth about you felt genuinely real. Not believed — felt."*
  — If the client cannot answer, you have evidence the Gap is significant. If they can answer, the moment they describe is diagnostic — what conditions allowed it?
- *"When you read that you are beloved, what happens in your body?"*
  — Somatic probe for the covenant identity response. Bypassing and performed acceptance feel different in the body than genuine reception.
- *"If the way God actually feels to you right now were a weather pattern, what would it be?"*
  — Metaphorical entry. Allows clients who cannot speak directly about God-experience to access it through image. Read the image carefully.
- *"What would God have to do — specifically — for you to feel secure with him right now?"*
  — Surfaces the conditional structure underlying the God-relationship. The answer almost always reveals the schema operating in the God-attachment.

### God-Representation
- *"You described God as [A5 verbatim]. When did God first feel that way to you?"*
  — The Rizzuto question. Moves from the confessed God to the experienced God, and from the present to the formation moment.
- *"If God were watching you right now, what expression would be on his face?"*
  — Highly diagnostic. Shame schema clients almost always place a disappointed or evaluating expression. Abandonment schema clients often place an absent or distracted one.
- *"Is the God you talk to in prayer the same God you read about in Scripture? Or do they feel like different people?"*
  — Names the split between the implicit and explicit God-image without requiring psychological framework language.
- *"When you fail — not when you reflect on it later, but in the moment — what happens between you and God?"*
  — Indicative/imperative reversal probe. The client who describes distance or shame at failure is operating from a contingent God-relationship.
- *"Has God ever surprised you — acted differently than you expected? What happened?"*
  — Opens space for the client's genuine experience of God rather than only the formative wounding. Also surfaces whether the client's God-representation has any flexibility.

### Present-Story and Felt-Sense
- *"Finish this sentence out loud: The story I'm currently telling about who I am is..."*
  — Record verbatim. This is the B2 field. Do not rephrase what the client says.
- *"Where in your body does [the presenting problem] live? Describe the physical sensation."*
  — B1 field. Clients who cannot access this are disconnected somatically — note it.
- *"When did this story start feeling true about you?"*
  — Bridges the present-story to its origin. Often surfaces the formation moment without requiring a direct wound question.
- *"Who in your life reinforced that story — even without meaning to?"*
  — Relational origin probe. Connects schema formation to specific attachment figures.

### Covenant Identity Self-Assessment Deepening
- *"[After hesitation at a specific layer] What would have to be true about your life right now for that statement to feel real?"*
  — Surfaces the condition the client has placed on the layer. The condition is almost always schema content.
- *"[After rejection or blank response] What's on the other side of that? If that isn't true about you, what is?"*
  — Surfaces the operating belief that is filling the space where the covenant truth should be.
- *"Which of these, if it became fully operational, would change the most in your day-to-day life?"*
  — Identifies the primary covenant identity layer gap for the engagement.
- *"Which one feels most dangerous to believe — like something bad might happen if you fully trusted it?"*
  — Surfaces competing commitment material early. The answer to "what's dangerous about this truth" often contains the Big Assumption.

### Landing
- *"What do you want to remember from today?"*
- *"What are you sitting with that you didn't expect to be sitting with?"*
- *"Is there anything that didn't get said that needs to?"*

## 4. Coach Observation Guide

### Body and Voice

The performed answer and the genuine answer feel different — physically and vocally. The performed answer is smooth, slightly elevated in pitch, theologically polished, and arrives quickly. The genuine answer is slower, quieter, often incomplete, and sometimes arrives as affect before it arrives as language.

Train yourself to wait for the genuine answer. If you fill the silence after the performed answer, you will never get the genuine one.

**Voice changes to track:**
- Pitch drops when the client makes contact with real material
- Speech slows when something is genuinely difficult
- The sentence trails off — not from distraction but from the edge of what the client can articulate
- Throat clearing or swallowing before answering a question that touches shame content

**Body changes to track:**
- Posture contraction (shoulders in, chest closing) when shame material is near
- Gaze drops — down and left — often indicates genuine internal contact. Do not fill this silence.
- Physical stillness is not the same as calm. A client who goes very still in response to a question has often just made contact with something significant.
- Leaning forward = engagement. Leaning back = protection or distance. These shift within a session as the material shifts.

### Spiritual Bypassing Markers in Phase 1

Phase 1 is the phase where bypassing is most likely to go unaddressed because the coach is still building the alliance and doesn't want to challenge too early. Challenge bypassing even in Phase 1 — gently, but clearly.

**Markers:**
- The client answers the God-representation question with a doctrinal statement rather than a felt description. Redirect: *"I'm not asking what you believe about God. I'm asking how God feels to you right now — today."*
- Rapid recovery after an emotional moment — the client feels something, then immediately moves to a theological reframe before the emotion has been received. The reframe is not wrong, but it is premature. *"Wait — before you get to that. What just happened?"*
- *"I know I shouldn't feel this way, but..."* — This is the indicative/imperative reversal in real time. The client is performing toward a standard they don't believe they've met. Name it: *"You just apologized for having a feeling. What do you think I'm going to do with that?"*
- Fluent theological explanation of their own wound, delivered without affect. The explanation is not the work. *"You described that very clearly. Where do you feel it in your body right now?"*

### Performed vs. Genuine Response

If Phase 1 contains primarily performed responses, the container is not yet safe enough for Phase 2. Do not move forward. The goal is at least one moment of genuine, unguarded engagement before the phase is complete.

Performed response: complete sentences, appropriate affect, theologically coherent, arrives quickly, resolves cleanly.

Genuine response: halting, trails off, sometimes doesn't arrive as language at first — only as sensation or silence or a shift in the room. The client may look slightly surprised by what came out.

### Anxious vs. Avoidant God-Attachment

Kirkpatrick's attachment-to-God framework maps directly onto the clients you will see:

**Anxious attachment to God:** The client is hypervigilant to God's approval, experiences significant distress when they sense God is disappointed, oscillates between closeness and distance, often describes their prayer life as unstable. They want the relationship desperately and don't trust it.

**Avoidant attachment to God:** The client has reduced emotional investment in the God-relationship to protect against repeated disappointment. Describes God as "there" but not experienced as close. Prayer feels one-directional. They have stopped expecting much and explain it as contentment or maturity.

Both patterns look like spiritual stability in intake forms. Neither is. Read the God-representation question responses with this framework in mind.

## 5. Common Pitfalls

**1. Treating session 1 as intake review rather than real work.**
The intake form was completed before session 1. Session 1 is not a review meeting — it is the beginning of the relationship and the diagnostic work simultaneously. Clients feel the difference between being interviewed and being truly seen within the first ten minutes. If you spend session 1 confirming what was on the form, you have wasted the highest-leverage relational moment in the engagement.

**2. Accepting the confessed God as the working God-image.**
The most common Phase 1 error. The client says "God is good" or "I know God loves me" and the coach moves on. The Rizzuto question — *"When did God first feel that way to you?"* — must be asked in every engagement. The experienced God and the confessed God are rarely identical in clients who are stuck. The coaching happens in the gap between them.

**3. Skipping the felt-sense and present-story questions because they feel awkward.**
These questions are the B-section of the Intake Addendum. Without them, the growth documents in this system lose their most powerful before/after evidence. The discomfort of asking them is the coach's problem, not the client's. Do not skip them.

**4. Presenting the covenant identity architecture as teaching.**
The 9-layer self-assessment is a diagnostic conversation, not a theology lesson. If you are explaining more than listening during it, the phase has broken down. Administer it slowly, observe the responses, and move on. The processing happens over the full engagement.

**5. Moving to Phase 2 before the Gap has been named and received.**
The Gap must be named explicitly and the client must receive it — not just hear it. There is a difference. *"What does it mean to you that the Gap is not evidence of weak faith?"* If the client cannot answer that, they have intellectually acknowledged the reframe without internalizing it. Stay.

**6. Interpreting too early.**
Phase 1's job is observation and alliance-building. Heavy interpretation in sessions 1-2 — naming schemas, connecting childhood experience to present patterns, drawing conclusions — closes the client down. Hypotheses belong in the coach's notes, not the conversation, until the working hypothesis naming at the end of session 2. And even then, held lightly.

**7. Conflating warmth with the absence of challenge.**
Phase 1 should feel warm. It should not be soft. A client's spiritual bypassing left unchallenged in session 1 becomes the norm for the engagement. The alliance can hold gentle challenge from the first session. In fact, appropriate early challenge is often what signals to the client that this coach will not let them stay comfortable — which is precisely what they need to trust.

## 6. Integration Notes

**What feeds into Phase 1:**
- **Intake Addendum Part A (A1-A12):** Review every field before session 1. The verbatim language is your material. Highest-yield fields for Phase 1: A5 (God description), A6 (God at failure), A8 (failure meaning), A10 (shame content).
- **The Intake Tally form responses:** Do not rely on your memory of what the client wrote. Have the fields available in session.

**What Phase 1 produces:**
- **Intake Addendum Part B** (B1-B4): Must be completed within 24 hours of session 1. B1 (felt-sense), B2 (present-story statement), and B3 (covenant identity self-assessment) are gathered in session. B4 (coach synthesis statement) is written after.
- **Summary Baseline table** (bottom of Intake Addendum): Complete after session 2 once the working hypothesis is formed. This table is the orientation document for Phase 2.
- **Session Growth Notes, sessions 1-2:** The language evolution table (Section 4) begins in session 1 even if no shift has occurred yet — capture the baseline language as the "Then" column. Future entries will use it.

## 7. Transition Marker

**Phase 1 is complete when:**
- Part B of the Intake Addendum is fully populated
- The coach has a working hypothesis documented in the Summary Baseline table — primary schema, primary covenant identity layer gap, God-representation pattern, and Immunity to Change signal
- The client has named the Gap in their own words and received the reframe that the Gap is not evidence of failure
- The client's functional God-image has been surfaced and is distinct from their theological confession — the coach knows the difference between what the client confesses and what they experience
- The coaching alliance is strong enough to bear disagreement and emotion
- The client has demonstrated at least one moment of genuine (not performed) engagement in the session — a moment of real contact with their own material

**Phase 1 is not complete when:**
- The client is still entirely in performed-response mode
- The felt-sense and present-story data have not been gathered verbatim
- The God-representation conversation has not happened — *"I believe God is good"* is not a God-representation
- The covenant identity self-assessment was administered but not observed — the coach knows what the client said but not what their body did
- The coach cannot state a two-sentence working hypothesis

**If Phase 1 is not complete by session 2:** Extend to session 3. The cost of one extra session is low. The cost of entering Phase 2 without the container is the engagement.
