# Covenant Identity — Phase 2 Session Tools
## Wound Mapping
*Practitioner reference. Not a script — a map. Sessions 2-4 (sometimes extending to session 5). Read in conjunction with the Phase 1 Session Tools, Intake Addendum, and Session Growth Notes.*

---

## Phase Handoff Note
*What Phase 1 produces. What Phase 2 requires.*

Phase 1 ends with three things in place: a documented baseline (Intake Addendum Part B complete, Summary Baseline table populated), a named Gap (the client has received the reframe — the Gap is not evidence of failure), and a coaching alliance strong enough to bear exposure.

Phase 2 requires all three. It will not work without them. Do not enter Phase 2 because the calendar says session 3 — enter it because the container is ready.

**What Phase 2 inherits from Phase 1:**
- The Summary Baseline working hypothesis is the entry point for Phase 2. Begin with the primary schema hypothesis, not a blank excavation. You have a thread — pull it.
- The God-representation that emerged in Phase 1 is the theological ceiling for Phase 2. How the client experiences God will shape how they receive wound material. If the God-image is punishing, wound work will activate shame rather than produce freedom — and you will need to name that dynamic explicitly.
- Any incomplete or bypassed material from Phase 1 — layers the client went blank on, God-representation material that surfaced but wasn't fully processed, moments of activation that were grounded but not followed — belongs to Phase 2. It will return.

**What unresolved material from Phase 1 needs to be held:**
Any question from Phase 1 that produced a performed response rather than genuine engagement is unfinished. Track it. Phase 2 will return to it — often through the side door, not the front.

**What readiness for Phase 2 looks like in the client:**
The client is curious about their own patterns rather than only ashamed of them. They can name the Gap without it collapsing them. They arrived at the final Phase 1 session differently than the first — even slightly. The alliance has been tested at least once and held.

## 1. Phase Job Description

Phase 2 produces a map. Not healing. Not resolution. A map precise enough to do targeted work in Phase 3.

"Wound" here is not a trauma diagnosis. It means: the formative experiences, received messages, and interpreted meanings that produced the client's current operational theology — how they actually function as if certain things were true about themselves and God, regardless of what they confess. The wound is the gap between the declared identity and the experienced one, and Phase 2 is the work of understanding what created it and what keeps it in place.

**Two excavation paths, same job description (added 2026-07-14).** Everything above holds regardless of whether the client's formative material is injury-shaped or not. Most of this document is written in Allender's wound → vow → relational style → God-connection absence vocabulary, because most Phase 2 work runs through it. But not every client's gap was produced by a specific betrayal, terror, or ambivalence — see [[Covenant Identity — Character Wound Diagnostic Tool]], "Before Typing," for the signal. For a client whose Stage 1 material is diffuse conditioning rather than injury, this document's Primary Excavation section (below) has a parallel arc, and the Transition Marker (Section 7) has a parallel completion criterion. The map produced is the same kind of map either way — a specific schema, a specific competing commitment, a specific covenant identity layer under resistance. Only the excavation content differs.

**What Phase 2 produces:**
- A named schema (or schema cluster) with documented origin material — not just what happened, but what the client concluded from what happened
- A named competing commitment — the hidden obligation that makes change feel dangerous even when the client genuinely wants it
- The specific covenant identity layers most actively resisted, and the wound content underneath the resistance
- The beginning of narrative externalization — the client starting to see the story they have been living as a story rather than as reality
- At least one experience of bringing the wound honestly to God — even imperfectly, even briefly

**What Phase 2 is not:**
It is not therapy. It is not trauma processing, grief work, or inner healing prayer — though it may create readiness for any of these. It does not resolve the wound; it maps it. Coaches who try to bring healing in Phase 2 collapse the process. The map is the product. Trust it.

Phase 2 is also not the place for extended biblical teaching, identity declarations, or cognitive restructuring exercises. Those belong to Phase 3. Introducing them in Phase 2 is premature reframe — relief of discomfort at the cost of depth.

**What happens if Phase 2 is rushed:**
The coach ends up working generically rather than strategically. Without the map, Phase 3 interventions are aimed at the category (shame) rather than the specific content (the precise belief the client formed, the specific layer that severed, the particular vow that was made). Generic work produces generic results.

## 2. Session Structure

Phase 2 spans sessions 2-4, sometimes extending to session 5 with significant schema material. Each session follows the same basic structure with different content.

---

### Phase 2 Session (60-75 minutes)

**Opening — Follow-Up from Last Session (10 minutes)**

How the client carried the previous session's commitment matters. Do not treat non-follow-through as administrative failure. Treat it as the first wound material of the session.

*"What happened with [last session's commitment]?"*

If they followed through: *"What made it possible? What did you notice in yourself?"*

If they didn't: *"What got in the way? Not logistically — what was happening inside you?"*

Non-follow-through in Phase 2 almost always has schema content underneath it. The Defectiveness client who committed to a difficult conversation and avoided it is not lazy — they are protecting themselves from anticipated rejection or shame. The Unrelenting Standards client who followed through but added three more tasks onto the commitment is not diligent — they are over-compensating. Read the follow-through data carefully.

**Primary Excavation (30-35 minutes)**

The error in Phase 2 is excavating too many things simultaneously. Choose the highest-yield thread — typically the primary schema hypothesis from the Summary Baseline — and stay with it until something real surfaces. Breadth is not the goal. Contact is the goal.

Dan Allender's formation arc is the structural guide for this section when an injury-shaped wound is present: *wound → vow → relational style → God-connection absence.*

The wound is the originating experience. The vow is the survival decision — what the child decided to do (or not do) so that would never happen again. The relational style is the behavioral expression of the vow — compliance, aggression, or withdrawal. The God-connection absence is where the arc lands: the wound and the vow, compounded over time, have produced a way of relating to God that keeps the covenant identity from landing.

Move through this arc one layer at a time. Do not skip to God-connection before the wound and the vow have been named clearly.

**Parallel arc — no injury-shaped wound present (added 2026-07-14):** If Stage 1 produced an appetite-misdirection picture rather than a wound picture — the Vision-Led Entry in [[Covenant Identity — Diagnostic Lens Transition Logic]], or a Wound-Led entry where the Character Wound Diagnostic Tool's "Before Typing" signal came back negative — use this arc instead: *formative pattern → felt inadequacy or vision gap → God-connection misdirection.* The formative pattern is the conditioning that produced the current operating theology — not a survival response to harm, but a habituated way of securing safety, worth, or belonging that was reinforced rather than forced. The felt inadequacy or vision gap is this arc's equivalent of the vow — not a decision made to survive a threat, but the point where the client can feel, not just describe, that the pattern isn't producing what it promised (see [[Covenant Identity — Felt Inadequacy and the Vacancy — Mechanism]] and Condition 2-Alt in the Transition Logic document for the theological grounding of felt-versus-intellectual). God-connection misdirection is the same landing point as God-connection absence: the pattern, sustained over time, has produced a way of relating to God that keeps covenant identity from landing — the difference is that the client's relationship with God was quietly rerouted, not damaged.

Move through either arc one layer at a time. Do not skip to God-connection before the first two layers have been named clearly, regardless of which arc is operating.

**Immunity to Change — Four-Column Conversation (15-20 minutes, typically in sessions 3-4)**

Kegan and Lahey's four-column process is the most reliable tool in Phase 2 for surfacing what is maintaining the Gap. Do this as a live conversation, not a worksheet exercise. The power comes from the coaching — asking each column's question and staying with the answer before moving to the next.

Column 1: *"What is the genuine goal here — what do you really want?"*
Column 2: *"What are you doing, or not doing, that works against that goal? Specifically."*
Column 3: *"What is the hidden payoff of the behavior in column 2? What would you lose, or what would be dangerous, if you gave it up?"*
Column 4: *"What does column 3 tell you you believe to be true — about yourself, about others, about God? What is the assumption this whole system is protecting?"*

Column 4 is the Big Assumption. It is almost always a schema or a wound belief. When the client names it, stay. Don't immediately move to the reframe. *"What does it feel like to say that out loud?"*

**Working with What Surfaces (10 minutes)**

When wound material surfaces — when the client names something real, makes contact with their formation story, or articulates the Big Assumption — do not immediately resolve it. Stay with it. The impulse to relieve discomfort (the client's and the coach's) by moving quickly to the covenant truth is a form of abandonment. The wound has waited decades to be named clearly. Give it a moment.

*"What does it feel like to say that out loud?"*
*"How long have you been carrying that?"*
*"What does it cost you — daily — to carry it?"*

Titrate. Go toward the wound and then back. The therapeutic principle from Levine applies here: work at the edge of the window of tolerance, not inside overwhelm. The client should be able to make contact with the material without being consumed by it.

**Lament Introduction (5-10 minutes, when appropriate)**

Lament is not a technique. It is the bringing of the real self — unedited, unperformed — to God. Brueggemann's arc (orientation → disorientation → new orientation) describes the Psalms as the theological permission structure for this: the lament psalms do not resolve the pain, they bring it into the presence of God without sanitizing it first.

Many clients in Phase 2 discover they have never done this. Their prayer life has been performed, cleaned-up, or reduced to petition. The wound has never been brought to God because they don't trust God to receive it — or because the wound involves God himself (a prayer that was not answered, a season that felt abandoned).

When wound material has surfaced clearly, ask: *"Is this something you've ever brought to God? Not the theological version — the actual thing you just named."*

If not: *"What would it sound like if you did? You don't have to have it together. You don't have to be fair. What would you say?"*

Do not require this. Do not make it an assignment. Open the door and see if the client walks through it. The response — willingness, resistance, or deep avoidance — is itself diagnostic.

**Landing (10 minutes)**

Do not summarize the session for the client. Ask them to:

*"What do you know now that you didn't know when we started today?"*

Their summary is the next piece of data — listen to what they emphasize, what they omit, whether they stay with the wound or rush to the reframe. Then close with a specific, small commitment that comes from what surfaced — not an assigned exercise, a natural next step from the session's content.

## 3. Primary Question Library

### Schema Origin
- *"Where did you first learn that [presenting belief about self]?"*
  — Moves directly to origin. Useful once the schema has been named. Clients sometimes answer with a season rather than a moment — that's fine. Follow the answer.
- *"Who first made you feel [shame content from A10]? How old were you?"*
  — Attaches the schema to a relational context. The answer often surfaces an attachment figure who shaped the God-representation as well.
- *"What did you decide about yourself the first time [wound event] happened?"*
  — The most important question in Phase 2. The wound is not the event — it is the conclusion. Press past the event narrative to the interpreted meaning.
- *"What would a child have had to conclude about themselves to survive that environment?"*
  — Externalizing frame. Creates compassionate distance from the schema while still naming its content. Useful when the client is self-condemning about their own patterns.
- *"What message did you receive about your worth — not what was said, but what you understood?"*
  — Distinguishes stated messages from functional messages. Many clients received verbally affirming messages alongside functionally shaming environments. This question surfaces the gap.
- *"If you could go back to the moment when this became your operating system, when would it be?"*
  — Useful when the client can describe the schema but hasn't located its origin. The body often knows the answer before the mind does — watch for the physical response to this question.

### Pattern Origin (non-injury parallel to Schema Origin — added 2026-07-14)

*Use this set instead of Schema Origin when the client's Stage 1 material is diffuse conditioning rather than a specific injury — see the parallel arc above.*

- *"When did this become how you operate? Not a single event — a season, a habit, a way of moving through the world that got reinforced."*
  — The pattern equivalent of "where did you first learn." Do not press for a wound event that isn't there; the client may not be able to name a moment, only a drift.
- *"What did you get for operating this way — attention, safety, approval, a sense of being on top of things?"*
  — Names the reinforcement, not the injury. Diffuse conditioning is sustained by reward, not survival — the question surfaces what the pattern was actually paying out.
- *"What would a child who was rewarded for [pattern] have concluded about what makes them safe or lovable?"*
  — The externalizing-frame equivalent of "what would a child have had to conclude to survive." Same compassionate distance, different causal shape: conditioning shaped this, not a threat.
- *"Is there a person, or is it more like an atmosphere — a whole environment that trained you into this?"*
  — Directly checks whether the material is actually injury-shaped after all. If a specific person surfaces here who should have protected and didn't, or who was consistently unsafe, redirect to the Character Wound Diagnostic Tool and the standard Schema Origin set — the pattern arc was the wrong read.

### Vow and Self-Protection
- *"What did you decide to do — or not do — so that would never happen again?"*
  — The vow question. The client may not have thought of it as a decision. Help them see it was: it was survival intelligence at the time. It is now costing them.
- *"What behavior are you most committed to that you've never examined?"*
  — Surfaces the schema maintenance behavior. The answer is often the behavior the client mentions in passing as just who they are.
- *"What would be dangerous about fully living from [covenant identity layer]?"*
  — Brings the Immunity to Change structure into the theological frame. The danger is almost always the wound belief — if I actually believed I was beloved, I would have to stop [protective behavior], and then I would be exposed to [the wound I'm protecting against].
- *"If you stopped [self-protective behavior], what are you afraid would happen?"*
  — Direct competing commitment probe. The answer is the hidden payoff of the behavior they're trying to change.

### Immunity to Change / Competing Commitments
- *"You genuinely want [stated goal]. And you're also doing [behavior that works against it]. What is the payoff of the behavior? What are you getting from it that you wouldn't want to lose?"*
  — Column 3 of the four-column process. The payoff is not laziness or weakness — it is protection. Stay curious, not confrontational.
- *"What obligation — not chosen, just operating — makes change feel risky?"*
  — Names the competing commitment without requiring the client to have identified it consciously.
- *"If you fully believed you were beloved by God right now — today, not after you've cleaned yourself up — what would you have to stop doing? What would you have to let go of? What would you lose?"*
  — The most powerful competing commitment question in the theological frame. The answer is the Big Assumption in covenant identity language.
- *"What would it mean about you — about your past — if [covenant identity layer] were true and always had been?"*
  — Surfaces grief as a competing commitment. Some clients resist covenant truth because receiving it means grieving the years they lived without it.

### Narrative Externalization
- *"You said [B2 present-story statement verbatim]. Where did that story come from?"*
  — Connects the present-story to its origin. Begins the externalizing process — the story has a history; it was not always the story.
- *"Is that story yours — or did someone hand it to you?"*
  — The externalization question. Direct and often surprisingly powerful. Many clients have never considered that their narrative about themselves was received rather than discovered.
- *"What has the story cost you? Name it specifically."*
  — Forces the client to account for the real price of the old narrative. This is not shaming — it is the honest reckoning that makes change worth the work.
- *"What has the story protected you from?"*
  — The other side. The story is not only costing the client — it is also protecting them. Naming both sides without collapsing either is the externalizing work.
- *"If that story weren't true, what would be possible?"*
  — Future-oriented. Used after the story has been named and examined — not before. Premature vision work bypasses the excavation.
- *"What would a different narrator say about the same events?"*
  — Narrative reframe from within the client's own material. Does not require the coach to supply the alternative story — asks the client to find it.

### Wound and God-Connection
- *"Has God ever been present in any of this — not theologically, but in felt experience?"*
  — Opens the question of God's presence without requiring a tidy answer.
- *"Is this [wound material] something you've ever brought to God honestly? Not the cleaned-up version."*
  — The lament door. Read the response carefully — avoidance, willingness, anger at the question itself.
- *"What would you need to say to God about this — if you let yourself, without editing?"*
  — Lament prompt. Does not require the client to speak to God in the session. Asks what they would say if they did.
- *"What do you imagine God's response would be to what you just named?"*
  — Surfaces the God-representation in the context of the wound. The answer is almost always more revealing than the general God-description from Phase 1.
- *"What would it mean if [covenant identity layer] were true in the middle of this wound — not after it resolved, but inside it?"*
  — The indicative in the wound. Not resolution — presence. This question plants the seed that becomes Phase 3 installation work.

### Pattern and God-Connection (non-injury parallel to Wound and God-Connection — added 2026-07-14)

*Use this set instead of Wound and God-Connection when the pattern arc is operating. The lament door still applies — a client can lament a misdirected appetite the same way they lament a wound; the object of grief is different, the movement is not.*

- *"Has God ever been present in this pattern — not theologically, but in felt experience? Or has this always run on its own, somewhere God wasn't invited?"*
  — Opens the presence question without presupposing absence-through-injury; a rerouted appetite is often simply un-visited, not defended against.
- *"Is this pattern something you've ever brought to God honestly — not the version where you already know the answer is 'try harder'?"*
  — The lament door, adjusted. Many clients bring besetting patterns to God only as confession-and-resolution requests, never as honest naming of the pattern's actual pull.
- *"What would you need to say to God about how long this has been running the show — if you let yourself, without editing?"*
  — Lament prompt, pattern-flavored. Still does not require the client to speak to God in session.
- *"What do you imagine God's response would be to this pattern — not to the sin content of it, but to you, still caught in it?"*
  — Surfaces the God-representation in the context of the pattern rather than an injury; watch for whether the imagined response is disciplinary or personal.
- *"What would it mean if [covenant identity layer] were already true, right now, in the middle of still doing this — not after the pattern is broken, but inside it?"*
  — The indicative inside the pattern, mirroring the indicative-in-the-wound question. Plants the same Phase 3 seed via a different route.

### Somatic and Body
- *"Where in your body does [wound material] live right now?"*
  — Somatic grounding. Keeps the work from being purely cognitive.
- *"What happens physically when you imagine [feared outcome from competing commitment]?"*
  — Locates the competing commitment somatically. The body's response is often more honest than the cognitive answer.
- *"Stay with that sensation for a moment. Don't explain it. Just tell me what it's doing."*
  — Slows down the session. Useful when the client is at the edge of genuine contact and about to move into narration.
- *"Your body just changed. What happened?"*
  — Names the somatic shift directly. Signals to the client that the coach sees more than what is being said.

## 4. Coach Observation Guide

### Somatic Tracking in Phase 2

Phase 2 generates more activation than Phase 1. The window of tolerance narrows when wound material is near. The coach's primary somatic job is to keep the client in the window — not in overwhelm, not in numbness, but in the zone where real contact is possible.

**Flooding:** The client's narrative accelerates, affect rises, they begin reliving rather than narrating. The story is running them rather than the other way around. Slow it down. *"Stay right here with me. Take a breath. What's happening in your body right now?"* Do not chase the flooded narrative — ground first, then return.

**Freeze:** The client goes flat, stops making eye contact, voice drops to monotone. Often the most significant material — the body has moved into the wound faster than the mind. This is not disengagement. This is deep contact. Do not push. Stay present. *"You went somewhere just now. Where did you go?"* Give time. The client needs to come back to the room before they can speak.

**Dissociation markers:** The client begins speaking about themselves in third person ("people like me..."), loses the thread mid-sentence, seems to need to reorient to the room. These are subtle, not dramatic. Do not pathologize. Name gently: *"I notice you're speaking about yourself from a distance. Can you say that again from the inside?"*

**Somatic avoidance:** The client responds to body-based questions with cognition — explaining rather than sensing. *"I think what's happening is..."* rather than *"there's a tightness in my chest."* Redirect without shaming: *"I hear the explanation. What about the sensation — where in your body is this?"*

### Schema Activation Patterns

**Defectiveness/Shame:** The client prefaces truth-telling with apology, minimizing, or qualification. *"This probably sounds stupid..."* *"I know I shouldn't feel this way..."* *"It's not that big of a deal, but..."* Do not normalize these reflexes. Name them specifically: *"You apologized before you finished the sentence. What do you think I'm about to decide about you?"*

**Unrelenting Standards:** The client deflects from the wound by pivoting to performance — *"I know what I need to do, I just need to do it."* This is the overcompensation schema maintenance behavior: the escape hatch from the wound into strategy. Do not follow the client into strategy. Return to the wound. *"Before we get to what to do — what is it like to be you right now, in this?"*

**Abandonment/Instability:** The client monitors the coach carefully for signs of disappointment, impatience, or withdrawal. The session work is filtered through *"is the coach still with me?"* This client will sometimes create a minor crisis to test the relationship — a statement designed to produce rejection or rescue. Stay steady. Maintain presence. If you feel pulled away from the client by complexity or discomfort, the client is already feeling that pull.

**Emotional Deprivation:** The client takes up very little space. Answers questions without expanding. Does not ask for what they need. Minimizes their own experience. *"What else?"* is often more useful than a new question. This client also needs the coach to name the wound more than most — they may not be able to name it themselves until they have seen the coach name it without flinching.

### Genuine Contact vs. Insight Performance

Phase 2's most common false summit is insight performance — the client who can describe their wound fluently, connect it to patterns, apply a theological frame, and move on. It looks like breakthrough. It rarely is.

Insight without somatic contact is a new story about the old story. The client has updated the narration without changing the operating system. Test it: *"You named that clearly. Does it feel different to have named it? Where do you feel the difference?"*

If they can't answer the second question, the insight has been cognitive. The work is not finished. Stay.

Genuine contact looks different: slower, less fluent, often involves affect, sometimes involves the client sitting in silence with something they can't yet put into words. There may be tears. There may be anger that surprises the client. There may be a long pause followed by a sentence that begins *"I've never said this before, but..."* This is the material.

### The Lament Threshold

Many clients have a managed prayer life — they bring God what is theologically appropriate and protect God (and themselves) from the raw material. This protection is not disrespect; it is usually fear. They do not believe God can receive the real thing.

When wound material has been named clearly, watch for the moment the client reaches the lament threshold — the place where the wound connects to their experience of God and the question *"why"* or *"where were you"* is present but unspoken. Name what you see: *"It sounds like there's something you want to say to God about this that you haven't said."*

If the client moves toward lament — even haltingly — stay with it. Do not rush to Psalm 22 or to resolution. The disorientation is the work. Brueggemann is right: lament is not the absence of faith. It is the most honest expression of a relationship that has enough trust in it to bring the real thing.

## 5. Common Pitfalls

**1. Chasing insight rather than contact.**
The client who can explain their wound eloquently without feeling anything has not done Phase 2 work. They have done Phase 2 language. The goal is not understanding — it is the client making contact with their wound material in a way that opens the possibility of change. Insight without somatic contact produces cognitive maps that do not reach the formation level.

**2. Moving to the reframe before the wound has been fully named.**
The urge to relieve the client's discomfort — and the coach's — by quickly moving to the covenant truth is the most common Phase 2 error. The wound has been carrying the weight of years. It needs to be fully named, received, and held before the reframe can actually land. Premature reframe produces the appearance of resolution without the substance. The client nods, agrees theologically, and carries the wound into the parking lot.

**3. Running the Immunity to Change process as a worksheet.**
The four-column process is powerful because it surfaces the Big Assumption — the belief the client has never consciously named — through the experience of staying with each question. If it is filled out as a form, column 4 becomes intellectual. If it is done as a live coaching conversation, with the coach holding space at each column before moving to the next, column 4 often produces the most significant moment of the engagement.

**4. Avoiding the lament conversation because it feels presumptuous.**
Many coaches hesitate to ask *"Is this something you've brought to God honestly?"* because it feels like a pastoral intrusion. It is not. It is both diagnostic data and a door. Clients who have never lamented have a protected God-relationship. That protection is schema material — and it is one of the primary things maintaining the Gap. Avoiding the question is avoiding the work.

**5. Stopping at "what happened" instead of pressing to "what you concluded."**
The wound is not the event. It is the meaning the client made from the event. A client who describes a difficult childhood experience has not named the wound until they have named what they decided about themselves in response to it. *"And what did you conclude? What did you decide that meant about you?"* is the question that gets below the story to the formation.

**6. Failing to use the working hypothesis.**
Phase 2 has a thread — the working hypothesis from the Summary Baseline. Many coaches abandon it when the session takes an unexpected direction, and end up excavating broadly without specificity. The hypothesis may need revision, but it should be revised consciously, not abandoned accidentally. Stay on the thread until there is a specific reason to leave it.

**7. Moving to Phase 3 because the calendar says so.**
If Phase 2 material is still actively surfacing significant content at session 4, stay. The map is not finished. Moving to Phase 3 installation work without a complete map is like prescribing medication before the diagnosis is clear. One extra session in Phase 2 is not a failure — it is precision.

## 7. Transition Marker

**Phase 2 is complete when:**
- The primary schema has been named, traced to its formation point, and connected to the specific covenant identity layer it is severing
- **Either** the vow has been named — the survival decision the client made that is now costing them (injury-shaped wound) — **or** the formative pattern and its felt inadequacy/vision gap have been named and connected to the specific covenant identity layer it is severing (non-injury pattern; added 2026-07-14) — whichever arc actually applied
- The competing commitment has been surfaced and received — the client understands what the hidden obligation is and what Big Assumption underlies it, and they have felt the weight of it rather than only understanding it intellectually
- The narrative externalization has begun — the client can speak about their story as a story, at least in moments, rather than only from inside it
- The client has had at least one experience of bringing the wound *or pattern* material to God — however incomplete, however halting
- The coach can state in two sentences: *"The Gap is maintained by [specific schema content and maintenance behavior]. The formation point was [named — wound or pattern], the competing commitment is [named], and the covenant identity layer most severed is [specific layer]."*

**Phase 2 is not complete when:**
- The wound or pattern has been named but the formation level has not been reached — the client can describe what happened without naming what they concluded
- The competing commitment is named intellectually but not felt — the client can explain it but it has not moved them
- The God-connection piece has been bypassed — the wound or pattern has been mapped without any contact with how it has shaped the client's experience of God
- The coach cannot state the primary schema and its maintenance behavior in two sentences
- **The wrong arc was forced** (added 2026-07-14): the coach ran the wound → vow arc on a client with no identifiable injury, producing a manufactured wound narrative — or ran the pattern arc on a client who actually has a specific, nameable injury, missing the character-wound deepening that client's presentation needed. If this is discovered mid-Phase 2, re-check the signal in the Character Wound Diagnostic Tool's "Before Typing" section and switch arcs; this is a correction, not a restart — material already surfaced under the wrong frame usually still belongs to the map.
