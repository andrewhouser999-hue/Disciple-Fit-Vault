# AI Prompt — Calibrated Reading Guide

*Generation prompt for [[Covenant Identity — Calibrated Reading Guide]]. Saved for re-generation, revision, and adaptation.*

## Purpose

Build `Covenant Identity — Calibrated Reading Guide.md` — a client-facing bibliography organized by character wound type, with a universal section for books that apply across all types. Supplements the between-session library by giving clients vetted reading calibrated to what they are working on. Books only — Scripture is covered by the Scripture Saturation & Meditation Guide.

---

## Source Constraint

All books must be drawn from [[Covenant Identity — Author & Resource Reference]]. Do not introduce titles not already vetted there. If a book appears in that reference with a practitioner-only flag or concern, either omit it from the client guide or handle it as specified in the calibration notes below.

---

## Design Decisions

- **Wound type is the primary organizing structure.** Warrior, Hermit, False Noble — each type gets its own section. Client is directed to their type section plus the Universal section.
- **Phase is secondary.** Each entry carries a brief phase note so clients understand when a book is most relevant. This prevents premature reading and reduces performance pressure ("I should have read all of these by now").
- **Maximum 4 titles per wound type, 3–4 universal titles.** A shorter vetted list is more useful than a comprehensive one.
- **Client-facing descriptions are 2–3 sentences.** Not contributor-level annotations. What will this book do for this client at this stage.
- **Tripp omitted.** Legalistic tendency in popular content; prefer Ferguson + Bridges for the application layer.
- **Barton (*Invitation to Solitude and Silence*) — Hermit type, Phase 3+ only.** Solitude is a co-optation risk in Phase 2 for the Hermit (withdrawal pattern). Practitioner reference notes this; the client-facing entry carries a "Phase 3 onward" note.
- **Allender (*To Be Told*) placed in Universal section.** Crosses all wound types; narrative re-authoring applies across Phase 2–3 regardless of type.
- **Do not include Reference Only titles** (Robertson, Horton, Fee, etc.) — these are practitioner reading, not client-facing.

## Book Selections by Category

### Warrior (Betrayal / Weakness / Control)

1. **Curt Thompson — *The Soul of Shame*** | Phase 2–3
Shame as an attack on being, not on behavior. Thompson names what the Warrior carries as the shame of weakness — the belief that needing others, being exposed, or not winning means something is fundamentally wrong with them. The most direct read on the Warrior's core wound.

2. **Dan Allender — *The Healing Path*** | Phase 2
How the stories we have endured shape the identity we carry — and how God meets us inside those stories rather than above them. Written for people who have been betrayed and need a framework for what that has done to them.

3. **Andrew Murray — *Absolute Surrender*** | Phase 3–4
Very short (under 120 pages). Murray's central claim: surrender — not effort, not strategy — is the posture through which transformation comes. For the Warrior who has organized their life around control, this is the most challenging and most necessary read.

4. **Dietrich Bonhoeffer — *Life Together*** | Phase 3–4
What real community looks like versus what a wounded person imagines it must be. Bonhoeffer names how the "dream of community" — the idealized version that requires no risk and no cost — is actually a barrier to belonging. For the Warrior who has been betrayed and holds people at distance.

---

### Hermit (Terror / Too Much / Withdrawal)

1. **David Benner — *The Gift of Being Yourself*** | Phase 1–2
Short, accessible (under 130 pages). Directly addresses the question at the Hermit's core: what does it mean to be fully known and not found wanting? Benner argues that self-knowledge and God-knowledge are inseparable, and that receiving God's knowing of us is the path rather than constructing a self to present.

2. **Curt Thompson — *Anatomy of the Soul*** | Phase 2–3
The science of how human beings are made to be known and what happens when they are not. Thompson's relational attunement framework describes what the Hermit's wound disrupted and what healing through being known actually involves. Less about what to do, more about understanding the mechanism.

3. **J. Todd Billings — *Union with Christ*** | Phase 3
The identity the Hermit most needs: already received, already in Christ, already inside rather than outside. Billings grounds this not in abstract doctrine but in covenantal participation — you are in, not because you earned entry, but because Christ carried you in. Accessible academic level.

4. **Ruth Haley Barton — *Invitation to Solitude and Silence*** | Phase 3 onward
*Best used in Phase 3 or later.* Barton's solitude is not the Hermit's withdrawal — it is a movement toward encounter with God, not away from people. Read this only once enough session work has been done that solitude is a practice of presence rather than a return to hiding.

---

### False Noble (Ambivalence / Not Enough / Performance)

1. **Dallas Willard — *Renovation of the Heart*** | Phase 1–2
The governing framework for this entire coaching model. Willard's central distinction — training versus trying — is the most direct antidote to the False Noble's performance pattern. Transformation is not the result of trying harder to be holy; it is the Spirit working through conditions the whole person creates. Read early.

2. **Larry Crabb — *Inside Out*** | Phase 2
The ache underneath the False Noble's striving is not a competence gap — it is a covenant-shaped hunger that no amount of effort can fill. Crabb names why trying harder produces more exhaustion rather than more life, and what the ache is actually pointing toward.

3. **Sinclair Ferguson — *Children of the Living God*** | Phase 3
Adoption as the center: sonship and daughtership are received, not earned. Ferguson writes pastorally and accessibly on what it means to live from a received identity rather than a constructed one. For the False Noble, this is the most important theological frame — you are not a contractor working for approval; you are a child already home.

4. **David Benner — *Surrender to Love*** | Phase 3–4
Very short (under 110 pages). Written for people who need permission to stop. Benner's central claim is that surrender — receiving love rather than managing it — is the transformation condition the False Noble most resists and most needs. The practices he describes require nothing except openness.

---

### Universal (Applies Across All Wound Types)

1. **Neil Anderson — *Victory Over the Darkness*** | Phase 1
The most accessible entry point for covenant identity work. Anderson organizes Scripture's declarations about who the believer is in Christ into a practical, readable framework. Less theological depth than others on this list, but high everyday accessibility — good for clients who are new to thinking about identity in these terms.

2. **Gary Thomas — *Sacred Pathways*** | Phase 1–2
Nine spiritual temperaments — the distinct ways different people most naturally encounter God. Before assigning practices, it is useful to know which door the Spirit uses most naturally for you. Reduces the false conclusion that a practice "doesn't work" when the real issue is temperament misfit.

3. **Dan Allender — *To Be Told*** | Phase 2–3
Your life is a story that God is authoring — and reading it honestly is a spiritual practice. Allender provides a framework for engaging your own story without either minimizing it or being consumed by it. Crosses all wound types; especially useful as narrative re-authoring begins in Phase 3.

4. **Richard Foster — *Celebration of Discipline*** | Phase 2–3
The most widely used introduction to the spiritual disciplines as a coherent practice. Foster's framework (inward, outward, corporate disciplines) maps to the formation work in this engagement. Read alongside your Spiritual Disciplines Guide — Foster provides the broader context; your guide provides the wound-type and phase calibration.

## Document Structure

### Practitioner Reference

- Deployment: assign after wound type is named (Session 1 or 2), alongside the Formation Mechanism Guide
- Give the full guide; orient the client to their type section + Universal. They may read other sections, but their working list is their type + Universal.
- Assign 1 title at a time — not the full section at once. Coach assigns based on current phase and what is most live in the work.
- Practitioner notes per wound type (any calibration flags, co-optation risks, sequencing logic):
  - Warrior: Murray most useful after wound has been named and some trust established — Phase 3 minimum. Do not assign Bonhoeffer before the client has some relational safety in the coaching relationship.
  - Hermit: Barton is Phase 3 onward only — flag in practitioner section with co-optation reason. Benner (*Gift*) is the Phase 1–2 entry point.
  - False Noble: Willard first. *Renovation of the Heart* establishes the training/trying frame before anything else; if the client reads Crabb first they may turn the "ache" into another thing to address with effort.

### Client-Facing Section

Introduction: brief framing of how to use the guide, that books are assigned one at a time (not a syllabus to complete), and that phase indicators tell them when a book is most likely to land.

Then four sections: Warrior, Hermit, False Noble, Universal.

Each entry: **Author — *Title*** | Phase note. Then 2–3 sentence client-facing description. No clinical vocabulary.

## Register and Tone

- Client-facing, conversational, pastoral. Not an academic bibliography.
- Book descriptions speak to what the client will encounter in the book, not what the book argues theoretically.
- No clinical vocabulary. No contributor-tier language.
- Phase notes are brief and practical ("best in Phase 2–3," "Phase 3 onward," etc.).
