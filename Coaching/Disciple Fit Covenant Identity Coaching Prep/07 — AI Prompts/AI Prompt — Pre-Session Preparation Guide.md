# AI Prompt — Pre-Session Preparation Guide

## Purpose

Build `Covenant Identity — Pre-Session Preparation Guide.md` — a brief client-facing practice card for the five minutes before each session. Phase-universal (same practice across all four phases). Wound-type calibrated with light notes — one failure mode per type, not full differentiation. Three movements: Arrive, Orient, Open.

---

## Design Decisions

- **Universal, not phase-variant.** The posture does not change across phases. The content the client brings does. No phase-specific versions of this guide.
- **Five minutes maximum.** State this explicitly. Not a journaling exercise, not a meditation session.
- **Three movements, not a checklist.** Each movement has a distinct function: Arrive (slow down, set down the day), Orient (one sentence: what am I bringing?), Open (Spirit-invitation, not a formula).
- **Wound-type notes are client-facing and light.** One paragraph per type naming the specific pre-session failure mode. Not a full wound-type differentiation — just the one thing each type is most likely to do wrong before a session.
- **Deploy before Session 2.** Session 1 is intake and orientation, often runs long, and the client does not yet have enough context to use the practice. Session 2 onward.
- **Assign once; keep through the engagement.** This is not rotated or replaced by phase.

## Document Structure

### Practitioner Reference

- Deployment: before Session 2; universal across all phases; assign once
- What this is not: not a journaling prompt, not a therapeutic warm-up, not a between-session reflection tool — those are separate
- Coach note per wound type (slightly more diagnostic than the client-facing note):
  - *Warrior:* will arrive having constructed a session agenda — what needs solving, what they've decided the coach should address. The pre-session practice asks them to check whether the agenda is replacing what is actually present. If the Warrior skips the orientation step, they are in armor.
  - *Hermit:* the orientation step (naming what they're bringing) is the most likely skip point — naming anything, even to themselves, carries exposure risk. Do not reinforce the skip. If the Hermit reports having nothing to bring, ask what it was like to try the practice.
  - *False Noble:* will arrive having prepared a thorough answer to the orientation question — a week summary, a progress report, what they want the coach to know. The practice is not preparation for the coach. If a False Noble hands you a written summary at the start of a session, receive it warmly, then ask: *"What is present underneath this?"*

### Client-Facing Card

Introduction: five minutes, before every session. Brief framing of why it matters. Then three labeled movements. Then wound-type notes.

**Move 1 — Arrive**
Put down what you're carrying from the day. Not a technique — a decision. Sit, slow your breathing, let what just happened stay outside this conversation for now. One or two minutes.

**Move 2 — Orient**
One question, answered in one sentence — to yourself, not for the coach: *What am I bringing into this session?* Not an agenda for what should be covered. Not a summary of your week. What is actually present — what is heaviest, most alive, or most unresolved right now. One sentence. Hold it.

**Move 3 — Open**
A brief Spirit-invitation. Not a formula. Something like: *"Holy Spirit, I'm here. Open what matters. I'm listening."* Thirty seconds.

**Wound-type notes (client-facing, one paragraph each):** Warrior, Hermit, False Noble — named, with the specific pre-session pattern and what to do instead.

Close with Psalm 131:1–2 (ESV) as the governing image — arriving as the weaned child, not as the manager of the session.

- No clinical vocabulary.
- Biblical texts quoted in full, ESV, with citation.
- Wound type names used — client knows their type by Session 2.
- Length: client-facing section should fit on one page or close to it. Practitioner section brief.
