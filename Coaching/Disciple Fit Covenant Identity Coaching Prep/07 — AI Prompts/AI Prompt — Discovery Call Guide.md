## Prompt

---

### Role & Context

You are building the Covenant Identity Coaching Discovery Call Guide — a practitioner-facing conversation guide Andrew uses in every discovery call. This is a reusable document, not a per-client tool.

The discovery call has one primary job: **fit assessment** — determining whether this client and this model are the right match. Everything else in the call — behavioral staging, identity-gap detection, model introduction — serves that primary job. The call is not a free coaching session, not a sales pitch, and not a diagnostic intake. It is a structured, relational conversation that answers one question for both parties: *Is this the right fit?*

---

### Framework Stack

Build the guide from these frameworks in this priority order:

**1. Stages of Change (Prochaska & DiClemente) — Primary**
Used to locate the client's change readiness — but with a critical theological reframe for this model: the stages describe **readiness to receive**, not readiness to achieve. The question is not "How ready is this person to generate change?" but "How open is this person to receiving what God has already declared about them?" The change is God's. The readiness is the client's.

Stage indicators in this call are *behavioral*, not self-reported:
- Pre-contemplation: Attributes the problem entirely to external factors; shows no curiosity about internal patterns; theology is inherited, not personally owned; cannot name a moment when the gap cost them anything
- Contemplation: Aware that something internal is involved; names emotional weight around the question of identity; circular pattern language ("I keep ending up back here"); willing to look at the gap, ambivalent about what it will cost
- Preparation: Researching actively; asking specific questions about what coaching involves; can name exactly what they want different; energy around the topic
- Action: Already working something; reports specific changes and setbacks; identity language beginning to shift
- Maintenance: Consolidated; failure response regulated; God-relationship textured and stable — likely not the right fit for this model's depth

The goal of staging is not to label the client. It is to determine whether they are in Contemplation or Preparation — the minimum threshold for fit. Pre-contemplation is a referral or a "not yet." Action and Maintenance suggest possible wrong-fit or wrong-depth.

**2. Behavioral Question Design**
All diagnostic questions in this call are behavioral, not reflective. The reason: Christians with active identity divergence almost always know the theologically correct answer. Reflective questions ("Do you believe God loves you?") produce performances. Behavioral questions bypass the performance and surface what is actually operating.

Apply these design principles throughout the question bank:
- **Behavioral anchoring** — Ask about specific recent behaviors: "What did you actually do in the 24 hours after?" not "How do you tend to respond?"
- **Contrast probing** — "Tell me about a time when [X] felt most real vs. a time when it felt most distant." The contrast reveals the architecture
- **Indirect scenario framing** — Present a situation; ask for a response. "If someone you respected criticized your character publicly, what would happen inside you first?" Scenarios reduce theological performance
- **Frequency/duration indicators** — "How long does it typically take to feel right with God after a failure?" Duration is a behavioral marker of shame-cycle depth
- **Third-person softening** — "Some people find that when life gets hard, they go through the motions spiritually but feel disconnected internally. How much does that describe your experience?" Reduces social desirability pressure
- **Timeline questions** — "Was there a time when this felt different?" Surfaces origins and reveals whether the gap is situational or structural

**3. MI Engaging Stage (Miller & Rollnick)**
The conversational register for the entire call is MI Engaging: warm, genuinely curious, non-pressured, non-clinical. OARS throughout:
- **Open questions** — never yes/no; always invitational
- **Affirmations** — acknowledge what is true in what they bring; not praise, but genuine recognition
- **Reflections** — simple reflections to confirm understanding; complex reflections to go one layer deeper
- **Summaries** — transition between sections; confirm you heard them accurately before moving to the next area

The call should feel like a real conversation with someone who takes them seriously — not an intake interview, not a pitch.

**4. Tally Intake Domains as Thematic Territory**
The six thematic domains probed in the Tier 2 intake (Covenant Identity Intake — Tally Build Guide.md) also govern the discovery call — but at a behavioral rather than reflective level. The intake gets reflection; the call gets behavior.

| Intake Domain | Discovery Call Behavioral Territory |
|---|---|
| Presenting problem (Q1-Q2) | What has the pattern actually cost? What has the client done about it? |
| Life arc / narrative (Q3) | Does the client describe themselves as agent or subject? |
| God-relationship (Q5) | What does God-to-client communication actually look like on a hard week? |
| God-at-failure (Q6) | What does the client actually do in the first hours after significant failure? |
| Identity gap (Q9) | Where specifically does the gap show up in daily behavior? |
| Motivation (Q11) | What cracked open the urgency now vs. six months ago? |

**5. The Covenant Identity Model (Divergent Identity as Core Diagnostic Target)**
The central diagnostic question of the discovery call: **Is this person operating from divergent identities?**

Divergent identity operation = the gap between:
- The theological identity they hold (who God says they are)
- The functional identity they live from (who they actually behave as under pressure, in failure, in conflict, and in ordinary life)

This gap is the coaching terrain of this model. A client operating entirely from integrated covenant identity doesn't need this work. A client with no awareness of the gap is likely pre-contemplation. The sweet spot is a client who can feel the gap, has experienced its cost, and is moving toward readiness to address it at the root level — not just behaviorally.

Behavioral markers of divergent identity operation (the call is listening for these):
- God-relationship description differs significantly from behavioral God-response under pressure
- Failure response involves rapid shame spiraling or performance-recovery attempt
- Identity language is entirely external (role-based, achievement-based, relational) with little or no covenant-grounded self-description
- The client can tell you what they believe about themselves theologically but struggles to name when it felt experientially true
- Prolonged duration of the same pattern despite genuine attempts — indicates the gap is maintained by competing commitments, not just lack of effort

#### Section 3: Call Structure — Five Movements

Not a rigid script. Movements. Know where you are and what the movement is for.

**Movement 1 — Opening (2–3 min)**

Purpose: Establish tone. The client is nervous. They don't know what this call is. Create genuine warmth and set expectations clearly so the conversation can be honest.

Language guidance:
- Warm and direct; no performative enthusiasm
- Name what the call is: "This is a conversation for both of us to figure out if this is the right fit. I'll ask you some questions, you'll have space to ask me anything, and by the end we'll both have a clear sense of whether to move forward."
- Set permission for honesty: "There's no right answer here. The most useful thing you can bring is what's actually true."

**Movement 2 — Behavioral Staging (10–12 min)**

Purpose: Locate the client's change readiness stage through behavioral questions. Listen for agency language, pattern description, and what the problem has actually cost them.

Primary territory: Presenting problem and narrative arc at behavioral level.

Questions to draw from (select 2–3 based on Tier 1 read):
[Behavioral question bank — see Section 4]

What you're assessing: Are they in pre-contemplation (external attribution only), contemplation (internal dimension named, ambivalent), or preparation (specific, ready, resourced)?

Red flags for wrong fit: Cannot name any internal contribution to the problem; attributes everything to others or circumstances; wants a strategy not a formation process.

Note (added 2026-07-15): Domain 1's pattern-and-cost questions work regardless of Entry Fork read, but instruct the guide not to assume a disruption/crisis narrative is coming. A named-pattern, non-injury client will answer "what has this cost you" with something quieter — lost intimacy, flatness, distance — not a breaking marriage or collapsing role. Quiet is not the same as absent.

**Movement 3 — Identity Gap Indicators (10–12 min)**

Purpose: Surface behavioral evidence of divergent identity operation — the gap between theological identity and functional identity.

Primary territory: God-relationship, failure response, and the specific places the gap shows up in daily life.

Questions to draw from (select 2–3):
[Behavioral question bank — see Section 4]

What you're assessing: Is there a live gap? Is the client aware of it? Can they feel its cost? Does the gap appear structural (maintained by competing commitments) or situational (triggered by a recent event)?

Distinguish: A client who says "I struggle with shame" is reporting. A client who says "Every time I fail, I spend three days performing my way back into feeling okay with God before I can actually be present" is revealing the gap. Listen for the behavior, not the self-assessment.

**Movement 4 — Model Introduction and Agreement (7–9 min)** *(retitled and extended 2026-07-15 to match the 2026-07-10 alliance-formation revision already live in the output document)*

Purpose: Introduce the Covenant Identity coaching framework at accessible depth — enough for the client to know what they're saying yes to — and reach two explicit points of agreement before fit is declared: what the work will specifically target for this client, and what the client themselves said they want, confirmed in their own words. A model explained is not the same as a goal agreed to; this movement must do both, not just the first.

Structure of the introduction:
1. Name the gap: "What I'm hearing is a distance between who you know yourself to be in Christ and who you actually experience yourself to be — especially under pressure. That gap has a name and it has a structure."
2. Name the work: "This coaching works at the root of that gap — not just behavior, not just goals. We're working at the level of identity: what do you actually believe about yourself, about God, about what's safe? And how does that belief show up in how you live?"
3. Name the grammar: "The theological framework underneath all of it is that your identity in Christ is already declared — it's not something you build, earn, or perform toward. The coaching work is closing the distance between what has already been declared and what you actually live from."
4. Name the pace: "This is formation, not productivity. It moves at the pace of real change — which is slower than most people expect and more lasting than most approaches produce."
5. Invite response: "Does that resonate with what you're carrying? What questions do you have about what this actually looks like?"
6. **Individualize the task:** Replace the generic four-phase description with what phase one would specifically target for this client, using what actually surfaced in Movements 2–3 — not the model in the abstract. "Based on what you've described about [the specific pattern, gap, or dependency named earlier in the call], the early work would focus on [specific territory] — that's what this looks like for you, not just the four phases in general." Skipping this step and only ever describing the model generically is a signal the call stayed diagnostic and never reached task agreement.
7. **Confirm the goal, in their own words:** Reflect back what the client said they wanted — using their own language from Domain 6, not the coach's theological reframing of it — and ask for explicit confirmation before moving to Movement 5. "Earlier you said [quote or close paraphrase of their own words about what they want to be different]. Is that still the right way to put it, or would you say it differently now?" The point is client-owned goal language the client has explicitly confirmed — not coach-approved language the client has merely heard and not objected to. If the client cannot restate or affirm a goal in their own words here, that is itself diagnostic information about readiness, not a step to skip past.

Tone: Plain. Not a pitch. Not a theological lecture. Offer it and listen to what it lands.

What to avoid — add to the existing list below: moving to Movement 5 on the strength of the client's silence or polite agreement rather than an explicit, individualized goal confirmation.

**Movement 5 — Fit Assessment and Next Steps (5–7 min)**

Purpose: Make the fit determination transparent, mutual, and clear. Do not hedge. Do not over-sell. Do not under-sell.

If fit is confirmed:
- State it plainly: "Based on what you've shared, I think this is a real match. What I'm hearing maps directly to what this coaching is built for."
- Name the next steps: Coaching agreement, Tier 2 intake (to complete before Session 1), Session 1 date
- Do not over-explain. The client has enough information.

If fit is not confirmed:
- State it honestly: "I want to be straight with you. Based on our conversation, I don't think this specific model is what you need right now." Name the specific reason.
- Offer a referral or alternative if you have one.
- Do not soften it into ambiguity. An unclear close is worse than a clean no.

If more information is needed:
- "I want to take a day to sit with what you've shared before I give you a clear answer. I'll be back to you within 24 hours."
- Do not use this as a hedge. Use it when you genuinely need to think.

#### Section 4: Behavioral Question Bank

Organized by domain. For each call, select 2–3 questions per movement — not all of them. This is a conversation, not a questionnaire. The question bank gives you options; the client's responses determine which direction to go.

**Domain 1: Pattern and Cost (Movement 2 — Behavioral Staging)**

Questions:
- "When did you first notice this pattern? Walk me through the earliest time you can remember it showing up."
- "What has staying in this pattern actually cost you? Not in general — specifically. What relationships, what opportunities, what version of yourself?"
- "The last time you tried to address this — walk me through what happened. Not just that it didn't work, but where specifically it broke down."
- "What are you doing today — not what you wish you were doing — that you suspect is keeping this in place?"

What these reveal: Agency level, schema/ITC depth, whether change attempts have been behavioral (surface) or identity-level (root), pattern duration.

**Domain 2: Narrative and Agency (Movement 2 — Behavioral Staging)**

Questions:
- "If you had to describe this season of your life in a sentence or two — not a résumé, just what it's actually felt like — what would you say?"
- "In the story of how you got here — are you more the person things happened to, or the person who made choices that led here? Or somewhere between?"
- "Is there a version of yourself you can see but can't seem to inhabit? Describe what that person looks like."

What these reveal: Narrative contamination level, agency language, identity aspirational language (a marker of contemplation or preparation stage readiness).

**Domain 3: God-Relationship Under Pressure (Movement 3 — Identity Gap)**

Questions:
- "On a hard week — not a crisis, just a genuinely difficult week — what actually happens to your faith? Not what you hope happens. What does."
- "What does it look like when you and God are actually close — as opposed to when you're going through the motions? What's the difference?"
- "When was the last time you heard something from God that you didn't want to hear? What did you do with it?"
- "When life gets hard, do you move toward God or away from him? Be honest."

What these reveal: God-attachment pattern (secure, anxious, avoidant), lament capacity, spiritual bypassing level, God-representation under pressure vs. in ordinary life.

**Domain 4: Failure Response (Movement 3 — Identity Gap)**

Questions:
- "When you fail at something that matters — morally, relationally, at work — what do you actually do in the next 24 hours? Walk me through it."
- "After a significant failure, how long does it typically take to feel right with God again? What does that process look like?"
- "When you make a mistake in front of someone you respect, what happens inside you first — before you respond?"

What these reveal: Shame-cycle depth, indicative/imperative grammar reversal severity, performance-recovery pattern, whether failure triggers identity collapse or situational correction.

**Domain 5: Identity Gap Specificity (Movement 3 — Identity Gap)**

Questions:
- "If I asked you to name one thing God says about who you are — not what you've achieved, but who you are — what would you say? And does it feel true?"
- "Is there a version of what God says about you that you believe in theory but can't seem to feel in your actual life? What is it?"
- "Where in your life does the gap between who you believe you are and how you actually live feel the widest?"

What these reveal: Covenant identity layer integration level, specific gap territory, whether the gap is diffuse or located in a specific domain, readiness to engage the identity level.

**Domain 6: Motivation and Urgency (Movement 2 or 3)**

Questions:
- "What's the real thing that made you reach out now — not six months ago, not a year from now, but now?"
- "What would it mean about you if nothing in this area changed in the next year?"
- "If this coaching worked exactly as it should — what would be different? Not just in your behavior. In who you experience yourself to be."

What these reveal: SDT motivation quality (intrinsic vs. introjected vs. external), precipitating event, identity-level aspiration, readiness for the depth of work.

#### Section 5: Stage-Readiness Indicators

**What to listen for — not what to ask directly.** These are behavioral signals that locate the client's stage. You are listening for them throughout the conversation; you are not staging them explicitly.

**Pre-contemplation signals (not ready — referral or pause):**
- Every answer attributes the problem to external factors: other people, circumstances, the past
- Cannot name any pattern they are contributing to
- Spiritual life is described in terms of activity (church attendance, devotional habits) with no experiential texture
- Shows no emotional weight when the identity gap is named
- Wants practical strategies, accountability, or goal-setting help — not formation

**Contemplation signals (minimum threshold for fit):**
- Names an internal dimension to the problem, even tentatively: "I think part of it is me..."
- Shows emotional weight when talking about who they "should" be vs. who they are
- Has tried to change and can describe where it broke down at a behavioral level
- Feels the gap between theological identity and lived identity, even if they don't have language for it
- Ambivalent about what addressing it will cost, but genuinely willing to look

**Preparation signals (strong fit):**
- Came in with specific questions about the model or what coaching involves
- Can name exactly what they want to be different — not just the behavior but the underlying thing
- Has resources and support in place (community, therapy if needed, time)
- Energy when talking about what becomes possible if the gap closes

**Action/Maintenance signals (assess for wrong fit or wrong level):**
- Already doing significant identity work; may not need this specific model
- OR: Describes impressive spiritual discipline with no change in the underlying gap — this is actually a Contemplation/Preparation client who has been addressing the wrong level. Strong fit for this model.

#### Section 6: Divergent Identity Behavioral Markers

**Present and coachable — right fit for this model:**
- Theology and behavior diverge consistently under pressure (can describe both)
- Failure response involves shame cycling, performance recovery, or extended disconnection from God
- Identity language is external (role, achievement, relational position) — covenant identity is known but not felt as operative
- Duration of pattern is significant; attempts at change have been behavioral, not root-level
- Client can feel the gap when it's named and is curious about it rather than defensive

**Absent — wrong fit (may need a different coaching model):**
- Gap is genuinely small; client's behavior reflects integrated covenant identity
- Issue is primarily practical (career direction, decision-making, skill development) without identity-level underpinning
- Client is looking for accountability or performance coaching — clear mismatch of model to need

**Present but clinical — referral indicated:**
- Gap is so severe that it's producing functional impairment (significant depression, inability to function in key life domains)
- Trauma is actively destabilizing the client's ability to engage reflectively
- Identity collapse is acute and crisis-level — needs clinical stabilization before coaching
- Client is describing symptoms of clinical-level mood disorder, dissociation, or psychosis

#### Section 7: Model Introduction — Accessible Language

Use this as a template, not a script. Adapt to what the client actually said in the call.

**The one-minute version (when time is tight or the client seems overwhelmed):**
"The coaching I do works in the gap between who you know yourself to be in Christ and who you actually experience yourself to be in daily life — especially under pressure. Most growth approaches work on what you do. This one works on who you live from. The foundation is that your identity in Christ is already given — not something you build or earn. The work is closing the distance between that declaration and how it actually operates in your life."

**The three-minute version (standard introduction):**
1. Name the gap: "What I'm hearing you describe is a distance between [specific thing the client said about their theological identity] and [specific behavior or experience they described]. That gap is real and it has a structure."
2. Name the architecture: "This coaching is built on the idea that your identity in Christ is already declared — it's the indicative, the given. The work isn't to build it. It's to close the distance between what's already declared and what you actually live from. The gap is maintained by specific formation material: early messages you believed, vows you made, competing commitments that make change feel dangerous even when you genuinely want it."
3. Name the work: "We work through that gap in four phases — getting the baseline, mapping the specific wounds and lies underneath, doing targeted installation work, and then integrating and closing. It's not fast. It moves at the pace of real formation. But what changes, changes at a depth that holds."
4. Invite response: "Does that match what you're actually carrying? What questions does that raise?"

**What to avoid in the introduction:**
- Theological vocabulary the client hasn't used themselves
- Overselling outcomes
- Making it sound like a program or curriculum rather than a relational process
- Filling silence after the introduction — let it land

**Alternate framing — named-pattern, non-injury presentation (added 2026-07-15):**
Include a second version of both the one-minute and three-minute scripts, used instead of the standard version when the Entry Fork read (Section 2) surfaced a named pattern without an injury behind it. The theological content and four-phase container stay identical; only injury-presupposing vocabulary changes:
- The one-minute version is unchanged — it was never injury-specific.
- In the three-minute version, "vows you made" becomes "patterns you were trained into, ways of operating that got reinforced," and "mapping the specific wounds and lies underneath" becomes "mapping the specific patterns and beliefs underneath."
- Instruct the coach to choose whichever framing matches what actually surfaced in Movements 2–3, not to default to the standard (wound) version by habit. If the Entry Fork read was ambiguous, instruct the coach to ask rather than guess: "Does 'wounds and lies' land, or does it feel more like a pattern than a wound?" — the client's own reaction to the word is itself diagnostic data.

#### Section 8: Transition Language

**Confirmed fit — moving forward:**
"Based on what you've shared, I think this is the right work for where you are. What I'm hearing maps directly to what this coaching is designed for. Here's what happens next: I'll send you the coaching agreement and a fuller intake form to complete before our first session. Once those are in, we'll schedule Session 1. Any questions before we close?"

**No fit — graceful and honest:**
"I want to be straight with you — I don't think this specific model is the right fit for you right now. [Name the specific reason — not the right stage, practical coaching need, clinical-level material, theological mismatch.] That's not a judgment; it's precision about what this model is built for. What I'd suggest instead is [referral / alternative resource]. I'm happy to help point you in the right direction."

**Pause — clinical or readiness concern:**
"There's something important I want to make sure we address before moving forward. [Name the specific concern — clinical screening answer, active destabilization, therapy coordination needed.] I don't want to rush past that. Let's [specific next step — connect with a therapist, follow up after therapy is stable, schedule a second conversation in X weeks]. I'll reach out on [specific date]."

**Unclear — need to think:**
"I want to sit with what you've shared before I give you a clear answer. I'll be back to you within 24 hours by email." *(Use this only when genuinely needed — not as a soft no.)*

**4. Decision record:** Did you send Tier 2? Yes — on what basis? No — what was the specific reason?
