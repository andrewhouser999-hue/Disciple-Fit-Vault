# AI Prompt — Accountability Companion Guide

*Generation prompt for [[Covenant Identity — Accountability Companion Guide]]. Saved for re-generation, revision, and adaptation.*

---

## Purpose

Build `Covenant Identity — Accountability Companion Guide.md` — a client-facing guide for identifying and engaging one person in the client's life as a formation companion. Not a coach, not a therapist — a trusted person who knows the client is in this process and is willing to witness it without managing it.

Makes the fellowship discipline from the Spiritual Disciplines Guide concrete and structured. The companion relationship is one of the most important formation resources outside the coaching sessions — and the most likely to be either misused or avoided.

---

## Design Decisions

- **"Formation companion," not "accountability partner."** The word "accountability" invites the wrong framing — reporting to someone who monitors behavior. The companion is a witness, not a monitor. This language distinction matters and should be named explicitly in the client-facing section.
- **Three clear sections for the client:** (1) How to identify the right person, (2) What to tell them, (3) What to ask of them. Plus a fourth section on what the companion is *not* being asked to do — the scope boundary.
- **Wound-type calibration, client-facing.** Each type has a characteristic way of either misusing or avoiding the companion relationship. This belongs in the client-facing section because the client needs to catch their own pattern before it shapes who they choose and how they engage.
- **The companion does not need to read anything or receive training.** The guide itself is the client's tool — they use it to prepare for the conversation that invites the companion in.
- **Deploy in Phase 2 or 3** — after enough session work has been done that the client has something real to share with the companion, and before Phase 4 so the companion can witness some of the integration. Too early (Phase 1) and the client has nothing to name yet. Too late (Phase 4) and the companion has no time to function.
- **Bonhoeffer's "Life Together" framing** — real community vs. idealized community. The companion relationship is not perfect support; it is real, limited, human presence. Name this explicitly so the client does not discard a genuine companion because they are imperfect.

## Document Structure

### Practitioner Reference

- Deployment: Phase 2–3, after wound work has begun and before Phase 4
- Coach's role: help the client identify the right person in session before the client goes to find them alone — the coach may ask: "Who in your life already knows something is happening in you? Who has stayed present when things were hard?" Those are the companion criteria.
- Wound-type watch-fors (coach only):
  - *Warrior:* will choose someone they can manage — a person who will not push back, who is safe because they are controllable. Challenge gently: "Would this person be able to tell you something hard?"
  - *Hermit:* will struggle to identify anyone — the pool of safe people feels empty, or the exposure of asking feels too costly. The coach may need to help the Hermit stay with "who is *least* unsafe" rather than "who is safe."
  - *False Noble:* will choose someone who will be impressed by the work rather than someone who will witness it honestly. Challenge gently: "Would this person still be present if nothing impressive was happening?"
- What this is not: not a supervision relationship, not a co-coaching arrangement, not a replacement for the coaching engagement

### Client-Facing Guide

Four sections:

**Section 1 — What a Formation Companion Is**
Brief framing: not an accountability partner who monitors behavior, not a therapist who processes your story, not a discipleship mentor who teaches you. A witness — someone who knows you are in this process and is willing to be present to what it is doing in you, without needing to fix, direct, or manage it. One person. This is deliberate — not a group, not a rotation.

Name the Bonhoeffer distinction briefly: the companion you need is a real person with real limitations, not the idealized version of support that exists only in the imagination. A companion who shows up imperfectly is worth more than a perfect companion who does not exist.

**Section 2 — How to Identify the Right Person**
Three criteria — the companion should be:
1. Someone who has already stayed present when things were hard for you — not because they fixed it, but because they did not leave
2. Someone who will not need you to perform okayness for them — they can hold honest information without it collapsing the relationship
3. Someone who will tell you something true even if it is not what you want to hear — not harshly, but honestly

What the companion does *not* need to be: a trained counselor, someone further along in their own formation, theologically sophisticated, or available constantly.

Wound-type note per type: Warrior, Hermit, False Noble — each type's pattern in companion selection named (who each type is tempted to choose and why that person usually cannot actually witness them).

**Section 3 — What to Tell the Companion**
A guide for the initial conversation — what to say, in plain language:
- What the coaching engagement is (brief, simple)
- What you are asking the companion to do (witness, not fix)
- What the companion can expect from you (occasional honest updates, not a weekly report)
- What you are not asking the companion to do (named explicitly — see Section 4)

Include language the client can actually use, not just principles. Something they could say in a 10-minute conversation.

**Section 4 — What to Ask of the Companion**
Three asks:
1. *Be present* — check in occasionally; the frequency is the client's choice, not the companion's
2. *Receive honestly* — when the client shares something, reflect it back or ask one question; do not immediately advise, fix, or spiritualize it away
3. *Hold what is shared* — do not bring it up at inopportune times, do not share it with others, do not make it a project

What the companion is NOT being asked to do: coach, counsel, provide biblical teaching, hold the client accountable to behavior, evaluate their progress, or be available for crisis (the crisis protocol is for crises — not the companion's role).

## Wound-Type Items in Client-Facing Section

**Warrior:** tends to choose someone who feels controllable — a person who will not push back or hold them to honest account. The companion who always affirms is a companion who cannot actually witness. The Warrior needs someone with enough security in their own identity to say a true thing.

**Hermit:** tends to decide that no one qualifies — the pool of people who are safe enough feels empty, or the act of asking feels like too much exposure before the relationship has been tested. Start with the least unsafe person you know, not the ideal person. A partial companion is better than an imagined one.

**False Noble:** tends to choose someone who will be impressed by the work — a person whose admiration the client is also managing. The companion relationship can become another performance context. The right companion is someone who would still show up if nothing impressive was happening.

- Direct about what the companion is not: the boundaries protect the companion as much as the client
- No wound-type names in the section headers — integrated into the natural flow of each section as "some people find..." framing, then named by type
