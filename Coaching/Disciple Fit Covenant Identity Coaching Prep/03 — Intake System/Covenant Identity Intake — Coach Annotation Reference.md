# Covenant Identity Intake — Coach Annotation Reference
*Quick-reference for reviewing completed intake forms before session 1. No design rationale — only the diagnostic key. For full design rationale see: Covenant Identity Intake — Design Basis & Annotation.md*

---

## How to Use This Document

1. Open the completed client intake alongside this reference
2. Read each answer through the corresponding annotation
3. After reading all answers, write a 3-5 sentence working hypothesis (see template at end)
4. Select 2-3 verbal in-session follow-up areas based on what the form revealed
5. Set the form aside — session 1 starts with the client's present state, not the form

---

## Section A — What's Happening Now

---

### Q1 — Presenting Problem
*"What's happening in your life right now that made you reach out?"*

**Probing:** Presenting problem, narrative contamination level (initial), motivation quality (surface).

**Read for:**
- Ratio of external description (circumstances, other people) to internal description (feelings, identity, self-awareness). High external = limited self-reflective access or avoidance. Shapes how quickly to move to identity work.
- Behavioral language ("I want to stop doing X") vs. identity language ("I want to actually feel like who God says I am"). Identity language = readiness for this model.
- Vocabulary that is already identity-adjacent = client has been in this territory before; can move faster.
- **No presenting problem named, but a specific and felt growth desire present = Vision-Led signal, not low self-reflective access.** Confirm against Tier 1 responses and the discovery call before finalizing this read — see Entry Fork in [[Covenant Identity — Diagnostic Lens Transition Logic]].

**Referral watch:** Crisis language, acute hopelessness, self-harm ideation.

**Follow-up to prepare:** "When you say [specific phrase], what does that mean at depth — not just the surface behavior, but what it says about who you are?"

---

### Q2 — Duration and Failed Attempts
*"How long has this been present, and how many times have you tried to address or change it?"*

**Probing:** Immunity to Change signal.

**Read for:**
- 3+ serious attempts at the same pattern over months or years = Immunity to Change almost certainly operating. The competing commitment is present and protected.
- Short duration + first attempt = may be circumstantial trigger, not deep schema. Different approach.
- Note the word they use to describe the attempts: "tried," "worked on," "attacked," "prayed about," "asked God to fix." The verb reveals their operating model for change.

**Referral watch:** Years of duration across multiple life domains + pervasive functional impact may indicate chronic depression requiring clinical assessment, not just coaching.

**Follow-up to prepare:** "Walk me through what happened the last time you tried to change this. Where specifically did it break down?"

## Section B — Your Story

---

### Q3 — Life Arc / Narrative
*"How would you describe the overall arc of your life so far?"*

**Probing:** Narrative contamination level (primary). Narrative Identity Theory — is the operative story a contamination narrative (decline, victimhood, stuckness) or a redemptive narrative (suffering toward meaning, growth arc)?

**Read for:**
- Client as agent vs. subject: Do things happen *to* them, or do they participate in shaping outcomes? Agency language = stronger identity foundation.
- Redemptive threads they're not seeing — name them. The coach may see more redemption in the story than the client does.
- "Circling the same ground" = Immunity to Change at life-narrative level. This is significant.
- Tone: flat/resigned = possible depression or chronic discouragement. Energized even in difficulty = resilient base to work from.

**Referral watch:** Pure victimhood narrative, no threads of agency, no positive relational figures at all — may indicate complex trauma. Coaching can proceed but slowly; deeper therapeutic work may be needed alongside.

**Follow-up to prepare:** "In the arc you described — where do you see God? What role does He play in the story?"

---

### Q4 — Early Messages / Schema Origin
*"What messages did you receive, spoken or unspoken, about whether you were enough?"*

**Probing:** Schema origin (primary). Early attachment signals (secondary). Indicative/imperative reversal source.

**Read for:**
- Conditional love: "I was accepted when I performed / when I didn't cause problems / when I was invisible." = Subjugation schema, Conditional Worth schema, reversed grammar entrenched early.
- Absence: "No one said anything — I was just expected to function." = Emotional Deprivation schema, possible avoidant attachment.
- Contradiction: "They said they loved me but the way they treated me said something different." = Gap between stated and behavioral messages = core schema conflict, often the load-bearing structure.
- Note the gap between what was said vs. what was modeled. The modeled message is almost always the operative one.

**Attachment signals from this answer:**
- Consistent, warm, responsive care described = secure base.
- Inconsistent or emotionally unavailable care = anxious attachment signal.
- Emotionally withdrawn or dismissive = avoidant attachment signal.
- Frightening or chaotic = disorganized attachment (referral consideration).

**Referral watch:** Descriptions of abuse, severe neglect, or trauma. These are contextual information for the coaching — not the coaching's focus. If the wound needs processing, refer to therapy.

**Follow-up to prepare:** "How did those messages shape what you came to believe about yourself — and do you see those beliefs still running today?"

## Section C — Your Experience of God

---

### Q5 — God-Experience vs. God-Belief Gap
*"Describe your relationship with God as it actually feels right now — not theologically, but what it genuinely feels like."*

**Probing:** God-experience vs. God-belief gap (THE most diagnostically important question in the form). Attachment to God. Spiritual bypassing level.

**Read for:**
- **Answer matches theology AND has texture** (specific language, emotional range, some negative content alongside positive) = genuine integration. Secure attachment to God.
- **Answer matches theology but is flat, formulaic, or impossibly clean** = spiritual bypassing. The client has learned to give the right answer rather than the real one. The authentic experience is being managed, not expressed.
- **Answer contradicts theology** ("I know God loves me but it feels like He's disappointed in me / He's far away / I have to perform to re-access Him") = God-representation gap. This is gold. Locate it precisely.

**God-attachment signals:**
- Close, safe, accessible = secure attachment to God.
- Close but monitored (always checking for God's approval, performing for it) = anxious attachment to God.
- Distant, uninvolved, "manage yourself" = avoidant attachment to God (functional deism).

**Referral watch:** God described as threatening, punishing, or actively malevolent = may indicate spiritual abuse history. Proceed carefully. Therapeutic work may need to precede coaching in this area.

**Follow-up to prepare:** "When did God start feeling [the way they described]? Was there a time when it felt different?"

---

### Q6 — God's View at Failure / Indicative-Imperative Reversal
*"When you fail at something that matters, what does your gut tell you about how God sees you in that moment?"*

**Probing:** Defectiveness/Shame schema (primary). Indicative/imperative grammar reversal (secondary — is God's view of them contingent on performance?). Conditional worth schema (tertiary).

**Read for:**
- Immediate God-as-disappointed or God-as-turned-away after failure = reversed grammar fully operational. Performance-based God-representation. Defectiveness schema activated.
- "I feel like I let God down, but I know His love doesn't change" = partial integration. The covenant truth is landing cognitively but not breaking through to the gut. Phase 3 work needed.
- "God understands but I feel terrible about myself" = Shame schema active but not directed at the God-relationship. The internal critic is the primary driver here — slightly different target.
- Note the speed of the reported response: "immediately" vs. "eventually" is diagnostically meaningful. Schema activation speed indicates schema strength.

**Referral watch:** God's rejection described as total and permanent + self-condemnation language = depressive cognition, may require clinical assessment.

**Follow-up to prepare:** "Where did you learn that failing means something about how God sees you? When does that script first appear in your story?"

---

### Q7 — Lament Capacity / Spiritual Bypassing
*"Have you ever brought anger, grief, confusion, or doubt directly to God — unfiltered?"*

**Probing:** Lament capacity. Spiritual bypassing level. Attachment security with God (secure attachment allows protest; insecure attachment does not).

**Read for three answer types:**

1. **Yes, and it was transformative or okay:** Genuine lament capacity. Secure enough God-attachment to protest. Can work through Phases 1-4 without prerequisite lament work. Move forward.

2. **Yes, but it felt wrong, dangerous, or punished:** Attempted lament but the God-representation or a received theology penalized it. Explicit theological permission for lament is needed before it becomes usable. Address this in session 1.

3. **No — read the "what stops you" answer carefully:**
   - "I feel like I shouldn't be angry at God" = theology of respectability. Authentic self is filtered before God. Significant bypassing.
   - "I'm afraid of what He'd think" = anxious attachment, performative prayer life.
   - "I don't believe it would matter" = avoidant attachment, functional deism. The most spiritually dangerous — God has been effectively removed from emotional life.
   - "I've tried and nothing happens" = grief and disillusionment. Lament work may need to be the first order of business.

**Referral watch:** Total emotional shutdown around God + God described as threatening = may indicate spiritual abuse or trauma.

**Follow-up to prepare:** "What do you imagine would happen if you brought the full, unfiltered thing to God — the anger, the confusion, the grief? What's the worst-case version of how He'd respond?"

## Section D — Your Identity

---

### Q8 — Defectiveness/Shame Schema
*"When you fail at something important, what does that mean about you as a person?"*

**Probing:** Defectiveness/Shame schema (most direct probe in the form). Failure schema. Narrative contamination via self-attribution.

**Read for:**
- **Globalization** — extending a specific failure to a global self-statement. "It means I'm fundamentally not good enough / a disappointment / there's something wrong with me." = high schema activation.
- **Permanence** — treating the self-statement as stable and unchangeable. "I always do this / I'll never really change." = high schema + hopelessness component.
- **Situational attribution** — "It means I need to learn something / I misjudged the situation." = low schema activation or healthy attribution.
- **Temporary self-criticism** — "I feel bad about myself for a while but then it passes." = schema present but not dominant. Note the duration — "a while" could mean hours or months.

The schema signature is in how automatically and how globally the failure is attributed to the self. This is one of the first answers to read. It orients almost everything else.

**Referral watch:** Failure meaning that includes hopelessness, self-harm content, or suicidal ideation = immediate referral before coaching proceeds.

**Follow-up to prepare:** "When you say [their exact globalized phrase], when did you first know that about yourself? When was the first time that felt true?"

---

### Q9 — Covenant Identity Integration Scale
*Scale 1-10: How much does what God says about who you are actually feel true in lived experience?*

**Probing:** Covenant identity integration level. Quantified gap between declared identity and lived/felt identity.

**Read for:**
- **1-3:** Large gap. Covenant truth operating only at cognitive level. Do not rush to behavioral application. Embodiment and installation work in Phase 3 must be patient and thorough.
- **4-6:** Moderate gap. Partial integration. Some layers have landed, others haven't. The coaching work is identifying which specifically — ask in session 1.
- **7-9:** Smaller gap, or aspirational answer. Hold with curiosity. Ask: "Which areas feel most true? Where is the gap still the widest?"
- **10:** Almost always aspirational or performance. No client with active schema material or meaningful Immunity to Change rates a genuine 10. Use it as a conversation opener, not as a baseline.

**Follow-up to prepare:** "Where in your life does it feel most true? And where does the gap still feel the widest?"

---

### Q10 — Shame Content / Fear of Being Known
*"What do you most fear people would discover about you if they truly knew you?"*

**Probing:** Shame content (most direct probe for the specific content of the Defectiveness schema). Abandonment fear. Isolation schema.

**Read for four content types:**

1. **Moral/behavioral content** — "That I'm not as holy as I appear / that I struggle with X / that I've done Y." = Performance-based identity, likely Unrelenting Standards schema alongside Defectiveness. Spiritual perfectionism is often present.

2. **Character content** — "That I'm fundamentally selfish / that I'm empty / that I'm not who people think I am." = Core Defectiveness schema. Often combined with Impostor dynamic. This is the most common answer from Christians in this coaching context.

3. **Relational content** — "That I don't actually love people as much as I show / that I'm cold underneath." = Often Emotional Deprivation schema inverted — the client fears being discovered as the depriving one because they feel deprived themselves.

4. **Capacity content** — "That I'm not as competent / together / spiritually mature as people think." = Failure schema, Unrelenting Standards schema.

This answer is one of the first to read on any completed form. It orients the rest of the session 1 listening.

**Referral watch:** Content involving serious moral failure, ongoing patterns with legal or relational consequences, or secrets that may indicate double life — handle with discernment in session 1. Ensure the coaching container is appropriate before proceeding.

**Follow-up to prepare:** "When you imagine someone discovering that — what do you imagine happening? What's the worst version of how they'd respond?"

## Section E — What You're Looking For

---

### Q11 — Motivation Quality
*"What's actually driving you to pursue coaching right now?"*

**Probing:** SDT motivation quality. Urgency vs. readiness. Whether coaching is being sought as the right tool or as a substitute for something else.

**Read for:**

| Answer type | Signal | Coaching implication |
|---|---|---|
| "I'm tired of living this way and want something different for myself" | Intrinsic / integrated motivation | Most sustainable. Will hold through discomfort. |
| "This genuinely matters to me, even if it's uncomfortable" | Identified motivation | Workable. Commitment is real. |
| "I feel like I should be further along / I feel guilty / someone told me I should" | Introjected motivation | Fragile. Will perform initially, break down when challenged. Naming this early can convert it. |
| "My wife / pastor / friend thinks I should do this" | External motivation | Highest risk. May not have personal buy-in. Explore in session 1 whether the client genuinely wants this. |
| "I don't have a specific problem — I want to become who I actually am in Christ" | Intrinsic/integrated motivation, Vision-Led signal | Workable, often unusually durable since it isn't crisis-dependent. Confirm against the Lateral Reroute Signal before treating as straightforwardly healthy. |

Also note: the "rather than six months ago" clause often surfaces the precipitating event — a loss, a relational crisis, a ministry failure, something that cracked the managed self open. That event is often the most alive material for session 1.

**Follow-up to prepare:** "What would it mean about you if nothing changed in the next year?"

---

### Q12 — Referral Screening
*"Are you currently in active mental health treatment, processing significant trauma, or experiencing clinical-level depression or anxiety?"*

**Probing:** Referral screening. Fit assessment.

**YES answer — discernment conversation before proceeding:**
Ask in discovery call:
- Is the client currently stable in their therapeutic work?
- Is the therapy focused on past trauma processing (clearly separate from the coaching agenda)?
- Has the therapist been informed about coaching and is open to it?
- Are the coaching and therapy agendas clearly distinct?

**Coaching can proceed if:** Client is stable, therapy and coaching agendas are distinct, therapist is aware and in support.

**Defer coaching if:** Active trauma destabilization is occurring. Client is in crisis. The therapy and coaching agendas overlap (both trying to work on the same wound).

---

### Q13 — Open Field (Optional)
*"Is there anything else you want me to know before we meet?"*

**Probing:** Unsurfaced content. What the client most needed to say that the other questions didn't reach.

**Read for:** What they chose to bring when given total freedom is what is most alive. This is often the real session 1 starting point. It may be: a confession, a fear about the coaching process, a past church wound that needs acknowledgment, a hope they couldn't offer until explicitly invited, or a piece of context that reframes everything else in the form.

**Do not skip this field.** When a client takes time to fill out an optional question, what they write is almost always the most important thing in the form.
