# Covenant Identity Intake — Design Basis & Annotation
*Medium-agnostic master reference. All future medium-specific intake guides are built from this document. Contains full design rationale, diagnostic targets, complete question set with coach annotations, and session integration notes.*

---

## I. Primary Job of This Form

Most intake forms fail by trying to do too many jobs at once. This form has one primary job and one secondary job.

**Primary job — Diagnostic:** Establish where the client is in relation to the Covenant Identity architecture before the coach walks into session 1. The coach should finish reading a completed form with a working hypothesis — a specific, informed orientation — about:
- Which covenant identity layers are integrated, cognitively held but not embodied, or actively disbelieved
- Which early maladaptive schemas (EMS) are most likely operational
- What the primary narrative contamination looks like
- What the client's experience of God actually is (as opposed to what they believe theologically)
- Where Immunity to Change is likely operating
- What the client's lament capacity is
- Whether referral is indicated

**Secondary job — Client Self-Orientation:** The act of completing the form begins moving the client into the model's frame before session 1. The questions themselves — by what they ask and how they ask it — signal that the coach understands a specific kind of stuck. A well-designed form makes a right-fit client think: *"This person has seen what I'm going through before."*

Everything else (screening, session 1 agenda-setting, baseline documentation) is tertiary and must not compete with the two primary functions.

---

## II. Design Principles

**1. The diagnostic framework orients the coach's listening — it must not close it.**
Two to three questions in the form are open-ended and unstructured, allowing the client to bring what the form wasn't designed to find. Leave room for the Spirit to surface something the framework didn't anticipate.

**2. Client-facing language is experience-level, not clinical or theological.**
The client sees accessible, warm, experiential questions. The coach reads those answers through the theological-psychological lens. The form meets the client where they are; the coach translates. "When you fail at something that matters to you, what does your gut tell you about how God sees you in that moment?" is accessible to almost anyone. The coach reads it as a Defectiveness schema probe and a God-representation diagnostic.

**3. Schema probes are indirect.**
Schemas are self-concealing. Asking "Do you struggle with shame?" produces denial from most people with significant shame — because shame operates by hiding itself. Effective diagnostic questions approach schemas through behavior and experience, not direct self-assessment. "What do you most fear people would discover about you if they truly knew you?" surfaces shame content without naming it.

**4. The form is written pre-session only — it does not become session 1.**
The written form is for the coach's preparation. It informs listening and question-direction in session 1 but does not become session 1's structure. Session 1 starts with what is most alive for the client now, not with "let me review what you wrote." The form shapes the coach's ears; it does not script the session.

**5. Screening is embedded as discernment, not gatekeeping.**
The screening question is framed as mutual discernment ("I want to make sure this model is what you actually need") rather than a test the client might fail. This preserves honest self-disclosure and honors the coaching relationship's dignity.

**6. The annotation layer is the instrument.**
The client-facing questions are the surface. The coach annotations — invisible to the client — are what make the form a diagnostic instrument rather than a questionnaire. Every question has a parallel annotation containing: what it's probing, how to read different answer types, follow-up questions to prepare, and referral indicators. Without the annotation layer, the form is data collection. With it, the form is clinical preparation.

**7. Sequence moves from low vulnerability to high.**
Present state and what brought them here → Life story and formative experiences → Experience of God → Identity and the gap → Expectations and fit. Each section builds on the safety established by the previous one. Jumping to deep shame content before safety is established produces defensive answers or abandonment of the form.

**8. The form's secondary job is orientation by exposure, not instruction.**
The questions introduce the model's vocabulary and assumptions without lecturing. By the time the client completes the form, they are already thinking about identity (not just behavior), about the gap between what they believe and what they feel, and about their actual experience of God — not just their theology. They arrive at session 1 partly oriented, without having been taught.

## III. Core Design Tensions and Resolutions

**Depth vs. Completion Rate:**
*Resolution:* Two-tier structure. Written pre-session (10-13 questions, 12-15 minutes) captures the most diagnostically essential data. Verbal in-session component (5-7 areas, not scripted questions) goes deeper based on what the written form revealed. The coach selects which areas need triangulation or expansion.

**Client-Facing Language vs. Coach-Facing Utility:**
*Resolution:* Parallel layers. Client-facing question (accessible, warm, experiential). Coach annotation (diagnostic target, interpretive key, follow-up questions). The client sees only the surface. The annotation is a separate coach-facing reference document.

**Model Specificity vs. Openness to Surprise:**
*Resolution:* 2-3 open-ended, unstructured questions with no annotation target — allowing the client to bring what the coach wasn't looking for.

**Pre-Session Completion vs. In-Session Discovery:**
*Resolution:* The form informs the coach's listening; it does not become session 1's agenda. Session 1 starts with the client's present state, not a form review.

**Theological Frame vs. Client Readiness:**
*Resolution:* Client-facing questions use experience-level language. The coach reads through the theological lens. The form never asks the client to evaluate their own theological categories.

**Screening vs. Welcome:**
*Resolution:* Screening embedded as mutual discernment, not gatekeeping. Framed as fit, not pass/fail.

## IV. Diagnostic Target Map

| Diagnostic Target | What It Tells the Coach | How It's Surfaced |
|---|---|---|
| Presenting problem | What's stuck, what brought them here | Q1 — direct |
| Immunity to Change signal | Competing commitment likely operating | Q2 — duration and failed attempts |
| Narrative contamination | Story arc: decline/victim vs. redemptive | Q3 — life arc description |
| Schema origin | Early maladaptive schema formation context | Q4 — early messages |
| God-experience vs. God-belief gap | Attachment to God, theological vs. experiential divergence | Q5 — God description |
| Defectiveness/Shame schema + indicative/imperative reversal | Core schema signature, reversed grammar operating | Q6, Q8 |
| Lament capacity / spiritual bypassing | Phase 2 needs, bypass level | Q7 — anger/grief with God |
| Covenant identity integration level | Quantified gap | Q9 — linear scale |
| Shame content / abandonment fear | Primary schema content | Q10 — fear of being known |
| SDT motivation quality | Intrinsic vs. introjected/external | Q11 — actual motivation |
| Referral indicators | Clinical needs, ethical responsibility | Q12 — screening |
| Unsurfaced content | What the form didn't reach | Q13 — open field |

## V. Complete Question Set with Coach Annotations

---

### SECTION A — What's Happening Now

---

**Q1**
*Client sees:*
"What's happening in your life right now that made you reach out? What are you most stuck in, frustrated by, or longing for? Take as much space as you need."

*Coach annotation:*
**Probing:** Presenting problem, narrative contamination level (initial), motivation quality (surface).
**How to read:** Note the ratio of external description (circumstances, other people, events) to internal description (feelings, identity, self-awareness). A client who describes only external circumstances may have limited self-reflective capacity or high avoidance — this shapes how quickly you can move to identity work. Note whether the "longing" language is behavioral ("I want to stop doing X") or identity-level ("I want to actually feel like who God says I am"). The latter signals readiness for this model. Note any vocabulary that is already identity-adjacent — this is a client who has been in this territory before.
**Follow-up to prepare:** "When you say [specific phrase they used], what does that mean to you at depth — not just the surface behavior but what it says about who you are?"
**Referral watch:** Crisis language, acute hopelessness, language suggesting active self-harm ideation.

---

**Q2**
*Client sees:*
"How long has this been present — and how many times have you tried to address or change it? Be as specific as you can."

*Coach annotation:*
**Probing:** Immunity to Change signal (primary). Duration and repetition of failed attempts is the clearest external indicator that a competing commitment is operating beneath the stated goal.
**How to read:** Three or more serious attempts at change over a significant time period (months to years) with the pattern returning = Immunity to Change is almost certainly operating. The longer the duration, the more deeply the schema or competing commitment is entrenched. Short duration (recent onset) may indicate circumstantial trigger rather than deep schema pattern — different coaching approach.
**Follow-up to prepare:** "You've tried X times. What happened each time — not just 'it didn't work,' but where specifically did it break down?" This locates the Immunity to Change mechanism precisely.
**Referral watch:** Duration of years combined with pervasive life-area impact may indicate chronic depression — not just a coaching issue.

---

### SECTION B — Your Story

---

**Q3**
*Client sees:*
"How would you describe the overall arc of your life so far? Not a résumé — more like: what has the overall journey actually felt like? Has it felt like it's been going somewhere, or more like you've been circling the same ground?"

*Coach annotation:*
**Probing:** Narrative contamination level (primary). Narrative Identity Theory — is the client's operative story a contamination narrative (decline, victimhood, stuckness, shame as permanent condition) or a redemptive narrative (suffering toward meaning, growth arc, agency present)?
**How to read:** Listen for: (1) the client as agent vs. subject — do things happen *to* them, or do they participate in shaping what happens? Agency language indicates stronger identity foundation. (2) Redemptive threads they're not seeing — the coach may need to name what the client is missing in their own story. (3) "Circling the same ground" language — often the clearest sign of Immunity to Change operating at the life-narrative level.
**Follow-up to prepare:** "In the overall arc you described — where do you see God? What role does He play in the story?" (Surfaces God-representation and sovereignty theology in experiential terms.)
**Referral watch:** Story of pure victimhood with no threads of agency or redemption, combined with absence of any positive relational figures — may indicate complex trauma requiring therapeutic processing before coaching can be effective.

---

**Q4**
*Client sees:*
"Think back to when you were younger — childhood, adolescence. What messages did you receive, spoken or unspoken, about who you were, whether you were enough, or what you had to do to be loved and accepted?"

*Coach annotation:*
**Probing:** Schema origin (primary). Early attachment signals (secondary). Indicative/imperative reversal source (tertiary). This question is the closest thing to an origin story for the operational schemas.
**How to read:** "Spoken or unspoken" is critical — most formative messages were behavioral, not verbal. Watch for: conditional love language ("I was accepted when I performed well / when I didn't cause problems / when I was invisible"); absence language ("No one said anything about who I was — I was just expected to function"); contradiction language ("They said they loved me but the way they treated me said something different"). The gap between stated messages and behavioral messages is itself diagnostic — it often produces the core schema conflict.
**Attachment signals:** Consistent, warm, responsive care described = secure base. Inconsistent or emotionally unavailable care = anxious attachment. Emotionally withdrawn or dismissive care = avoidant attachment. Frightening or chaotic care = disorganized attachment (referral consideration).
**Follow-up to prepare:** "How do you think those messages shaped what you came to believe about yourself — about whether you were enough? And do you see those beliefs still running today?"
**Referral watch:** Descriptions of abuse, severe neglect, or trauma — the coach is not the right container for processing these. They are contextual information for the coaching but should not become the coaching's focus. If the client needs to process the wound, refer to therapy.

### SECTION C — Your Experience of God

---

**Q5**
*Client sees:*
"Describe your relationship with God as it actually feels right now — not as you know it should be theologically, but what it genuinely feels like day to day. Is God close or distant? Engaged or absent? Pleased, neutral, or disappointed?"

*Coach annotation:*
**Probing:** God-experience vs. God-belief gap (primary — the single most diagnostically important question in this form). Attachment to God (secondary). Spiritual bypassing level (tertiary).
**How to read:** This question produces two types of revealing answers: (1) *The answer matches theology* — "God feels close and present." If this is genuine, it indicates a relationally secure faith. If it feels polished or too clean, it may indicate spiritual bypassing — the client has learned to give the right answer rather than the real one. Watch for flatness in the description, use of theological formulas, or absence of any negative emotional content. Real relational descriptions have texture; performed ones do not. (2) *The answer contradicts theology* — "I know God loves me but it feels like He's disappointed in me" or "God feels far away most of the time." This is gold diagnostically — it locates the God-representation gap with precision. This gap is almost always an attachment pattern, a schema, or both, projected onto God.
**God-attachment signals:** Close/safe/present = secure. Close but anxious (monitoring for disappointment, performing for approval) = anxious attachment to God. Distant/uninvolved/you-have-to-manage-yourself = avoidant attachment to God.
**Follow-up to prepare:** "When did God start feeling [the way they described]? Was there a time when it felt different?" (Surfaces the relational wound or event that shaped the God-representation.)
**Referral watch:** God described as threatening, punishing, or actively malevolent — may indicate spiritual abuse history, requiring therapeutic attention before identity work proceeds.

---

**Q6**
*Client sees:*
"When you fail at something that matters to you — morally, relationally, in your work — what does your gut tell you about how God sees you in that moment? (Not what you know the right answer is. What it actually feels like.)"

*Coach annotation:*
**Probing:** Defectiveness/Shame schema (primary). Indicative/imperative reversal (secondary) — specifically, whether the client experiences God's view of them as contingent on their performance. Conditional worth schema (tertiary).
**How to read:** The parenthetical "(Not what you know the right answer is)" is doing critical work — it explicitly invites the honest answer rather than the theological performance. Watch for: (1) Immediate shift to God as disappointed, turned away, or less approachable after failure = reversed grammar operating, performance-based God-representation, Defectiveness schema activated. (2) Separation of behavior from identity ("I feel like I let God down, but I know His love doesn't change") = partial integration, covenant identity partially landing but not fully felt. (3) "God understands but I still feel terrible about myself" = Shame schema active but not directed at God-relationship — points to internalized critic rather than external divine judge. This is a slightly different therapeutic target.
**Follow-up to prepare:** "Where did you learn that failing means something about how God sees you? When does that script first appear in your story?"
**Referral watch:** Answers that describe God's rejection as total and permanent, combined with self-condemnation language, may indicate depressive cognition requiring clinical assessment.

---

**Q7**
*Client sees:*
"Have you ever brought anger, grief, confusion, or doubt directly to God — unfiltered? What happened? Or if you haven't — what stops you?"

*Coach annotation:*
**Probing:** Lament capacity (primary). Spiritual bypassing level (secondary). Attachment security with God (tertiary — secure attachment allows protest; insecure attachment does not).
**How to read:** Three answer types: (1) *Yes, and it was transformative/okay* — this client has some lament capacity, real attachment security with God, and an honest prayer life. They can do the full range of Phases 1-4 without significant lament prerequisite work. (2) *Yes, but it felt dangerous or wrong* — this client has attempted lament but their God-representation penalized it. The internal critic (or a theology they were taught) shut it down. This is significant — they need explicit theological permission for lament before they can use it. (3) *No — answers to "what stops you" are the most diagnostic responses in the form.* "I feel like I shouldn't be angry at God" = theology of respectability operating, authentic self hidden from God. "I'm afraid of what He'd think" = anxious attachment, performative prayer. "I don't really believe it would matter" = avoidant attachment, functional deism. "I've tried and nothing happens" = may indicate grief and disillusionment requiring lament work as first order of business.
**Follow-up to prepare:** "What do you imagine would happen if you brought the full, unfiltered thing to God — the anger, the confusion, the grief? What's the worst case?"
**Referral watch:** Complete shutdown around emotional expression with God, combined with descriptions of God as threatening — may indicate spiritual abuse or trauma.

### SECTION D — Your Identity

---

**Q8**
*Client sees:*
"When you fail at something important, what does that mean about you as a person — not just about the thing that went wrong? Try to answer from the gut, not from your theology."

*Coach annotation:*
**Probing:** Defectiveness/Shame schema (primary — most direct probe in the form). Failure schema (secondary). Narrative contamination via self-attribution (tertiary).
**How to read:** This question is designed to surface *globalization* (extending a specific failure to a global statement about the self) and *permanence* (treating the statement as stable and unchangeable). High schema activation: "It means I'm fundamentally not good enough / a disappointment / I'll never really change / I always do this / there's something wrong with me." Low schema activation: "It means I need to learn something" or "It means I misjudged the situation" — external or situational attribution without self-targeting. The schema signature is in how automatically and how globally the failure is attributed to the self. Moderate: "I feel bad about myself for a while but then it passes" — schema present but not fully dominant.
**Follow-up to prepare:** "When you say [their exact globalized language], when did you first know that about yourself? When was the first time that felt true?"
**Referral watch:** Failure meaning that includes hopelessness ("it means I'll never change and there's no point"), self-harm content, or suicidal ideation — immediate referral.

---

**Q9**
*Client sees:*
"On a scale of 1 to 10, how much does what God says about who you are actually feel true in your lived experience?
- 1 = I know it intellectually but it doesn't reach how I actually live
- 10 = It genuinely shapes how I experience myself and my life day to day"

*Coach annotation:*
**Probing:** Covenant identity integration level (primary). Quantified gap (secondary — this number is the most direct measure of the distance between declared covenant identity and felt/lived identity).
**How to read:** 1-3 = Large gap. Covenant truth is operating only at the cognitive level. Significant embodiment and installation work needed in Phase 3. Approach with patience — do not rush to behavioral application. 4-6 = Moderate gap. Partial integration. Some covenant identity layers have landed; others have not. The coaching work is identifying which specifically. 7-9 = Smaller gap. Either genuine integration in progress, or the client is answering aspirationally. Hold with curiosity — follow up in session with "Which areas of your life does it feel most true? Where does it still feel hollow?" 10 = Almost always aspirational or performance. No one with significant Immunity to Change material or active schemas rates a 10 honestly.
**Follow-up to prepare:** "Where in your life does it feel most true? And where does the gap still feel the widest?"
**Referral watch:** N/A — this is a scaling question.

---

**Q10**
*Client sees:*
"What do you most fear people would discover about you if they truly knew you?"

*Coach annotation:*
**Probing:** Shame content (primary — the most direct probe for the specific content of the Defectiveness schema). Abandonment fear (secondary). Isolation schema (tertiary).
**How to read:** The answer to this question is often the exact content of the Defectiveness schema — the specific thing the client believes disqualifies them from love and belonging. Watch for: (1) Moral/behavioral content ("That I'm not as holy as I appear / that I've done X / that I struggle with Y") = performance-based identity, likely spiritual perfectionism or Unrelenting Standards schema alongside Defectiveness. (2) Character content ("That I'm fundamentally selfish / that I'm empty / that I'm not who people think I am") = core Defectiveness schema, often combined with Shame schema and possible Impostor dynamic. (3) Relational content ("That I don't actually love people as much as I show / that I'm cold underneath") = often Emotional Deprivation schema inverted — the client fears being discovered as the depriving one because they feel deprived. (4) Capacity content ("That I'm not as competent / spiritual / together as people think") = Failure schema, Unrelenting Standards schema. This answer is one of the first things the coach reads when the form comes in. It orients almost every other piece.
**Follow-up to prepare:** "When you imagine someone discovering that about you — what do you imagine happening? What's the worst version of how they'd respond?"
**Referral watch:** Content that involves serious moral failure, ongoing sin patterns, or secrets that carry legal or relational consequence — the coach needs to handle this with care in session 1, ensuring the coaching relationship is the right container for it.

### SECTION E — What You're Looking For

---

**Q11**
*Client sees:*
"What's actually driving you to pursue coaching right now? Try to go beneath the 'good answer' — what's the real, honest thing that's making you reach out now rather than six months from now?"

*Coach annotation:*
**Probing:** SDT motivation quality (primary — intrinsic, identified, introjected, or external). Urgency vs. readiness (secondary). Whether coaching is being sought as the right tool or as a substitute for something else (tertiary).
**How to read:** Integrated/intrinsic motivation ("I'm tired of living this way and I genuinely want something different for myself") = best foundation. Will sustain the work. Identified motivation ("This is genuinely important to me, even if it's uncomfortable") = workable. Introjected motivation ("I feel like I should be further along / someone I respect suggested this / I feel guilty for not doing more") = fragile. Will perform initially but break down when challenged. External motivation ("My wife/pastor/friend thinks I should do this") = significant risk. May not have personal buy-in for the deeper work. The "rather than six months from now" clause surfaces urgency and may reveal the precipitating event — loss, crisis, relationship pressure — that is the real driver. That event is often the most alive coaching material.
**Follow-up to prepare:** "What would it mean about you if nothing changed in the next year?"

---

**Q12**
*Client sees:*
"Are you currently in active mental health treatment, processing significant trauma with a therapist, or experiencing clinical-level depression or anxiety that significantly impacts your daily functioning?"
*(Yes / No)*

*If Yes, client sees:*
"Thank you for sharing that. This matters. I want to make sure you're receiving the right kind of support for where you are. This doesn't automatically mean coaching isn't right for you — sometimes coaching and therapy work well together. We'll talk through this together in our discovery call before we proceed."

*Coach annotation:*
**Probing:** Referral screening (primary). Fit assessment (secondary).
**How to read:** "Yes" does not automatically disqualify coaching. It triggers a discernment conversation in the discovery call. Questions to clarify: Is the client currently stable in their therapeutic work? Is the therapy focused on past trauma (not overlapping with coaching)? Has the therapist been informed and is open to coaching complement? Is the coaching agenda clearly separate from the therapeutic agenda? If active trauma processing is happening and the client is in a destabilized state, coaching should wait. If the client is stable, working with a therapist on trauma, and wants to use coaching for identity/forward movement, this can be viable with coordination.
**Referral watch:** Any "Yes" answer requires discovery call discernment before proceeding.

---

**Q13**
*Client sees:*
"Is there anything else you want me to know before we meet — something the questions didn't touch, something that feels important to name, or something you're hoping I'll understand about where you are?"

*Coach annotation:*
**Probing:** Unsurfaced content (primary). What the client is most burdened by that the form didn't reach. What they most want the coach to understand.
**How to read:** This question often produces the most honest and diagnostically rich material in the form — because it's unstructured and the client chooses what to bring. What they choose to name here, when given total freedom, is what is most alive for them. Read it as: "What did this person most need to say that the other questions didn't create space for?" Sometimes it's a confession, a fear about the coaching, a past experience with church or Christian community that needs to be acknowledged, or a hope that feels too vulnerable to offer unless explicitly invited.
**Follow-up to prepare:** Whatever they bring here is often session 1's real starting point.

## VI. Session 1 Integration Notes

The form is preparation, not session 1 content. The coach reviews the completed form 24-48 hours before session 1 and does the following:

**Step 1 — Form a working hypothesis:**
Based on the full form, the coach identifies: (1) the most likely primary schema, (2) the covenant identity layer most likely disbelieved or unintegrated, (3) whether Immunity to Change is indicated and where it's likely operating, (4) the lament capacity level and Phase 2 needs, (5) any referral flags requiring discernment before proceeding.

**Step 2 — Identify the 2-3 richest areas:**
Select the verbal in-session areas that need depth or triangulation. These become the coach's question pool for session 1 — not a script, but territory to enter if the session opens it.

**Step 3 — Open session 1 with the client's present state, not the form:**
"What's most alive for you today?" — not "Let me review your intake." The form has done its work. Now the coach listens to the live client with the diagnostic orientation already established.

**Step 4 — Verbal in-session areas (select 2-3 based on what the form revealed):**
- Follow-up on the schema origin question (Q4) — "When did you first know that about yourself?"
- God-representation origin (follow-up on Q5) — "When did God start feeling that way?"
- Immunity specifics (follow-up on Q2) — "Walk me through what happened the last time you tried to change this"
- Lament capacity exploration (follow-up on Q7) — "What do you imagine would happen if you brought the full, unfiltered thing to God?"
- Shame content specifics (follow-up on Q10) — "What does it feel like in your body when you imagine someone discovering that about you?"

## VII. Medium Adaptation Notes

This Design Basis is medium-agnostic. When adapting to another medium, the questions, diagnostic targets, and annotations remain constant. Only the format, question type options, and delivery mechanism change.

**Tally.so (current implementation):**
Supports multi-section forms, long text, linear scale, yes/no with conditional follow-up, progress bar, custom welcome/thank you pages. Native Airtable integration for response routing. See separate Tally Build Guide.

**Airtable Form:**
Already in coaching stack. Responses route directly to client management base. Fewer question type options than Tally (primarily text, single select, rating). Conditional logic is limited. Best for short-form intake or as complement to Tally.

**PDF Fillable / Paper:**
Loses conditional logic (Q12 follow-up becomes a note on the form instead). Gains tangibility — some clients engage more honestly with pen and paper. Form should be printed as two-column where possible for readability. Annotations become a separate one-page coach guide.

**Google Form:**
Free, familiar to most clients. Limited question types (paragraph text, linear scale, multiple choice). No native conditional logic without Google Workspace workarounds. Responses to Google Sheets automatically. Viable secondary option.

**Verbal / Discovery Call:**
Questions become a structured conversation guide. Coach works through sections A-E in order, adjusting for conversational flow. The annotation layer becomes the coach's mental checklist during the call. Best for clients who resist written intake or for initial discovery calls before the written form is sent.

**Calendly Intake Questions:**
Limited to short answers only. Cannot replace full intake. Can capture Q1 (presenting problem) and Q12 (screening) at booking stage — reducing the form to 11 questions for the main intake.
