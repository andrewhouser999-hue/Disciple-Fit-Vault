# Covenant Identity — Mid-Engagement Snapshot Template
*Prepared by the coach for presentation in session 4-5. This is a client-facing document — it is read aloud together in session, not given to the client to read alone first. Built from the Intake Addendum (Part A and B) and Session Growth Notes (sessions 1-3 or 1-4). Target length: one to two pages. Tone: honoring, specific, and grounded in evidence — not motivational.*

---

## COACH PREPARATION NOTES
*(Remove this section before presenting to client)*

**Pull from these sources:**
- Intake Addendum Part A: A1 (presenting problem), A5 (God description), A8 (failure meaning), A9 (Q9 score), A10 (shame content), B2 (present-story statement)
- Session Growth Notes: Section 4 (language evolution), Section 3 (identity votes), Section 2 (growth moments), one-sentence summaries
- **Section 7 (Coach Observations) from all session Growth Notes** — pull the somatic, relational, and behavioral observations here rather than reconstructing from memory. These are the most compelling growth evidence precisely because they are recorded observations, not recollection. The "What I Have Noticed" section of the Snapshot is populated primarily from Section 7 across sessions.
- Re-administer Q9 in this session verbally before presenting the snapshot

**Presentation note:** Read the "Where You Started" column first — slowly, using the client's own words. Let it land. Then read the "Where You Are Now" column. Do not rush the comparison. The silence after is part of the work.

**What to watch for when presenting:** The client's body when they hear their own intake language. Surprise, grief, gratitude, recognition. Name what you see: *"I notice something happening in you as you hear that. What is it?"*

**Designed Alliance check:** Before presenting the Snapshot, review the Designed Alliance — the agreed-upon coaching approach and relational contract. Does the second half of the engagement match what was agreed? Has the client's readiness, phase, or material changed enough to warrant revisiting the agreement? The Snapshot session is a natural moment to re-contract if needed: after the client has received the evidence of what has happened, they are often ready to recommit to the remaining work with greater specificity.

> **Coach Note — Downward Q9 Protocol:** If the re-administered Q9 score is lower than the intake baseline, do not treat this as failure and do not skip past it. Two distinct patterns produce a lower score:
>
> **Pattern A — Increased honest self-perception:** The client's baseline score was inflated by a defended or dissociated starting point (common in Warrior and False Noble presentations). As the engagement has deepened, the client is now more honest about how much covenant truth is actually landing versus how much they were performing reception. A lower score here signals formation progress — the person is now close enough to their actual interior to report it accurately. Diagnostic tell: the client seems grounded and relatively calm despite the lower score; they can explain what changed in their self-assessment.
>
> **Pattern B — Regression or plateau:** The schema has reasserted, the installation work has not landed, or the client has hit significant external stressors. A lower score here is meaningful data, not to be resolved quickly. Diagnostic tell: the client seems discouraged, defended, or surprised by the lower score; they struggle to explain it or attribute it to failure.
>
> **How to hold it in the room:** Name the number without evaluating it. *"Your score now is [X], compared to [Y] at intake. Before we do anything with that, I'm curious what is true for you about that difference. What do you make of it?"* Do not interpret the change before the client does. Their interpretation is the diagnostic. Then, if Pattern A: name what you observe — *"I wonder if part of what's different is that you can actually see yourself more clearly now."* If Pattern B: shift the Snapshot presentation to hold the evidence of growth specifically — the language evolution, the identity votes, the coach observations — so the client encounters specific contrary evidence before leaving the session with only the number.

## Where This Is Going

*We are approximately halfway through our time together. Here is what I believe the second half holds:*

[Coach's honest assessment — not a promise, not a pep talk. What the remaining work looks like, what is still in motion, what you believe needs to happen next for the growth to hold and deepen.]

> **Coach Note — Slower-Than-Expected Movement:** If Phase 3 installation work has not yet begun at the mid-engagement Snapshot session, name it honestly rather than skipping past it. The Snapshot does not require Phase 3 evidence to be meaningful — Phase 1 and Phase 2 movement is real and worth naming. But the "Where This Is Going" section must be truthful about where the engagement actually is.
>
> If Phase 3 has not started: *"The second half holds the identity installation work — we haven't entered that yet. What that requires is what Phases 1 and 2 have been building: a clearer picture of the lie and schema we're working, and enough trust in this relationship that going toward it is possible. What I believe is ready now is..."*
>
> If the engagement arc is being extended: Name it directly in this section and in the session itself. *"We are taking more time than the standard arc. That is not a problem — it is an honest assessment of where the work needed to go. Here is what the extended arc holds..."* Do not use the "we are halfway through" language if the arc has extended; replace it with honest framing of where the engagement is and what the remaining structure looks like.
>
> The Snapshot's value is not in signaling progress toward a predetermined finish line; it is in making visible what has actually happened and what the honest second-half picture is. A Snapshot that inflates where the engagement is to maintain the client's confidence undermines the document's core function.
