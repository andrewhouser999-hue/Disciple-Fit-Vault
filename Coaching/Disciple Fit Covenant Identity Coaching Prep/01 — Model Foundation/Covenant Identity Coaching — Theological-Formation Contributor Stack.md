# Covenant Identity Coaching — Theological-Formation Contributor Stack

**What this is:** A twelve-layer practitioner reference answering the doctrinal and change-theory question underneath Covenant Identity Coaching — why the covenant framework is true, why Scripture's declarations carry the authority they claim, and how sanctification actually moves a person from cognitive assent to lived conviction. Compiled across multiple practitioner-formation research sessions (2026-05 through 2026-07); Layers 11–12 added 2026-07-21 following a /scrutinize pass.
**Who it's for:** Andrew, for his own formation and for building session tools with confidence in their doctrinal ground. Not client-facing in this form.
**Migrated from Claude's cross-session memory system 2026-07-21** — this content was originally built up as internal working notes across several sessions and had accumulated real practitioner value that belonged in the vault, not sitting outside it. This is now the canonical version; the memory copy is a short pointer only.

> **Scope note — this is a different system from the CIC session-tool author mapping.** `Covenant Identity — Author & Resource Reference.md` and `Covenant Identity — Author-Framework Mapping.md` (in `09 — Resource Library/`) map specific authors to specific session-tool fields (Lie Eliminator, Identity Installer, Holy Spirit Partnership, Intergenerational Transmission, Community Coaching). This stack answers a prior, deeper question — why the doctrine and change theory behind those tools is sound — for practitioner formation, not session deployment. The two lists barely overlap (Willard, Welch, Thompson, Allender, Tripp are the only shared names). A contributor absent from this stack (e.g. Brueggemann, Anderson, Billings, Ferguson, Barton, Payne, N.T. Wright, Fee) may still be a primary, actively-deployed source in the session-tool architecture — check those two documents, not just this one, before concluding a contributor is "not part of CIC."
>

Layer 1 and Layer 11 of this stack extend that document's Approach 1 and Approach 3 into the fuller doctrinal-formation and change-theory architecture; read that document first if the covenant-relationship exegesis itself is the open question, read this one if the question is which contributors ground the practice's change theory.

## Layer 1 — Covenant Structure (Biblical Theology)

### Peter Gentry & Stephen Wellum — *Kingdom Through Covenant* (2012, 2nd ed. 2018)
**Function:** Primary covenant structure anchor. "Progressive covenantism" — not Reformed covenant theology, not dispensationalism. Baptist/broadly evangelical.
**Key contribution:** Covenant as the means by which God establishes his kingdom and forms an image-bearing people. Grounds "covenant identity" as a genuine biblical category receivable across traditions.
**Use:** Practitioner formation. Not session-applicable.

### Graeme Goldsworthy — *Gospel and Kingdom* (1981) / *According to Plan* (1991)
**Function:** Accessible entry to the biblical-theology covenant narrative.
**Key contribution:** "God's people, in God's place, under God's rule" — covenant as kingdom structure. Client-translatable telos language.
**Use:** Practitioner formation + generates accessible covenant-identity language.

*The framework these two anchor is explicitly not Reformed Covenant Theology — no covenant of works/grace structure, no paedobaptism implications, no Westminster-specific law/gospel distinction. It is biblical theology's own covenant narrative, affirmed across Baptist, Anglican, and broadly evangelical traditions, traceable through the whole canon without requiring any single tradition's systematic commitments.*

**Function:** Answers the practical telos question — what does covenant-faithful life actually look like?
**Key contribution:** Covenant creates a missional image-bearing people. The *imago Dei* chapters provide the anthropological telos for coaching.

**Function:** Accessible shalom/sin framework.
**Key contribution:** Shalom = world as God designed it. Sin = vandalism of shalom. Most accessible bridge from covenant theology to client-facing telos language.
**Use:** Moderate direct applicability. More accessible than Bavinck or Wright.

## Layer 5 — Grace-Forward Change Theory

**Vault footprint check (confirmed 2026-07-11):** Keller, Bridges, Fitzpatrick, and Miller/Serge all appear in the CIC session-tool docs, but only inside the standing Tripp caution note ("pair with Fitzpatrick, Bridges, Keller, or Miller/Serge to balance the application layer" — `Author & Resource Reference.md` line 72, `Author-Framework Mapping.md` line 11). None has an independent tier assignment, dedicated entry, or worksheet seat there. Their claims below are practitioner-formation claims and stand on their own terms — but "high applicability" here does not mean they're operationally seated in CIC the way Ferguson, Robertson, Tripp, Welch, Thompson, or Allender are.

### Tim Keller — *The Prodigal God* (2008) / *Counterfeit Gods* (2009)
**Function:** Grace-forward covenant identity + idolatry diagnostic.
**Key contribution:** Same diagnostic precision as Tripp (counterfeit gods / idolatry as root) but entirely grace-framed. Elder-brother/younger-brother framework names both failure modes of covenant identity (legalism and license) as equally distortions. Gospel-driven identity formation.
**Use:** High direct applicability. *The Prodigal God* is short and client-translatable. *Counterfeit Gods* is the operational idolatry diagnostic.
**Compatibility:** High with Powlison, Bridges, Fitzpatrick, Willard.
**Note:** Presbyterian/Reformed background but these books are not tradition-dependent.

### Jerry Bridges — *The Discipline of Grace* (1994) / *The Gospel for Real Life* (2003)
**Function:** Grace-saturated practical sanctification. Corrects Tripp's legalistic tendency.
**Key contribution:** Sanctification is habitual application of the gospel to every area of life — not willpower applied to sin-identification. Daily return to gospel grace as the change mechanism.
**Use:** High direct applicability. Very accessible. Widely trusted across traditions. Should be on every practitioner's short reading list.

### Elyse Fitzpatrick — *Because He Loves Me* (2010) / *Comforts from the Cross* (2009)
**Function:** Grace-forward CCEF-adjacent practical sanctification.
**Key contribution:** Transformation through sustained contemplation of what Christ has accomplished — not through increased sin-identification effort. Holds CCEF diagnostic categories but tilts change mechanism toward grace reception.
**Use:** High applicability for both practitioner formation and client-facing language. Accessible and concrete.
**Why she matters:** Same tradition as Tripp, same diagnostic categories, explicitly grace-forward. Pairs with Tripp to correct the legalistic tendency without abandoning the framework.

### Jack Miller / Serge — Sonship Theology
**Function:** Explicit covenant-identity formation organized around gospel grace. Anti-legalism architecture.
**Key contribution:** "You are more sinful than you ever imagined, and more loved than you ever dared hope." Change through reception of the Father's love as adopted son/daughter in Christ — displaces performance-based false identity. The Sonship course materials are directly adaptable to coaching context.
**Use:** High for practitioner formation. The Sonship course is the primary artifact (not Miller's individual writings).

## Layer 6 — Operational Change Framework

### David Powlison — *How Does Sanctification Work?* (2017) / *Seeing with New Eyes* (2003)
**Function:** PRIMARY operational change theory. Most important CCEF contributor for this model.
**Key contribution:** *How Does Sanctification Work?* is specifically a sanctification change theory — the most direct available answer to "what produces movement in the Christian life?" More theologically precise than Tripp. More explicitly engaged with the psychology question. Written work is notably more grace-saturated than Tripp's video content.
**Use:** Moderate-high direct applicability. Should be read before building any operational session protocols.
**Replaces:** Collins as practical reference contributor.
**Priority-vs-deployment mismatch (confirmed 2026-07-11):** In the vault's actual CIC session-tool docs, Powlison has no dedicated entry, tier assignment, or worksheet seat — he appears exactly once, as a parenthetical inside the Tripp caution note ("Prefer Powlison when available"; `Author & Resource Reference.md` line 72, `Author-Framework Mapping.md` line 11). This is not a contradiction of any specific claim about Powlison, but it means the theoretical priority stated here (Powlison primary, Tripp secondary) is inverted in what's actually operationalized into the client-facing tools — the tools run on Tripp's framework, with the standard legalism caution attached, not Powlison's.

### Paul David Tripp — *Instruments in the Redeemer's Hands* (2002) / *How People Change* with Lane (2006)
**Function:** SECONDARY operational change framework. Accessible operationalization of Powlison's theory.
**Key contribution:** "Love, know, speak, do" counseling sequence. Heart/idolatry/Word framework in accessible form. *How People Change* is the shortest, most session-ready version.
**Use:** High practical applicability with explicit grace-forward pairing required. Also active in the CIC session-tool architecture (Lie Eliminator "Therefore I Will," Identity Installer "What I'm Putting Off" / "Today I Will") — the same legalism caution applies there and is flagged in both systems.
**STANDING CAUTION:** Perceived legalistic bent in video/popular content. Always pair with Keller, Bridges, or Fitzpatrick. Prefer Powlison for theoretical framework. This is a permanent flag, not a one-time note — apply it every time Tripp's framework is used, in practitioner formation or in any session tool.

## Layer 7 — Affections Theory

### Jonathan Edwards — *Religious Affections* (1746)
**Function:** Theological root of the affections work in the model.
**Key contribution:** Affections = the will's orientation toward objects of love. Twelve signs of genuine vs. counterfeit Spirit-wrought transformation. Diagnostic framework for distinguishing real covenant transformation from psychological improvement or religious performance.
**Use:** Practitioner formation essential. The affections framework is directly applicable. The twelve signs require translation work before functioning as coaching diagnostic criteria — they were written for revival discernment, not ongoing sanctification coaching. Translation not yet done; flag as needed.
**Caution:** Assumes regenerate client in community of faith. Several signs are ecclesially grounded.

### Thomas Chalmers — *The Expulsive Power of a New Affection* (1855, single sermon)
**Function:** Owen's mortification mechanism in the most accessible format available.
**Key contribution:** Sinful desires cannot be destroyed by direct assault (willpower); they can only be displaced by a stronger competing affection — love for God in Christ. This is the change mechanism stated in terms a client can grasp.
**Use:** Highest practical applicability of any theological source on the list. Short (one sermon, 30 minutes to read). Should generate a client-facing summary document.
**Why it's undervalued:** It's a single sermon, not a book — doesn't appear in bibliography-style lists.

## Layer 11 — Doctrine of Scripture's Covenant Authority

**Added 2026-07-21**, prompted by the question: why does a covenant declaration carry supreme, non-negotiable authority — not as an asserted doctrine, but structurally? This layer answers that, and it is new territory for this stack: none of Layers 1–10 address the authority-of-Scripture question directly. It extends Approach 3 (ANE Treaty Background Study) in `Developing a Biblical Understanding of Covenantal Relationship with God.md`, which already anchors Kline's treaty-form thesis for the covenant-relationship exegesis — this layer carries that same scholarship further, into the doctrine of canon and the mechanism of felt authority.

### George Mendenhall — *Law and Covenant in Israel and the Ancient Near East* (1954/1955)
**Function:** Founding scholarship. First systematic demonstration that Israel's Sinai covenant matches the formal structure of 2nd-millennium BC Hittite suzerain-vassal treaties (preamble naming the sovereign, historical prologue, stipulations, document/deposition clause, witnesses, blessings and curses).
**Key contribution:** Covenant in Scripture is not a relational metaphor layered onto the text — it is a specific, binding ANE legal-literary form. A vassal owes the sovereign's stipulations unconditional loyalty; the form itself carries no provision for the vassal negotiating terms.

**Quote** (sourced via Wikiquote, moderate-high confidence; confirm page number before formal citation):
> "The messages of the prophets are essentially indictments of Israel for breach of covenant." — *Law and Covenant in Israel and the Ancient Near East*, 1954

**Why this quote matters:** It reframes the entire prophetic corpus — not as isolated moral scolding, but as covenant-lawsuit prosecution: the prophets functioning as the treaty's own enforcement mechanism, indicting Israel for breach of binding terms. That is a direct, load-bearing illustration of "covenant declaration as non-negotiable stipulation," stated by Mendenhall himself, in the founding 1954/55 work.

A second quote, from a later, separate work — do not conflate with the 1954/55 founding scholarship above — is thematically useful for why covenant authority is *felt*, not only asserted:
> "The thunderstorm at the mountain powerfully reinforced the sacred quality and value of the covenant delivered there by Moses." — *Ancient Israel's Faith and History*, 2001 (posthumous collection; sourced via Wikiquote, page number TBD)

**Use:** Practitioner formation only.
**Caution — contested, not consensus:** Dennis McCarthy (*Treaty and Covenant*, 1963/1978) challenged how precisely Israelite texts match 2nd-millennium Hittite vs. 1st-millennium neo-Assyrian treaty forms — entangled with the Deuteronomy-dating debate. Present this as "an influential, substantially-evidenced argument," never as "settled fact." Overclaiming here is the fastest way to lose credibility with anyone who knows the OT-scholarship literature — which directly undercuts the purpose this layer exists to serve.

### Meredith Kline — *The Structure of Biblical Authority* (1972) / *Treaty of the Great King* (1963)
**Function:** Applies Mendenhall's treaty-form finding directly to the doctrine of canon. This is the source that gives "unchallengeable declaration" an actual textual/historical basis rather than an assertion.
**Key contribution:** The treaty-literary form is not incidental to Scripture's content — it is the form canon itself takes. Authority is structural, built into the genre, not merely doctrinal.

**Quotes** (search-verified across independent sources; confirm exact page numbers against primary text before formal citation):
> "Our thesis is then that whatever the individual names of the several major literary genres of the Old Testament, as adopted in the Old Testament their common surname is Covenant."

> "The origin of the Old Testament canon coincided with the founding of the kingdom of Israel by covenant at Sinai. All Scripture is covenantal, and the canonicity of all the Scripture is covenantal. Biblical canon is covenantal canon."

**Why this is the answer to the original question:** Scripture's declarations carry supreme, unchallengeable authority not because a doctrine asserts it but because the text's own literary form is a sovereign's covenant document to covenant people — a legal category with authority built into its structure, independent of whether the reader feels its weight.
**Use:** Practitioner formation only — dense, technical, not client-facing in this form.

### John Frame — *The Doctrine of the Word of God* (2010)
**Function:** Most direct contemporary theological statement of Scripture-as-covenant-document. Develops the "lordship attributes" — control, authority, presence — as what Scripture necessarily carries because God is covenant Lord.

**Quotes** (search-verified; page numbers TBD):
> "He [God] is our covenant Lord. So his word to us reflects his lordship attributes of control, authority, and presence."

> "The written Word is the covenant constitution of the people of God, and its authority is absolute, because the authority of its author is absolute."

> "God's relation to us is always covenantal, so we should expect a written, covenant document."

**Caution — Reformed-specific epistemology:** Frame's broader argument rests on a presuppositionalist/transcendental epistemology — a Reformed (Van Tillian) distinctive. The *covenant-document* claim itself stands on the tradition-neutral Mendenhall/Kline historical argument; his *epistemological justification* for why that claim should be treated as basic is tradition-specific. Use the covenant-document claim; do not import the apologetic method wholesale. (Consistent with this practice's governing position that the covenant framework is broadly evangelical biblical theology, not Reformed covenant theology.)
**Use:** Practitioner formation only.

### Kevin Vanhoozer — *First Theology* (2002) / *The Drama of Doctrine* (2005)
**Function:** Supplies the mechanism for why a spoken declaration is an act of authority in the moment it occurs, not merely a report about God — speech-act theory applied to Scripture.
**Key contribution:** God's presence and authority come "through speech acts," not spatially or causally. Scripture is divine communicative action, not only a set of true propositions.

**Quotes** (search-verified; page numbers as found — confirm before formal citation):
> "God's presence is neither spatial nor substantive but communicative" — it is "the presence of personal address and response." (*First Theology*, p. 90)

> God comes to the world "in, and as, Word" — through "speech acts." (*First Theology*, p. 149)

> "Scripture is the Word of God . . . and Scripture may become the Word of God." (Vanhoozer's revision of Barth's formula)

**Why this matters here specifically:** Explains why speaking or reading a declaration aloud is not "reciting an idea" but standing under a live act of address — closer to what "impact" actually requires than a purely propositional account of Scripture provides.
**Use:** Practitioner formation only.

### J.I. Packer — *"Fundamentalism" and the Word of God* (1958)
**Function:** Most accessible, least tradition-loaded case for Scripture's self-authenticating authority.

**Quotes** (search-verified; page numbers TBD):
> "To deny the normative authority of Scripture over the Church is to misconceive the nature of Christianity, and in effect, to deny the Lordship of Christ."

> "The proper ground for believing a thing is that God says it in His written Word, and a readiness to take God's word and accept what He asserts in the Bible is thus fundamental to faith."

Packer also cites Calvin's own formulation approvingly — believers come to be "unshakeably convinced—as though we saw God Himself present in it—that it has come to us, by the ministry of men, from God's very mouth" (Packer quoting Calvin's *Institutes* — verify book/chapter before citing).
**Use:** Most accessible source in this layer; flag separately if adapting this (not the denser sources above) toward advanced client-facing use is ever wanted. Not attempted now.

### STANDING CAUTION — Lament and Protest Are Covenantally Sanctioned, Not Exceptions to Authority

**Applies to:** All of Layer 11, every time it is used — a permanent caution, the same treatment given above to Tripp's legalism risk (Layer 6), not a one-time flag.

The treaty form's own language is that the vassal has no standing to contest the sovereign's stipulations — but the covenant relationship Scripture actually depicts is not a silent, submission-only vassal contract. Scripture itself sanctions protest, lament, and argument *within* the covenant:

- Abraham bargaining with God over Sodom (Gen 18:22–33)
- Moses interceding, and God relenting (Ex 32:9–14)
- The entire book of Job — extended protest that God does not rebuke as covenant treachery
- The lament Psalms (13, 22, 44, 88)
- Habakkuk's "How long, O Lord?" (Hab 1:2)

**Why this caution exists:** If "unchallengeable declaration" is modeled — even implicitly, in coaching posture — as "you don't get to question, grieve, or wrestle," the practice drifts from formation (cooperating with the Spirit's work of helping a client actually receive covenant truth) into correction (directively suppressing honest doubt). That is a theological and posture failure, not a tone issue.

**The distinction to hold:** **covenantally binding** (what's declared is true and does not require the client's ratification to be true) is not the same claim as **"you have no standing to bring grief or questions"** — Scripture's own covenant tradition makes explicit room for both. Any future session tool built from Layer 11 material that instructs a client to affirm a declaration as a way of shutting down their own doubt or grief, rather than making room for both together, would be this caution being violated, not a stylistic variant of the model. Watch this compounding with Tripp's legalistic tendency (Layer 6) if both are ever drawn on in the same tool unguarded — the two failure modes reinforce rather than offset each other.

## Layer 12 — Implicit Belief Reconsolidation Mechanism

**Added 2026-07-21.** Answers a different question than Layer 11: given that Scripture's authority is covenantally grounded, why does a client sometimes cognitively affirm it while still operating, under pressure, as though it were negotiable? This layer is mechanism, not doctrine — same subordinate, confirmatory status as Layer 8 (Welch/Thompson).

### Bruce Ecker, Robin Ticic, Laurel Hulley — *Unlocking the Emotional Brain* (2012)
**Function:** Explains why explicit teaching — better doctrine, a clearer argument — does not by itself update an implicit belief that is already operative and felt as true.
**Key contribution:** Memory reconsolidation research shows an implicit emotional schema updates only when (1) reactivated into felt, conscious experience, and (2) held simultaneously with a vivid, contradicting ("juxtaposition") experience. Teaching stacked on top of an unaddressed implicit schema adds a second, competing belief — it does not erase the first. That is the mechanism behind a client affirming sovereignty cognitively while still, under pressure, operating from an implicit belief that God's claims are negotiable.

**Quotes** (independently verified — confirmed via Goodreads' dedicated quotes page in addition to general search, high confidence on wording; page numbers TBD):
> "Emotional memory converts the past into an expectation of the future, without our awareness, and that is both a blessing and a curse."

> "Memory research has established that learnings accompanied by strong emotion form neural circuits in subcortical implicit memory that are exceptionally durable, normally lasting a lifetime."

> "We have seen that when an emotional learning or schema is the underlying cause of a therapy client's presenting symptom, the schema can be retrieved into direct, explicit experience and then profoundly unlearned and dissolved by the same sequence of experiences that neuroscientists identified in reconsolidation research."

**Caution 1 — category error risk (wound vs. formation gap):** Ecker's model assumes the implicit schema formed through an actual adverse or attachment-based experience. If a client's disconnect is simply an ordinary discipleship gap — never taught, never practiced — this framework is the wrong tool; Willard's VIM (Layer 4) is sufficient and correct. Diagnose which is present before reaching for this framework. Treating every under-formed conviction as a wound to be reconsolidated over-pathologizes normal formation gaps.
**Caution 2 — scope guardrail, same treatment as Allender (Layer 9):** The *theory* (why explicit teaching doesn't reach implicit belief) is safe practitioner-formation background. The *protocol* (deliberately activating a loaded schema and holding it against disconfirming material) is trauma-processing technique, not a coaching move — do not self-administer as a session technique without the same referral-posture guardrail already active for Allender's tools. Import the explanation; do not import the clinical protocol.
**Caution 3 — the Spirit is the agent, not the technique:** Ecker's framework is purely mechanistic neuroscience with no theological agent built in. In coaching, the natural mechanism it describes is something the Spirit works *through*, not something a technique causes. Treating "we did the reconsolidation move" as what changed the client's belief is the tool-dependence failure mode this practice's governing mechanism (Spirit as primary agent; coach and tools create conditions, not transformation) explicitly warns against.

### Robert Kegan & Lisa Lahey — *Immunity to Change* (2009)
**Function:** Coaching-discipline diagnostic (not clinical) for locating the specific competing commitment protecting an implicit belief — the correct first move, before Ecker's mechanism is even relevant.
**Key contribution:** A stated commitment ("I believe God's Word is authoritative") can coexist with a hidden competing commitment protecting something the client doesn't want to lose. Kegan's image: "one foot on the gas, the change goal, and one on the brakes[,] the hidden one." Beneath the competing commitment sits a "Big Assumption" the client "behave[s] like [is] absolutely, irrefutably true," usually untested.
**Use:** High direct applicability, squarely coaching-discipline territory — no category-error risk the way Ecker carries one. Apply first: surface what the client believes they would lose, or lose control of, if God's declaration were actually non-negotiable. That is usually where the implicit resistance lives, and surfacing it is coaching's job, not therapy's.

**Sequencing note — this does not require a new technique:** Kegan/Lahey surfaces the competing commitment (coaching-safe); Gendlin's felt-sense work — already seated in the CIC session-tool architecture's Stage 4b Route B — is where a declaration lands somatically. Ecker's theory explains *why* that matters; it does not call for adopting a new clinical protocol on top of tools already in the system.
