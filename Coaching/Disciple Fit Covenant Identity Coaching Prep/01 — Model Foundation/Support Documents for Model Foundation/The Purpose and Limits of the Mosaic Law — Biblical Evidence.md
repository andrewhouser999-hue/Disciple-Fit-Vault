# The Purpose and Limits of the Mosaic Law — Biblical Evidence

**Tags:** `[BIBLE-FRAMEWORK]` `[CURRICULUM]`
**Date:** 2026-05-09

> **Summary:** Supporting research for the 2 Corinthians 3 footnote in the Biblical Case document. Traces the biblical evidence — from Exodus through the Prophets to Paul — for the argument that the Mosaic law exposes human inability to keep it and points forward to the need for inner transformation. The explicit theological argument is largely Pauline; the OT supplies the narrative pattern and prophetic anticipation.

## Interpretive Caution Up Front

The explicit theological claim that the Mosaic law's *purpose* includes demonstrating human inability to keep it is primarily a New Testament argument — developed especially by Paul. The Old Testament texts supply the narrative and prophetic foundation for that claim; they do not state it directly. These must be kept distinct.

---

## What Exodus Contains

### Exodus 19:5-8 + Exodus 32 — The Narrative Sequence as Argument

Israel accepts the covenant with full confidence: "All that the LORD has spoken we will do" (19:8). The elaborate law-giving of chapters 20-31 follows. Then, while Moses is still on the mountain receiving it, Israel constructs and worships the golden calf (chapter 32) — breaking the first two commandments before the tablets reach the ground.

The narrative sequence is the argument. The text does not say "see, you cannot keep this" — but the architecture shows it. Israel's immediate and catastrophic failure is not incidental; it is the first data point in a pattern the rest of the OT will confirm.

### Exodus 20:20 — The Law as Test

> "Moses said to the people, 'Do not fear, for God has come to test you, that the fear of him may be before you, that you may not sin.'"

The law is given explicitly as a *test* (*nasah*). This opens the door to the law's diagnostic function — but the frame here is positive: the test is meant to produce the fear that prevents sin, not simply to expose inevitable failure. The Reformers built the "pedagogical use of the law" on texts like this, but that is a theological extension of what the text explicitly states.

---

## Deuteronomy — The Pentateuch's Own Acknowledgment

### Deuteronomy 29:4 — The Missing Internal Capacity

> "But to this day the LORD has not given you a heart to understand or eyes to see or ears to hear."

Moses says this after forty years of signs, wonders, covenant instruction, and direct divine provision. Israel still lacks the internal capacity to understand what they have witnessed. This is one of the most remarkable statements in the Pentateuch: the covenant framework, by itself, has not produced the inner transformation it requires.

### Deuteronomy 30:6 — Heart Circumcision as Future Promise

> "And the LORD your God will circumcise your heart and the heart of your offspring, so that you will love the LORD your God with all your heart and with all your soul, that you may live."

If God must circumcise the heart in the future to produce covenant obedience, the present uncircumcised heart cannot produce it. The Mosaic covenant is implicitly shown to be insufficient — not because the law is flawed, but because the heart it addresses is not yet transformed.

### Deuteronomy 31:16-21 — God Predicts Covenant Breaking

> "Behold, you are about to lie down with your fathers. Then this people will rise and whore after the foreign gods among them... they will forsake me and break my covenant that I have made with them."

God tells Moses before his death that Israel *will* break the covenant — stated as certainty, not warning. Moses is instructed to write the song of Deuteronomy 32 as a witness *against* Israel for when this occurs (31:19-21). The law is not given with the expectation that it will succeed in producing sustained covenant faithfulness.

---

## The Prophetic Payoff — Jeremiah 31:31-34

> "Behold, the days are coming, declares the LORD, when I will make a new covenant with the house of Israel and the house of Judah, not like the covenant that I made with their fathers on the day when I took them by the hand to bring them out of the land of Egypt, my covenant that they broke..."

Jeremiah gives the explicit rationale for the new covenant: Israel broke the old one. The defining difference of the new covenant is internalization — "I will put my law within them and write it on their hearts." The old covenant was external (stone tablets) and could not produce what it required because the heart remained untransformed. The new covenant addresses exactly what Deuteronomy 29:4 named as the missing element.

This is the OT's own most direct statement that the Mosaic framework had a structural limitation — not a flaw in the law itself, but in the unregenerate heart it addressed.

---

## Paul's Explicit Theological Argument

Paul draws out what the OT narrative implies but does not fully state:

- **Romans 3:20** — "through the law comes knowledge of sin"
- **Romans 7:7** — "I would not have known what it is to covet if the law had not said, 'You shall not covet'"
- **Galatians 3:19** — "Why then the law? It was added because of transgressions, until the offspring should come to whom the promise had been made"
- **Galatians 3:24** — "the law was our guardian until Christ came, in order that we might be justified by faith"

Paul reads the Exodus narrative and Deuteronomic pattern and draws an explicit theological conclusion: the law's purpose includes exposing sin and human inability, functioning as a guardian (*paidagōgos*) that escorts Israel to Christ. This is not a contradiction of the OT but its canonical interpretation.

---

## The Full Argument in Sequence

1. **Exodus narrative** — Israel fails immediately and catastrophically (golden calf)
2. **Deuteronomy prediction** — Moses declares failure inevitable; God states it as certain
3. **Jeremiah promise** — new covenant required because the old was broken; internalization is the solution
4. **Paul's theology** — the law was the schoolmaster that drove Israel — and humanity — to Christ

The sequence is defensible and coherent. It must be presented in this order. No single Exodus proof text carries the full weight of the argument.
