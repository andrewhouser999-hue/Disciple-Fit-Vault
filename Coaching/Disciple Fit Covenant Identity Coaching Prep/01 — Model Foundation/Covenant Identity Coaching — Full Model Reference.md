# Covenant Identity Coaching — Full Model Reference
*Comprehensive reference for coach development. All technical terms are explained alongside plain-language descriptions. This document provides the detailed framework, psychological integration, and session structure. For the governing definition, three-discipline architecture, contributor table, boundary testing, and precision levels, see the Practice Definition document.*

---

## I. What It Is — The Core Claim

Covenant Identity Coaching is a coaching model in which:

- **Covenant** is the organizing theological structure — not a metaphor or a decorative biblical theme, but the actual framework for understanding the client's identity, the coaching relationship, and the change process itself
- **Identity** is the primary terrain — the central work is not behavior change but *identity integration* (growing into who the client already is before God, closing the gap between declared reality and lived experience)
- **Coaching** is the methodology — client-agenda-driven, generative (forward-focused and possibility-oriented), with the coach as evoker and challenger rather than teacher or director
- **Integration** means genuine synthesis — not Bible verses bolted onto psychology, but a unified *epistemology* (framework for how we know what we know about human nature) in which Scripture governs the understanding of who people are and psychology provides descriptive precision for what we observe

**The core claim:** The client's deepest problem is almost never a behavior problem or even a motivation problem. It is an identity problem — a failure to live from who they already are in covenant with God. The coaching work is closing the gap between declared covenant identity and lived, felt, operational identity.

**Three contributing disciplines — how they unite:**
This model draws from three disciplines in a specific relationship. None is sufficient alone:
- **Spiritual Formation** governs the telos and posture — the client is being *formed* by the Spirit toward restored image-bearing; the coach cooperates with what the Spirit is already doing; the practice's tools function as formation disciplines creating conditions for transformation, not causing it
- **Biblical Counseling** provides diagnostic precision — idolatry and disordered worship as the root, the heart as the center of functioning, affections / false identity / character wounds as the primary diagnostic tools
- **Christian Life Coaching** provides the relational container — forward-oriented partnership, client agency within God's sovereignty, coaching competencies, and structured engagement

**Covenant Identity** provides the specific content of what formation is moving toward: restored image-bearing as God's covenant person in Christ, with false identity displaced and true identity received, practiced, and lived.

**Model architecture — how the layers relate:**
These elements are not co-equal. The **biblical framework is the governing lens** — the epistemological authority, the entry point, and the interpretive structure for all the work. **Spiritual formation posture** is the practitioner's governing orientation — cooperating with what the Spirit is already doing, not producing transformation. **Coaching methodology is the delivery vehicle**, operating within and through that framework. **CBT-informed tools are deployed selectively** — specifically when cognitive distortions are blocking the client's reception of covenant truth. The sequence: enter through the biblical framework and coach from within a spiritual formation posture; reach for CBT tools when the work requires deeper psychological integration. CBT serves the framework; it does not lead it.

## II. Why Covenant Specifically — Not Just "Biblical"

"Biblical coaching" is a broad claim that could mean many things. The *covenant* frame is specific and carries distinctive theological weight. It is worth understanding why covenant — and not just "biblical truth" or "Christian worldview" — is the organizing structure.

### What Covenant Is

Ancient Near Eastern *suzerainty treaties* (the formal covenant structure used between a sovereign king and a vassal nation — and the form God repeatedly uses with His people in Scripture) followed a consistent structure:

1. **Preamble** — Who the covenant-maker is ("I am the LORD your God")
2. **Historical prologue** — What the sovereign has *already done* for the vassal — the record of prior grace that grounds the entire relationship
3. **Stipulations** — How the vassal is to live *in light of* what has been done for them
4. **Blessings and curses** — What follows from faithfulness or unfaithfulness
5. **Witnesses and renewal provisions**

The critical structural feature — the one that determines everything about this coaching model — is that **stipulations always follow the historical prologue.** Obligation follows gift. Identity is declared before behavior is required.

The Ten Commandments are the clearest example: "I am the LORD your God who brought you out of Egypt, out of the house of slavery. *You shall*..." The indicative (statement of who you are and what has been done) precedes the imperative (call to action). This is the grammar of covenant — and it is the structural opposite of every performance-based religious system.

### What This Means for Coaching

- The client's identity is *given before it is grown*
- Belonging precedes behavior — the client does not earn their way into covenant identity through performance
- The coaching question is not secular coaching's "Who do you want to become?" It is: "Who has God already declared you to be — and what is standing between you and living from that?"

## III. The Indicative / Imperative Grammar — The Structural Spine

*Indicative* — a grammatical term for a statement of what is true: "You are a new creation."
*Imperative* — a grammatical term for a command or call to action: "Put on the new self."

This distinction is the hinge on which the entire model turns.

| Grammar | Form | Example |
|---------|------|---------|
| **Indicative** | Statement of what is already true | "You are a new creation" (2 Cor 5:17) |
| **Imperative** | Call to action based on what is true | "Put on the new self" (Eph 4:24) |

In Scripture, the imperative is always grounded in and made possible by the indicative. The command is "Be who you are" — an invitation to inhabit a reality already established, not to construct a new one through effort.

**Legalism reverses this grammar:** Perform → then receive identity and belonging. "Do enough and you will be acceptable." This produces the shame cycle — the treadmill — the persistent sense of never being enough regardless of how much is accomplished. It is the structural error underlying most Christian stagnation.

**Covenant Identity Coaching restores the grammar:** Identity declared → belonging established → behavior flows from that ground.

Every client presenting with stuck patterns, persistent shame, compulsive behavior, chronic under-performance, or spiritual dryness is — at some level — operating from reversed grammar. They are trying to perform their way into an identity they haven't yet received. The coaching work is identifying where the grammar flipped, what caused it to flip, and restoring the correct order.

## IV. The Epistemological Foundation — What Makes This Different

*Epistemology* — the branch of philosophy concerned with how we know what we know; the framework that determines what counts as true and authoritative.

Three types of Christian coaching can be distinguished by their epistemology:

**Type 1 — Secular coaching with Christian assumptions:**
The coaching methodology is entirely secular. Biblical worldview operates in the background but doesn't structurally shape the work. Scripture is occasionally referenced but does not function as an authoritative reference point. The client's own preferences, values, and goals are the sole standard for what counts as good or true. God is in the room in the coach's heart — but not in the architecture.

**Type 2 — Directive biblical coaching (the *nouthetic* tradition — from the Greek *noutheteo*, meaning "to admonish" or "to confront with truth"):**
Scripture is authoritative and prescriptive. The coach or counselor is the expert who applies biblical truth to the client's situation. The client is primarily receptive. This can produce good outcomes in specific contexts but violates the core architecture of coaching methodology — specifically, the principles of client self-determination and evocative (drawing out rather than pushing in) practice.

**Type 3 — Covenant Identity Coaching:**
The client is the authority on their *experience.* God is the authority on the client's *identity.* These are distinct — and both are fully honored. The coach holds both simultaneously: respecting the client's lived experience without reducing identity to it, and holding covenant truth as the reference point without weaponizing it against the client's reality.

### What This Looks Like in Practice

When a client says: *"I feel completely worthless."*

- **Type 1 coach:** Validates the feeling and explores it curiously. Does not bring covenant truth. May help the client understand the feeling but has no authoritative reference point beyond the client's own perception. The feeling is the final word.

- **Type 2 coach:** Corrects the feeling with Scripture — "But the Bible says you're fearfully and wonderfully made!" Well-intentioned, often sincere. Usually produces either shutdown or *performance agreement* (the client learns to say the right thing while feeling the same thing). The theology overrides the experience rather than engaging it.

- **Covenant Identity Coach:** Honors the feeling as real data ("That sounds like a deep and familiar weight — tell me about it"). Gets genuinely curious about its source. Holds covenant truth as the orienting frame throughout — not as a weapon against the feeling, but as the larger reality the feeling is being held inside of: "I'm curious — when you feel that way, what does it feel like about who God says you are?" Works in the gap between the felt experience and the declared reality.

The gap between felt identity and covenant identity is not resolved by dismissing either pole. It is the primary coaching terrain.

## V. The Covenant Identity Architecture — What the Client Has Actually Received

Covenant identity is not a single statement ("you're loved by God"). It is a layered architecture — multiple declared realities, each one addressing a specific wound or *schema* (deeply held belief about self, others, and the world — see Section VI). Understanding these layers allows the coach to work precisely rather than generically.

| Layer | Declaration | Schema It Counters |
|-------|------------|-------------------|
| **Imago Dei** (Gen 1:26-27) — "image of God" | You bear the image of God. Your worth is ontological (belonging to your very being, not contingent on anything you do), not earned. | Defectiveness / Shame — "I am fundamentally flawed" |
| **Known Fully and Chosen** (Ps 139, Rom 8:29) | God sees everything about you — including what you hide — and chose you anyway. | Shame + Abandonment — "If people really knew me, they would leave" |
| **New Creation** (2 Cor 5:17) | The old is gone. The new has come. This is positional fact (your legal standing before God), not aspiration. | Failure / Permanent Defectiveness — "I'll never really change" |
| **Adopted Sonship / Daughtership** (Rom 8:15) | You are not a servant earning favor. You are a child with full inheritance rights. | Subjugation / Conditional Worth — "My needs don't matter; I have to earn my place" |
| **Co-heir with Christ** (Rom 8:17) | The full inheritance of Christ is shared with you. | Deprivation / Scarcity — "There's never enough; I'll be left without" |
| **Indwelt by the Spirit** (1 Cor 6:19) | The very presence of God lives in you. You are not abandoned to your own resources. | Abandonment / Isolation — "I am ultimately alone" |
| **Seated in Heavenly Places** (Eph 2:6) | Your current status before God is secure and established. This is not future — it is now. | Vulnerability / Instability — "The ground could disappear at any moment" |
| **Loved as the Father Loves the Son** (John 15:9) | The quality and depth of love directed at you equals the love between the Father and Son. This is not metaphor — it is the direct statement of Jesus. | Emotional Deprivation — "No one will ever truly see or meet my needs" |
| **Ambassador / Sent One** (2 Cor 5:20) | You carry God's presence and message into specific people and places. You are not accidental. | Meaninglessness / Purposelessness — "My life doesn't matter; nothing I do counts" |
| **Being Conformed to Christ's Image** (Rom 8:29) | You are not a finished product. You are a person in a divinely-superintended (God-overseen and God-driven) formation process. | Failure / Impatience with growth — "I should be further along; I'm a disappointment" |

Each layer is not simply a cognitive correction — a better thought to replace a bad thought. It is a *covenant declaration* — an ontological (concerning the nature of being itself) claim about who the client is in reality, regardless of how it currently feels.

**The diagnostic work:** Identify which layers the client has received and integrated (they can feel the truth of it, not just recite it), which are cognitively assented to but not *embodied* (they believe it with their mind but it doesn't reach their gut or their behavior), and which are actively disbelieved or unfelt (the declaration produces no resonance, or produces fear and resistance).

## VI. The Psychological Integration — Where the Frameworks Map

One of the central convictions of this model is that the psychological frameworks do not contradict Scripture — they describe the same underlying human reality from different vantage points. Scripture names these realities theologically; psychology provides *descriptive precision* (specific language for mechanisms, patterns, and processes) that helps us understand what we're working with.

---

### Narrative Identity Theory (McAdams)

*Narrative identity* — the psychological theory that humans form a sense of self by constructing an internalized, evolving story about who they are, where they came from, and where they're going. Identity is not just a list of traits; it is a narrative with characters, plot, and meaning.

Humans form identity by integrating past, present, and anticipated future into a coherent story. The presenting problem in coaching is almost always a *narrative problem* — a story being told about oneself that is either false (a lie agreed to) or incomplete (missing the covenant chapter).

McAdams identifies two narrative patterns relevant to this work:
- ***Narrative contamination*** — a life story dominated by decline, victimhood, failure, or shame. The story is structurally stuck because the dominant plot is loss.
- ***Redemptive narrative*** — a story that moves *through* suffering toward meaning, growth, or contribution. The bad thing happened but it led somewhere.

The biblical meta-narrative (the overarching story of Scripture) is the original redemptive narrative: *creation → fall → redemption → restoration.* The client's personal story is not a standalone — it is a chapter in that larger story. One of the most powerful coaching moves is helping a client locate their personal story within the larger redemptive arc.

**Coaching application:** Help the client identify the current story they're telling. Ask: Where does that story intersect with the larger one? What chapter are they actually in? What has already been redeemed that they haven't named? What does the story look like if God's covenant faithfulness is a character in it?

### Schema Therapy (Young)

*Early maladaptive schemas* — "schema" comes from Greek, meaning "form" or "shape." In psychology, schemas are deep organizing patterns. Early maladaptive schemas (EMS) are deeply held beliefs and emotional patterns about self, others, and the world, formed in childhood in response to unmet core needs. They feel like facts rather than beliefs — "this is just how things are" — and they resist change precisely because challenging them feels like challenging reality itself.

The schemas most relevant to identity work:

- **Defectiveness/Shame** — "I am fundamentally flawed at the core. If people knew the real me, they would reject me."
- **Abandonment/Instability** — "The people I depend on will ultimately leave or be unavailable."
- **Subjugation** — "My own needs, wants, and feelings don't matter. I must suppress them to avoid rejection."
- **Failure** — "I have failed, am currently failing, and will inevitably fail at anything important."
- **Emotional Deprivation** — "No one will ever truly understand or meet my emotional needs."
- **Unrelenting Standards** — "I must achieve constantly and at the highest level, or I am worthless."

These schemas are the psychological mechanism of what Paul calls *strongholds* (2 Corinthians 10:4-5 — *ochyrōma* in Greek, literally "a fortified place, a fortress"). They are deeply entrenched cognitive-emotional-behavioral patterns that resist surface-level intervention. Paul's language of "taking every thought captive to obey Christ" (v.5) is essentially *cognitive restructuring* (see CBT below) at the schema level — systematically identifying and dismantling beliefs that oppose covenant truth.

The covenant identity declarations in Section V are structurally opposed to each schema. This is not *proof-texting* (using Bible verses in isolation to score points) — it is theological schema replacement: displacing the operational belief with the covenant reality, practiced through repetition and embodiment until it becomes the new default.

**The schema replacement process:**
1. Identify the active schema — name it clearly
2. Name its origin — "Where did you first learn that about yourself? When did that feel true for the first time?"
3. Name the covenant truth that directly contradicts it — specifically, not generically
4. Build embodied practice of living from the covenant truth
5. Track evidence — "Where is the covenant truth proving itself true in your actual experience?"

### Internal Family Systems (Schwartz) — Parts Work

*Internal Family Systems (IFS)* — a therapeutic model developed by Richard Schwartz that views the mind as containing multiple distinct sub-personalities or "parts," each with their own perspectives, feelings, and roles. The goal is to help all parts relate to the core *Self* (capital S) as their leader.

IFS identifies:
- **The Self** (capital S) — the undamaged, untraumatized, undefended core of the person. In IFS language, the Self has qualities like curiosity, calm, clarity, compassion, courage, creativity, confidence, and connectedness. This is not constructed — it is revealed when the parts stand aside.
- **Protectors** — parts that developed to keep the person safe. *Managers* (proactive protectors that try to control situations and environments) and *firefighters* (reactive protectors that use impulsive or numbing behaviors to suppress pain when it breaks through).
- **Exiles** — wounded, often young parts that carry pain, shame, fear, or grief from past experiences. Protectors work to keep these parts locked away and hidden.

**The theological mapping:**
- The Self maps onto the *imago Dei* — the original, unmarred image of God within the person, never fully extinguished even in fallenness. It is what remains of who God made them when the protective structures step aside.
- The exiles map onto the wounded inner self — the places of unmet need, grief, and shame that have been locked away, often since childhood.
- The protectors (managers) map onto what Paul calls the *flesh* (Greek: *sarx*) patterns in Galatians 5 — adaptive strategies that served survival in a broken world but now obstruct the life of the Spirit.
- The firefighters map onto compulsive patterns — impulsive or numbing responses to exile pain breaking through.

**The IFS goal:** Unburden the exiles and help all parts relate to the Self as their internal leader.
**The theological goal:** Put off the old self; put on the new creation (Galatians 2:20, Ephesians 4:22-24).
Both are describing the same interior movement from different vantage points.

**Coaching application:** When a client is stuck in a pattern they genuinely want to change, IFS language gives them nonjudgmental access to their interior: "What part of you is driving that? What is that part trying to protect? What would it need in order to trust the new-creation self to lead instead?"

### Attachment Theory (Bowlby, Ainsworth)

*Attachment theory* — developed by John Bowlby and Mary Ainsworth, this framework describes the psychological need for a *secure base* (a reliable, responsive caregiver from whom a child can explore the world knowing they can return for safety) and how early relational experiences shape lifelong patterns of relating to others and regulating emotion.

The three primary adult attachment styles relevant to coaching:

**Secure attachment:** The person experienced consistent, responsive care. They can use the coaching relationship without shame, receive challenge as care rather than threat, and report non-follow-through honestly without catastrophizing. They can be held without feeling controlled.

**Anxious attachment:** The person experienced inconsistent care — sometimes available, sometimes not. They tend to over-commit (saying yes to please the coach rather than because they genuinely want to), work hard for approval, catastrophize failure, and experience the accountability check-in as an evaluation of their worth. *Coaching adjustment:* Slow down commitment-making. Increase warmth. Probe: "Do you actually want this, or does part of you just want me to be satisfied?"

**Avoidant attachment:** The person learned that depending on others was unsafe or unreliable, so they became self-sufficient and emotionally closed. They tend to minimize non-follow-through, disengage from accountability conversations, commit verbally without building structures, and become increasingly opaque about what's actually happening for them. *Coaching adjustment:* More accountability pressure deepens avoidance. Name the pattern gently instead: "I notice you don't say much about what happened between sessions. I'm curious about that." Build safety before building structure.

**The theological dimension:** The client's experience of God is almost always shaped by their early attachment experiences. This is called *God-representation* — the internal, often unconscious image of God that operates emotionally and relationally, distinct from theological belief. A client with avoidant attachment often experiences God as distant and uninterested. An anxiously attached client often experiences God as unpredictable and easily disappointed. These experiential templates operate *beneath* theological belief — the client may sincerely affirm God's love while emotionally experiencing abandonment.

The covenant identity contains the authoritative God-representation: the father who runs toward the prodigal while he's still a long way off, the shepherd who leaves the ninety-nine, the God who is "closer than our next breath" (Psalm 139:7-10). Covenant Identity Coaching identifies where the client's experiential God-representation diverges from the revealed covenant God, and builds practices that create new attachment experiences with God: *contemplative prayer* (slow, receptive, attentive prayer that cultivates felt awareness of God's presence), lament (see Section VIII), Ignatian imagination (entering Scripture scenes with imagination to encounter the living Christ), and deep Psalm engagement.

### CBT — Cognitive Behavioral Therapy

*CBT* — Cognitive Behavioral Therapy, developed by Aaron Beck. Based on the principle that thoughts (cognitions), feelings, and behaviors are interconnected — changing one influences the others. The *ABC model*: Activating event → Belief (automatic thought about the event) → Consequence (emotional and behavioral response). The belief, not the event itself, drives the consequence.

The biblical parallel is direct: "As a man thinks in his heart, so is he" (Proverbs 23:7 KJV). "Be transformed by the renewing of your mind" (Romans 12:2) — Paul's language here names cognitive transformation as the mechanism of spiritual formation. The mind (*nous* in Greek — the faculty of perception, understanding, and moral reasoning) is renewed; the transformation follows.

The Covenant Identity Coaching application of CBT is not merely positive self-talk or thought replacement. It is:
1. Identifying the *automatic thoughts* (beliefs that fire instantly and beneath conscious awareness in response to triggers) that contradict covenant identity
2. Tracing them to the underlying schema
3. Replacing the operational belief with the specific covenant declaration — not generically, but precisely
4. Practicing the replacement through repetition, embodiment, and lived experience until the new belief becomes the automatic default

Cognitive assent alone (agreeing that a verse is theologically true) does not produce transformation. The brain requires repetition and *embodied experience* (felt, physical, behavioral engagement with the truth) to rewire.

### Immunity to Change (Kegan & Lahey)

*Immunity to Change* — a framework developed by Robert Kegan and Lisa Laskow Lahey explaining why capable, motivated people fail to follow through on commitments they genuinely mean to keep. The central insight: non-follow-through is often not a motivation problem. It is a *competing commitment* problem — a hidden, protective commitment that is equally or more powerful than the stated commitment, and completely invisible without structured inquiry.

**The Immunity to Change Map:**

| Column | Question | Example |
|--------|----------|---------|
| 1. Improvement Goal | What do I want to change? | Be more vulnerable in relationships |
| 2. Doing / Not Doing | What behaviors work against this? | Staying surface-level, deflecting intimacy with humor |
| 3. Hidden Competing Commitment | What am I committed to that this behavior protects? | Not being seen as needy; keeping control of how I'm perceived |
| 4. Big Assumption | What would have to be true for the competing commitment to make sense? | "If I'm truly known, I will be rejected or used against me" |

The competing commitment and the big assumption are not irrational. They are the person's *immune system* — protective structures developed for good reasons in earlier contexts where the assumption was actually true (or felt true). The system is just running in a context where it no longer serves.

**The theological extension:** The big assumption in Column 4 is almost always an operational lie — a belief formed in wound and held as reality. In Covenant Identity Coaching, the map goes one level deeper than standard ITC work: not just "Is this assumption true?" but "What would it mean about who God is and who you are *if this assumption were wrong?*" The theological dimension of the assumption is often the load-bearing structure. Addressing it only psychologically leaves the theological root intact.

When a client repeatedly breaks the same commitment, stop re-assigning it. Immunity to Change is almost certainly operating. The question is no longer "What will you do?" — it is "What hidden commitment is being served by not doing this?"

### Kegan's Orders of Mind — Developmental Accountability

Beyond Immunity to Change, Kegan's full developmental model reveals that a client's *developmental stage* (the complexity of their meaning-making system — how they construct and hold their own identity, values, and relationships) determines what kind of accountability and coaching will actually be accessible to them.

**The three most relevant Orders:**

**3rd Order — The Socialized Mind (majority of adults):**
Identity and accountability are derived from external expectations, relationships, and social roles. "I keep my commitment because you're watching, because it matters to my community, because I don't want to disappoint you." The self is constituted by its relationships and the approval of significant others. Accountability works through relational connection and social contract. *Risk in coaching:* May commit to things they don't genuinely want in order to maintain the coaching relationship. The commitment is driven by the relationship, not by internal values.

**4th Order — The Self-Authoring Mind (roughly 35-40% of adults):**
Identity is generated from an internally authored value system — the person has a self that stands apart from their relationships and can evaluate them. "I keep my commitment because I decided it matters to me." Accountability works through values alignment and self-directed reflection. *Risk in coaching:* Can become rigid, overconfident in their own framework, resistant to challenge.

**5th Order — The Self-Transforming Mind (rare):**
Holds multiple frameworks simultaneously, aware of the limits of any single system including their own. Accountability becomes dialectical — commitments are held thoughtfully and revisited.

**Practical application:** A Socialized Mind (3rd Order) client pushed too quickly toward purely autonomous self-determination may feel unmoored — they need the *relational* accountability structure as much as the internal one. The coaching relationship itself IS the accountability. A Self-Authoring Mind (4th Order) client responds well to values-based reflection and internal structures. Meeting the actual developmental stage present — rather than the stage you wish were present — is a key coaching competency.

## VII. The Primary Coaching Terrain — Working the Gap

The central territory of Covenant Identity Coaching is the gap between:

**Declared covenant identity** ← → **Felt, lived, operational identity**

**The Affections Layer — first diagnostic step:**
Before working the gap directly, the coach surfaces the affections layer: what the client is prioritizing, trusting, and relying on outside of God. Affections reveal functional identity — who the client is actually living from beneath their presenting story. Trust is integral to affections: what a person depends on for security, significance, or identity is the functional god the false identity serves. The affections picture feeds directly into gap identification — the false identity revealed by the affections is the identity the coaching then brings covenant truth against.

Diagnostic sequence: **Affections discovery → false identity named → covenant truth spoken → CBT tools (if distortion blocks reception).**

See: *Affections Discovery Process — Affections, Trust & Priorities* for the full practitioner guide, question bank, and diagnostic signals.

This gap is not a sign of failure or weak faith. It is the normal, expected condition of the Christian life in the *already/not yet* (the theological phrase for the tension between what Christ has already accomplished — our positional identity — and what is not yet fully realized in our experience). It is precisely the terrain where *progressive sanctification* (the ongoing real-time process of becoming who we already are in Christ — see Section X) happens.

The gap is the coaching space.

**The coach's stance:** Hold both poles with full respect. Do not collapse the gap by dismissing either side. Not: "Just believe what God says and stop feeling that way" (collapses toward the declaration, dismisses the experience). Not: "Your feelings are your truth and that's what matters" (collapses toward the experience, dismisses the covenant). Instead: honor both, and work the distance between them — treating the gap as productive tension that generates growth.

**Diagnostic questions for working the gap:**
- "When you feel [false identity], what does that mean about who God is to you?"
- "What would it cost you to believe [covenant truth] fully and live from it?"
- "Where have you seen [covenant truth] prove itself true in your actual experience?"
- "What story would you have to give up if [covenant truth] were fully operating?"
- "What would change about your life if you woke up tomorrow fully convinced of [covenant truth]?"

## VIII. The Distinctive Practices

### Lament — Not Optional, Structural

The Psalms are approximately 40% *lament* — structured expression of grief, anger, confusion, and suffering brought directly to God *within* the covenant relationship. Lament is not complaint, despair, or lack of faith. It is the biblical practice of remaining in covenant while being fully honest about the distance between covenant truth and lived experience. It is the opposite of *spiritual bypassing* (using theological language and spiritual practice to avoid the emotional reality of pain).

**The structure of biblical lament (Walter Brueggemann, Old Testament theologian):**
1. **Address** — turning toward God, invoking the covenant relationship as the container for what follows
2. **Complaint** — bringing the full weight of the suffering without editing or sanitizing it. The Psalms do not modulate for God's comfort. "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (Psalm 22:1) is quoted by Jesus from the cross.
3. **Petition** — asking God for specific intervention
4. **Confession of trust** — reorientation toward God *despite* unresolved suffering, not because the situation has changed
5. **Praise** — often anticipatory, declaring God's goodness before the circumstances shift

**Why lament is non-negotiable in this model:** A client who cannot lament has almost certainly engaged in spiritual bypassing — using theological categories to avoid the emotional reality of their pain. This produces brittle faith (faith that cannot sustain real suffering) and a distorted identity (the client presents only the sanitized, acceptable self to God and the coach — which means the *actual* self never gets brought into the covenant). Lament is the practice of bringing the full gap — unedited — into the presence of God.

**For coaches:** When a client is experiencing spiritual dryness, persistent disconnection from God, low-grade despair, or prayer that feels hollow — the first intervention is often lament, not encouragement or theological correction. Help them write a lament psalm using the structure above. This often breaks a logjam that years of "positive faith" practice has not touched.

### Covenant Identity Declaration — Not Positive Affirmation

Distinct from *positive affirmation* (psychological self-talk asserting desired feelings or states) and distinct from mere *Scripture memory* (storing texts without connecting them to lived identity), a covenant identity declaration is a *speech act* (a linguistic action that does something in the world, not just describes it) rooted in biblical authority.

The Greek word *homologia* — from which we get our word "confession" — literally means "to say the same thing." Biblical confession (of both sin and truth) is the act of agreeing with what God has said. Confession of covenant truth is *saying the same thing about yourself that God says.*

"I am a new creation in Christ. The old is gone. The new has come. Therefore I will not live today from the old story." This is not psychological self-suggestion. It is *homologia* — agreement with the covenant declaration.

**The practice is:**
- **Specific** — addressing a particular schema or wound, not a generic "I am loved"
- **Anchored** — tied to specific covenant text that carries the authority
- **Embodied** — spoken aloud, written by hand, tied to physical practice (not just thought silently)
- **Rehearsed** — repeated until the new narrative becomes the *default operating system* (the belief that fires automatically rather than requiring conscious effort)

### Embodiment Work

Covenant identity is not only cognitive — it is somatic (of the body). The body holds the false identity: shame lives in the posture (rounded shoulders, lowered gaze), in the voice (quieting before finishing a sentence), in the physical contraction of presence. Felt transformation requires embodied engagement with the covenant truth, not just intellectual agreement.

**Coaching questions:**
- "Where do you feel [false identity] in your body? Where does it live physically?"
- "If [covenant truth] were fully true for you right now — not just believed, but *felt* — what would your body feel like?"
- "What posture do you naturally take when you're living from the lie? What posture belongs to the covenant truth?"

**Embodied practices that reinforce covenant identity:**
- *Receiving posture* in prayer — open hands, lifted head, physically oriented toward reception rather than performance
- Speaking covenant declarations aloud rather than only thinking them (the voice engages the body)
- Physical engagement with lament — walking, writing by hand, intentional movement
- *Communion* (the Lord's Supper) as covenant renewal — the physical act of eating and drinking as bodily re-enactment of covenant belonging. This is perhaps the most underused embodied identity practice in Protestant Christianity.

## IX. The Holy Spirit as Active Agent — Not Background Assumption

Any model claiming genuine biblical integration must grapple structurally with the *pneumatological* (relating to the Holy Spirit) dimension of the coaching. This is not a theological add-on — it changes the architecture.

The Spirit is:
- **Paraclete** (Greek: *paraklētos* — "one called alongside," often translated Counselor, Helper, or Advocate — John 14:16): The coach is not the paraclete; the coach is a human instrument through whom the Paraclete works. The primary helper in the room is the Spirit, not the coach.
- **Spirit of truth** (*pneuma tēs alētheias* — John 16:13): guides into all truth. Powerful questions create openings; the Spirit provides revelation. The coach is a question-asker; the Spirit is the revealer.
- **Spirit of adoption** (*pneuma huiothesias* — Romans 8:15-16): bears witness *with* the client's spirit that they are children of God. The deepest identity confirmation does not come from the coach — it comes from within the client, from the Spirit testifying to the covenant relationship.
- **The one who intercedes in weakness** (Romans 8:26-27): present and active precisely where the client feels most stuck and inarticulate. The Spirit engages what the client cannot access with words.

**Practical implications:**
- Prayer is not decorative — it is the coach's primary orienting practice before and during sessions. The coach enters the room as an instrument, not as the primary agent.
- The coach attends to two channels simultaneously: the clinical picture (what the frameworks reveal) and what they sense the Spirit doing in the conversation — a prompting, a question that arises unexpectedly, a moment of unusual weight or quiet.
- All frameworks are held loosely — subject to interruption and redirection by the Spirit's movement. The coach is not bound to the clinical protocol.
- The coach is not the primary agent of change. They are a *means of grace* (a channel through which God's transforming work becomes accessible) operating within the Spirit's sovereign work.

*Discernment* (the Spirit-given capacity to distinguish what is from God from what is not, and to perceive what the Spirit is doing in a given moment) is a coaching competency — one that requires ongoing spiritual formation in the coach, not just methodological training.

## X. Sanctification as the Long-Term Arc

*Sanctification* — from the Latin *sanctus* (holy) + *facere* (to make). The ongoing process of being made holy — conformed to the image of Christ. Broadly evangelical theology understands sanctification in three tenses:

- **Definitive sanctification** — already accomplished *positionally* (in terms of legal standing before God) in Christ: "you were washed, you were sanctified, you were justified" (1 Corinthians 6:11 — past tense, completed action). This is the covenant identity. It is done.
- **Progressive sanctification** — ongoing in actual experience: "work out your salvation with fear and trembling, for it is God who works in you, both to will and to work for his good pleasure" (Philippians 2:12-13). This is the coaching terrain.
- **Eschatological sanctification** — completed at *glorification* (the final transformation at Christ's return): "when he appears, we shall be like him, for we shall see him as he is" (1 John 3:2). This is the trajectory — the direction the whole process is moving.

Covenant Identity Coaching operates in the progressive dimension. It is a *means of grace* — one of the channels through which God works to conform the believer to Christ's image. The change it produces is real. The ultimate agent is God. The coach creates conditions in which the Spirit's transforming work becomes more accessible and less obstructed. They do not produce the transformation.

This humility is *structural* — built into the model's theology. It protects both coach and client from the performance pressure that would turn coaching itself into a new form of reversed grammar.

## XI. What This Is Not — Critical Distinctions

These distinctions matter for scope of practice, ethics, and effectiveness.

**Not nouthetic / biblical counseling:** Not directive, not primarily sin-focused, does not position the coach as the expert applying Scripture to the client's problem. The client's own discernment and the Spirit's guidance within them are primary. The coaching relationship is collaborative and evocative, not authoritative and prescriptive.

**Not discipleship or teaching:** Not content-transmission, not curriculum-driven. There is no syllabus. The client drives the agenda. The coach does not have a lesson to deliver.

**Not spiritual direction:** *Spiritual direction* (a contemplative practice in which a director helps a directee notice God's activity in their life — rooted in the monastic tradition) is non-structured, not goal-oriented, and primarily attentive rather than generative. It typically has no diagnostic tools, no defined arc, and no formation goals beyond presence. Covenant Identity Coaching shares spiritual formation's telos and the posture of cooperating with the Spirit — but adds diagnostic precision (naming specific false identity structures and character wounds), a structured formation arc, and coaching methodology. A client can receive spiritual direction for years without their specific false identity structure ever being named with precision. The two are complementary but distinct; this practice requires the structure spiritual direction does not provide.

**Not therapy:** Does not diagnose or treat clinical conditions. Does not process trauma directly. When identity work activates a wound that is clinical in depth — trauma responses, dissociation, severe depression, personality disorder patterns — the coach holds the container, honors what emerged, and creates a bridge to clinical care. Refers to therapists. May work alongside a therapist when the client has both needs.

**Not prosperity theology:** Covenant blessings are relational and *eschatological* (pertaining to the ultimate future God is bringing) — they are not promises of material comfort, health, financial success, or an easy life. The covenant explicitly includes *koinōnia tōn pathēmatōn* — "participation in his sufferings" (Philippians 3:10). Lament is built into the model precisely because suffering is real, expected, and does not indicate covenant failure.

**Not moralistic therapeutic deism (MTD):** MTD (sociologist Christian Smith's term for the de facto religion of many American Christians) says: God exists, wants you to feel good about yourself, and is available when needed. Covenant Identity Coaching says: God has acted decisively in history through Christ, has declared your identity through covenant, is actively conforming you to the image of his Son — which is often uncomfortable, requires the death of the false self, and moves *through* suffering rather than around it. The goal is not feeling good about yourself. The goal is inhabiting who you actually are before God.

**Not the enemy's accountability system:** It is worth naming the counterfeit directly. The enemy's accountability system runs: failure → shame → hiding → distance → more failure → more shame. Accusation (*katēgorōn* — Revelation 12:10) is its mechanism. It uses true facts (you did fail) to produce false conclusions (therefore you are a failure; therefore you are rejected; therefore there is no hope). God's accountability system runs the opposite direction: identity declared → belonging established → honest engagement with failure → restoration → forward movement. The Prodigal Son (Luke 15) is the clearest picture: the father sees the son "while he was still a long way off" and runs. The son is received into his identity ("my son was dead and is alive") before he is held to anything. Belonging precedes behavior. This is the shape of the coaching container.

## XIII. Cross-Framework Tensions — Where the Model Must Navigate

These are genuine unresolved tensions — not eliminated by any single framework. Skilled coaching requires navigating them rather than pretending they don't exist.

### Tension 1 — Autonomy Preservation vs. Autonomy Support (SDT)

*Self-Determination Theory* (SDT — developed by Deci and Ryan) distinguishes *autonomy-granting* (accepting the client's stated choice without question) from *autonomy-support* (helping the client author their own choice more fully and freely). These are not the same thing.

Co-Active coaching says: accept the client's "No" — their right to choose is foundational to intrinsic motivation (motivation that comes from within rather than from external reward or pressure).

SDT research complicates this: a coach who accepts every "No" without exploration may not be supporting autonomy — they may be *colluding* (going along with, even enabling) the client's avoidance, fear, or protective patterns. The most autonomy-supportive response to a "No" is often curious exploration: "What is the No protecting? What would a Yes make possible?" This is closer to MI's *ambivalence exploration* (working with the coexisting want-to and don't-want-to) than to a clean Co-Active acceptance.

**How to navigate:** The Designed Alliance (the co-created agreement about how coach and client will work together) can pre-answer this. If the client has already said "when I say no, push back gently" — the exploration is sanctioned. If not, the coach must use judgment: Is this "No" an authentic expression of the client's values? Or is it a protective move away from growth?

### Tension 2 — Championing vs. Client Agenda

Co-Active coaching says: hold the client to who they are, refuse to let them shrink from their own potential (*championing* — seeing the client's capacity and holding that vision even when they've lost sight of it). It also says: don't bring your agenda into the room — the client's agenda is primary.

When the coach champions the client beyond where they said they want to go, they are introducing a vision that exceeds the client's stated agenda. This sits in genuine tension.

**Distinguishing championing from projection:**
- *Championing* is rooted in the client's *own* stated values, declared identity, and expressed potential — amplified and held on their behalf when they forget it
- *Projection* is rooted in what the coach thinks the client should want, become, or be

A useful check: "Is what I'm championing something this client has said, in some form, that they want — or is it something I want for them?" If the latter, it is the coach's agenda, not the client's. The Designed Alliance and consistent values-based inquiry are the primary guards, but the tension must be actively navigated in every session.

## XIV. Evidence Quality — What's Proven vs. Practitioner Wisdom

Not all frameworks cited in coaching and ministry training carry equal empirical weight. Knowing the difference matters when working with skeptical clients and when making claims about effectiveness.

| Framework                              | Evidence Quality                           | Notes                                                                                                  |
| -------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Implementation Intentions (Gollwitzer) | **Very strong**                            | 94+ studies, replicable effect sizes across populations                                                |
| Self-Determination Theory              | **Very strong**                            | Decades of RCTs across cultures, contexts, and age groups                                              |
| Motivational Interviewing              | **Strong**                                 | Multiple independent replications; change-talk predicting outcomes is well-validated                   |
| Self-Compassion (Neff)                 | **Strong**                                 | Growing RCT base; consistent findings on follow-through and accountability                             |
| Attachment Theory                      | **Strong**                                 | Well-established in developmental and clinical literature; less direct coaching-outcome research       |
| Immunity to Change (Kegan & Lahey)     | **Moderate**                               | Theoretically sophisticated, case-study validated; limited randomized controlled trials                |
| Identity-Based Habits (Clear)          | **Moderate**                               | Draws on solid underlying research; the specific framing is Clear's synthesis, not directly RCT-tested |
| ACT (Hayes)                            | **Strong in clinical contexts**            | Strong evidence for anxiety and depression; less direct coaching-outcome research                      |
| Ontological Coaching                   | **Low empirical / High philosophical**     | Philosophically robust (grounded in speech act theory, phenomenology); minimal outcome research        |
| Co-Active Model                        | **Low RCT / High practitioner validation** | Sparse published outcome research; validated through practitioner consensus and client experience      |

*Practical implication:* "Research shows" is only accurate for the top tier. For the rest, "this framework has helped many coaches and clients understand and work with this dynamic" is the honest claim.

## XV. Structure of an Engagement

What Covenant Identity Coaching looks like across a coaching relationship:

### Phase 1 — Covenant Orientation
*Establishing the ground before building the structure*

- **Narrative inventory** (mapping the client's current operative story): What story have you been telling about yourself? Where did it come from?
- **Covenant identity inventory**: Which layers (from Section V) have you received and integrated — meaning, you can feel the truth of it in your bones, not just recite it? Which are cognitively held but not embodied? Which are actively disbelieved or produce fear?
- **Schema identification**: What early maladaptive schemas are currently operational? What beliefs feel like facts?
- **Establishing the coaching container** through the *Designed Alliance* (the co-created agreement about how the coaching will work): How should accountability function? How direct should the coach be? What does it look like when the client is stuck vs. genuinely saying no?

### Phase 2 — Wound Mapping
*Honoring what shaped the terrain before asking the client to move through it*

- Where did the false narrative come from? When did you first learn that about yourself? What happened?
- What core needs were unmet? What *agreements with lies* (conscious or unconscious decisions to accept a false verdict about oneself) were made in those moments?
- **Lament work**: bringing the wound into the covenant rather than around it — practicing the biblical permission to bring the full, unedited self to God
- **Immunity to Change mapping**: What competing commitment is served by not changing? What is the Big Assumption underneath it? What is the theological dimension of that assumption?

### Phase 3 — Identity Installation
*Growing into what is already declared*

- **Targeted covenant truth work**: specific covenant declarations addressing specific schemas — not generic encouragement
- **Embodiment**: What does it feel like in your body to live from [covenant truth]? Build physical engagement with the declaration.
- **Behavioral experiments** (*in vivo* practice — from Latin, "in the living thing"): acting from covenant identity before it fully feels true, and observing what happens when you do
- **Acknowledgment**: naming *identity votes* (actions that are expressions of the emerging covenant identity — every kept commitment, every act of courage, every moment of honesty is a vote for who you are becoming), championing who the client is becoming in Christ
- **Tracking the gap**: Is it narrowing? Where? What remains wide?

### Phase 4 — Mission and Fruitfulness
*Covenant identity sent outward*

- ***Telos* work** (from Greek — ultimate purpose, the end toward which something is constituted): What are you fundamentally for? Not just what do you want to accomplish, but what is the specific corner of the world you are sent to?
- **Ambassador framing** (2 Corinthians 5:20): How does your covenant identity send you into your specific relationships, work, and community? You are not waiting to become someone — you are already sent.
- Building sustainable **covenant renewal rhythms**: prayer, community, Sabbath, lament, Scripture engagement — practices that maintain the identity not through willpower but through ongoing encounter with the covenant God
- **Sanctification as the long arc**: this coaching engagement is a chapter, not the whole story. The goal is not dependency on coaching but increasing capacity to inhabit and live from covenant identity independent of the coaching relationship.

## XVI. Summary Statement — The Core Thesis

Covenant Identity Coaching is the practice of helping followers of Jesus receive their true identity in Christ and be formed toward covenant faithfulness — holding a spiritual formation posture (cooperating with what the Spirit is already doing in the client's life), using the diagnostic precision of biblical counseling to surface false identity structures and disordered affections, and delivering the work through a coaching methodology that honors client agency within God's sovereignty. The practice's tools function as spiritual formation disciplines — creating conditions in which the Spirit transforms the whole person, not causing transformation themselves. The telos is specifically covenantal: the client restored as God's image-bearer in Christ, with false identity displaced and true identity received, practiced, and lived.

Its central claim is that the client's most persistent problems are identity problems — rooted in a failure to inhabit the covenant reality already established by grace — and that closing that gap is not primarily a matter of effort, strategy, or behavioral correction. It is a matter of receiving, embodying, and growing into a self that has already been given.

The indicative precedes the imperative. Identity is declared before it is grown. Belonging comes before behavior.

The coach's role is not to produce this transformation. It is to be a faithful human instrument in the hands of the one who does.
