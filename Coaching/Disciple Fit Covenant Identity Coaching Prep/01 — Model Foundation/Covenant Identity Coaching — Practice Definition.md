# Covenant Identity Coaching — Practice Definition

*Governing definition document. All other model documents, tools, and session protocols reference this document for what the practice is. When documents conflict with this definition, this document governs.*

---

## Summary

This document defines Covenant Identity Coaching with precision across five dimensions: telos, mechanism, container, practitioner role, and client role. It distinguishes the practice from its three adjacent disciplines — Christian life coaching, biblical counseling, and spiritual formation — and states the definition at three levels of compression for internal, practitioner, and client-facing use.

---

## What This Practice Is

### The Three Contributing Disciplines

Covenant Identity Coaching draws from three distinct disciplines. None of the three alone is sufficient. Each contributes a specific element to a unified whole.

**Christian Life Coaching** contributes: relational methodology, forward-oriented partnership posture, coaching competencies (powerful questions, accountability, client agency), and a structured time-limited engagement.

**Biblical Counseling** contributes: diagnostic precision — idolatry and disordered worship as the root of false identity, the heart as the center of human functioning, and specific tools for surfacing affections, false identity structures, and character wounds.

**Spiritual Formation** contributes: the governing telos (Christlikeness / restored image-bearing), the process frame (Spirit-initiated whole-person formation), the account of the practitioner's role (cooperating with what the Spirit is already doing), and the understanding of disciplines as conditions for transformation — not causes of it.

**Covenant Identity** provides the specific content of what the person is being formed toward: not Christlikeness generically, but specifically — restored image-bearing as God's covenant person in Christ, with false identity displaced and true identity received, practiced, and lived.

---

## Dimension 1 — Telos: What This Practice Is For

The telos is the progressive restoration of the client's covenant identity — being formed into a person who bears God's image faithfully, trusts God's covenant character, and lives as God's person in every domain of life, as the Spirit conforms them to Christ.

**The covenant-specific content:** This is not generic spiritual growth. It is specifically the displacement of false identity built on false covenant objects, and the reception and practice of true identity as God's image-bearer in Christ — known by God, defined by his covenant faithfulness, living under his rule rather than under the governance of performance, wound, or fear.

**Scripture grounding:**
- 2 Cor. 3:18 — *"transformed into the same image from one degree of glory to another"*
- Gal. 4:19 — *"until Christ is formed in you"*
- Col. 3:9–10 — *"put off the old self... put on the new self, which is being renewed in knowledge after the image of its creator"*
- Eph. 4:22–24 — put off false identity, be renewed in the spirit of your mind, put on the new self created in true righteousness and holiness

**What a transformed client looks like:**
- Near-term: False identity named and exposed; affections beginning to reorient toward God; false covenant objects losing governing power; true covenant identity consciously received
- Long-term: A person whose desires, affections, imagination, habits, and character are increasingly conformed to Christ — who lives as God's covenant person not by striving but by Spirit-formed character

**What this telos is not:**
- Psychological integration or reduced shame
- Emotional healing or wound resolution
- Goal achievement or life optimization
- Problem resolution and restored obedience
- Generic human flourishing

### Positional Identity vs. Identity Formation — A Necessary Distinction

The telos statement above — "the progressive restoration of the client's covenant identity" — describes a *process*. But the identity being restored is not something this practice, the client's cooperation, or the coach's skill brings into being. Two things must not be collapsed into one:

**Positional identity** is the objective, already-accomplished reality conferred on the client at new birth and union with Christ — instantaneous, complete, and monergistic (the Spirit's act, not the client's achievement or the coach's production). Scripture states this in the past tense, as a finished fact: the old self *was* crucified (Rom. 6:6), the client *has* died and their life *is* hidden with Christ (Col. 3:3), and if anyone is in Christ, they *are* a new creation (2 Cor. 5:17). This is already true of every client who meets the spiritual baseline for entry (Dimension 3) — true from the day they trusted Christ, before this practice, or any coaching relationship, ever touched their life.

**Identity formation** — what this practice actually does — is the Spirit-enabled, cooperative process of bringing the client's conscious self-understanding, felt sense of self, affections, and will into alignment with the positional identity that is already theirs. This is present-tense and progressive: renewal of the mind (Rom. 12:2), putting off the old self / putting on the new (Eph. 4:22–24). It is the work of *realizing and inhabiting* a reality that already exists — not constructing one that doesn't yet exist.

**Why this distinction is load-bearing, not academic:**
- It protects the practice from a subtle works-righteousness drift. The client is not earning or building their identity through the coaching engagement; they are being brought into conscious, lived alignment with what the Spirit has already secured. This keeps Mechanism Layer 1 (Dimension 2) — "the Spirit is already at work... the coach does not produce transformation" — anchored to a specific theological fact rather than a general sentiment.
- It answers, directly, a predictable objection to the practice's declarative tools (Lie Eliminator, Identity Installer, targeted declaration): that they function as manifestation-style self-persuasion — "declare it until you believe it." They do not. The client is not declaring a self-chosen outcome into existence; they are declaring an objective, Spirit-accomplished reality back to a mind and affective system that has not yet caught up to it. This is the same logical structure as Rom. 6:11's *logizomai* ("consider/reckon yourselves dead to sin and alive to God") — reckoning with what is already true, not manifesting what is not yet true.
- It gives the practice precise language for clients who cognitively know their new identity but do not feel it: the gap is not a deficiency in their positional identity — nothing is missing there — it is a gap in formation, where awareness, felt reality, and lived alignment have not yet caught up to a status that is already secure. This reframes a client's felt distance from God as the normal condition formation work addresses, not as evidence the transformation hasn't started.

**What this means for the telos statement above:** "Progressive restoration of covenant identity" should be read as progressive *formation into conscious alignment with* an identity that is not itself progressive or partial — it is complete at new birth. The practice does not form the identity into existence. It forms the client into the felt, functional, lived experience of an identity the Spirit has already established.

## Dimension 2 — Mechanism: What Produces Transformation

Transformation happens through a five-layer sequence. Each layer is distinct. Collapsing them produces one of three failure modes.

**Layer 1 — The Spirit is the primary agent.**
The Spirit is already at work in the client's life before the coach arrives. The coach does not produce transformation. The practice creates conditions; the Spirit transforms. This includes the client's positional identity in Christ — already established at new birth and union with Christ, prior to and independent of this practice's involvement (see Dimension 1, "Positional Identity vs. Identity Formation").

**Layer 2 — The coaching relationship creates a formation context.**
Attunement, relational honesty, and the coach's own formation posture create conditions in which the client can be genuinely known. This is the relational condition in which the Spirit's diagnostic and formative work can be received rather than defended against.

**Layer 3 — Diagnostic work surfaces false identity.**
The Affections Discovery, Character Wound Diagnostic, and related tools expose the false covenant objects and false identity structures blocking formation. These tools surface what the Spirit is already surfacing — they create conditions for the client to receive the exposure, not produce it.

**Layer 4 — Covenant truth is applied and received.**
The client encounters what God says about their identity in covenant. This is not information transfer. It is the Spirit's illumination of truth through the Word, applied in the coaching relationship. The practice's tools (Lie Eliminator, Identity Installer, Secured in Father) function as formation disciplines here — training the whole person (will, mind, emotions, body) to receive and inhabit covenant truth, not just know it cognitively.

**Layer 5 — Between-session formation disciplines sustain and deepen the work.**
Disciplines assigned between sessions do not cause transformation — they create conditions in which the Spirit continues the formation work outside the session. The session plants and orients; the disciplines cultivate. This is why the practice's tools produce prompting, emotional connection, faith building, and conditioning: they function as spiritual formation disciplines, not technique delivery.

**The three failure modes this mechanism guards against:**

| Failure mode | Description | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Legalism | Disciplines as willpower applied to sin-identification | Tripp without grace-framing |
| Insight without formation | Clarity about false identity without between-session practice | Coaching-only approach |
| Tool dependence | Treating the tools as causes of transformation, not conditions | Technique trap |

## Dimension 3 — Container: What Holds This Work

**In-session:** A coaching conversation organized around formation goals, not presenting problems alone. Uses diagnostic tools to surface false identity, applies covenant truth, and designs between-session formation work. The governing question is not "what do you want to achieve?" but "what is the Spirit doing here, and how do we cooperate with it?"

**Between-session:** Formation disciplines calibrated to the client's identified formation need — the primary location where the Spirit's transformation work continues. Sessions create Vision and Intention; the disciplines provide the Means (Willard's VIM framework: Vision = clear picture of the person being formed toward; Intention = deliberate decision to cooperate with that formation; Means = specific disciplines that train the whole person).

**Arc:** A defined formation engagement with a clear beginning, development, and completion. Structured enough to hold the work; flexible enough to follow the Spirit's movement.

**What distinguishes this container:**

| Feature | Life Coaching | Biblical Counseling | Spiritual Direction | This Practice |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Entry condition | Goal or desire | Presenting problem | Spiritual hunger | Formation readiness + faith baseline |
| Orientation | Forward / goals | Problem / sin | Presence / accompaniment | Formation telos |
| Structure | High / defined | Variable | Low / pace of Spirit | Defined arc with formation flexibility |
| Between-session | Action steps | Scripture / prayer | Unstructured | Formation disciplines |
| Posture | Partner / questioner | Authority / diagnostician | Witness / accompany | Formation partner |

**Spiritual baseline required at entry:**
- Active or genuine opening faith in Jesus Christ
- Willingness to receive Scripture as authoritative
- Willingness to engage formation disciplines between sessions
- Sufficient stability for formation work — acute crisis triggers referral protocol, not this practice

## Dimension 4 — Practitioner Role: What the Coach Does

The practitioner holds three roles simultaneously, in order of priority:

**Primary — Formation partner.**
Cooperating with what the Spirit is already doing in the client. Reading the movement of the Spirit in the client's story, affections, and patterns. Naming what God appears to be doing. Holding the long-arc formation telos when the client loses sight of it. Designing the formation practices that match the client's specific formation need.

**Secondary — Covenant identity diagnostician.**
Using the diagnostic tools to surface false identity structures, false covenant objects, and character wounds blocking formation. Not prescriptively diagnosing — creating the conditions through which the Spirit surfaces the client's own recognition. The coach holds diagnostic awareness; the Spirit does the exposing.

**Tertiary — Coaching partner.**
Using coaching methodology — powerful questions, forward orientation, client agency, accountability — to structure the engagement and move the formation work forward.

**The skill combination this role uniquely requires:**
- Coaching competency (conversational structure, powerful questions, accountability)
- Biblical diagnostic literacy (affections, false identity, character wounds, covenant truth)
- Spiritual formation discernment (reading the Spirit's work, designing formation disciplines)
- Scripture fluency as governing framework, not reference material
- Whole-person attunement (reading implicit signals, emotional awareness)

**What this role is not:**
- A goal coach who adds prayer and Scripture
- A biblical counselor who asks good questions
- A spiritual director who uses structured worksheets
- A therapist who stays within coaching scope

**The coach's own formation is a prerequisite.**
The coach cannot hold a formation posture toward a client they have not practiced toward themselves. The attunement, discernment, and formation-reading this role requires are not skills acquired by study alone — they are developed through the practitioner's own ongoing formation in the same disciplines, diagnostic tools, and covenant identity work the practice uses with clients. A coach who is not themselves a formation apprentice cannot credibly hold the formation posture this practice requires.

## Dimension 5 — Client Role: What the Client Does

The client is a **formation apprentice** — not primarily a goal-setter, a penitent, or a passive recipient, but an active cooperator in the Spirit-initiated work of becoming who God made them to be in covenant.

*Note on language: "Formation apprentice" is precise for internal use but should not be used with clients without translation. A client who hears "apprentice" may read it as passive or deferential — waiting to be shaped. The intended meaning is the opposite: active, willing, practicing cooperation with the Spirit's work. Client-facing language should emphasize agency: "You are choosing to cooperate with what God is doing in you."*

**Healthy engagement looks like:**
- Willingness to be honestly known — bringing their actual inner life, not just presenting problems
- Active engagement with between-session formation disciplines as practices, not homework
- Returning to covenant truth when false identity reasserts — and it will
- Growing capacity to recognize their own affections, false identity patterns, and the Spirit's movement

**Resistance indicates diagnostically:**
- Secondary gain from the false identity structure — the false self is serving a function
- Insufficient spiritual baseline — the formation mechanism requires means of grace not yet engaged
- Shame or fear of exposure — diagnostic material, not a reason to stop; reason to slow and attend
- Wrong-fit — needs therapy, pastoral care, or a different helping relationship; referral protocol engages

## Boundary Testing

### vs. Christian Life Coaching

**Shared:** Relational methodology, forward orientation, partnership posture, coaching competencies, time-limited engagement.

**Divergence:** Life coaching's anthropology assumes the client is resourceful and has the answers within. This practice's anthropology assumes the client's deepest resource is the Spirit of God and the covenant identity Christ has secured — not internal capacity. The coach is not drawing out the client's inner wisdom; the coach is creating conditions for the Spirit to form what is not yet there and restore what has been displaced.

**What a Christian life coach would say this practice gets wrong:** "You're overriding client autonomy by holding a prescribed telos. The client should own their goals."

**Response:** The formation telos is not imposed — it is the actual end for which the client was made. Holding that telos is faithfulness to the client's truest identity, not manipulation of their agenda. The client's agency is real and essential to the process; what changes is what the agency is oriented toward. A good coach holds the client's deepest good, not just their stated preferences.

---

### vs. Biblical Counseling

**Shared:** Diagnostic precision, idolatry as root, heart as center, Scripture as governing framework, Spirit as change agent.

**Divergence:** Biblical counseling is reactive (addresses presenting problems) and directive (counselor applies the Word with authority). This practice is proactive (pursues formation regardless of crisis) and co-operative (coach creates conditions; Spirit and client do the identifying and applying). The governing frame is formation, not correction — which changes posture without eliminating diagnostic depth.

**What a biblical counselor would say this practice gets wrong:** "You're insufficiently directive with Scripture and too soft on sin as the presenting category. The Word should be applied with authority, not just created conditions for."

**Response:** The diagnostic tools surface sin and false identity with precision — idolatry and disordered worship remain the root diagnostic categories. But the governing frame is formation, not correction. This changes the posture without abandoning the diagnostic depth. The Spirit confronts; the coach creates conditions for the client to receive that confrontation rather than defend against it. Directive application of Scripture often produces compliance without formation — which is the failure mode this practice is specifically designed to avoid.

---

### vs. Spiritual Formation / Spiritual Direction

**Shared:** Formation telos, whole-person scope, Spirit as primary agent, disciplines as means of grace, attentive posture toward what the Spirit is doing.

**Divergence:** Spiritual direction is non-directive and pace-of-the-Spirit — no diagnostic tools, no defined arc, no formation goals beyond presence. This practice brings diagnostic precision and structured formation sequence into the formation context. The covenant content is more specific: this practice names false identity structures and targets covenant faithfulness specifically.

**What a spiritual director would say this practice gets wrong:** "You're too structured and goal-oriented. You risk managing the Spirit rather than cooperating with him. Formation cannot be scheduled."

**Response:** The structure serves the formation work — it does not impose on the Spirit. The diagnostic tools surface what the Spirit is already surfacing; they don't replace his initiative, they make space for the client to receive it. A defined arc creates a container that holds the work; it does not constrain the Spirit's movement within it. Unstructured direction, while valuable, can leave clients without the diagnostic clarity needed to recognize what the Spirit is actually doing in their specific false identity patterns. Structure and Spirit-cooperation are not opposites.

## Distinctiveness Test

**What this practice does that Christian life coaching cannot do:**
Diagnoses and addresses the root of false identity — disordered affections, false covenant objects, character wounds — and forms new covenant identity through Spirit-initiated disciplines. Life coaching cannot do this without the biblical diagnostic framework and formation posture. Drawing out the client's inner resourcefulness cannot restore what is genuinely displaced or form what is not yet there.

**What this practice does that biblical counseling cannot do:**
Holds a proactive formation posture that pursues covenant identity restoration regardless of presenting crisis. Provides a structured formation arc with client agency in the process. Does not require a problem to enter. Biblical counseling does not typically function in a proactive, formation-oriented container with coaching partnership — it is designed to address what is broken, not to pursue what is being formed.

**What this practice does that spiritual formation / spiritual direction cannot do:**
Brings diagnostic precision to the formation work — naming specific false identity structures, character wounds, and disordered affections that block formation — and delivers a structured formation arc with tools calibrated to the client's specific formation need. Spiritual direction does not typically provide this diagnostic specificity. A client can receive spiritual direction for years without their false identity structure ever being named with precision.

**What this practice refuses to do:**
- Make goal achievement the governing telos
- Be primarily reactive or crisis-triggered as its entry condition
- Be unstructured and pace-of-the-Spirit alone, without diagnostic engagement
- Treat the tools as causes of transformation rather than conditions for it
- Produce compliance without formation

**The single most distinctive element:**
The integration of spiritual formation's telos and posture with biblical counseling's diagnostic precision, delivered through a coaching methodology and structured formation arc — with the practice's tools functioning as formation disciplines rather than technique delivery. This configuration does not exist in any of the three adjacent disciplines individually, nor in common hybrid approaches.

## Precision Definitions — Three Levels

### Level 1 — Governing Paragraph
*For internal use. Governs all document and tool development.*

Covenant Identity Coaching is a spiritually formed, covenantally grounded practice that helps followers of Jesus receive their true identity in Christ and be formed toward covenant faithfulness in every domain of life. Holding a spiritual formation posture — cooperating with what the Spirit is already doing in the client's life — the practice uses the diagnostic precision of biblical counseling to surface false identity structures, disordered affections, and character wounds that block formation. Coaching methodology provides the relational container: forward-oriented partnership, client agency within God's sovereignty, and a structured formation arc. The practice's tools function as spiritual formation disciplines — creating conditions in which the Spirit transforms the whole person, not causing transformation themselves. The telos is specifically covenantal: the client restored as God's image-bearer in Christ, with false identity displaced and true identity received, practiced, and lived.

### Level 2 — Working Definition
*For practitioner reference documents and collaborator orientation.*

Covenant Identity Coaching is a structured, Spirit-cooperating practice that uses the diagnostic precision of biblical counseling, the relational methodology of coaching, and the posture of spiritual formation to help followers of Jesus identify and displace false identity, receive their covenant identity in Christ, and be formed by the Spirit toward faithful image-bearing. The practice's tools function as formation disciplines — creating conditions for Spirit-initiated whole-person transformation between and within sessions.

### Level 3 — One-Sentence Summary
*For conversation, client-facing introduction, and public description.*

A structured, Spirit-cooperating coaching practice that surfaces false identity, applies covenant truth, and forms disciples toward faithful image-bearing in Christ through the practice of formation disciplines.

## Open Precision Gaps — Resolve Before Full Deployment

1. **Client qualifier:** "Followers of Jesus" is the stated entry condition. If the practice serves pre-Christians or early-faith clients, covenant identity language requires a client-facing translation layer. Define the target client with precision.

2. **Formation arc scope:** "Structured formation arc" is unspecified. Define the standard engagement length (number of sessions, formation goal-setting process, completion criteria) in a separate container document.

3. **Between-session design:** If the tools function as formation disciplines — which practice evidence confirms — the between-session design must explicitly structure them as formation practices, not homework. Ensure delivery matches the mechanism claim.
