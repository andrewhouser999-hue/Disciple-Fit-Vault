## Research Output — Approaches, Framework & Coaching Integration

**What this is:** A practitioner research document surveying the best approaches to build a rigorous, deployable understanding of God's covenantally structured relationship with believers — grounded in exegesis, organized for coach development, and applicable to identity formation and client work.
**Who it's for:** Andrew as Disciple Fit practitioner and curriculum builder. Not client-facing in this form.

## Advisor Flag — Framing Check

Before answering the question asked, the framing needs a challenge.

"Covenantal Identity" as a coaching category is only as strong as the theological claim underneath it. The risk in this space: importing "covenant" as inspirational vocabulary without the exegetical structure to back it. Clients will feel the weakness — not as a theological critique but as *it doesn't hold weight when life is hard.*

The question is well-formed *only if* the goal is genuine, defensible, exegetically grounded covenantal theology — not a framework dressed in covenant language. Assuming that's the intent, proceeding.

## Part 1 — Biblical Research Framework

### 1. Text & Context — What "Covenant" Actually Is

The Hebrew *berith* (בְּרִית) appears ~285 times in the OT. It denotes a solemn, binding agreement ratified by oath and usually by ceremony (oath-swearing, cutting of animals, shared meal, blood). The phrase *karat berith* — "to cut a covenant" — reflects the ancient practice of cutting animals and walking between the halves (cf. Gen 15), invoking self-curse: "If I break this, may what happened to these animals happen to me."

**Critical distinction:** Not all covenants are structurally identical.

- **Suzerain-vassal treaties** (Mosaic covenant) — Great King initiates; vassals obligate themselves to conditions; blessings/curses are bilateral. Parallels Hittite treaty form (Meredith Kline's seminal work, *Treaty of the Great King*, 1963). **Conditionally structured.**
- **Royal grant covenants** (Abrahamic, Davidic) — King grants land/dynasty to loyal servant. Unilateral. The granting party assumes obligation, not the recipient. **Unconditionally structured.** This matters enormously for identity work: the Abrahamic covenant is a royal grant God alone walked through (Gen 15:17 — God as smoking pot/flaming torch, Abraham in deep sleep). God took the curse on himself.

**The New Covenant** (Jer 31:31-34; Ezek 36:25-27; Luke 22:20; Heb 8-10) — fulfills and supersedes the Mosaic, extends the Abrahamic, and installs the Davidic King permanently. It is unilateral in initiation (Christ ratifies it with his blood), but bilateral in participation (faith, repentance, Spirit-filled living). This is the operative covenant for believers.

### 2. Language — Key Terms

| Term | Hebrew/Greek | Semantic Range | Translation Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| *Berith* | בְּרִית | Covenant, treaty, bond | "Agreement" flattens — obscures oath-bound solemnity |
| *Hesed* | חֶסֶד | Covenant loyalty, lovingkindness, steadfast love | "Love" alone loses the *covenantal obligation* — this is not just affection, it is bound faithfulness |
| *Emunah* | אֱמוּנָה | Faithfulness, trustworthiness, reliability | Root of "amen." Often translated "faith" in NT contexts (πίστις/*pistis*) but carries weight of *relational trustworthiness*, not just cognitive belief |
| *Hesed ve-emet* | חֶסֶד וֶאֱמֶת | Lovingkindness and truth/faithfulness | Hendiadys: covenant love *and* covenant faithfulness together. Appears at key covenant renewal moments (Ex 34:6; Gen 24:27; John 1:14 — "grace and truth" = the Greek equivalent) |
| *Diatheke* | διαθήκη | Covenant, will/testament | LXX uses this over *syntheke* (mutual agreement) — emphasizing that God's covenant is not a negotiated contract but a gracious disposition |

**High-leverage insight for coaching:** *Hesed* is the single most important term. It is covenantally structured love — God's faithfulness to you is not temperamental affection, it is oath-bound commitment. When a client hears "God loves you," they hear sentiment. When they understand *hesed*, they hear a sworn covenant. That is a different psychological load-bearing structure.

### 3. Historical-Cultural Context

Ancient Near Eastern covenant context is irreplaceable for this question:

- **Suzerainty treaty structure** (Hittite, Assyrian) — Preamble (who I am), historical prologue (what I've done for you), stipulations (what you must do), deposit/public reading, witnesses, blessings/curses. The Mosaic Torah follows this structure almost exactly (Meredith Kline, *The Structure of Biblical Authority*, 1975). The original audience would have recognized this immediately as a political document — the Great King of the universe formally binding himself to a vassal people.
- **Covenant meals** — Shared meals sealed covenants (Gen 31:54; Ex 24:11; the Passover; the Lord's Supper). The covenant is not only believed — it is embodied and enacted. This has direct coaching implications for embodied identity work.
- **Covenant name** — To be named within a covenant is to have a new identity. Abram → Abraham. Jacob → Israel. Simon → Peter. Saul → Paul. Name change = identity change = covenant installation. This maps directly to Covenant Identity work.

### 4. Intertextual Connections — The Single Covenantal Thread

The dominant scholarly framework for understanding the covenant's unity is **Progressive Covenantalism** (Gentry & Wellum, *Kingdom through Covenant*, 2018 — the most rigorous current treatment). The argument:

- Each covenant *advances* the one before without annulling it
- The Abrahamic covenant is the mother promise; every subsequent covenant is a development of its three components: seed, land, blessing
- The Mosaic covenant is a temporary administration (Gal 3:19-25) — a guardian until Christ
- The Davidic covenant installs the king through whom the Abrahamic promises come
- The New Covenant is the *fulfillment* of all prior covenants in Christ

Key intertextual backbone:
- **Gen 12:1-3 → Gal 3:8** — Paul calls Gen 12 "the gospel preached in advance." The Abrahamic promise IS the gospel.
- **Ex 19:4-6 → 1 Pet 2:9-10** — "Kingdom of priests, holy nation" transferred to the church. The church is covenant Israel expanded.
- **Jer 31:31-34 → Heb 8:6-13** — The New Covenant makes the Mosaic "obsolete." But not the Abrahamic.
- **Ezek 36:25-27 → Acts 2** — The Spirit poured out = New Covenant installation in real time.
- **Gen 15 → Rom 4** — Abraham's faith credited as righteousness, *before* circumcision. Paul's point: the covenant is older than the law, older than the ritual sign. It is faith-structured from the beginning.

### 5. Theological Weight

Three load-bearing theological claims that everything else rests on:

**Claim 1: The covenant is the structure of grace, not a transaction.**
God initiates. God obligates himself. God ratifies in blood (his own, ultimately). The believer enters by faith — not by performance. Covenant identity is received, not earned.

**Claim 2: Union with Christ is the mechanism of New Covenant participation.**
"In Christ" appears ~164 times in Paul's letters. This is not metaphor — it is covenantal location. To be in Christ is to be inside the covenant because Christ is the covenant keeper and the covenant mediator simultaneously (Heb 9:15). The believer's covenantal standing is Christ's standing, credited by union.

**Claim 3: The covenant is the ground of both identity and expectation.**
God's covenantal faithfulness (*hesed*) is the basis for both who you are and what you can count on. Covenant identity is not self-generated — it is received from the covenant relationship and confirmed by the covenant community.

### 6. Interpretive Debate — Where Credible Scholars Disagree

| Question | Positions | Stakes for Coaching |
|---|---|---|
| Continuity/discontinuity between covenants | Covenant theology (one covenant of grace) vs. Dispensationalism (distinct programs) vs. Progressive Covenantalism (organic development) | Determines whether OT promises apply to NT believers directly, typologically, or not at all. |
| Israel and the Church | Replacement theology vs. two-peoples theology vs. one-people-through-Christ | Affects whether OT provision texts belong to the client's covenant story |
| Conditionality of New Covenant | Fully unconditional vs. bilateral participation required | If unconditional: assurance is absolute. If bilateral: perseverance and apostasy become live questions. Most Reformed and Baptistic scholars hold it is initiated unconditionally, sustained bilaterally through Spirit-enabled faith. |
| "Blessings and curses" applicability under the New Covenant | Mosaic blessings/curses NOT directly applicable (Gal 3:10-14) — Christ absorbed the curse; believers receive the Abrahamic blessing through Christ. | Crucial for coaching: Mal 3:10 tithe promises are Mosaic; applying them as direct New Covenant guarantees is exegetically weak. The *pattern* of provision is valid; the *contractual mechanism* has changed. |

**The last point is the most coaching-critical.** Many Covenant Identity frameworks borrow OT provision promises wholesale without accounting for the covenantal shift at Calvary. The safer and more defensible move: root New Covenant provision promises in the Abrahamic (unconditional) and New Covenant (Heb 13:5, Matt 6:25-34, Phil 4:19, Rom 8:32) rather than the Mosaic. This survives exegetical scrutiny.

### Approach 1 — Redemptive-Historical Biblical Theology Method
`[CURRICULUM]` `[SELF-COACH]`

**What it is:** Reading the whole Bible as one unified story, tracing the progressive unfolding of God's redemptive covenant from creation → fall → redemption → new creation. Each covenant is a chapter in one story, not isolated events.

**Why it works theologically:** This is the methodology Jesus himself uses (Luke 24:25-27, 44-47 — "beginning with Moses and all the Prophets, he explained what was said in all the Scriptures concerning himself"). Paul uses it throughout Romans and Galatians. It is the native reading strategy of the NT authors.

**Best primary resources:**
- Gentry & Wellum, *Kingdom through Covenant* (2018, 2nd ed.) — **Established scholarly standard.** Most rigorous academic treatment of the covenants in print.
- Thomas Schreiner, *Covenant and God's Purpose for the World* (2017) — accessible academic entry point into progressive covenantalism.
- Greg Beale, *A New Testament Biblical Theology* (2011) — NT fulfillment of OT covenant themes; comprehensive.
- Geerhardus Vos, *Biblical Theology* (1948) — foundational for the whole approach; dated but irreplaceable.

**For Andrew:** Study *Schreiner* first (shorter, accessible), then *Gentry & Wellum* for depth. Vos for historical grounding. This approach gives Andrew the spine that holds everything else together.

**Verdict: Essential. Non-negotiable foundation for the practitioner. Cannot build a defensible Covenantal Identity framework without it.**

### Approach 2 — Covenant Word Study (Lexical-Exegetical Method)
`[CURRICULUM]` `[SELF-COACH]`

**What it is:** Deep study of the key covenant terms — *berith*, *hesed*, *emunah*, *diatheke* — tracking them through Scripture with lexicons and commentaries.

**Why it works:** The vocabulary carries the theology. When a practitioner doesn't know what *hesed* actually means, they are working with a diluted concept that won't hold psychological weight.

**Tools:**
- Brown-Driver-Briggs (BDB) Hebrew Lexicon — standard OT lexical reference
- BDAG (Bauer-Danker-Arndt-Gingrich) — standard NT Greek lexical reference
- Katharine Doob Sakenfeld, *The Meaning of Hesed in the Hebrew Bible* (1978) — **the** monograph on *hesed*; academic but definitive.
- John Oswalt, *The Bible Among the Myths* (2009) — contextualizes Israelite covenant theology against ANE background.

**For coaching deployment:** The lexical work stays at the practitioner level. But the *results* become the vocabulary of the coaching system. "God's hesed is not affection — it's oath-bound loyalty" is a coaching statement. The study behind it is what makes it land with weight.

**Verdict: High value for Andrew. Medium effort, high return.**

### Approach 3 — ANE (Ancient Near Eastern) Treaty Background Study
`[CURRICULUM]` `[SELF-COACH]`

**What it is:** Studying the Hittite suzerainty treaty structure and its parallels to the Mosaic covenant; understanding the royal grant form and its parallel to Abrahamic/Davidic covenants.

**Why it works:** The original audience heard covenant documents through this cultural grid. Understanding it unlocks the full weight of what is being claimed — the God of the universe is formally adopting the structures of international law to bind himself to a people.

**Key resource:**
- Meredith Kline, *Treaty of the Great King* (1963) and *The Structure of Biblical Authority* (1975) — foundational. Shows Deuteronomy as a treaty document structurally.
- Paul Kalluveettil, *Declaration and Covenant* (1982) — broader ANE covenant comparative study.

**For Andrew:** This is practitioner-level depth, not client-facing. It answers "why does the covenant have this structure?" with exegetical precision rather than intuition.

**Verdict: High value for credibility and precision; not urgent for initial deployment. Medium priority.**

### Approach 4 — Narrative Immersion (Story-Based Entry)
`[CURRICULUM]` `[BIBLE-SESSION]`

**What it is:** Entering the covenantal story as a participant rather than an observer. Framing the believer's life as a continuation of the one covenant story of Scripture — not a reader of the story but a character within it.

**Biblical grounding:** This is Paul's argument in Galatians 3-4 and Romans 4. "If you belong to Christ, then you are Abraham's seed, and heirs according to the promise" (Gal 3:29). Not metaphorically heirs — actually heirs. The covenant story is now your story.

**N.T. Wright's "5-act drama" framework** (*The New Testament and the People of God*, 1992) is useful here: Creation → Fall → Israel → Jesus → Church/New Creation. The believer lives in Act 5, improvising faithfully within the established story.

**Why it works psychologically:** Narrative identity theory (Dan McAdams, *The Stories We Live By*, 1993 — **Established**) demonstrates that identity is story-shaped. People know who they are by what story they inhabit. Entering the covenant story is not a metaphor for identity formation — it is identity formation. McAdams's finding: people with coherent, redemptive personal narratives show greater psychological resilience and wellbeing. The covenant story provides the most robust possible redemptive narrative.

**Coaching application:** This is highly deployable with clients. "You're not reading Abraham's story — you're in it. You're his heir. The same God who appeared to him and made an unconditional promise has now made the same promise to you, ratified in Christ's blood." This is a reframe with deep roots.

**Verdict: Highest client-facing value. Directly connects to identity formation work.**

### Approach 5 — In-Depth Study of the "In Christ" Formulations (Pauline Covenant Location)
`[CURRICULUM]` `[BIBLE-FRAMEWORK]` `[SELF-COACH]`

**What it is:** Systematic study of the ~164 "in Christ / in him / in the Lord" phrases in Paul's letters, understanding them as covenantal location markers — not devotional language but theological precision.

**Why it's the New Covenant mechanism:** In the OT, covenant location was territorial (the land), genealogical (Abraham's seed), and ritual (circumcision, temple, Passover). In the New Covenant, the location is *personal*: in Christ. Christ is the covenant. To be in him is to have his covenantal standing.

**Key texts to study:**
- Romans 8:1 — "No condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus" — covenantal legal verdict
- Ephesians 1:3-14 — Every spiritual blessing "in the heavenly realms in Christ" — inventory of covenant benefits
- 2 Corinthians 5:17 — "In Christ, new creation" — ontological covenantal identity shift
- Galatians 2:20 — "I have been crucified with Christ; I no longer live, but Christ lives in me" — mutual indwelling as covenant union
- Colossians 3:1-4 — "Your life is hidden with Christ in God" — security as covenantal hiddenness

**Best resource:** Constantine Campbell, *Paul and Union with Christ* (2012) — comprehensive scholarly survey of the "in Christ" language. Rigorous and accessible.

**Coaching application:** This is the theological backbone of Covenant Identity declarations. Every declaration should be traceable to a specific "in Christ" reality. "I am forgiven" is sentimental without the covenantal ground: "In Christ, I have redemption through his blood, the forgiveness of sins" (Eph 1:7). The "in Christ" anchor transforms declarations from affirmations (self-generated) to covenant claims (externally grounded).

**Verdict: Critical for the Covenant Identity system specifically. High priority.**

### Approach 6 — Declaration and Embodied Covenant Practice
`[BIBLE-SESSION]` `[CLIENT-TOOL]` `[SESSION-TOOL]`

**What it is:** Regular, repeated declaration of covenantal realities as a formational practice — not mere affirmation but covenant-speech: speaking what is true about who God has declared you to be.

**Biblical grounding:**
- Deuteronomy 6:4-9 (*Shema*) — Covenant declaration was daily, embodied (hands, forehead, doorposts), and communal. Formation through repetition is the Mosaic approach to covenant internalization.
- Psalm 103:1-5 — "Forget not all his benefits" — deliberate covenant memory rehearsal
- Revelation 12:11 — "They overcame by the blood of the Lamb and the word of their testimony" — declaration as covenantal weapon
- Romans 10:9-10 — Confession (*homologeo* — to say the same thing as) with the mouth as part of covenant entry

**Psychological grounding:**
- **Affirmations literature caveat:** Generic positive affirmations have weak evidence and can backfire for people with low self-esteem (Wood et al., 2009 — *Psychological Science* — **Established**). The difference with covenantal declarations: they are *externally grounded* (in what God says), not internally generated (what the person wishes were true). This is not self-talk — it is covenant-speech.
- **Neuroplasticity** — Donald Hebb's principle: neurons that fire together wire together. Repeated declarative rehearsal of truth reshapes neural pathways associated with identity (Hanson, *Hardwiring Happiness*, 2013 — **Supported**; note: popular-level synthesis).
- **Narrative therapy** (White & Epston, *Narrative Means to Therapeutic Ends*, 1990) — re-authoring the dominant story through alternative story-thickening. Covenant declarations function as alternative story documents.
- **ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy)** — Values clarification and committed action; covenantal identity provides the values structure that ACT assumes but doesn't supply (Hayes, *Get Out of Your Mind and Into Your Life*, 2005 — **Established**).

**Coaching application:** The Identity Installer and Lie Eliminator are already in this space. The coaching refinement: declarations must be exegetically traceable, not constructed. The coach's job is helping the client locate the specific covenantal truth that counters the specific lie — not generating feel-good statements.

**Verdict: Already in the system. Needs rigorous exegetical backing to remain defensible. High priority to audit existing declarations for textual grounding.**

### Approach 7 — Covenant Promise Mapping (Systematic Inventory)
`[CURRICULUM]` `[SELF-COACH]` `[BIBLE-FRAMEWORK]`

**What it is:** Building a systematic map of New Covenant promises organized by category: identity, provision, protection, relationship, purpose, future. Each promise traced to its covenantal source (which covenant, which mechanism, how it applies under the New Covenant).

**Why it matters:** Prevents the common error of applying Mosaic promises directly to New Covenant believers without accounting for Christ's fulfillment. Builds a defensible promise inventory that survives theological scrutiny.

**Categories to map:**
1. **Identity** — Who am I in covenant (child, heir, priest, new creation, friend)
2. **Access** — My standing before God (boldness at the throne, Heb 4:16; no condemnation, Rom 8:1)
3. **Provision** — God's material/spiritual provision (Phil 4:19, Rom 8:32, Matt 6:25-34)
4. **Protection** — God's covenantal preservation (Ps 91, Rom 8:38-39, John 10:28-29)
5. **Purpose** — Covenant calling and gifting (Eph 2:10, 1 Pet 2:9, 1 Cor 12)
6. **Community** — Life within the covenant people (Heb 10:24-25, 1 Cor 12, Eph 4)
7. **Future** — Inheritance, resurrection, new creation (Rom 8:18-25, 1 Pet 1:3-5, Rev 21-22)

**For each promise:** (a) text, (b) which covenant it belongs to, (c) how it applies under the New Covenant, (d) what it means for the client today.

**Verdict: High value as a practitioner reference and eventual authorship resource. Medium-term project.**

### Approach 8 — Covenant Community Formation (Ecclesial Approach)
`[CURRICULUM]` `[BIBLE-SESSION]`

**What it is:** Understanding that covenant identity is never solo — it is always located within a covenant people. Individual identity formation happens within corporate covenant community.

**Biblical grounding:**
- The covenant is always made with a people, not just individuals. Even when God addresses Abraham, the covenant is about becoming a people.
- 1 Peter 2:9-10 — The covenantal identity markers (royal priesthood, holy nation, God's own possession) are *plural*. They cannot be held individually.
- Ephesians 4:11-16 — The body builds itself up in love. Covenant identity is formed and maintained in community.
- Hebrews 10:24-25 — "Not giving up meeting together" is a covenant community obligation, not an optional spiritual discipline.

**Psychological grounding:**
- **Attachment theory** (Bowlby, Ainsworth) — Secure attachment requires consistent, responsive, available relational figures. The covenant community functions as a human scaffold for the secure attachment God provides. **Established** (decades of empirical support).
- **Social identity theory** (Tajfel & Turner, 1979 — **Established**) — Group membership is a core component of self-concept. The covenant community provides the group identity within which personal covenant identity is formed and confirmed.
- **Interpersonal neurobiology** (Siegel, *The Developing Mind*, 2012 — **Supported**) — The brain is a social organ; identity is co-constructed in relationship.

**Coaching application:** For clients without a covenant community (local church), the identity work has a structural vulnerability. The coach is not a replacement for the covenant community — name this explicitly. Part of the coaching arc should include community rootedness as a covenant identity anchor, not a spiritual elective.

**Verdict: Often overlooked in identity-focused coaching systems. Important structural piece, especially for isolated clients.**

## Part 3 — Coaching Translation

### The Core Psychological Mechanism

Covenantal identity addresses the *heteronomous* vs. *autonomous* self-concept problem:

- **Self-generated identity** (autonomous) — "I am who I decide I am." Psychologically fragile; collapses under threat, failure, or loss. Requires constant maintenance.
- **Externally-grounded identity** (heteronomous from a trustworthy source) — "I am who God has declared me to be, which he has bound himself to in covenant." Psychologically load-bearing because the ground is outside the self and outside circumstance.

This maps to **Self-Determination Theory's** (Deci & Ryan, 2000 — **Established**) finding that intrinsic motivation and stable identity require internalized values from trustworthy external sources — not self-generated affirmations. The covenant is the most stable external ground available.

It also maps to **attachment theory's secure base**: the client who has internalized God as *hesed* — reliably, responsively, covenantally faithful — has the psychological secure base that produces exploration, resilience, and risk-taking. The client stuck in anxious or avoidant patterns may *intellectually* affirm covenantal truth while *functionally* operating from an insecure working model. The coaching work is closing that gap.

### What Must Be True for This to Land with a Client

The client needs to understand:
1. **What a covenant is** (not a contract, not a feeling — a sworn, unconditional bond)
2. **That they are inside one** (in Christ = in the covenant)
3. **What that means for who they are** (not who they might become — who they already are)
4. **That this is not based on their performance** (the oath was sworn and ratified before they could do anything)
5. **That the community confirms what the covenant declares** (not alone in the truth)

The client needs to *feel*:
- Security (the covenant cannot be revoked)
- Belonging (inside a covenant people, not just an abstract relationship)
- Dignity (royally granted identity, not earned rank)
- Purpose (covenant calling is not optional)

## Advisor Verdict

**Overall verdict: Solid framework with one persistent structural risk.**

The risk: building an identity formation system that uses covenant vocabulary without the exegetical precision to back it. This creates a system that *sounds* strong but cannot answer a serious theological challenge from a skeptical client, a critical pastor, or a future publisher.

**What must be done to prevent this:**
1. Approaches #1 and #5 (Biblical Theology method + "In Christ" study) are non-negotiable foundations. Without them, everything else is decoration.
2. Every declaration in the Identity Installer should be auditable back to a specific text and a specific covenantal claim. Run that audit.
3. The Mosaic/New Covenant distinction (Approach #1, interpretive debate section) must be built into how you use OT provision texts. Sloppy application here is the most common theological error in faith-based coaching.

**What's working:** The existing system (Identity Installer, Lie Eliminator, somatic framework) is structurally aligned with where the exegesis lands. The architecture is right. The depth work is what remains.

**Priority sequence for Andrew:**
1. Read Schreiner's *Covenant and God's Purpose for the World* (short — 128 pages) — immediate foundational gain
2. Study Campbell's *Paul and Union with Christ* — backbone of the "in Christ" identity mechanism
3. Run the declaration audit — check every Identity Installer declaration for textual ground
4. Build the Covenant Promise Map (#7) — this becomes a major practitioner reference document
