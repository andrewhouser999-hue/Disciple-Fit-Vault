# Covenant Identity — Spiritual Disciplines Guide

*Practitioner reference with embedded client-facing guides. The training/trying distinction (Section II) must be given in session before any discipline is assigned. Coach assigns disciplines sequentially — one or two at a time — calibrated to the client's wound type and phase. See co-optation signal tables before assigning.*

---

## How to Use This Document

1. **Give the training/trying distinction in session first** (Section II). Without this frame, disciplines become law — effortful attempts at self-transformation that produce performance rather than formation.
2. **Assign one or two disciplines at a time.** Never hand a client a comprehensive discipline list. Sequential assignment prevents the wound from selecting which ones to co-opt and which to perform.
3. **Know the co-optation risks before assigning.** For each wound type, the most commonly practiced disciplines are also the most co-opted ones. A Warrior practicing service, a Hermit practicing solitude, and a False Noble practicing prayer can all be reinforcing the wound while appearing faithful. See Section IV.
4. **Follow the phase sequencing grid** (Section V) for deployment order. The most formative and most resisted discipline for each type belongs in Phase 3 — not Phase 1.
5. **Phase 4 closes with rule-of-life construction** (Section VII). This is the transition from therapeutic tool to lifelong formation rhythm.

---

## Section I — Theological Frame: Training, Not Trying

*Give this in session, in your own language, before assigning any discipline.*

Most people approach formation by trying — applying direct effort to produce transformation. *I will try harder to trust God. I will try harder not to control. I will try to feel what I know is true.* Trying is an act of will applied directly to the target. And it fails, consistently, because the will alone cannot reach the implicit layer where the wound lives.

Willard's insight is different. Formation works through training — not trying. You do not learn to swim by trying harder to stay afloat. You train: you get in the water repeatedly, in conditions designed to build the skills, until the body knows how to swim. The trying-harder swimmer drowns. The training swimmer does not.

Spiritual disciplines are training for the whole person — body, mind, will, social dimension, soul — to receive what the Spirit works. They do not produce transformation. The Spirit produces transformation through the conditions the disciplines create. This is the distinction that makes disciplines formative rather than legalistic.

Willard names two categories:

**Disciplines of Abstinence** — creating space by setting something aside: *silence, solitude, fasting, secrecy, submission, frugality.*

**Disciplines of Engagement** — actively engaging something: *study, worship, prayer, fellowship, confession, service, celebration.*

Both are needed. Abstinence without engagement produces detachment without community. Engagement without abstinence produces activity without interiority.

The disciplines assigned in this coaching engagement are not a measure of your spiritual performance. They are a training program — targeted to your wound type, your current phase, and the specific formation work the Spirit is doing. You are not trying harder to be holy. You are training your whole person to receive what the Spirit offers.

## Section II — Client Frame: What Disciplines Are and Are Not

*Give this in session before assigning any discipline. Adapt to the client's language.*

**What they are:**
Spiritual disciplines are practices that train the whole person — body, mind, will, and relational life — to receive what God is already working. They create the conditions in which the Spirit can reach the layers of you that direct effort cannot access.

**What they are not:**
- They are not works of righteousness — ways of earning God's favor
- They are not metrics of spiritual performance
- They are not things to do correctly — there is no perfect discipline practice
- They are not a way of trying harder at transformation

The person who practices the disciplines faithfully is not one who does them perfectly. They are one who shows up — and who brings what actually happens in the practice back into the coaching conversation, including the resistance, the failure, and the co-optation.

When a discipline feels very uncomfortable, that is usually formation happening, not failure. When a discipline feels very natural and productive, that may be formation — or it may be the wound wearing the discipline as a costume. Bring both to session.

## Section III — The Discipline Library

*Brief practitioner reference. The full theological treatment is in the Practitioner Theory library: Spiritual Formation.md and Spiritual Direction — Ignatian.md.*

### Disciplines of Abstinence

**Silence** — refraining from speech and noise to create interior space. Trains the will away from self-expression toward receptivity. Engages the soul.

**Solitude** — withdrawing from social engagement to be alone with God and the interior self. Trains the social dimension away from identity-in-crowd. Engages the soul and will.

**Fasting** — refraining from food (or another normal comfort) for a period. Trains the body's appetites toward spiritual dependence. Engages the body. *Caution: physiological impact is clinically significant. Fasting activates the body's stress response — cortisol elevation, blood sugar disruption, increased emotional reactivity — which compounds the already elevated physiological load of wound work in Phase 2. For clients already carrying significant stress or trauma activation, adding fasting during Phase 2 can dysregulate the emotional system rather than create conditions for formation. Contraindicated in Phase 2 for Hermit (adds physiological stress to an already overwhelmed system); caution in Phase 2 for all types; appropriate in Phase 3 once the client is emotionally stable and the wound mapping has plateaued. Consult client's health context before assigning regardless of phase — eating disorders, chronic illness, or medication schedules may make any fasting contraindicated.*

**Secrecy** — performing acts of service, giving, or prayer without anyone knowing. Trains the social dimension away from performance and recognition. Engages the will and social dimension. *Taxonomy note: Willard classifies Secrecy as a discipline of the tongue — withholding what one has done from public knowledge — making it a form of silence extended to action. Foster classifies it under Corporate Disciplines, emphasizing communal secrecy (the left hand not knowing what the right hand does, Matt 6:3). The distinction matters for assignment: Willard's framing suits individual performance patterns (False Noble, Warrior); Foster's framing suits clients who need the community dimension of hidden service. In this model, Secrecy is assigned primarily within Willard's framing — individual acts not reported or recognized — because the False Noble's primary formation need is interrupting the recognition loop, not communal practice.*

**Submission** — placing the self under the direction of another person without controlling the outcome. Trains the will away from autonomy-as-safety toward trust. Engages the will and body.

**Frugality** — restraint in consumption and acquisition. Trains the appetite away from provision-seeking. Engages the body and will.

**Sabbath** — full weekly cessation from productive work and accomplishment, taken before everything is done. Trains the will and body away from identity-in-work toward the experience of receiving rest as a covenant rhythm. Engages the will and body. *Taxonomy note: Sabbath does not fit cleanly in Willard's abstinence/engagement categories — Willard treats it as a foundation-level rhythm rather than a numbered discipline; Foster classifies it under Corporate Disciplines. In this model it is treated as a primary discipline of abstinence, assigned first to False Noble clients because it directly confronts the striving pattern: rest is taken before completion rather than earned by it.*

### Disciplines of Engagement

**Study** — sustained, disciplined engagement with Scripture and formative texts. Trains the mind toward truth. Engages the mind. *See [[Covenant Identity — Scripture Saturation & Meditation Guide]] for formational (vs. informational) engagement.*

**Worship** — corporate and personal engagement with God's worth. Trains the affections toward their proper object. Engages the soul and affections.

**Prayer** — sustained, varied dialogue with God. Trains the whole person toward alignment with God's will and presence. Engages the whole person. *See [[Covenant Identity — Lament Practice Guide]] for the lament dimension.*

**Fellowship** — regular, intentional presence with the body of Christ. Trains the social dimension toward community as formation context. Engages the social dimension.

**Confession** — regular honest disclosure of sin and struggle to God and to one other person. Trains the will and social dimension toward transparency. Engages the will and social dimension.

**Service** — regular acts of care for others, especially those who cannot reciprocate. Trains the will away from self-serving toward other-orientation. Engages the will and body.

**Celebration** — intentional rejoicing in God, His works, and community. Trains the affections toward gratitude and joy. Engages the affections and social dimension.

## Section IV — Wound-Type Calibration

*The highest-value content in this guide. Read the co-optation section for the relevant wound type before assigning any discipline.*

---

### WARRIOR — Discipline Calibration

**Formation need:** The Warrior needs to learn what it feels like to be held rather than in control. The formation target is the control pattern — the body and will that have learned that autonomy is safety and vulnerability is betrayal. The disciplines that reach this are the ones that directly engage the control pattern.

**Most formative disciplines:**

**1. Submission** — the primary Warrior discipline. Placing the self under another's direction without modifying or improving the instruction. In practice: doing something a spouse, pastor, or trusted friend asks without adjusting the outcome to their preference. The formation: the Warrior's body and will learn that non-control does not produce the betrayal they expect. This is the discipline most resisted by the Warrior and most formative for the Warrior — assign it in Phase 3, not before.

**2. Confession** — sharing weakness and failure with one other person. Not a public confession and not a comprehensive theological disclosure — one honest thing, to one safe person, regularly. The Warrior discovers through repetition that disclosure of weakness does not produce the betrayal or contempt they protect against. This is relational healing enacted as a discipline.

**3. Silence and Solitude** — being alone with the interior without managing it. The Warrior's interior is where the wound material lives; silence forces contact rather than management. Start with short, bounded periods (10–15 minutes). The Warrior will feel the pull to fill the silence productively — this pull is the data.

**Co-optation risks and signals:**

| Discipline | Formative Signal | Co-optation Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Submission | Reports discomfort; notices their resistance; surprised that it was survivable | Reports doing it efficiently and correctly; modified the instruction to improve it |
| Confession | Reports vulnerability; fear about how they were received; relief | Reports confessing from a position of self-aware dignity; maintained control of the disclosure |
| Service | Reports being surprised by their own need; moments of receiving from those they served | Reports satisfaction in what they accomplished; remained in a superior position throughout |
| Study | Reports being unsettled or moved; the text reached something | Reports what they concluded and what they will apply; no report of being formed |
| Fasting | Reports spiritual awareness; dependence; some discomfort | Reports completing the fast; pride in duration; body mastery as the takeaway |
| Silence | Reports what surfaced; discomfort; something they didn't want to notice | Reports successfully quieting the mind; achieved a state of calm |

**Discipline to defer:** Leadership roles in community as a formation practice — the Warrior will maintain position rather than be formed. Assign service in non-leadership roles in Phase 3.

---

### HERMIT — Discipline Calibration

**Formation need:** The Hermit needs to discover that relationship is survivable — that they can be known without being consumed or abandoned. The formation target is the isolation pattern — the body and social dimension that have learned that self-erasure is safety. The disciplines that reach this are the relational ones the Hermit most avoids.

**Most formative disciplines:**

**1. Fellowship** — regular, intentional presence with safe community. This is the non-negotiable discipline for the Hermit and the first one to establish. The wound formed in relationship; it heals in relationship. The discipline is specifically showing up and staying — not performing, not contributing content, but being present. Start with one community context, one consistent commitment.

**2. Confession** — sharing the interior with one safe person. Not a comprehensive disclosure — one honest thing, regularly. The Hermit's deepest protection is interior hiddenness; confession creates the relational exposure that heals it in small, survivable doses. Assign after fellowship is established and the safe person is identified.

**3. Celebration** — receiving and sharing joy. The Hermit often cannot celebrate because celebration requires being seen in positive experience, which feels exposing. Training: participate in one act of celebration per week, actively (not as observer). The formation: joy can be shared without being consumed by the sharing.

**4. Service** — structured acts of care for others. This provides relational contact with a container — a purpose and role — that makes the contact bounded enough for the Hermit to enter. Service gets the Hermit into relational contact without requiring full self-disclosure.

**Co-optation risks and signals:**

| Discipline | Formative Signal | Co-optation Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Fellowship | Reports one moment of genuine connection; felt seen in a small way; discomfort | Reports attending without engaging; it was fine; sat at the edge |
| Confession | Reports fear before and relief after; surprised it was received | Reports explaining their situation; remained somewhat exterior |
| Solitude | Followed by relational engagement; reports specific interior content from the time | Followed by further withdrawal; reports general peace; increased isolation |
| Service | Reports contact with others; moments of seeing and being seen | Reports completing tasks efficiently; minimal relational content |
| Prayer | Complements rather than replaces relational engagement | Used as primary reason not to need community ("I'm close to God") |

**Critical co-optation warning:** Solitude and silence are legitimate, formative disciplines — and they are the Hermit's primary co-optation risk. The Hermit's solitude must always have a community bookend. Assign solitude in Phase 3 only when fellowship is already established and non-negotiable. Never assign solitude alone to a Hermit without the fellowship counterweight.

**Discipline with caution:** Fasting — physiological stress under already emotionally demanding wound work (Phase 2 especially) intensifies the withdrawal impulse. Contraindicated in Phase 2. Use with caution in Phase 3.

---

### FALSE NOBLE — Discipline Calibration

**Formation need:** The False Noble needs to discover that receiving is possible — that God gives freely, that rest is permitted, and that their value is not a function of their output. The formation target is the striving pattern — the will and social dimension that have learned that sufficiency must be earned. The disciplines that reach this are the ones that interrupt striving at the behavioral level.

**Most formative disciplines:**

**1. Sabbath** — weekly stopping before everything is done. This is the primary False Noble discipline and the first one to establish. Sabbath is structurally incompatible with the striving pattern: it requires non-completion — the work was not finished and rest is taken anyway. This is not a recovery strategy; it is a formation practice. Assign it as: stop working at a set time on a set day, regardless of what remains.

**2. Secrecy** — giving, serving, or praying without anyone knowing. One act per week that no one will observe or recognize. The formation: the act has value independent of being witnessed. The False Noble discovers that what they offer has worth that does not require an audience.

**3. Celebration** — receiving and expressing joy in what God has done, not what the False Noble has done. This is a distinct discipline from general celebration — it is specifically oriented toward God's agency. The formation: God is the giver; the False Noble receives.

**4. Submission** — placing the self under another's direction without input into the decision. One act per week: do something at someone else's request without adjusting it, improving it, or offering a better approach. The formation: the False Noble's body and will learn that their sufficiency is not required.

**Co-optation risks and signals:**

| Discipline | Formative Signal | Co-optation Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Sabbath | Reports discomfort with incompletion; anxiety about what wasn't finished; rested anyway | Reports "completing" Sabbath; used rest to recover for more productivity; tracked the Sabbath |
| Secrecy | Reports the strangeness of doing something unwitnessed; felt incomplete without recognition | Reports keeping track of secret acts; reports this as a spiritual achievement |
| Service | Reports serving from a full place; chose not to serve once and it was okay | Reports serving more than ever; volunteers first; guilt when they don't serve |
| Prayer | Reports receiving; sitting with God without petitioning | Reports praying more when they feel distant; monitors prayer life for sufficiency |
| Fasting | Engaged as one discipline among others | Reported as achievement; extended beyond assigned period; pride in duration |
| Confession | Reports genuine exposure; fear of not being understood | Reports well-organized, comprehensive confession; relief at having done it properly |
| Study | Reports being moved; the text received | Reports spiritual content accumulated; quantity of reading as evidence of formation |

**Critical co-optation warning:** The False Noble can co-opt nearly any discipline by turning it into a performance. The signal across all disciplines: the False Noble reports doing the discipline well, consistently, and with evident effort. Faithful co-optation is the False Noble's specific danger. When everything is going well, probe: *"What are you not doing? What did you skip? What happened when you didn't perform it correctly?"*

## Section V — Phase Sequencing Grid

*Coach reference. Assign from this grid when closing a session. One or two disciplines at a time — not the full column.*

| Phase | Warrior | Hermit | False Noble |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Phase 1** | Silence (10–15 min/day), Scripture engagement | Service (low-demand, bounded role), one fellowship commitment | Scripture engagement, introduce Sabbath concept (not yet full practice) |
| **Phase 2** | Silence, lament prayer | Lament prayer, fellowship commitment, service | Lament prayer, Sabbath (begin practice), secrecy (one act/week) |
| **Phase 3** | Submission (primary), confession (one person), solitude | Fellowship (non-negotiable), confession (one person), celebration | Sabbath (non-negotiable), secrecy (regular), celebration, submission |
| **Phase 4** | Rule of life: submission rhythm + confession + one engagement discipline | Rule of life: community commitment + confession rhythm + celebration practice | Rule of life: Sabbath + secrecy + one engagement discipline |

**Phase 2 caution for all types:** Fasting introduces physiological stress during emotionally demanding wound work. Do not assign fasting in Phase 2 unless the client already has an established fasting practice and is stable.

## Section VI — God-Rep Calibration Notes

*Practitioner-facing. Brief flags for disciplines that directly activate the God-rep. The God-Representation Between-Session Practices guide is the primary reference.*

**Punitive God-rep:** Extended silent prayer is activating — the client braces for accusation in the silence. Assign silence for short, bounded periods only until the God-rep has shifted. Confession feels like standing before the judge — frame explicitly with mercy language before assigning: *"You are confessing to the God who already knows and already received you in Christ."*

**Absent/Distant God-rep:** Solitude before God feels like being alone with an empty room. Fellowship and service are more accessible entry points. Do not assign extended contemplative prayer as a primary discipline until the sense of God's address has been established through the God-rep practices.

**Transactional God-rep:** Every discipline risks becoming a transaction trigger. Frame each assignment explicitly: *"This is not an input that produces God's response. You are training in the presence of the God who already gave everything."* Monitor prayer practices especially — petitionary prayer for the transactional God-rep client easily becomes a performance-trigger loop.

**Capricious/Unsafe God-rep:** Disciplines that depend on consistent experience of God (daily prayer rhythm, lectio divina, extended silence) may be destabilizing early — when nothing happens or an unexpected emotional response occurs, it confirms God's unpredictability. Begin with covenant-anchored practices that do not rely on experienced consistency: journaling promises (writing out covenant texts), service (the practice occurs regardless of felt encounter), and Sabbath framed as covenant sign rather than encounter practice. Frame each assignment: *"You are practicing whether or not you feel it working. The covenant is the anchor, not the experience."*

**Overwhelming/Consuming God-rep:** Any discipline involving extended direct encounter with God (contemplative prayer, extended worship, silent retreat) is contraindicated until encounter safety is established. Start with body-engaged disciplines (service, secrecy, Sabbath) that are formation practices without requiring extended direct encounter.

## Section VII — Phase-Variant Client-Facing Practice Guides

---

### DISCIPLINES — Phase 1 Practice Guide
*Establishing the formation container. Low demand, habit-forming.*

---

**What this is**

Before we work with specific disciplines calibrated to your wound and formation work, we establish the practice of formation itself — the daily rhythm of showing up. The disciplines assigned in Phase 1 are the foundation, not the full structure.

Disciplines are not things to do correctly. They are training — arranged practices that train your whole person to receive what the Spirit works. There is no perfect discipline practice. Show up. Bring what actually happens to your next session.

**Your Phase 1 disciplines** *(your coach will specify from the options below):*

*Warrior:* Silence for 10–15 minutes per day. No agenda — sit and notice what surfaces. Write one sentence afterward: what came? Bring it to session.

*Hermit:* One fellowship commitment per week — show up, stay the full time. Do not sit at the edges if you can help it. After: one sentence about what you noticed. Bring it to session.

*False Noble:* Begin reading about Sabbath this week *(your coach will assign a text)*. Notice your response to the concept. What resistance arises? One sentence. Bring it to session.

---

### DISCIPLINES — Phase 2 Practice Guide
*Wound-supportive disciplines. The wound is open; practices hold and ground without forcing resolution.*

---

**What changes in Phase 2**

The disciplines in Phase 2 support what the sessions are opening — they do not accelerate it further. You are not trying to work through the wound between sessions. You are practicing staying present to what is true without managing it into closure.

**Your Phase 2 disciplines** *(your coach will specify):*

*All types:* Continue the lament prayer practice from your Lament Guide. This is the primary Phase 2 discipline.

*Warrior:* Continue silence. Add one small submission practice — one thing per week done at someone's request without adjusting it. Notice the resistance. Bring it to session.

*Hermit:* Continue fellowship commitment. Add lament prayer. Do not add solitude as a discipline this phase — if you notice yourself increasing solitude, name it to your coach.

*False Noble:* Begin Sabbath practice. One day per week: stop work at a set time. Do not complete the unfinished things before stopping. Notice what arises. One sentence. Bring it to session.

---

### DISCIPLINES — Phase 3 Practice Guide
*Wound-specific formation disciplines. The most formative and most resisted.*

---

**What changes in Phase 3**

The disciplines in Phase 3 are the ones your wound most resists — which means they are the ones the Spirit most uses. The discomfort you feel in these practices is not failure. It is the wound encountering the training it needs.

Your coach will assign your primary Phase 3 discipline. It will feel hard. Bring what happens — including the resistance, the failure, and the moments when something shifted — to every session.

**Primary Phase 3 disciplines by wound type:**

*Warrior:* Submission — your coach will give you a specific form of this practice for this week. Practice it once. One sentence afterward: what happened in you when you were not in control? Bring it.

*Hermit:* Fellowship — non-negotiable. One consistent community commitment per week. Show up even when you don't want to. After: what was it like to be present? Was there one moment of contact? Bring it.

*False Noble:* Sabbath — non-negotiable. Set a stop time. Stop at that time. Do not complete the unfinished things. What arose when you stopped? Bring it.

**Secondary Phase 3 discipline** *(your coach will assign):* Confession — one honest thing, to one safe person, once per week. Not a comprehensive disclosure. One thing. Notice what it was like to be received.

---

### DISCIPLINES — Phase 4 Practice Guide
*Building a rule of life. The disciplines transition from therapeutic tool to formation rhythm.*

---

**What changes in Phase 4**

The coaching engagement is closing. The disciplines you have practiced during this engagement are not ending — they are becoming yours. Phase 4 is the work of choosing, intentionally, which 2–3 disciplines will sustain your formation beyond this engagement.

This is called a rule of life — a simple, sustainable set of formation practices built into your regular rhythm. Not all the disciplines. Not the ones you perform best. The ones that actually formed something.

**Work through these questions with your coach:**

1. *Which disciplines moved something in you during this engagement?* Not which ones you practiced most consistently — which ones reached you.
2. *Which discipline was most resisted?* The discipline you most wanted to skip is often the one most worth keeping.
3. *What is the co-optation risk of your chosen disciplines?* For each one, name: what does the wound look like using this discipline as a costume? How will you know if that is happening?
4. *What is the form, frequency, and accountability?* For each discipline: when, how often, how long, and who in your life knows you practice it.

## Section VIII — Rule-of-Life Construction

*Coach-facilitated in Phase 4. This is the closing deliverable of the engagement.*

A rule of life is a simple document — one page or less — that names the formation practices the client commits to sustaining after the engagement closes. It is not a performance contract; it is a formation intention.

**Structure:**

**My Formation Practices** *(select 2–3):*
- Discipline: ___
  - Form: what it looks like in practice
  - Frequency: how often
  - Duration: how long each time
  - Co-optation signal: what to watch for
  - Accountability: who knows and checks in

**What I am training toward:** *(one sentence — the Vision layer from Willard's VIM)*

**What I will do when I miss it:** *(name this in advance — not guilt, but return)*

*The rule of life is built in session, written by the client, and kept by the client. The coach may retain a copy for the Growth Report reference — include the rule of life in the Growth Report's "What Continues" section (Section XI) as the named forward structure the client carries out of the engagement.*
