# Covenant Identity — Accountability Companion Guide

*Practitioner reference with embedded client-facing guide. Assigned in Phase 2 or 3 — after wound work has begun, before Phase 4. Helps the client identify and engage one person as a formation companion for the duration of the engagement and beyond.*

---

## Section I — Practitioner Reference

### Deployment

Assign in Phase 2–3, after wound work has surfaced enough material that the client has something real to share with a companion, and before Phase 4 so the companion can witness some of the integration. Phase 1 is too early — the client has not yet named what they are carrying. Phase 4 is too late for the companion to function meaningfully.

**In-session before assigning:** help the client identify the right person before they leave the session to find one alone. Ask: *"Who in your life has already stayed present when things were hard for you — not because they fixed it, but because they did not leave?"* That is the primary criterion. Help the client hold it against the wound-type patterns below before they make a choice.

### Coach Watch-Fors by Wound Type

**Warrior:** will gravitate toward someone they can manage — a person who will not push back, who affirms consistently, who is safe because they are controllable. The Warrior needs a companion with enough security to say something true. Ask: *"Would this person be able to tell you something hard?"*

**Hermit:** will conclude that no one qualifies — the pool of safe people feels empty, or the exposure of asking feels too costly before the relationship has been tested. Help the Hermit shift from "who is safe" to "who is least unsafe." A partial companion is more useful than a perfect companion who does not exist.

**False Noble:** will choose someone who will be impressed by the work — a person whose admiration is also being managed. The companion relationship can become another performance context. Ask: *"Would this person still show up if nothing impressive was happening?"*

### What This Is Not

The companion is not a co-coach, a therapist, a discipleship mentor, or a crisis resource. Do not frame it that way to the client. The Crisis & Referral Protocol governs crises; the companion is not equipped or responsible for that. If the client is leaning toward someone who will function as a second coach, gently reframe before the guide is assigned.

## Section II — Client-Facing Guide

*Read this before you identify your formation companion. It will give you a clear picture of what you are looking for, what to say when you find them, and what you are — and are not — asking of them.*

---

### What a Formation Companion Is

This is not an accountability partner.

That word — accountability — invites the wrong picture: someone who monitors your behavior, receives your weekly report, and assesses whether you did what you said you would do. That is not what this is.

A formation companion is a witness. One person who knows you are in this process and is willing to be present to what it is doing in you — without needing to fix it, direct it, or manage it. Someone who can hold honest information about your life without the relationship collapsing. Someone who will still be there on the hard weeks.

One person. Not a group, not a rotation — one.

The companion you are looking for is a real person with real limitations. Dietrich Bonhoeffer wrote that the person who insists on finding the idealized version of community before entering real community will destroy whatever real community they have. The companion you need is not perfect. They are present. A companion who shows up imperfectly is more valuable than a perfect companion who exists only in your imagination.

---

### How to Identify the Right Person

Three things to look for:

**1. They have already stayed when things were hard.**
Not because they fixed it — because they did not leave. You have some evidence that this person can hold difficult things without needing to resolve them immediately. You do not need certainty. You need evidence.

**2. You do not need to perform okayness for them.**
This person can receive honest information about you — not your polished version, not your progress report — without it changing the relationship. If you would only tell this person good news, they are not the companion you need.

**3. They will tell you something true.**
Not harshly. But honestly. If this person has never said anything that cost them socially to say to you, they may not be the right choice. You need someone whose honesty you can trust, not just someone whose presence is comfortable.

What your companion does not need to be: a trained counselor, theologically sophisticated, further along in their own formation, or available on demand. They need to be present and honest. That is the full requirement.

---

**A note on who you are tempted to choose:**

*If you tend toward the Warrior pattern:* you may be drawn to someone who affirms you consistently — someone who feels safe because they will not challenge you. The companion who always agrees cannot actually witness you. Look for someone who has enough security in their own identity to say something true to you even when it is uncomfortable.

*If you tend toward the Hermit pattern:* you may conclude that no one qualifies — that the pool of people safe enough to ask is empty, or that asking itself feels like too much exposure before the relationship has been tested. Start with the least unsafe person you know. You are not looking for a safe person. You are looking for the least unsafe person available. That is enough to begin.

*If you tend toward the False Noble pattern:* you may be drawn to someone who will be impressed by what you are doing — someone whose admiration you are also managing. Notice whether you are picturing how they will respond when things go well. The right companion is someone who would still show up if nothing impressive was happening.

---

### What to Tell the Companion

When you find the right person, ask for ten minutes. You do not need to explain the entire coaching framework. Here is what to cover:

**What the engagement is:**
*"I'm in a coaching engagement focused on identity formation — working through some patterns that have shaped how I experience myself and God. It's significant work and it's going well, and I'd like to have one person outside the coaching relationship who knows what's happening."*

**What you are asking them to do:**
*"I'm not asking you to coach me or counsel me or have any particular expertise. I'm asking you to check in with me occasionally and receive what I share honestly. When I tell you something, I'd find it most helpful if you reflected it back or asked one question rather than jumping to advice or a solution. Just being present is the main thing."*

**What they can expect from you:**
*"I'll reach out occasionally — not on a schedule, just when something is worth sharing. I don't need a weekly call. I just want someone who knows this is happening."*

---

### What to Ask of the Companion

Three asks. Keep them simple.

**1. Be present.**
Check in occasionally — or receive it when you do. The frequency is yours to set, not theirs to manage.

**2. Receive honestly.**
When you share something, ask them to reflect it back or ask one question before advising. "What I heard you say is..." or "What was that like for you?" is exactly right. Advice and spiritual solutions come later, if at all.

**3. Hold what is shared.**
What you tell them stays between the two of you. They do not bring it up at inopportune times. They do not make it a project.

---

**What your companion is NOT being asked to do:**

- Coach you or provide structured direction
- Counsel you or process your story therapeutically
- Teach you or provide biblical instruction
- Hold you accountable to behavioral commitments
- Evaluate your progress or tell you whether you are doing the work correctly
- Be available in crisis — that is not their role and it is not fair to ask it of them

If something rises to crisis level, the Crisis & Referral Protocol your coach provided is what governs that — not your companion.

---

### After the Conversation

When you have identified your companion and had the initial conversation, tell your coach. You do not need to share what you told the companion — just that you have one. Your coach may have one or two questions to help you make the most of the relationship.

The companion does not replace the coaching engagement. They extend it — into the ordinary weeks between sessions, into the parts of your life the coach cannot see. They are a thread of honest witness that holds across the whole engagement and, ideally, beyond it.
