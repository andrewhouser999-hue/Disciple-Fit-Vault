# Covenant Identity — God-Representation Between-Session Practices

*Practitioner reference with embedded client-facing practice guides. The explicit/implicit God-image distinction must be given in session before this guide is assigned. Coach identifies the God-rep type, selects the appropriate section, and hands the client-facing portion to the client.*

---

## How to Use This Document

1. **Identify the God-rep type in session before assigning any practice.** The type must be named and the client must recognize it as their functional experience — not merely a theological category. If the client cannot yet distinguish between their stated theology of God and their lived experience of Him, give the explicit/implicit frame (Section I) before the assignment goes home.
2. **Address shame first.** Some clients carry significant shame about their God-rep: *I know I shouldn't feel this way about God.* Shame about the distortion must be named and released in session before transformation practices are assigned — otherwise the client performs the practice while hiding the real image.
3. **Select the phase-appropriate practice.** Phase 1 is observational only — noticing, not transforming. Phase 2 deepens awareness. Phase 3 is the primary transformation phase; Gospel encounter practices are the central mechanism here. Phase 4 sustains what has shifted.
4. **For phase-level and God-rep/wound-type precondition logic,** see [[Covenant Identity — Between-Session Support Materials]] and [[Covenant Identity — Character Wound Between-Session Practices]].
5. **Gospel encounter is the primary Phase 3 mechanism.** See Section II for how to assign and frame Gospel encounter practices. Do not substitute information-based reading.

## Section I — The Explicit/Implicit Distinction

*Give this in session, in your own language, before assigning any practice. The client needs this frame to understand why the practices are needed.*

Most people who have been Christians for any length of time can state what they believe about God: He is loving, faithful, good, present, forgiving. These are true. They are also often disconnected from what the person functionally experiences when they actually come to God.

The explicit God-image is what you say and think about God. The implicit God-image is what you feel and expect when you come to Him — the image that operates below conscious theology, shaped by early relational experience and the particular wounds your story carries. The two can differ significantly.

A person can correctly state that God is not punishing and yet flinch in prayer, bracing for accusation. A person can state that God is present and yet carry a settled sense that God is not particularly interested in them. These are not intellectual failures — they are implicit images, stored in relational memory before the theology was formed.

The implicit God-image shifts through relational experience — through encounter with the God who is actually there, repeated over time, until the body and the deep self begin to know what the mind already confesses. This is why these practices are not primarily about learning more about God. They are about encountering Him in ways that reach below the surface.

## Section II — Gospel Encounter: The Primary Phase 3 Mechanism

*Frame this for the client before assigning Phase 3 practices. Gospel encounter is not Bible study.*

The most effective practice for shifting the implicit God-image is reading the Gospels slowly, attending to how Jesus actually treated people — not extracting principles, but encountering the God made visible in flesh. This is not Bible study. It is encounter.

**How to read a Gospel encounter:**
1. Read the passage slowly, once through.
2. Ask: *Where do I find myself in this story?*
3. Ask: *What does Jesus's response feel like — directed at me?*
4. Do not extract an application or lesson. Receive what you notice.
5. Bring what comes up to your next session.

The Spirit uses these texts to make God's character experientially real — not propositionally known, but relationally encountered. The implicit image shifts as the client repeatedly meets a Jesus who does not match the distorted representation.

**Gospel Encounter Library by God-Rep Type:**

| God-Rep | Passage | What It Addresses |
|---|---|---|
| Punitive | John 8:1–11 | Jesus did not condemn — "Neither do I condemn you" |
| Punitive | Mark 1:40–42 | Jesus touched the untouchable — "I will; be clean" |
| Absent/Distant | Luke 19:1–10 | Jesus sought Zacchaeus by name before Zacchaeus sought Him |
| Absent/Distant | Mark 10:46–52 | "What do you want me to do for you?" — Jesus asked the individual |
| Absent/Distant | John 11:32–36 | Jesus wept — He was not unmoved |
| Permissive/Safe | Mark 10:17–22 | Jesus loved him and said something hard |
| Permissive/Safe | John 21:15–17 | Reinstatement through honest confrontation |
| Transactional | Luke 15:11–24 | The father ran before the rehearsed speech landed |
| Transactional | Romans 5:8 | While we were still sinners — before any response |
| Capricious/Unsafe | Luke 18:1–8 | The persistent widow — God's response to honest, consistent prayer is not arbitrary |
| Capricious/Unsafe | Mark 6:45–52 | "Take heart; it is I. Do not be afraid" — consistent, identifying presence in chaos |
| Capricious/Unsafe | John 6:37 | "Whoever comes to me I will never cast out" — structural consistency, not probability |
| Overwhelming | Matthew 11:28–30 | I am gentle and lowly — rest, not overwhelm |
| Overwhelming | Matthew 17:7 | Jesus touched them and said "Rise, have no fear" |
| Overwhelming | John 20:19 | Peace be with you — to frightened, hiding disciples |

> **Cross-assignment caution:** Do not assign Permissive/Safe passages (Mark 10:17–22; John 21:15–17) to Punitive-type clients. These texts feature Jesus saying something hard to someone He loves — which serves the Permissive/Safe client by introducing honest confrontation, but lands for the Punitive client as confirmation that God finds fault. A client who is already bracing for accusation will read the "hard word" passages through a punitive lens and have their image reinforced rather than challenged. Reserve these texts exclusively for clients whose functional image is one of comfortable, unchallenging approval.

## PUNITIVE GOD-REPRESENTATION

### Type Profile
**Distortion:** God is a demanding judge who monitors for failure and whose love is conditional on performance. The client's theology knows God is gracious; their functional experience is one of being watched and found wanting.
**How it shows up:** Prayer feels like confession-or-else. Mistakes trigger shame spirals connected to God. Scripture lands as accusation. Grace feels suspicious — unearned and therefore temporary. God's silence feels like disapproval.
**Wound-type correlation:** Frequently pairs with Warrior (the betraying authority demanded performance; God feels the same).
**Spirit's transforming work:** Making God's mercy and delight in this person experientially real — not as doctrine but as encounter. The punitive image shifts as the client repeatedly meets the God who ran toward the prodigal, who did not condemn the woman in adultery, who blesses the poor in spirit.

---

### PUNITIVE — Phase 1: Noticing
*Observational only. Do not assign transformation practices yet.*

**Client-facing:**
Once this week, notice: *When did God feel like a judge watching for failure?* One sentence — no analysis. Bring it to the next session.

---

### PUNITIVE — Phase 2: Awareness Deepening
**Practitioner note:** The client may feel shame about this observation. Normalize it: the image is not their fault; it formed for reasons. The practice surfaces it, not condemns it.

**Client-facing:**
**1. Functional God-image journal — once**
Write one paragraph answering this question honestly — not theologically, experientially: *What does God feel like after you make a mistake?*

Do not write what He is supposed to feel like. Write what He actually feels like. Bring it to session.

**2. Gospel encounter**
Read John 8:1–11 slowly, once through. Follow the encounter reading method from your coach. One question to sit with: *What does Jesus's response to her feel like — directed at you?*

---

### PUNITIVE — Phase 3: Encounter-Based Transformation
**Practitioner note:** The primary practices are encounter-based (Gospel reading + prayer from mercy, not demand). Declaration is supporting, not primary. Watch for the client receiving the mercy passage intellectually while their body remains braced. Ask in session: *What did it feel like — not what did it mean?*

**Client-facing:**
**1. Mercy-entry prayer — daily, 2–3 minutes**
Begin prayer from God's invitation, not your adequacy. Speak one sentence before anything else:

> *Lord, I come not because I have performed well enough, but because You invited me.*

Hold it for a moment. Then continue the prayer as you would.

**2. Weekly Gospel encounter**
Once per week, read one passage from the Punitive God-Rep list. Use the encounter method your coach gave you. Assign yourself one question only: *What does this show me about who God actually is toward me?*

**3. Declaration — spoken aloud daily**
> *God is not waiting for me to fail. He is for me.* (Romans 8:31, ESV)

Speak it slowly. If it feels untrue, speak it anyway. Formation reaches the body through repetition before conviction arrives.

---

### PUNITIVE — Phase 4: Sustainability

**Client-facing:**
**Personal mercy library:** Compile 4–5 Gospel encounters that have genuinely shifted something in your experience of God. Return to them annually — not to extract new lessons, but to mark how far the image has moved.

**When the punitive image resurfaces** (it will): Named practice — speak it directly to God: *Lord, I notice I am experiencing You as judge right now. I know this is not the whole of who You are. Show me who You are.* This is itself a form of lament and honest address. Bring it to Him rather than managing it alone.

## ABSENT/DISTANT GOD-REPRESENTATION

### Type Profile
**Distortion:** God is real in theology but experientially uninvolved — indifferent, unresponsive to this particular person. Prayer feels like speaking into silence. God may be present for others but not for the client specifically.
**How it shows up:** Prayer feels futile or one-directional. Scripture feels like information about God rather than encounter with Him. Significant life events feel unwitnessed. God's silence is interpreted as indifference, not mystery.
**Wound-type correlation:** Warrior (abandoned by those who should have protected) or False Noble (God gives to others, not to me specifically).
**Spirit's transforming work:** Making God's attentiveness to this specific person real — not God-in-general, but God who pursues and attends to the individual. The absent image shifts as the client encounters the God who sought Zacchaeus, who asked Bartimaeus by name what he wanted, who wept at Lazarus's tomb.

---

### ABSENT/DISTANT — Phase 1: Noticing

**Client-facing:**
Once this week, notice: *When did God feel absent, uninvolved, or uninterested in what you were carrying?* One sentence. Bring it to session.

---

### ABSENT/DISTANT — Phase 2: Awareness Deepening
**Practitioner note:** The counterevidence journal is not denial of the absence experience — it is actively looking for what the absent image has filtered out. Frame it this way to the client.

**Client-facing:**
**1. Counterevidence journal — once**
Write one paragraph: *Where has God shown up — even quietly — in the last month?* Do not include only the obvious moments. Look for the small ones the absent image would have passed over. Bring it to session.

**2. Gospel encounter**
Read Luke 19:1–10 slowly, once. Zacchaeus did not seek Jesus — Jesus sought him, named him, and insisted on coming to his house. One question: *What does it mean that God sought this one person out by name?*

---

### ABSENT/DISTANT — Phase 3: Encounter-Based Transformation
**Practitioner note:** The "witnessed prayer" is the primary practice — it trains the habit of assuming God's presence and attention rather than His absence. This is a behavioral practice as much as a spiritual one. Monitor whether the client is doing it or finding it too strange to sustain; the strangeness is the distortion resisting.

**Client-facing:**
**1. Witnessed prayer — daily, 3 minutes**
Once per day, speak one sentence to God about something that happened today — as if to someone who was there and cares:

> *Lord, today I experienced ___. I'm bringing it to You.*

That is the whole practice. You are not asking for anything. You are practicing the assumption that He is present and attentive. Do this even when it feels performative. The feeling follows the practice, not the other way around.

**2. Weekly Gospel encounter**
Once per week, read one passage from the Absent/Distant list. One question: *What does it mean that Jesus attended to this one specific person?*

**3. Declaration — spoken aloud daily**
> *God sees me. He is not indifferent to what I am carrying.* (Psalm 139:1–3, ESV)

---

### ABSENT/DISTANT — Phase 4: Sustainability

**Client-facing:**
**Weekly witnessed reflection:** Once per week, a 5-minute conversation with God about the week — naming what happened as if to someone who attended to it with you. This is not a prayer list; it is a relational account. The absent image is countered by repeatedly practicing the presence that is already real.

**Annual encounter return:** Once per year, return to the Gospel texts that most concretely showed you God's attentiveness to a specific person — Luke 19:1–10 (Zacchaeus sought by name), Mark 10:46–52 (Bartimaeus heard across the crowd), John 11:32–36 (Jesus wept). Read one slowly. Notice whether your experience of the text has changed — not whether you believe it differently, but whether it lands differently in your body. The absent image recedes through accumulated encounter; this is a formation audit, not a theology review.

## PERMISSIVE/SAFE GOD-REPRESENTATION

### Type Profile
**Distortion:** God is entirely affirming, never confrontational, always validating. He has no demands and no edges. He is safe in the sense of presenting no challenge. This image feels comfortable — which is precisely its danger. The permissive God does not call anything into question, and so nothing changes.
**How it shows up:** Spiritual life feels pleasant but surface-level. Scripture's harder texts are consistently softened. No experience of holy weight or sacred invitation to change. God's love is experienced as blanket approval rather than transforming love.
**Wound-type correlation:** Any wound type — the permissive image is often a defensive construction protecting against the more threatening alternative underneath. Moving the permissive image too quickly may surface the punitive one; proceed carefully.
**Spirit's transforming work:** Introducing the God who loves too well to leave the client where they are — whose love is not permissive but pursuing, not approving of the current state but calling the person toward their true self. Holy and tender held together.

---

### PERMISSIVE/SAFE — Phase 1: Noticing
**Practitioner note:** This type is the most difficult to surface — the client may not experience anything as wrong. Frame the noticing practice in terms of God's absence of response, not His failure.

**Client-facing:**
Once this week, notice: *Was there a moment when God felt like He had no response to something — just blanket approval, no engagement?* One sentence. Bring it to session.

---

### PERMISSIVE/SAFE — Phase 2: Awareness Deepening

**Client-facing:**
**1. Imagination exercise — once**
Write one paragraph responding to this question: *What would it mean if God actually had a response — not punitive, but real — to something you're currently doing or avoiding?* What does that possibility feel like?

**2. Gospel encounter**
Read Mark 10:17–22 slowly, once. Jesus looked at him and loved him — and then said something hard. The love and the hard word came together. One question: *What does that combination feel like?*

---

### PERMISSIVE/SAFE — Phase 3: Encounter-Based Transformation
**Practitioner note:** The "holy love" prayer is deliberately open-ended — it invites the Spirit to define what holy love looks like rather than the coach prescribing it. Watch for the client filling the prayer with their own content prematurely. The practice is to hold the request and wait.

**Client-facing:**
**1. Holy love prayer — weekly, 5 minutes**
Once per week, speak this prayer and hold it:

> *Lord, I want to know You as You actually are — not only as the God who approves, but as the God who loves me too well to leave me where I am. Show me.*

Sit with it afterward. Do not produce anything. Let the Spirit work with the request.

**2. Weekly Gospel encounter**
Once per week, read one passage where Jesus's love included a hard word or a real call. One question: *What does love that actually transforms look like, distinct from love that only affirms?*

**3. Declaration — spoken aloud daily**
> *God loves me too well to leave me where I am. His love is the love that makes me whole.* (Hebrews 12:10, ESV)

---

### PERMISSIVE/SAFE — Phase 4: Sustainability

**Client-facing:**
**Annual prophetic text:** Once per year, read one prophetic text that holds holy love and holy weight together — Hosea 11 (God's anguished love for Israel) or Ezekiel 34 (God's anger at false shepherds and His tender seeking of the lost sheep). Let the combination expand the image of who God is.

## TRANSACTIONAL GOD-REPRESENTATION

### Type Profile
**Distortion:** God as a ledger-keeper. When you perform — prayer, obedience, service, faith — He gives. When you don't, He withholds. The relationship is contractual rather than covenantal. Grace is not free; it must be triggered by sufficient inputs.
**How it shows up:** Prayer is heavily petitionary and performance-linked. Spiritual practices feel obligatory — skipping them produces anxiety about God's response. Blessings are interpreted as rewards; suffering as punishment or evidence of insufficient faith.
**Wound-type correlation:** False Noble (striving to receive what should come freely). The transactional image and the False Noble wound reinforce each other directly.
**Spirit's transforming work:** Making grace as unearned gift genuinely real — not assented to as doctrine but received as lived experience. The client needs to encounter God's initiative before their performance, His giving before their deserving.

---

### TRANSACTIONAL — Phase 1: Noticing

**Client-facing:**
Once this week, notice: *When did your relationship with God feel transactional — if I do ___, then God will ___?* One sentence. Bring it to session.

---

### TRANSACTIONAL — Phase 2: Awareness Deepening
**Practitioner note:** The counterevidence journal for the transactional type looks for God giving before performance. This surfaces data the transactional image systematically filters out.

**Client-facing:**
**1. Prior-initiative journal — once**
Write one paragraph: *When did God give you something — grace, provision, help, presence — before you had done anything to trigger it?* Look for the moments the transactional image would have credited to your performance. Bring it to session.

**2. Gospel encounter**
Read Luke 15:11–24 slowly, once. The father ran while the son was still a long way off — before the rehearsed speech landed, before the apology was complete. One question: *What does that prior initiative feel like?*

---

### TRANSACTIONAL — Phase 3: Encounter-Based Transformation
**Practitioner note:** The receiving posture prayer is the most important practice for the transactional type — it behaviorally enacts non-transaction. Watch for the client turning the prayer itself into a transaction ("if I receive posture correctly, God will give"). Name this risk explicitly when assigning.

**Client-facing:**
**1. Receiving posture prayer — daily, 3 minutes**
Sit. Hands open, palms up. Speak one sentence:

> *Lord, I receive what You give — not because I have earned it, but because You give it freely.*

Hold the posture. Do not petition. Do not list what you need. This practice is receiving, not requesting. The Holy Spirit meets the open hand; you are not generating the gift.

**2. Weekly Gospel encounter**
Once per week, read one passage where God's action came before the person's merit or response. One question: *Where did God's action come before mine?*

**3. Declaration — spoken aloud daily**
> *God gives to me because of who He is, not because of what I have done.* (Romans 8:32, ESV)

---

### TRANSACTIONAL — Phase 4: Sustainability

**Client-facing:**
**Sabbath as anti-transactional practice:** Weekly Sabbath — stopping before the transaction is complete, resting in provision that does not depend on output. This is the discipline that structurally interrupts the transactional pattern. Frame it explicitly: *I am stopping before I have finished. God's provision does not require my completion.*

---

## CAPRICIOUS/UNSAFE GOD-REPRESENTATION

### Type Profile
**Distortion:** God is real and present but unpredictable and potentially threatening. His responses to faithful behavior cannot be anticipated — blessings and harm arrive without discernible pattern. No rule can be found that produces safety. Even obedience and prayer offer no guarantee of protection from an arbitrary negative response.
**How it shows up:** Prayer is tentative and hedging — the client does not fully commit to requests because God might respond unexpectedly. Spiritual disciplines are maintained with low expectation. Significant life events are approached with bracing rather than trust. Intimacy with God is avoided because closeness increases exposure. Theology explains suffering through "mystery" but functionally lives as though God is dangerous.
**Wound-type correlation:** Hermit (specifically when the terror came from unpredictable or volatile relational experience, not merely overwhelming intensity). Warrior in presentations where betrayal was arbitrary — where faithfulness did not prevent abandonment and no pattern could be found. The Capricious image forms when early relational figures were present but volatile, or when significant suffering arrived without coherent theological explanation.
**Spirit's transforming work:** Making God's covenant consistency experientially real — not God as rule-follower but God as faithful to His own self-revealed character. *Hesed* (steadfast love that does not waver with circumstance) is the theological anchor. The capricious image shifts as the client encounters the God who is the same yesterday, today, and forever — whose covenant faithfulness is not subject to arbitrary revision.

---

### CAPRICIOUS/UNSAFE — Phase 1: Noticing
**Practitioner note:** This type often presents with a surface theology of God's goodness alongside a functional experience of needing to stay on guard. The noticing practice may initially surface as low-grade vigilance rather than named fear. Name it carefully — do not push toward the fear language before the client finds it.

**Client-facing:**
Once this week, notice: *When did God feel unpredictable — when did you brace for an arbitrary or negative response, even when you had done nothing wrong?* One sentence. No analysis. Bring it to session.

---

### CAPRICIOUS/UNSAFE — Phase 2: Awareness Deepening
**Practitioner note:** The consistency journal asks the client to find reliability where the capricious image has filtered for unpredictability. Frame it explicitly: this exercise does not deny the painful experiences — it asks whether the image has been filtering out evidence of consistency that was also there.

**Client-facing:**
**1. Covenant consistency journal — once**
Write one paragraph: *Where has God been consistent — not dramatic, simply reliable?* What has He always done? What has He never done? Look for the pattern the capricious image would have labeled as luck or coincidence. Bring it to session.

**2. Gospel encounter**
Read Luke 18:1–8 slowly, once. Jesus's argument: the God who hears persistent, honest prayer responds consistently — He is not arbitrary about justice. One question: *What does it mean that God's response to honest, consistent prayer is itself consistent?*

---

### CAPRICIOUS/UNSAFE — Phase 3: Encounter-Based Transformation
**Practitioner note:** The covenant anchor prayer is the primary practice — it names God's self-revealed consistency before prayer begins, interrupting the default bracing posture. Watch for the client speaking the anchor sentence while the body remains tense. Ask in session: *What did it feel like to say that? Did anything in your body follow, or did it stay braced?*

**Client-facing:**
**1. Covenant anchor prayer — daily, 2–3 minutes**
Speak one sentence before you begin the rest of prayer:

> *Lord, You have told me who You are, and You do not change. I come to You from that.*

Hold it a moment. Then continue as you would. You are not trying to generate confidence — you are naming what is already true before approaching.

**2. Weekly Gospel encounter**
Once per week, read one passage from the Capricious/Unsafe list. One question: *Where is God's response consistent — not arbitrary?*

**3. Declaration — spoken aloud daily**
> *God is not unpredictable. He is faithful to who He has revealed Himself to be.* (Malachi 3:6; Hebrews 13:8, ESV)

---

### CAPRICIOUS/UNSAFE — Phase 4: Sustainability

**Client-facing:**
**Annual covenant faithfulness review:** Once per year, identify one or two places God has been consistent over the past year. Not dramatic — simply faithful. Name it specifically. The capricious image does not dissolve through theological argument; it recedes through accumulated encounter with the God who showed up in a particular way, in a particular place, for this particular person.

**When the capricious image resurfaces** (it will, especially in suffering or loss): Speak it directly to God: *Lord, I am experiencing You as unpredictable right now. I know You have said You do not change. Help me trust what I cannot feel.* This is honest address, not contradiction. Bring what surfaces to the next session rather than resolving it alone.

## OVERWHELMING/CONSUMING GOD-REPRESENTATION

> **Coach Note:** This God-rep type was added as a discrete fifth type 2026-05-19. It is clinically distinct from all other types and incompatible with their practices — do not assign Punitive, Absent/Distant, Permissive/Safe, Transactional, or Capricious/Unsafe practices to Overwhelming/Consuming clients. The [[Covenant Identity — God-Representation — Research Basis & Synthesis]] (Doc 11) was updated 2026-05-19 to include this type; the flag is resolved.

### Type Profile
**Distortion:** God's presence is too much — intense, consuming, unsafe to approach fully. Full encounter feels like annihilation or being swallowed. The client may genuinely desire God but comes to Him only partially, holding something back.
**How it shows up:** Avoidance of extended prayer, worship, or spiritual retreat — they trigger overwhelm or anxiety. Holding back in spiritual community. God's holiness experienced as threat rather than invitation. Sometimes expressed as *I'm afraid of what God might ask of me if I get too close.*
**Wound-type correlation:** Strongly correlates with Hermit (terror/too much). The Hermit's wound and this God-rep are often mirror images — the same relational fear transferred onto God.
**Spirit's transforming work:** Making God's gentleness and bounded safety experientially real. The client needs to encounter the God who is holy and tender simultaneously — whose holiness does not consume but gently purifies, whose presence the disciples survived, whose touch preceded the command to rise. *He will quiet you with his love* (Zephaniah 3:17, ESV).

---

### OVERWHELMING — Phase 1: Noticing
**Practitioner note:** Do not press the client to elaborate on this observation in early Phase 1. The noticing practice is already a mild form of approaching what feels unsafe. Hold it lightly.

**Client-facing:**
Once this week, notice: *When did approaching God feel dangerous, too intense, or like it might be too much?* One sentence only. No analysis required. Bring it to session.

---

### OVERWHELMING — Phase 2: Awareness Deepening
**Practitioner note:** The "what I hold back" journal is a significant ask for this type — naming what they keep from God can feel exposing. Frame it carefully: this is not a confession; it is an observation. They are not yet bringing the held-back material — only naming it.

**Client-facing:**
**1. What I hold back — once**
Write one paragraph, honestly: *What do you hold back when you come to God? What feels unsafe to bring fully?*

You are not yet bringing it. You are only naming what it is. Bring the paragraph to session.

**2. Gospel encounter**
Read Matthew 11:28–30 slowly, once. *I am gentle and lowly in heart.* One question only: *What does "gentle and lowly" mean about what it is like to come to Jesus?*

---

### OVERWHELMING — Phase 3: Encounter-Based Transformation
**Practitioner note:** The bounded encounter prayer is the most architecturally important practice for this type. The time limit is not a concession — it is the design. The Hermit/Overwhelming type needs to discover that encounter with God is survivable and contained before the boundary can expand. Do not encourage extension until the client consistently reports that the 5 minutes felt safe.

**Client-facing:**
**1. Bounded encounter prayer — daily, 5 minutes exactly**
Set a timer for five minutes. Sit. Speak:

> *Lord, I come to You for these five minutes. I trust that You will not overwhelm me. I am here.*

Stay for the full five minutes. When the timer ends, stop. Do not extend it — the boundary is part of the practice. You are learning that encounter with God is survivable, that He does not consume you, that you can leave and return.

**2. Weekly Gospel encounter**
Once per week, read one passage from the Overwhelming God-Rep list — particularly passages showing Jesus's response to the afraid and overwhelmed. One question: *What does Jesus do with fear? What does that mean about coming to God afraid?*

**3. Declaration — spoken aloud daily**
> *God will not consume me. He receives me gently. I can come.* (Zephaniah 3:17; Matthew 11:29, ESV)

---

### OVERWHELMING — Phase 4: Sustainability

**Client-facing:**
**Gradual expansion:** Once per month, extend the bounded encounter time by two to three minutes — only as the previous length consistently feels safe. Track this with your coach. The goal over time is not a fixed duration but a settled sense that God's presence is receivable, not dangerous.

**Annual return:** Read Zephaniah 3:17 and Matthew 11:28–30 together, once per year. Notice how your experience of "gentle and lowly" has changed from when you first read it.

## Practitioner Notes: What to Watch For Across All Types

| God-Rep Type | Common In-Session Signal | Response |
|---|---|---|
| Punitive | Client reports practicing but remains braced — body language of anticipating judgment | Ask: *What did it feel like — not what did it mean?* The body holds the image. |
| Punitive | Client intellectualizes the Gospel encounter rather than feeling it | *Where did you find yourself in the story?* Return to the narrative, not the theology. |
| Absent/Distant | Client reports the witnessed prayer felt like talking to no one | Normalize. The practice precedes the experience of presence. Ask: *What would it be like if He were actually there?* |
| Absent/Distant | Client credits to coincidence what may be God's attention | Surface it gently: *What if that wasn't coincidence?* |

## Load-Bearing vs. Modifiable

**Load-bearing** — these elements must not be adjusted without disrupting the model's integrity:

- **Shame addressed before transformation practices.** If the client carries shame about their God-rep distortion (*I know I shouldn't feel this way*), that shame must be named and released in session before any practice is assigned. Practices given to a shamed client produce performance compliance, not encounter.
- **Explicit/implicit distinction given in session before assignment.** Clients who cannot yet distinguish their stated theology from their functional experience of God do not know what they are working on. This frame is not optional — give it before any practice goes home.
- **Phase 1 is observational only.** No transformation practices in Phase 1. Observational practices surface the image; transformation practices require the image to be named and held with some stability before it can shift.
- **Gospel encounter is the primary Phase 3 mechanism.** It is not substitutable with information-based reading (commentary, devotional, doctrinal text). The encounter method works because it places the client in relational contact with the God made visible — extracting principles defeats the mechanism. Substituting Bible study for Gospel encounter misses the implicit level.
- **The Overwhelming type's 5-minute limit is structural, not arbitrary.** It exists because the Hermit/Overwhelming type needs to discover that encounter is survivable and bounded before the boundary can expand. Encouraging extension before the client consistently reports safety replicates the wound rather than healing it.

**Modifiable** — these elements can be adjusted to fit the client without disrupting the model:

- **Passage selection within a type's library** — the Gospel Encounter Library offers multiple options per type; the coach selects based on what has already landed or what addresses the most active distortion
- **Declaration wording** — the provided declarations are anchors, not scripts; coaches may substitute a Scripture passage in the client's own ESV text that carries the same function
- **Practice duration** (non-Overwhelming types) — the suggested durations (2–3 min, 3 min, 5 min) are starting points; adjust to the client's capacity and phase without removing the practice class
- **Practice frequency** — weekly and daily anchors are the design; modest adjustment (e.g., 4x/week instead of daily) is acceptable for clients with acute load; do not reduce below 3x/week for daily practices in Phase 3
