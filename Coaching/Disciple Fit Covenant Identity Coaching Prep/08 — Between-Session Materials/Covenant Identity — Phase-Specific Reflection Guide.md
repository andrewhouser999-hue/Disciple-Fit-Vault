# Covenant Identity — Phase-Specific Reflection Guide

*Practitioner reference with embedded client-facing reflection guides. The client frame (Section I) must be given in session before any phase guide is assigned. Coach assigns the phase-appropriate section and reviews what surfaces in the next session — but does not require the client to share entries.*

---

## How to Use This Document

1. **Give the client frame in session first** (Section I). Without it, clients default to analysis mode. The distinction between noticing and processing is the load-bearing frame for this entire tool.
2. **Assign the phase-appropriate section only.** Do not hand the client multiple phases at once. Assign Phase 1 in Phase 1, Phase 2 in Phase 2. Assigning Phase 3 prompts before the wound has been adequately mapped produces premature reorientation.
3. **Do not require the client to share entries.** Journaling-as-homework-to-be-graded produces performance — especially for False Noble clients. The journal is the client's own record. Sharing is their choice, offered to the coach if useful for session.
4. **Use what surfaces in session.** The primary practitioner benefit of this tool: the client arrives with material already noticed from the week. Begin the session by asking what they noticed — not by reviewing the journal.
5. **See wound-type calibration notes** in each phase section before assigning.

---

## Section I — Client Frame: What This Practice Is

*Give this in session, in your own language, before assigning any reflection practice.*

This is not journaling the way most people do it. Most journaling is processing — thinking through what happened, working toward a conclusion, analyzing what you feel and what it means. That has its place. This is different.

What these prompts ask you to do is notice. Not interpret, not resolve, not conclude — notice. What is present? What surfaced? What stayed with you from the session without you choosing it? What did you feel in your body? What did you hold back?

The distinction matters because formation happens below the level of what you can analyze. The Spirit works in the interior before understanding arrives. Your job in this practice is to observe what is there — not to make something happen or produce insight. You are a witness to your own formation, not its manager.

**Before you open the journal each time, speak or think one sentence:**

> *Holy Spirit, surface what You want me to notice. I'm listening.*

This is not a formula. It is an orienting posture — you are not processing alone.

**One rule across all phases:** Do not write what you think a person who is growing should write. Write what is actually true, even if it is incomplete, confused, or uncomfortable. Incomplete and honest is always better than polished and managed.

## PHASE 1 — Orientation

### Practitioner Notes
Phase 1 is observation without conclusion. The client is learning the framework — identifying their wound type, God-rep, and the gap between declared and lived covenant identity. They do not yet have enough information to conclude what things mean. Prompts here help them observe their current interior landscape before the excavation begins.

**Wound-type calibration:**
- *Warrior:* Will want to draw conclusions quickly — "So what this means is..." Remind them that the prompt is observation only. No interpretation required.
- *Hermit:* May write one sentence or nothing. Tell them explicitly: *"One sentence is enough. Write nothing if nothing comes — that is honest."* Do not push for more.
- *False Noble:* Will produce a well-organized entry showing they understand the framework. The prompt is not to demonstrate comprehension — it is to notice honestly. Name this in advance.

**God-rep calibration:** Prompt 4 ("God's presence or absence") is the most direct Phase 1 God-rep diagnostic. Receive what comes without reframing. **Punitive type** will likely report God as evaluating or watching rather than present — this is accurate data, not a problem to solve yet. **Absent/Distant type** may report nothing at all; "I didn't sense Him" is honest and useful. **Transactional type** may qualify presence with performance ("I sensed Him after I spent time in the Word") — note the correlation without challenging it in Phase 1. **Capricious/Unsafe type** may report variable or inconsistent experiences; receive both as honest. **Overwhelming/Consuming type** may feel anxious about the "where did you sense God" question itself — normalize.

**Time guidance:** 10–15 minutes. Longer becomes processing.

### PHASE 1 — Client-Facing Reflection Guide

*Use these prompts once or twice per week between sessions. Choose 1–2 that feel relevant — you do not need to answer all of them. Begin with the Spirit-invitation your coach gave you.*

---

**Prompt 1 — Noticing patterns**
What did you notice about yourself this week — in your reactions, your emotional responses, or your habitual ways of responding to difficulty or demand? Not what you think it means. Just what you noticed.

**Prompt 2 — Presence to yourself**
When did you feel most like yourself this week? When did you feel least like yourself? Describe the moments briefly — where you were, what was happening.

**Prompt 3 — What stayed**
What word, image, or moment from this week's session has stayed with you — returned without you choosing it? Write it down without explaining why.

**Prompt 4 — God's presence or absence**
Where did you sense God this week — even faintly? Where did you notice His absence? One sentence for each, or nothing if nothing came. Both are honest answers.

## PHASE 2 — Wound Mapping

### Practitioner Notes
The wound is open. This is the most delicate phase for reflection — prompts that push for insight, silver lining, or resolution will produce premature reorientation. The session has opened something; the between-session practice holds it with honesty, not management.

The primary failure mode in Phase 2 journaling is the client processing the wound to closure on paper before the session has adequately worked it. Prompts are deliberately open-ended and non-resolving.

**Wound-type calibration:**
- *Warrior:* High risk of intellectual management — analyzing what the wound means and concluding about it between sessions. Set a clock limit: 10 minutes maximum. Short entries are valid. If the entry is longer than half a page, the Warrior is probably processing, not noticing.
- *Hermit:* May not be able to access what they feel at all. Prompt 3 ("what did you hold back") may be the most accessible entry point — it requires less interior access than "what did you feel." Permission to write very little stands. Use a content ceiling rather than a clock limit: *"One reflection, however brief."* A time limit is Warrior-calibrated; for the Hermit, a content ceiling signals that the practice is complete when one honest thing has been named — not when a timer goes off.
- *False Noble:* Will produce entries that demonstrate they are handling the wound well. The anti-performance frame is most critical in this phase. Remind them: *"Do not write what a person who is healing should write. Write what is actually happening."*

**God-rep flag:** Prompt 4 (sensing God's response) may activate the punitive image — the client hears God responding with disappointment to what was opened in session. If this is a risk for this client, replace Prompt 4 with: *"What do you wish were true about how God is responding to what was opened this week?"* Hold the answer in the next session.

**Time guidance:** 10 minutes maximum for Warriors in Phase 2 (clock limit); one honest reflection for Hermits (content ceiling); 10 minutes for False Nobles with explicit anti-performance framing.

---

### PHASE 2 — Client-Facing Reflection Guide

*Use these prompts once or twice per week. Choose 1–2. Begin with the Spirit-invitation. Do not try to resolve what surfaces — hold it honestly.*

---

**Prompt 1 — What remained**
What from this week's session is still with you — not what you thought about it, but what is simply still present? Name it without explaining it.

**Prompt 2 — The body's record**
What did you feel in your body during or after the session — tightness, heaviness, relief, numbness, something else? Where in your body? You don't need to know what it means.

**Prompt 3 — What you held back**
What did you hold back — in the session itself, or in your reflection afterward? You don't have to bring it forward now. Just name that it is there.

**Prompt 4 — What is hardest to stay with**
What is hardest to remain present to this week? Don't answer it or explain it — just name it. *The hardest thing to stay with right now is ___.*

## PHASE 3 — Identity Installation

### Practitioner Notes
Something is shifting. The prompts here help the client notice and mark the shift — not perform it. The distinction matters: a client who notices *"I responded differently and I don't fully understand why"* is in formation; a client who writes *"I am clearly making progress in my identity"* is performing formation. The prompts are designed to surface evidence of actual change rather than self-reported progress.

Phase 3 reflection feeds the Mid-Engagement Snapshot — the language clients use in their entries is often the language the coach draws from for the snapshot. Listen for it.

**Wound-type calibration:**
- *Warrior:* Prompt 3 (catching the old pattern) is the most diagnostic for Warrior — they are often unaware of how automatic the pattern is until they are asked to track it explicitly. Ask them to note what happened after they noticed — did the pattern complete, or did something interrupt it?
- *Hermit:* Prompt 4 (one moment when something felt more true) may be more accessible than Prompt 1 (responding differently) — the Hermit's changes are often felt rather than behavioral in Phase 3. Either prompt is valid.
- *False Noble:* Highest performance risk in Phase 3 — the False Noble wants to report growth. Prompt 2 (what covenant truth is hardest to receive) is the most valuable for this type because it surfaces where the wound is still active beneath the apparent progress. Assign this one specifically.

**God-rep flag:** Prompt 4 ("something felt more true") may activate the transactional image — "I've worked hard enough that I'm finally getting the reward of feeling it." If this surfaces in session, reframe: the truth was always true; what changed is the capacity to receive it, which is the Spirit's work.

**Time guidance:** 15–20 minutes. Phase 3 has enough content to sustain a slightly longer reflection.

---

### PHASE 3 — Client-Facing Reflection Guide

*Use these prompts once or twice per week. Choose 1–2. Begin with the Spirit-invitation. You are a witness to your formation — not its manager.*

---

**Prompt 1 — Responding differently**
Where did you respond differently this week than you would have responded six weeks ago — even slightly? Describe the moment briefly. You don't need to explain why it was different.

**Prompt 2 — What is hardest to receive**
What covenant truth from your recent sessions are you finding hardest to receive — not hardest to understand, but hardest to actually take in as true about you? What makes it hard?

**Prompt 3 — Catching the old pattern**
Where did you catch the old pattern operating this week — the Warrior's armor, the Hermit's withdrawal, the False Noble's striving? What happened next? Did the pattern complete, or did something interrupt it?

**Prompt 4 — One true moment**
Name one moment this week when something felt more true than it used to — even briefly, even faintly. You don't need to sustain it or explain it. Just name it: *This week, ___ felt more true than it used to.*

## PHASE 4 — Integration

### Practitioner Notes
The engagement is closing. Phase 4 reflection looks back over the full arc — it surfaces the language the coach will use in the Growth Report and the language the client needs to own their own formation story. Prompts here are retrospective and consolidating.

These entries are the richest material in the whole reflection series — clients often access language in Phase 4 that they could not have articulated at intake. Listen for verbatim phrases. The Growth Report is built from this language.

**Wound-type calibration:**
- *Warrior:* Prompt 3 (what was hardest) will likely surface the most honest material. Warriors can be surprisingly direct in retrospect — the armor is less active when the pressure of the work has passed.
- *Hermit:* Prompt 1 (who you were when you came in) may require gentleness — the Hermit may be reluctant to name how significant the shift has been, as if claiming the change is itself too much to take. Give permission: *"You are allowed to notice what changed."*
- *False Noble:* Prompt 4 (what to carry forward vs. leave behind) is most useful — it externalizes the striving pattern as something that can be set down, rather than something they are. Frame it as permission to stop, not as a demand to change.

**God-rep calibration:** Phase 4 retrospective prompts may surface how the God-rep shifted (or did not) across the engagement. **Punitive type:** Watch for entries that still interpret the work as earning better standing. **Absent/Distant type:** May not connect the work to God at all in their reflection — name this gently in session: "I notice God doesn't appear much in what you wrote. What do you make of that?" **Transactional type:** May frame growth as the result of effort; Prompt 2 is a useful corrective — receive what changed as something that happened to them, not something they produced. **Capricious/Unsafe type:** May hold Phase 4 growth lightly, as if it could be taken away; stability language in the debrief is helpful.

**Deliberate omission — Growth Report sources:** Phase 4 reflection entries feed the Growth Report *indirectly* — language that surfaces in the journal often surfaces again in session, where the coach captures it in Session Growth Notes. The Growth Report does NOT source between-session journal entries directly; its sources are the Intake Addendum, Session Growth Notes, and direct session content. Do not quote from a client's journal in the Growth Report unless the client brought those words into a session. The distinction protects the privacy of the journal and maintains the Growth Report's evidentiary standard.

**Time guidance:** 20–30 minutes. This is the one phase where a longer entry is appropriate. The Growth Report depends on the richness of what surfaces here.

---

### PHASE 4 — Client-Facing Reflection Guide

*Take more time with these than with other phases — 20 to 30 minutes if you can. Use all four if possible. These are retrospective: you are looking back over the whole engagement.*

---

**Prompt 1 — Who you were**
How would you describe who you were when you came into this coaching engagement — what you were carrying, how you experienced yourself, how God felt to you then? Write as honestly as you can about that person.

**Prompt 2 — What is different**
What is different now — in how you experience God, how you understand yourself, or how the old patterns operate? You don't need to claim more than is true. Even small shifts count.

**Prompt 3 — What was hardest**
What was the hardest part of this work? Not what was most significant — what was hardest. Name it plainly.

**Prompt 4 — Carry forward / set down**
What do you want to carry forward from this engagement — a practice, a truth, a posture? What do you want to set down — a pattern, a lie, a way of relating to God or yourself that no longer fits?

## Practitioner Notes: Common Failure Modes Across All Phases

| Wound Type | Failure Mode | In-Session Response |
|---|---|---|
| Warrior | Entry is analytical — long, concluded, processed | *"What did you feel before you analyzed it? That's the line I want."* |
| Warrior | Entry is very short because "nothing came up" | *"What are you not writing? That's usually where the content is."* |
| Hermit | No entry — "I couldn't make myself do it" | Normalize. Ask: *"What stopped you? What felt too much about it?"* The resistance is the material. |
| Hermit | Entry is one word or sentence | That is enough. Receive it as data — ask what it was like to write even that much. |
| False Noble | Entry demonstrates growth eloquently | *"What did you leave out? What would you write if you weren't trying to do this well?"* |
| False Noble | Entry is self-critical in a polished way | The self-criticism is also a performance. Ask: *"What is actually true beneath the critique?"* |
| All types | Client shares the journal as a deliverable | Receive it warmly, then reframe: *"This is yours. I'm glad you shared it — what from it do you want to bring into our conversation today?"* |

*If a client's reflection practice surfaces material that doesn't settle between sessions — elevated activation persisting past 48 hours, or significant destabilization from a life event — see [[Covenant Identity — Between-Session Distress Protocol]].*
