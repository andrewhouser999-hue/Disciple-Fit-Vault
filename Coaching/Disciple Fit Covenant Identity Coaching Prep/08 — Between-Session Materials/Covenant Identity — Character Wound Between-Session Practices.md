# Covenant Identity — Character Wound Between-Session Practices

*Practitioner reference with embedded client-facing practice guides. Coach selects the relevant wound type and phase section, customizes as needed, and hands the client-facing portion to the client.*

---

## How to Use This Document

1. **Confirm the wound type in session before assigning any practice.** The wound type must be named and acknowledged by the client — not just diagnosed by the coach. Assigning Warrior practices to a client who hasn't acknowledged the Warrior pattern produces either compliance-as-performance or quiet rejection.
2. **Select the phase-appropriate section.** Each wound type has Phase 2, 3, and 4 variants. Deploying Phase 2 excavation-supportive practices in Phase 3 reopens what should be installing. Deploying Phase 3 installation practices in Phase 2 skips work that hasn't happened yet.
3. **Run the precondition check first.** See the table below before assigning any practice.
4. **Hand the client-facing section only.** The practitioner notes are for coach use. Extract the relevant phase section for the client; customize language as needed for that individual.
5. **For phase-level precondition logic**, see [[Covenant Identity — Between-Session Support Materials]].

## Practitioner Precondition Check

Before assigning wound-type practices, verify that the client's current God-representation permits the practice to function as designed. The following combinations are high-risk:

| Wound Type | God-Rep | Risk | Precondition Required |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warrior | Punitive | Surrender/trust practices produce performance compliance, not formation | God-rep work must have begun — at minimum, the distortion has been named |
| Warrior | Absent/Distant | Practices invoking God's presence feel hollow; Warrior reads absence as confirmation of untrustworthy God | Reframe God's presence in wound context before assigning presence-based practices |
| Warrior | Capricious/Unsafe | Warrior uses the capricious God-rep to rationalize self-protection — "can't trust God either." Covenant-trust practices feel naive rather than formative | Name the capricious pattern as a God-rep distortion, not theological reality, before assigning any covenant-trust or surrender practice |
| Hermit | Overwhelming/Consuming | Extended encounter practices (silence, soaking prayer) are activating, not settling | Anchor God-rep as gentle and bounded first; assign only short, low-demand practices until stabilized |
| Hermit | Absent/Distant | Hermit terror (too much) plus Absent/Distant distortion produces dual block: intensity and absence are both threatening. Encounter-based practices feel futile from both directions | Reframe God's presence as bearing quiet witness rather than intense visitation; use Ps 139 and Matt 28:20; assign noticing practices before encounter practices |
| Hermit | Capricious/Unsafe | Hermit terror plus unpredictable God creates maximum threat: not only is God too much, He is arbitrary. Activation risk is high; inconsistency confirms the distortion | Very small, very consistent practices only; name God's covenantal consistency explicitly (Lam 3:22–23); do not assign any emotional-exposure practice until God-rep stabilizes |
| Hermit | Transactional | Grace/receptivity practices feel unreal or performative | Name the transactional expectation explicitly in session before assigning receiving practices |
| False Noble | Absent/Distant | Practices feel like speaking to someone already present for others — God's apparent inattentiveness is experienced as personal exclusion, confirming the not-enough wound ("He attends to others, not to me specifically") | Surface God's individual attentiveness before assigning receiving practices — use texts where Jesus pursues a specific, overlooked person by name (Luke 19:1–10; Mark 10:46–52); probe to distinguish from Capricious/Unsafe routing: *"Is it that God doesn't attend to you, or that you can't find the pattern for who receives and who doesn't?"* |
| False Noble | Capricious/Unsafe | False Noble's ambivalence ("not enough") plus capricious God deepens the not-enough wound — if even God is arbitrary, the sense of being passed over feels confirmed | Covenant-stability work (promise texts: Mal 3:6, Num 23:19) must precede any receiving practice; name that God's generosity is consistent, not mood-dependent |
| False Noble | Transactional | The assignment itself becomes performance; striving transfers directly onto the practice | Name the risk explicitly in the assignment: "This is not something to get right" |

*For phase-level precondition logic (which practices are appropriate at each phase given God-rep type), see [[Covenant Identity — Between-Session Support Materials]].*

## WARRIOR

### Wound Profile
**Core wound:** Betrayal and experienced weakness. Someone who should have been trustworthy wasn't. The response is self-protection, control, and contempt for weakness — their own and others'.
**Sin pattern:** Domination, control, performance, hypervigilance.
**God-image distortion:** God is either weak (unable to protect) or demanding performance as the condition of relationship.
**Formation need:** Safety to be weak before God — not before an audience. Contemplative receptivity, not productive accomplishment.

### Theological Frame — Spirit-Dependency Anchor
The Warrior's core lie is that strength is the only safe posture. The Spirit's work in the Warrior is to make it safe to be held — to experience God as the one who neither abandoned them in the betrayal nor demands performance now. The practices below do not produce this. They create the conditions in which the Spirit can do this. The coach should name this to the Warrior explicitly: *the point is not to get the practice right — the point is to show up, and let God do what only He can do.*

Scripture anchors for the Warrior wound: Psalm 22 (My God, my God, why have you forsaken me — God present in forsakenness), Psalm 31:14–15 (I trust in you; my times are in your hand), Isaiah 54:10 (steadfast love shall not depart from you), Lamentations 3:22–23 (His mercies are new every morning).

---

### WARRIOR — Phase 2: Wound Mapping
*Excavation-supportive. The wound is being surfaced. Practices hold what was opened; they do not accelerate it further.*

**Practitioner notes:**
- In Phase 2 the Warrior may feel exposed after sessions and re-armor quickly. Practices should be short enough that re-armoring doesn't set in before they're done.
- Journaling is contraindicated in Phase 2 for most Warriors — it becomes analysis and intellectual management of the wound rather than contact with it. *Exception: a Warrior who entered the engagement with an established somatic journaling practice — one that reliably surfaces physical sensation and emotional texture rather than event analysis — may continue with a format constrained to one or two noticing sentences per session (e.g., "I felt tightness in my chest when I thought about this"). If uncertain, default to deferring journaling to Phase 3 when installation work requires written anchoring.*
- Watch for the Warrior turning the practice into a performance metric ("I did it every day"). Reframe in session: consistency is not the goal; contact is.

---

**Client-facing — Warrior Phase 2 Practices**

*Your coach has assigned these practices because of what we've been working on. They are not designed to produce a result — they are designed to create space for God to do what only He can do. The point is not to do them correctly. The point is to show up.*

**1. Held Weakness Prayer — daily, 2–3 minutes**
Sit. Hands open, palms up. Speak or think one sentence only:

> *Lord, I am not in control of what this session opened. I don't need to fix it. I'm here.*

That's the whole prayer. Don't add to it. Don't evaluate how it went. When the urge to analyze or resolve comes — and it will — return to the sentence.

**2. Betrayal Psalm — once, slowly**
Once this week, read one of the following psalms — slowly, once through. Don't extract application or a lesson. Let the psalmist voice what you may not yet be able to voice yourself.

Choose one:
- Psalm 22 — *My God, my God, why have you forsaken me*
- Psalm 55 — *It is not an enemy who taunts me*
- Psalm 31 — *In you, O Lord, do I take refuge*

Read it once. Set it down.

**3. Body check — once daily**
Once per day, ask yourself one question: *Where am I holding tension right now?*

Name the location — shoulders, jaw, chest, stomach. That's it. Don't try to release it or fix it. You're practicing noticing, not managing. This is harder than it sounds.

---

### WARRIOR — Phase 3: Identity Installation
*The wound has been named. Practices shift from excavation-support to installation — receiving covenant identity as one held by a faithful God.*

**Practitioner notes:**
- The Warrior in Phase 3 is learning to receive — which is a fundamentally different posture than performing. Watch for the creed or declarations being spoken at a fast clip (performance pace). Invite them to slow down.
- The behavioral practice (received help) is critical — schema change requires behavioral data, not just cognitive reframe. The Warrior's implicit memory needs repeated experience of receiving without catastrophe.
- The surrender posture may feel absurd or performative initially. Name this in advance: that reaction is diagnostic. Track it across sessions.

---

**Client-facing — Warrior Phase 3 Practices**

**1. Covenant Faithfulness Declaration — daily, spoken aloud**
Speak these sentences aloud once each day — morning is best, before the day's demands begin:

> *God has not abandoned me. He was present in what I experienced. He is faithful now. I do not have to protect myself from Him.*

Speak slowly. Don't rush through it. If it feels untrue, that is not a problem — speak it anyway. Formation works through repetition before conviction.

**2. One Act of Received Help — once per week**
Once this week, receive help from one person without minimizing it or immediately reciprocating. Accept the offer. Say thank you. Don't qualify it. Don't balance the ledger.

Write one sentence afterward: *This week I received ___ from ___.*

That's it. This is not a gratitude exercise — it is a behavioral interruption of the pattern that says you must be sufficient on your own.

**3. Psalm of Faithfulness — once, slowly**
Once this week, read one of the following slowly, once through:

- Psalm 89:1–8 — *I will sing of the steadfast love of the Lord forever*
- Psalm 111 — *He has caused his wondrous works to be remembered*
- Isaiah 54:10 — *My steadfast love shall not depart from you*

Read it once. You are looking for nothing. Let it land where it lands.

**4. Surrender Posture — 5 minutes, once this week**
Sit. Hands open, palms up. Five minutes. Don't produce, don't confess a list, don't evaluate.

When the urge to do something comes — and it will — notice it and return to the posture. The Holy Spirit meets you in the posture. You don't have to generate anything.

---

### WARRIOR — Phase 4: Sustainability
*Formation practices calibrated to the Warrior's ongoing vulnerability, built into a sustainable rhythm.*

**Practitioner notes (Phase 4):**
- The Warrior's primary Phase 4 vulnerability is resuming the self-sufficiency pattern once the accountability of the coaching relationship is removed. Name this explicitly in the final session: the schema will reassert — the question is whether the Warrior has a named person and a named practice for when it does.
- The most important structural element for the Warrior in Phase 4 is a relational accountability relationship — someone who knows the wound pattern and can name it when it resurfaces. This is not optional. Without it, the formation gains tend to be absorbed back into the self-sufficient posture within 6–12 months.
- Phase 4 is not a victory lap. Name the setback protocol explicitly before closing: when the old pattern comes back hard, what will the Warrior do? Who will they call? What is the earliest warning signal in their body?

**Client-facing — Warrior Phase 4 Practices**

*The Warrior's Phase 4 work is not the absence of the wound — it is building the structure that holds when the wound reasserts. You will have seasons when the control pattern returns. That does not mean the formation failed. It means the formation is real enough to be tested. You have what you need now to recognize it and return.*

**Daily non-control practice**
Once per day: one small act of not being in control. Let someone else choose. Receive something without qualifying it. Sit in an open question for two minutes without resolving it. This is practiced as a spiritual discipline — not a personality improvement strategy. The Warrior's covenant identity is one who is held by a faithful God; this practice enacts that identity in the ordinary. When it feels pointless, that is the pattern telling you it has no value. Keep doing it.

**Confession/accountability rhythm — one person, regular**
Maintain a brief, regular check-in with one person who knows your wound pattern — ideally weekly, at minimum monthly. Not a processing conversation. One honest sentence about where the control pattern showed up and where it didn't. This is the relational accountability that keeps the formation gains from being slowly absorbed back into self-sufficiency. The coaching relationship is closing; this is its continuation in your ordinary life.

**Annual Psalm return**
Maintain a relationship with 2–3 psalms that have spoken to your wound. Return to them annually — not to extract new insights, but to mark how you have changed and what still resonates. Psalm 22 (forsakenness held by faithfulness), Psalm 31 (I trust in you; my times are in your hand), and the psalms that first named the betrayal for you are the candidates. Don't move on from them because you've "done" them.

## HERMIT

### Wound Profile
**Core wound:** Terror and the experience of being "too much." The world is overwhelming; safety is found in self-erasure. Others cannot hold them, so they make themselves small enough to be tolerable.
**Sin pattern:** Withdrawal, hiding, isolation, over-adaptation to others, chronic self-minimizing.
**God-image distortion:** God is overwhelming and consuming — or God only wants the parts of them that are presentable. He cannot hold all of them.
**Formation need:** Gentle expansion. The Hermit needs to discover that God approaches them — that they are not too much for Him — in a way that does not replicate the overwhelm that formed the wound.

### Theological Frame — Spirit-Dependency Anchor
The Hermit's core lie is that they are too much to be safely known, so hiding is the only way to survive relationship. The Spirit's work in the Hermit is to make it safe to be seen — specifically, to experience God as one who approaches gently and is not overwhelmed by what He finds. The practices below create the conditions for this; they do not produce it. Extended, high-demand encounter practices (long silence, soaking prayer, intense contemplative work) are contraindicated in early phases — they replicate the overwhelm rather than healing it. The Spirit works in the Hermit through gentleness and gradual expansion, not confrontation.

Scripture anchors for the Hermit wound: Psalm 131 (I have calmed and quieted my soul like a weaned child), Isaiah 42:3 (a bruised reed he will not break), Matthew 11:29 (I am gentle and lowly in heart), Song of Songs 2:10–14 (arise, my beautiful one — God as gentle pursuer).

---

### HERMIT — Phase 2: Wound Mapping
*Excavation-supportive. The terror and self-erasure patterns are being named. Practices must be small enough that they don't replicate the overwhelm that caused the wound.*

**Practitioner notes:**
- The Hermit being seen in session is itself a significant act of courage. Between-session practices should have a low ceiling — don't pile on.
- The single-sentence naming practice is foundational for Hermits who have difficulty accessing their inner experience at all. Start here before anything else.
- The relational scaffolding practice is not optional for the Hermit in Phase 2 — solo encounter as the only between-session assignment leaves the Hermit isolated with material they cannot hold alone. Require the one-safe-person component.
- **If the client cannot name a safe person:** This is significant diagnostic data — note it and bring it to the next session. For the Phase 2 assignment, substitute: brief low-demand contact (a text exchange, a short phone call, sitting with other people without needing to interact). The goal is interrupting complete isolation, not processing. If no relational contact is possible, assign the verse and naming practices only, and address the relational isolation directly in the next session — a Hermit with no safe person is missing the relational scaffolding the healing requires.
- Give explicit permission: "You don't have to do anything with this" is often the most important thing a Hermit needs to hear before a practice.

---

**Client-facing — Hermit Phase 2 Practices**

*These practices are deliberately small. Small is not a concession — it is the right size for this work. You don't have to do them perfectly. You don't have to do anything with what comes up. Your job is only to show up.*

**1. One-sentence naming — daily**
Once per day, one sentence only:

> *Today I felt ___.*

No elaboration required. No analysis. Just name it and set it down. If nothing comes, write: *Today I felt nothing, or I couldn't tell.* That is a valid answer. You are practicing being present to your own interior experience — not producing insight.

**2. Safe presence — once this week**
Once this week, spend 10–15 minutes with one person who feels safe. No agenda. You don't need to process the session or explain what you're working on. Just be present with them. Ordinary conversation is fine. The point is practicing being with someone — not being alone with what the session opened.

**3. One verse — once**
Once this week, read one of these slowly, once through:

- Psalm 131 — *I have calmed and quieted my soul*
- Isaiah 42:3 — *A bruised reed he will not break*
- Matthew 11:28–29 — *Come to me... I am gentle and lowly*

You don't have to do anything with it. Read it once and let it be.

---

### HERMIT — Phase 3: Identity Installation
*The wound has been named. Practices now help the Hermit receive their covenant identity as one who is welcomed — not merely tolerated.*

**Practitioner notes:**
- The Hermit in Phase 3 can begin to expand — but carefully. The ceiling on practices rises slightly from Phase 2, but not dramatically.
- The "unhiding practice" must be assigned specifically per client — the coach selects the act based on what emerged in session, not a generic instruction. The act must be small enough to be doable and specific enough to be actionable.
- The identity declaration should be short and coach-assigned — not the full creed, which may feel too large. One statement, given by the coach, tailored to this client's specific wound picture.
- Watch for Hermit clients completing the practices in isolation and never reporting difficulty. The absence of difficulty is a flag — they may be performing compliance while the wound remains untouched.

---

**Client-facing — Hermit Phase 3 Practices**

**1. Welcomed by God Prayer — daily, 3–5 minutes**
Sit, eyes closed. Begin from God's initiative — not yours. Speak or think:

> *You see me. You are not overwhelmed by what You see. You came toward me.*

Stay with it. If it feels untrue, that is the wound speaking — not reality. Speak it again. The Holy Spirit is doing the work of making this true in your experience. You are creating the conditions for that work.

**2. Unhiding practice — once this week**
*(Your coach will give you a specific act for this week.)*

This week, allow yourself to be seen in one small way. [Coach inserts the specific assigned act here based on session content.]

Afterward, write one sentence: *This week I let myself be seen by ___.*

That's it. One act. One sentence.

**3. Psalm of God's Approach — once, slowly**
Once this week, read one of these slowly, once through:

- Psalm 139:1–12 — *You have searched me and known me*
- Song of Songs 2:10–14 — *Arise, my love, my beautiful one, and come away*

You are meeting God as one who approaches — who comes toward you rather than waiting for you to be presentable. Read once. Let the image sit.

**4. Identity Declaration — daily**
Speak this once each day:

> *I am known and not abandoned. I am seen and not rejected.*

If your coach has given you a personalized version, use that. Speak it aloud if you can. The voice matters — formation reaches the body through embodied practice.

---

### HERMIT — Phase 4: Sustainability
*Formation practices that sustain the Hermit's ongoing expansion without triggering the wound response.*

**Client-facing — Hermit Phase 4 Practices**

*The Hermit's Phase 4 work is not the end of expansion — it is the beginning of expansion becoming a way of life. These practices are not therapeutic tools. They are the ongoing rhythm of a person learning to live in the open rather than the hidden. You don't have to be fully healed to do them. You just have to keep showing up.*

**Regular safe community — non-negotiable**
Weekly presence in one small group, spiritual community, or with one consistent safe friend. This is not optional social activity — it is the primary formation container for the Hermit. The wound formed in isolation and self-erasure; it does not heal in isolation. The Holy Spirit works in the Hermit through the body of Christ, not only through solo encounter.

**Graded expansion rhythm**
Once per month: one act of being present that is slightly larger than what was comfortable the prior month. Track it briefly. This does not need to be dramatic — it is a slow, consistent expansion of the window of tolerance over time. The goal is not a destination but a direction.

## FALSE NOBLE

### Wound Profile
**Core wound:** Ambivalence and the experience of being "not enough." There is a fundamental insufficiency that performance can compensate for but never resolve. Compulsive goodness and striving are the primary coping strategies.
**Sin pattern:** Self-sufficiency masked as virtue, striving, over-helping from emptiness, compulsive productivity.
**God-image distortion:** God withholds specifically from them. He gives to others but not to the False Noble — perhaps because they haven't performed well enough, or perhaps for reasons they cannot identify.
**Formation need:** Receiving practices. The False Noble needs to experience grace as genuinely unearned — not as a theological category they assent to, but as a lived reality. The striving-earning dynamic must be interrupted at the behavioral level, not just the cognitive level.

### Theological Frame — Spirit-Dependency Anchor
The False Noble's core lie is that they must earn what others receive freely — that sufficiency must be achieved, not received. The Spirit's work in the False Noble is to make receiving possible — to break through the performance wall that blocks grace from landing. The primary risk in this tool is that the practices themselves become a performance. Every assignment given to a False Noble must carry the explicit frame: *this is not something to get right.* The Spirit does not work through the quality of the practice — He works through the posture of a person who has stopped trying to produce their own sufficiency and has, however briefly, opened their hands.

Scripture anchors for the False Noble wound: Romans 5:8 (while we were still sinners, Christ died for us), Ephesians 2:8–9 (not of works, lest any man should boast), Luke 15:20 (the father ran while the son was still a long way off), Psalm 84:11 (no good thing does he withhold from those who walk uprightly), Romans 8:32 (he who did not spare his own Son, how will he not also give us all things).

---

### FALSE NOBLE — Phase 2: Wound Mapping
*Excavation-supportive. The ambivalence and striving patterns are being named. The False Noble may feel relief that the pattern is named AND immediate anxiety about what to do with it. The between-session pull will be to fix it — which is the pattern.*

**Practitioner notes:**
- Journaling is high-risk for the False Noble in Phase 2 — it becomes a performance. If journaling is assigned, the frame must be explicit and the format must be constrained (one sentence, no more).
- The "not-doing prayer" is the most important and most resisted practice for the False Noble. Anticipate resistance; name it in advance in session.
- Watch for False Noble clients reporting they "did all the practices." That report is itself diagnostic — the pattern is performing the formation work.
- The received grace record is not a gratitude journal — this distinction matters and must be named when assigning it.

---

**Client-facing — False Noble Phase 2 Practices**

*Before you read these: there is nothing to get right here. These practices are not assignments to be completed well. They are invitations to stop producing — which is the hardest thing you will do. When you notice the urge to do them correctly, that urge is exactly what we are interrupting.*

**1. Not-doing prayer — daily, 5 minutes**
Set a timer for five minutes. Sit. Don't produce anything. Don't confess a list, don't plan what you'll do differently, don't even try to feel something. Just be present.

When the urge to make this productive comes — and it will, probably within thirty seconds — notice it. Don't fight it. Don't judge yourself for it. Just return to sitting.

The Holy Spirit is not waiting for you to generate something. You are practicing the posture of not generating. That is the whole practice.

**2. Received grace record — once this week, one sentence**
Once this week, write one sentence:

> *This week, something was given to me that I didn't earn: ___.*

This is not a gratitude journal. Gratitude can become a performance. This is specifically about receiving — noticing when something came to you without you earning it. One sentence. Once per week.

**3. One verse — once**
Once this week, read one of these slowly, once through:

- Romans 5:8 — *While we were still sinners, Christ died for us*
- Luke 15:20 — *He ran and embraced him and kissed him*
- Ephesians 2:8–9 — *Not a result of works, so that no one may boast*

Read it once. Don't extract an application. Notice what it stirs. Set it down.

---

### FALSE NOBLE — Phase 3: Identity Installation
*The wound has been named. Practices now help the False Noble receive their covenant identity as one who is given to — not because they performed, but because of who God is.*

**Practitioner notes:**
- The receiving declaration will be resisted — "it feels too easy." Name this resistance in advance: the discomfort with receiving freely is the wound. The declaration is true regardless of whether it feels true.
- The striving interrupt (non-completion behavioral practice) is the most architecturally important practice for the False Noble in Phase 3. Schema change requires behavioral data. The coach assigns the specific act per session content.
- Watch for the False Noble reframing the Sabbath practice as a productivity strategy ("I rest so I can work better"). This is the pattern co-opting the discipline. Name it.
- The identity declaration facets should be drawn specifically from covenant-gift language in the creed — not accomplishment language.

---

**Client-facing — False Noble Phase 3 Practices**

**1. Receiving declaration — daily, spoken aloud**
Speak these sentences aloud once each day:

> *I am already enough in Christ. I do not need to earn what has been freely given. God gives to me — not because of what I have done, but because of who He is.*

If it feels untrue or too easy, speak it anyway. The discomfort you feel with receiving freely is not a sign that the declaration is wrong — it is the wound telling you it is wrong. Formation works through repetition before conviction arrives.

**2. Striving interrupt — once this week**
*(Your coach will give you a specific act for this week.)*

Once this week, stop one striving behavior before it is complete. [Coach inserts the specific act here based on session content — e.g., don't send the follow-up email, don't volunteer for the additional task, stop working at the agreed time even if the project isn't finished.]

Afterward, write one sentence: *This week I stopped ___.*

The Holy Spirit uses the incomplete act to surface what the striving was protecting. Bring what came up to the next session.

**3. Psalm of God's giving — once, slowly**
Once this week, read one of these slowly, once through:

- Psalm 23 — *The Lord is my shepherd; I shall not want*
- Psalm 84:11 — *No good thing does he withhold*
- Romans 8:32 — *He who did not spare his own Son*

Read once. You are receiving the text, not studying it.

**4. Identity declaration — daily**
*(Your coach will give you a specific statement from your identity work.)*

Speak the assigned statement once each day. Draw it from the facets of covenant identity that address receiving — not accomplishment or performance. Speak slowly.

---

### FALSE NOBLE — Phase 4: Sustainability
*Formation practices that sustain the False Noble's ongoing interruption of the striving-earning dynamic.*

**Client-facing — False Noble Phase 4 Practices**

*The False Noble's Phase 4 work is not achievement — it is the sustained practice of receiving what has already been given. These are not obligations to perform. They are the ongoing form of a life that no longer has to earn its own sufficiency. When the striving returns — and it will — you know what to do with it now.*

**Sabbath — weekly, non-negotiable**
Weekly Sabbath practice is the primary sustainability discipline for the False Noble — not because rest is productive, but because Sabbath is structurally incompatible with the striving pattern. Sabbath requires stopping before everything is done. It enacts the truth that the world does not depend on your output and that God's provision does not require your striving.

Frame it as a spiritual discipline that directly targets your wound — not a rest strategy or a productivity tool.

**Annual received review**
Once per year: list what was received — not achieved — in the prior year. Gifts, help accepted, grace encountered, provision that came without you earning it. Coach-facilitated if possible. This is the False Noble's formation accountability practice: tracking the evidence of being given to.

## Coach Quick Reference — Co-optation Signals by Wound Type

*Scannable reference. Use before debrief. The formative signal and co-optation signal are diagnostic opposites — both look like "doing the practice."*

### Warrior

| Practice | Formative Signal | Co-optation Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Held Weakness Prayer | Reports discomfort sitting in non-control; returns to the sentence when urge to resolve arises | Reports completing it efficiently; "I got good at it" |
| Betrayal Psalm | Reports the psalmist voicing something they couldn't; something landed | Reports reading it and extracting what it teaches about betrayal |
| Body check | Can name where tension lives; surprised by what they noticed | Reports "no tension" or describes what they concluded about the tension |
| Covenant Faithfulness Declaration | Reports speaking it slowly; noticed resistance; something shifted even slightly | Reports saying it confidently every day; no resistance reported |
| Received Help act | Reports discomfort; surprise at surviving receiving; couldn't qualify it immediately | Reports doing it well; immediately reciprocated or improved the receipt |
| Surrender posture | Reports urge to produce; returned to the posture; something unfamiliar happened | Reports achieving a calm state; quieted the mind successfully |

**Cross-cutting Warrior signal:** Everything reported is organized and correct. The co-optation is performance of the formation — the Warrior is doing the practice the right way. Ask: *"What happened that surprised you? What didn't go as expected?"*

---

### Hermit

| Practice | Formative Signal | Co-optation Signal |
|---|---|---|
| One-sentence naming | Reports even partial access to an interior state; surprised they had a word for it | Reports inability, but with some insight into what blocked it (this is useful data, not co-optation) |
| Safe presence practice | Reports one moment of contact; stayed even when uncomfortable | Reports attending and being fine; no relational content; "it was nice" |
| Single verse | Reports one word or image that caught; let it sit | Reports reading it for what it means; extracted a lesson |
| Welcomed by God prayer | Reports something landing, even briefly; noticed fear before the landing | Reports completing the prayer; experienced peace (peace may be numbing, not formation) |
| Unhiding practice | Reports fear before and a sense of being received; surprised it was survivable | Reports completing the task but remaining slightly exterior throughout |
| Identity declaration | Reports the words feeling strange or new; something happened in speaking aloud | Reports saying it fluently; it's "becoming natural" very quickly |

**Cross-cutting Hermit signal:** The absence of difficulty is the flag. A Hermit reporting that everything is going well is probably completing practices in isolation while the wound remains untouched. Ask: *"What was hardest? What do you wish you'd been able to do differently?"*

---

### False Noble

| Practice | Formative Signal | Co-optation Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Not-doing prayer | Reports the urge to produce within 30 seconds; sat with it; didn't succeed at "nothing" but showed up | Reports being able to be still and present; feels like they're getting good at it |
| Received grace record | Reports genuine surprise at noticing something unearned; sentence was small and awkward | Reports a thorough and well-organized list; covered multiple categories |

**Cross-cutting False Noble signal:** Everything is going consistently well. The False Noble's co-optation is reliable performance of the formation work. The suspicious signal is always: no difficulty, no strangeness, no resistance. Ask: *"What would you have written if you weren't trying to do this right?"*

## Load-Bearing vs. Modifiable

**Load-bearing (do not change):**
- Wound type acknowledged by client before any practice is assigned (How to Use, step 1) — assigning without acknowledgment produces compliance-as-performance or quiet rejection
- Precondition check run before assignment (God-rep intersection table) — the wrong combination can reinforce the wound rather than interrupt it
- Phase matching — deploying Phase 2 excavation practices in Phase 3 reopens what should be installing; deploying Phase 3 installation practices in Phase 2 skips work that hasn't happened yet
- Hermit Phase 2 one-safe-person component — the relational scaffolding is architecturally non-optional; solo between-session assignment leaves the Hermit isolated with material they cannot hold alone
- Spirit-dependency anchor given to client per wound type — remove this and the practices become coping strategies
- Warrior Phase 4 relational accountability relationship — without it, formation gains are absorbed back into self-sufficiency

**Modifiable (adjust per client):**
- Specific psalm selections — any psalm from the calibrated wound-type list is valid; the ones that catch the client are the ones to assign
- Declaration wording — coach personalizes language based on session content and the client's own vocabulary
- Duration of practices — the times given are defaults; adjust based on client capacity and phase
- Specific unhiding act (Hermit) and striving interrupt act (False Noble) — coach assigns per session content; the document provides the structure, not the specific act
- Format of received help record and grace record — length and medium are flexible
