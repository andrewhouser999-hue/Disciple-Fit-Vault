# Covenant Identity — What Blocks Hearing God's Voice

*Practitioner reference with embedded client-facing guide. Section II is assigned reactively — when a client names, in session, that they feel they aren't hearing from God or that prayer feels like it isn't landing — not deployed on a fixed phase schedule. Full exegetical basis: Ps 66:18; Isa 59:1-2; Prov 28:9; 1 Pet 3:7; Matt 5:23-24; Heb 3:7-4:2; Zech 7:11-13; Ezek 14:1-5; James 1:22-25; 1 John 3:21-22; Eph 4:30-31; 1 Thess 5:19-20; James 4:6-10.*

---

## Section I — Practitioner Reference

### Deployment

Assign only after the client has named the concern in their own words ("I don't feel like God is listening," "prayer feels like it's going nowhere," "I used to sense direction and I don't anymore"). This document is a mirror, not a routine screening — running it against every client who mentions distance from God will misfire (see caution below). Most natural window: Phase 2 onward, once some trust in the coaching relationship is established, since the content asks the client to examine themselves rather than their circumstances.

### The Caution That Governs This Document

Before assigning this, rule out — or at least hold open — the possibility that the client is in a season of legitimate lament, not a pattern of refusal. Job's friends made the mistake this document exists to prevent: they assumed his suffering and God's silence meant hidden sin, and God rebuked them for it (Job 42:7). Scripture has an entire tradition of the righteous experiencing God's apparent silence or hiddenness as part of formation, not correction (Ps 13, Ps 22, Ps 88). If the client's presentation is closer to grief, waiting, or unanswered longing than to a known, nameable pattern they're avoiding — route to the [[Covenant Identity — Lament Practice Guide]] instead. This document is for when something more specific is present: a known unconfessed matter, an unrepaired relationship, a pattern the client already half-recognizes in themselves when asked directly.

### Coach Deployment Notes by Wound Type

**Warrior:** may use this document to locate the "problem" and fix it through effort — treating distance from God as a performance failure to correct rather than something to bring honestly into the open. Watch for the client returning with a confession delivered like a status report. In session, ask: *"What would it mean to bring this to God without already having resolved it?"*

**Hermit:** may use this document to confirm an existing belief that they are too flawed for God to be near, treating every category as evidence against themselves rather than as a specific, addressable question. Watch for global self-condemnation replacing specific reflection. In session, ask: *"Which of these is actually present — not everything you fear might be true, but what you can name?"*

**False Noble:** will read this evaluatively, scoring themselves against each category to determine whether they are "doing it right." Watch for the client presenting a tidy answer that resolves the discomfort of the question rather than sitting with it. In session, ask: *"What did you not want to look at too closely on this list?"*

### What This Document Does Not Do

It does not diagnose a client's overall relationship with God, replace the Affections Discovery process, or function as a general spiritual health check. It addresses one specific, narrow question the client has already raised. If it surfaces wound material (a schema, a God-representation distortion, a character wound), bring that back into session rather than working it further from the document alone.

## Section II — Client-Facing Guide

*Your coach has given you this because you named that something feels off in your connection with God — that it feels quiet, or distant, or like your prayers aren't landing anywhere. This guide is not a test to pass or fail. It's a short, honest look at a specific set of things Scripture names as capable of getting in the way. Read it slowly. Don't rush to find yourself in all of it — most people find themselves in one, maybe two.*

### First — What This Is Not About

If what you're carrying right now is grief, or waiting, or a season where God feels far away and you don't know why — this guide is not for that. Scripture doesn't treat every experience of God's silence as a sign that something is wrong in you. Job's friends made that mistake, and God corrected them for it (Job 42:7). David wrote whole psalms from inside that exact silence — and kept writing to God anyway, not away from Him (Psalm 13; Psalm 22). If that's closer to where you are, bring that to your coach directly — it calls for a different conversation than this one.

What follows is for something more specific: a sense that there's a particular thing you already half-know about, that you haven't looked at directly.

### Seven Things Scripture Names

**1. Something you're holding onto instead of naming.**
*"If I had cherished iniquity in my heart, the Lord would not have listened."* — Psalm 66:18 (ESV)

Not something you slipped into once and grieved — something you're currently holding onto, protecting, or making room for. The prophet Isaiah put it plainly: *"your iniquities have made a separation between you and your God, and your sins have hidden his face from you so that he does not hear."* (Isaiah 59:2, ESV). Ask yourself: is there something right now I already know I'm doing, or refusing to stop doing, that I haven't brought into the open — with God, or with my coach?

**2. Something unrepaired with another person.**
*"Likewise, husbands... show honor to your wives... so that your prayers may not be hindered."* — 1 Peter 3:7 (ESV)

Scripture draws a direct line between how you're treating the people close to you and whether your prayers land. Jesus made the same point about worship generally: *"leave your gift there before the altar and go. First be reconciled to your brother, and then come and offer your gift."* (Matthew 5:23-24, ESV). Ask yourself: is there a relationship right now where I know I owe an apology, a repair, or an honest conversation I've been avoiding?

**3. A pattern of tuning God out that's become familiar.**
*"Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts."* — Hebrews 3:7-8 (ESV)

This isn't about one bad day. It's what happens when not-listening becomes a habit long enough that it stops feeling like a choice. The prophet Zechariah described a people who *"stopped their ears that they might not hear... therefore... as I called, and they would not hear, so they called, and I would not hear."* (Zechariah 7:11-13, ESV, paraphrased). Ask yourself: has "I'll deal with that later" become the way I usually respond to something I sense God pointing at?

**4. Something else you're actually looking to instead of God.**
Ezekiel describes leaders who came to inquire of God while carrying *"idols in their hearts"* (Ezekiel 14:3-5) — not people who'd abandoned God, but people quietly running to something else first for security, comfort, or answers, and only then asking God for His. Ask yourself: when something in my life is unsteady, what do I actually reach for first — before I reach for God?

**5. Knowing what's true and not living it.**
*"Be doers of the word, and not hearers only, deceiving yourselves."* — James 1:22 (ESV)

James's warning is specific: it's not that you don't know enough. It's that hearing truth and not acting on it quietly trains you to deceive yourself about what you actually believe. John connects this directly to confidence in prayer: *"we have confidence before God... because we keep his commandments and do what pleases him."* (1 John 3:21-22, ESV). Ask yourself: is there something I already know I should do — that I keep hearing again without doing?

**6. Resisting what God is already doing.**
*"Do not grieve the Holy Spirit... Do not quench the Spirit."* — Ephesians 4:30, 1 Thessalonians 5:19 (ESV)

Paul ties grieving the Spirit to bitterness, anger, and harsh speech toward others (Ephesians 4:31) — not abstract spiritual failure, but ordinary relational sin. Ask yourself: is there bitterness, anger, or contempt I'm carrying toward someone that I've stopped noticing because I've carried it so long?

**7. Approaching God as if you don't need to receive from Him.**
*"God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble... draw near to God, and he will draw near to you."* — James 4:6, 8 (ESV)

Pride here isn't arrogance in the dramatic sense — it's the quieter posture of managing your own life and checking in with God rather than actually needing Him. Ask yourself: am I coming to God to receive something, or mostly to report on how I'm doing?

### What to Do With This

Don't try to resolve whatever you found here on your own before your next session. Bring it — even just the one item that landed, even if you're not sure what to do with it yet. Naming it out loud to your coach is part of how this actually moves, not a separate step after you've already figured it out.

If nothing on this list landed and you still feel the distance — that's real information too. Say that plainly in session. It may mean this isn't the right lens for what you're carrying right now.
