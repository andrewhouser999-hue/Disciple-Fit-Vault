# Covenant Identity — Between-Session Support Materials
*Practitioner Reference with Client-Facing Practice Guides*
*For use from Phase 2 onward. Coach selects and adapts by God-representation type.*

---

## Coach Preamble: How to Use This Document

The six days between sessions are not passive. They are the majority of the engagement — the space where formation either extends or stalls. What the client does (or doesn't do) between sessions is not supplementary to the coaching; it is where the coaching lands or doesn't.

This document is not given to the client in full. The coach uses it to select appropriate practices based on the client's God-representation type and phase, then explains or assigns those practices verbally or via brief written instructions. The goal is not compliance with a program. The goal is extending the relational encounter that happens in session into the days that follow.

**Three things this document is not:**
- A spiritual discipline curriculum. These practices are not a program the client works through independently. They emerge from the specific terrain of the engagement.
- A performance standard. The between-session practices are experiments and observations, not obligations with pass/fail outcomes. The debrief in the following session is more important than whether the practice was completed.
- A substitute for the coaching relationship. Self-directed practice extends what the coaching relationship initiates. It does not replace it and cannot initiate deep transformation independently, particularly for God-representations rooted in relational trauma.

---

## The Precondition Framework

Not all practices are appropriate for all clients at all times. Two variables determine what is assigned:

**Variable 1 — God-representation type.** The single most important factor. The practices that heal one type can reinforce another. An Absent/Distant God type assigned extended solitary contemplation may deepen the very pattern being addressed. A Contingent/Transactional type assigned spiritual disciplines without explicit reframing will apply the performance frame to them. Type determines prescription.

**Variable 2 — Phase of the engagement.** Early phases require lighter, more oriented practices. Later phases can sustain more experiential and emotionally demanding work. The relational container must be established before intensive between-session practices are appropriate.

**The precondition that overrides both:** The coaching relationship must be established as a secure, consistent relational base before between-session practices reach toward deeply implicit God-representation material. Assign simple, bounded practices early. Increase intensity and vulnerability as the alliance deepens and the relational container is proven reliable.

---

## The Theological Frame for Between-Session Practice

Between-session practices in this model are not the equivalent of secular meditation or self-help exercises. The Covenant Identity model operates on the theological premise that the Holy Spirit is an active, present agent — *paraklētos* (one called alongside, John 14:16), Spirit of adoption bearing witness with the client's spirit (Romans 8:15-16), interceding in weakness where the client cannot find words (Romans 8:26).

This means that directed, expectant prayer — lament written to God, declarations spoken toward God, honest address naming what is actually being experienced — constitutes genuine encounter with a real Person who is described in Scripture as the primary agent of the transformation being pursued. This is not self-directed work in the secular sense. It is an invitation into encounter.

Frame the practices for the client in these terms: not "here are some exercises to do" but "here are ways to continue the conversation with God that we've been opening in our sessions." The practices are forms of address — to God, about real material, with the expectation that the address is heard.

## Universal Base Set
*These three elements apply to all clients regardless of God-representation type. They are the floor, not the ceiling.*

---

### 1. The Between-Session Commitment

Each session ends with a specific commitment tied to what surfaced in the session. This is not an assigned exercise — it is the client's own chosen next step. It is carried forward and debriefed at the opening of the next session.

**Coach framing at Phase 1 close:** Introduce the commitment as an invitation, not an assignment. Sample language: *"As we close today, I want to ask you to carry something into the week — not as homework, but as a way to hold the thread. What's one small thing that feels right to take from what we touched today?"* The client names it; the coach does not assign it. Phase 1 commitments should be small enough not to require significant vulnerability independent of the coaching relationship — the relational container is still forming.

**Coach note — debrief:** The debrief question is not "did you do it?" It is "what happened?" Non-follow-through is Immunity to Change data. Follow-through without internal noticing is incomplete. What the client discovered about themselves in the act — what they expected, what actually happened, what they noticed in their body — is the formation. Track this in Session Growth Notes.

**Watch for: compliance without engagement (False Noble pattern).** A client who consistently completes every practice and reports it smoothly may be performing the between-session work rather than inhabiting it. Tells: the debrief is well-organized and emotionally external; internal-column entries in the identity vote log read like observations about a third party; the client cannot name a moment of surprise or resistance. This pattern calls for a direct observation rather than continued encouragement: *"I notice your debrief is consistently clear and thorough. I'm wondering whether the practice is landing at the level of the work, or whether you're reporting on it from a slight distance. What would it look like to do the practice in a way you couldn't report smoothly?"*

---

### 2. The Identity Vote Log

A simple weekly record of one moment when the client acted from the covenant identity rather than the old story. Not a performance diary. A data-gathering tool.

**Client-facing format:**

| Situation | What I did | What I noticed inside |
|---|---|---|
| *(brief description)* | *(brief description)* | *(what I felt, expected, or discovered)* |

One row per week is sufficient. The practice is observation, not accumulation.

**Coach note:** Review this in session. The internal column is the formation data. A client who reports acting differently but notices nothing internally has completed a behavior without the formation. Stay with the internal column.

---

### 3. Weekly Reflection Prompt

One question to sit with during the week. Rotate weekly — do not repeat the same question. These are not journaling assignments; the client holds the question and notices what surfaces. If they write, the writing is for them, not for reporting.

**Prompt set (rotate across the engagement):**
- "Where did the old story run me this week without my noticing until later?"
- "Where did I act from who God says I am, even a little?"
- "What did I expect from God that happened? What did I expect that didn't?"
- "What am I protecting God from knowing about me right now?"
- "What part of the work from this week's session am I avoiding sitting with?"
- "Where did I catch the schema activating before it ran me?"
- "What do I most want God to know about how I'm doing right now — the version I haven't said out loud yet?"

## Type-Specific Practice Profiles

The Universal Base Set runs for all clients. The practices below are selected and added based on the client's primary God-representation type. Assign one or two at a time — not the full set.

---

### Punishing God Type
*Anxious attachment pattern / Defectiveness-Shame schema*

**What this type needs between sessions:** Practices that interrupt the guilt-shame-withdrawal cycle and bring honest experience directly to God rather than managing it. The Punishing God generates internal loops — the client confesses, feels temporarily relieved, sins or fails again, and returns to shame. The between-session work is to break the loop by redirecting it: bringing the experience to God before it has been cleaned up.

**What works:**

**Lament-of-guilt journaling.** Write honestly to God about what it feels like to live under his evaluation. Not a confession — a lament. The client names the weight, the guilt that doesn't resolve, the fatigue of the cycle. This is the externalization of the implicit God-image through language. Client instruction: *"Write a letter to God about what it feels like to bring him your failure. Don't clean it up. Don't move to resolution. Just describe it. You don't have to end with 'but I know you love me.' You can end wherever it actually ends."*

**Declaration-as-probe.** Speak one adoption or justification verse aloud once daily. The goal is not to feel it — it is to notice the body's response: resistance, flatness, partial reception, or softening. Write one sentence: "When I said that, I noticed \_\_\_\_\_." This makes the implicit God-image visible over time and provides data for the next session. Client instruction: *"This is not an affirmation practice. You're not trying to feel it. You're observing what happens when you say it."*

**Single honest prayer sentence.** One sentence to God before the day starts — not a request, not a confession loop, just a description of what is actually happening. *"God, I'm carrying shame about X right now, and I expect you to be disappointed."* Named and addressed. Client instruction: *"You're naming what's real, not managing how God receives it. That's the practice."*

**What to avoid or defer:**
- Confession practices that re-deepen shame rather than surface and release it
- Extended prayer focused on cataloguing failures
- Contemplative silence if it produces imagery of divine evaluation rather than presence

---

### Absent/Distant God Type
*Avoidant attachment pattern / Emotional Deprivation schema*

**What this type needs between sessions:** Active, directional address rather than receptive silence. The avoidant strategy is emotional self-sufficiency and low expectation of relational response. Extended solitary silence can reinforce this pattern — the client "prays" by being alone, which is the same strategy they use to manage all relationships. The between-session work is to introduce directed address and minimal expectation of response, then gradually build that expectation over time.

**Critical contraindication:** Do not prescribe extended solitary contemplative practice — centering prayer, prolonged silence, or receptive meditation — as a primary practice for this type before relational grounding is established. Research evidence (Mikulincer et al.) indicates that increased meditation frequency correlates with increased loneliness for avoidant-attached individuals. The practice can deepen the isolation rather than heal it. Contemplative practice becomes appropriate for this type *after* the coaching relationship has established a reliable relational experience and after the client has begun to build honest, directed address in prayer.

**What works:**

**One honest sentence daily.** Active address to God — not receptive silence, not petition, not liturgy. One sentence of honest description. *"I'm here. I don't feel you, but I'm speaking to you."* Or *"God, I noticed something today and I'm bringing it to you even though I don't expect much."* The practice is relational contact, however minimal. Over weeks, the sentences change. Track this in Session Growth Notes as prayer quality data.

**Ignatian imagination with a specific gospel scene.** Enter a gospel narrative using imagination — not as observer but as participant. A scene in which Jesus is present and responsive to a specific person: the woman who touched the hem of his garment, Zacchaeus, the father running toward the prodigal. The imagination provides a relational entry point that bypasses the defenses that abstract prayer triggers. The encounter is specific, embodied, and conversational rather than contemplative and silent. Client instruction: *"Read the scene slowly. Then close your eyes and put yourself in it — not watching, but present. Notice what Jesus does. Notice what you expect him to do. If what actually happens in the story is different from what you expected, stay there."*

**Directed grief naming.** If grief beneath the flatness has been identified, give it permission to surface in writing. *"Write one sentence about what it would have meant to you if God had shown up in [specific situation the client described]. What would that have changed?"* This is not forced gratitude — it is permission to notice the longing that the flatness is protecting. The longing is the alive edge.

**Community/corporate prayer.** Invite the client to pray with at least one other person between sessions, or to attend a corporate prayer context. The relational context of other humans praying alongside them provides the grounding that solo practice can't yet provide.

**What to avoid or defer:**
- Extended solitary silence — this is the primary contraindication for this type
- Receptive/listening prayer practices before directed address is established
- Any practice that can be done without any relational contact (God or human)
- Large emotional vulnerability without the coaching relationship as container

---

### Permissive/Safe God Type
*Secure-appearing, low-demand attachment pattern / Entitlement/Grandiosity or Emotional Inhibition schema*

**What this type needs between sessions:** Practices that introduce specificity and honest engagement — not distress, but curiosity-deepening. The client's prayer life is likely pleasant and untroubling. The between-session work is not to alarm the client but to surface whether anything real is happening in the formation: whether God is specifically engaged with this person, not merely generically present. The goal is a fuller, more real God — holy and tender held together — not a more threatening one.

**Secondary presentation note:** If the Permissive/Safe image is defensive (sitting over a Punitive one), practices that press toward specificity in God's engagement may surface low-grade anxiety. If the client reports anything other than mild curiosity, slow down and bring the observation to session before continuing.

**What works:**

**Specificity probe journaling.** Once per week, the client writes one paragraph responding to: *"What was God's specific response to something in my life this week — not general approval, but a response to a particular thing?"* The absence of any specific response is itself data. Client instruction: *"You're not looking for condemnation. You're asking whether God is engaged with who you specifically are and where you're specifically going — not just generically present. Bring whatever you notice, including if nothing came."*

**Gospel encounter: honest engagement texts.** Once per week, read Mark 10:17–22 or John 21:15–17 using the encounter method. One question only: *"What does it feel like that love and specificity came together in this scene?"* Client instruction: *"This is not a preparation for God to say something hard to you. It is an invitation to meet a God who loves you too well to be indifferent to the specific person you are."*

**Holy love prayer.** Once per week, hold this prayer for five minutes: *"Lord, I want to know You as You actually are — not only as the God who approves, but as the God who loves me too well to leave me where I am. Show me."* Sit with it. Do not produce anything. The Spirit works with the request.

**What to avoid or defer:**
- Confrontational framing — the goal is not to produce alarm but to deepen encounter; "specific" and "real" are more accurate aims than "challenging"
- Practices designed for Punitive clients — this type does not need more grace-softening; they need more specificity, not more reassurance
- Pressing toward the Punitive image underneath (if suspected) without the session container supporting that movement

---

### Contingent/Transactional God Type
*Anxious/ambivalent attachment pattern / Unrelenting Standards schema*

**What this type needs between sessions:** Practices that disrupt the transaction — that remove the performance element from the God-relationship and introduce the experience of receiving without earning. This type will absorb almost any spiritual practice into the performance frame if the coach doesn't actively reframe it. The between-session work must be structured to prevent performance from being the metric.

**What works:**

**Non-performance prayer.** A defined, brief period — five minutes — in which the client is specifically not asking for anything, not confessing anything, not pursuing spiritual growth. Just present. The client is told: *"This is not a discipline. It is a rest. You are not trying to be close to God or to feel anything. You are simply being with God without performing. If you feel the urge to make it productive, notice that urge and set it aside. That's the practice."* This is profoundly uncomfortable for this type at first — that discomfort is the signal that it is working.

**Grace receipt journaling.** One sentence per day: something received that was not earned. Not spiritual gifts — ordinary, daily receipts: the warmth this morning, the conversation that helped, the unexpected rest. The goal is training attention on receipt-without-earning in ordinary life, where the transaction is already not operative, as a way of making grace concrete before it can be abstract theology. Client instruction: *"You're looking for evidence that things come to you that you didn't earn. Not spiritual things — just any things. Write one per day."*

**Declaration probe with non-performance frame.** Same as Punishing God type, but with explicit reframing: *"Speak the declaration. Notice what you want to do to earn the right to say it. That wanting is what we're paying attention to."*

**The 'nothing owed' experiment.** Assign one day during the week in which the client does not do any formal spiritual practice — no Bible reading, no prayer time, no devotional. The instruction: *"Notice what you expect from God on a day when you've done nothing. Notice what you expect from yourself. Write one sentence about what that day felt like spiritually."* For this type, this experiment often produces anxiety, guilt, or a sense of spiritual danger — which is precisely the Contingent God in clear view.

**What to avoid or defer:**
- Spiritual disciplines framed as obligations or check-boxes
- Quantitative spiritual goal-setting ("pray for 20 minutes daily")
- Any practice that can be turned into a score or measure of spiritual compliance
- Asking the client to report how many times they completed the practice

---

### Capricious/Unsafe God Type
*Disorganized attachment pattern / Abandonment-Instability schema*

**What this type needs between sessions:** Consistency, smallness, and predictability. The capricious God produces approach-avoidance conflict — the client wants closeness but trusts it minimally, and any increase in emotional stakes can trigger the protective withdrawal. Between-session practices for this type must be small, consistent, and non-threatening. Regularity is more important than depth. The safety built through consistent small experiences is the foundation for later, deeper work.

**What works:**

**One consistent small practice, maintained exactly.** Choose one very small practice — two minutes maximum — and assign it at the same time of day, every day. The practice matters less than the consistency. The predictability itself is therapeutic data for this type: something showing up as expected, without volatility, without sudden demands for more. Client instruction: *"I'm asking you to do the same small thing at the same time every day. The practice won't feel significant. That's intentional. What we're building is the experience of something showing up the same way every day."*

**Written honest address — one sentence, no resolution required.** *"God, I'm not sure I trust you today. That's what's true."* No follow-up required. No pressure to move toward trust. The sentence is placed, addressed, and set down. The practice of naming honestly without requiring resolution is itself a small experience of safety. Client instruction: *"You're not trying to resolve this or move toward trust. You're just naming what's real and placing it in front of God. You don't have to know what happens next."*

**Noticing practice.** Ask the client to simply notice, once per day, one moment that could be God's presence — without requiring certainty or emotional response. A moment of quiet that felt like something. A small sense of not being alone. A thought that came from somewhere they didn't expect. Write one word or phrase about it. No interpretation required. Over weeks, these noticings accumulate. The Growth Report draws from them. Client instruction: *"You're not looking for certainty. You're not trying to feel anything in particular. You're just noting if anything felt like it might have been God — even slightly, even doubtfully. One word."*

**What to avoid or defer:**
- New, intense, or unfamiliar practices introduced suddenly — keep the environment predictable
- Practices requiring significant emotional vulnerability before strong relational safety is established
- Extended emotional exposure to God-encounter material without the coaching container established
- Any practice that increases the emotional stakes significantly from what the client has already tolerated

---

### Overwhelming/Consuming God Type
*Avoidant (threat-based) attachment pattern / Enmeshment-Undeveloped Self schema*

**What this type needs between sessions:** Small, bounded encounters with clear endings. Unlike the Absent/Distant type (who needs more encounter), this type needs safer encounter — the same depth but held in a smaller container until capacity expands. The goal is not less intimacy with God but a gradually widened window of tolerance for receiving what has previously felt destabilizing. Regularity of practice matters; intensity does not.

**What works:**

**One verse only, five minutes maximum.** Assign a single verse — a promise or description of God that speaks to the client's specific false identity or wound. Time the practice. When five minutes pass, the practice ends — even if the client wants more. The explicit time boundary is structural: it gives the self a container within which encounter is safe. The message is: *"You are not going to lose yourself in this. There is an end. You remain you throughout."* Client instruction: *"I'm giving you one verse and five minutes. Set a timer. When it goes off, you're done. You don't have to stop feeling what you feel — but the formal practice closes. This boundary is not a limitation; it's a container."*

**Short written address to God — three sentences maximum.** Name one thing that's true, one thing the client is asking for, and one thing they believe even if it's barely held. Close the practice after three sentences. No open-ended journaling that could pull the client deeper than the session container has prepared them for. Client instruction: *"Three sentences, then close. You're not writing a letter — you're placing something small. The practice is finished when the three sentences are done."*

**Noticing practice — with a naming clause.** Similar to the Capricious/Unsafe noticing practice, but with an added layer: after noticing a possible moment of God's presence, the client adds: *"That was God, and I'm still here."* This clause builds the implicit knowledge that encounter does not dissolve the self. Client instruction: *"One notice per day. One sentence. Add at the end: 'That was God, and I'm still here.' That's the whole practice."*

**What to avoid or defer:**
- Ignatian Gospel imagination — full imaginative immersion is contraindicated until encounter safety is established
- Extended contemplative prayer, extended silent retreat, extended worship formats
- Group prayer settings where the client cannot easily self-regulate
- Any framing that presents full surrender or complete self-abandonment as the immediate spiritual goal — this replicates the threat; reserve that language for Phase 4 when the safety foundation is built

## Written Practice Templates

These templates are offered as starting points. The coach adapts language to match the client's own words and context.

### Lament Template

*For Punishing God and Absent/Distant types primarily. Adapt for any client ready to bring honest experience to God in writing.*

```
God,

I'm bringing you this because I'm supposed to, not because I feel close to you right now.
[Or whatever honest opening fits the client's experience — do not perform closeness that isn't there.]

Here is what is actually true for me right now:
[Full, unedited complaint. What is hard. What doesn't make sense. What hasn't resolved. What I'm angry about, confused by, or grieving. Without cleanup.]

What I want from you that I haven't received:
[Name it specifically. Not generic blessing — what specifically would help. 
Even if it sounds childish or demanding. Especially if it does.]

What I'm still choosing to believe, even though it doesn't feel true right now:
[This is not forced positivity. It is the covenant truth the client can barely hold onto. One sentence is enough. If nothing comes, that's honest too — write "I don't know what I believe right now."]

I'm leaving this here.
[End without wrapping it up. The lament doesn't have to resolve. The Psalms that end in disorientation are still Scripture.]
```

### Identity Vote Log

*Universal. Used across all types from Phase 2 onward.*

**Instructions for client:** *Each week, write down one moment when you acted from who God says you are — even imperfectly, even partially. Not a heroic moment. Just one moment where you chose from the new story instead of the old one. Then write what you noticed inside when you did it.*

| Situation | What I did differently | What I noticed inside |
|---|---|---|
| | | |
| | | |
| | | |

**Debrief questions for the following session:**
- *"What happened inside you when you made that choice?"*
- *"What did you expect to happen? What actually happened?"*
- *"What does that moment tell you about who you're becoming?"*

---

### Weekly Reflection Guide

*Universal. One question per week. The client holds it — doesn't need to write unless they want to.*

**Week 1–2 (Phase 2 / early Phase 3):**
"Where did the old story run me this week? When did I notice it?"

**Week 3–4:**
"What did I expect from God this week? What happened instead?"

**Week 5–6:**
"Where did I catch the schema activating before it ran me all the way?"

**Week 7–8:**
"What has changed in how I approach God compared to when we began? Be specific."

**Week 9–10 (Phase 4):**
"What do I want to hold from what we've built here? What am I carrying forward?"

### Type-Specific Prayer Prompts

*Brief directional sentences for clients who need a starting point for between-session prayer. Give one at a time. Never a list.*

**Punishing God:**
- *"God, I'm carrying [specific thing] and I expect you to be disappointed with me. That's what's true right now."*
- *"God, what would it mean if you already saw all of this and weren't withdrawing?"*
- *"I'm here. I don't know if I'm welcome, but I'm here."*

**Absent/Distant God:**
- *"God, I'm speaking to you even though I don't feel you there."*
- *"I notice I've stopped expecting you to show up. I'm naming that."*
- *"If you were here the way you say you are — what would that change about this?"*

**Contingent/Transactional God:**
- *"God, I'm not bringing anything right now. I'm just here."*
- *"I notice I want to earn the right to be in this conversation. I'm setting that down."*
- *"What would it mean to just receive something from you today without giving anything back?"*

**Permissive/Safe God:**
- *"God, what is Your specific response — not Your general love, but Your response — to something particular about me right now?"*
- *"I'm asking to know You as more than approval. Show me who You actually are toward me."*
- *"You loved the rich young man and said something that cost him everything. What would You say to me that specifically?"*

**Capricious/Unsafe God:**
- *"God, I'm not sure I trust you today. That's honest."*
- *"I'm here for two minutes. That's what I can offer."*
- *"I noticed something today that might have been you. I'm not sure. But I'm noting it."*

**Overwhelming/Consuming God:**
- *"God, I'm here for [X minutes]. I trust You won't overwhelm me in that time."*
- *"I want to come to You and still be myself when I leave. I believe that's possible."*
- *"I noticed Your presence for a moment, and I'm still here."*

## Session Debrief Guide

How the coach opens the following session determines what formation data is gathered and what is lost. The debrief is not administrative — it is often where the most significant formation data from the between-session period surfaces.

**Never open with:** "Did you do the practices?"

**Open with:** "What happened between sessions?" Then follow the thread.

---

**For lament journaling:**
- *"What came out when you wrote? Did anything surprise you?"*
- *"Where did you end up? Did it resolve, or did you leave it open?"*
- *"What did it feel like to bring that to God — even in writing?"*

**For identity votes:**
- *"Walk me through what you did. Not the outcome — what was it like inside when you were doing it?"*
- *"What did you expect to happen? What actually happened?"*
- *"What does that tell you about who you're becoming?"*

**For prayer practices:**
- *"What did you expect when you came to God with that? What happened?"*
- *"Did God feel different at any point this week than your starting expectation? Describe it."*
- *"What sentence were you able to say to God that you couldn't have said earlier in our work?"*

**For non-follow-through:**
- *"What got in the way? Not practically — internally. What happened inside you when you thought about doing it?"*
- *"What would it have cost you to do it?"*
- Treat non-follow-through as Immunity to Change data. Note it in Session Growth Notes under Schema Activity. Do not lecture or reassign.

**For the Capricious/Unsafe type:**
- Keep debrief brief and non-pressured. *"You mentioned you tried the [practice]. What was it like?"* If the client is quiet, accept that. The consistency of the debrief — not its depth — is the therapeutic data for this type.
