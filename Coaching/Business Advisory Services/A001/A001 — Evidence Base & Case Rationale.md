# Part 1 — Evidence Base

## Stream 1 — Developmental Neuroscience

**Anchor researcher:** Bruce Perry, M.D., Ph.D. — Senior Fellow, ChildTrauma Academy; *The Boy Who Was Raised as a Dog* (2006); *Born for Love* (2010)

### Core Framework: Sequential Brain Processing

Perry's neurosequential model describes how the brain processes experience from lower to higher structures: brainstem → diencephalon → limbic system → cortex. Under conditions of perceived threat, the lower structures activate first and dominate processing. Higher cortical functions — planning, perspective-taking, impulse regulation, abstract reasoning — are available only when the lower structures are regulated.

### Key Finding for RPA

**The implication is direct:** A manager who is relationally threatened — by a difficult conversation, an employee's anger, a supervisor's criticism, a high-stakes decision — processes that threat from the brainstem up. The cortical skills they were trained in are temporarily offline. Training that deposits knowledge in the cortex does not transfer to behavior under activation.

This is not a character failure or a motivation failure. It is a neurobiological sequence. The manager is not choosing to shut down, avoid, or escalate — they are operating from the structure that is active.

### Organizational Application

The behaviors that drive operational cost in hospitality — OM withdrawal during conflict, escalation under pressure, inconsistency under stress — are not primarily skill deficits. They are pattern activations. Skill training addresses what managers know. RPA addresses what managers do when threat is present.

**Conversational form:** *"The research on how the brain processes stress — Bruce Perry's work, specifically — shows that the skills we train in calm conditions don't transfer to behavior under pressure. That's not a motivation problem. It's a neurobiological sequence. What actually changes behavior under activation is relational practice in conditions that mimic the activation — which is what the coaching component is designed to do."*

## Stream 2 — Interpersonal Neurobiology

**Anchor researcher:** Daniel J. Siegel, M.D. — Clinical Professor of Psychiatry, UCLA School of Medicine; *The Developing Mind* (1999); *Mindsight* (2010); *The Pocket Guide to Interpersonal Neurobiology* (2012)

### Core Framework: Window of Tolerance

Every person has a functional zone of arousal — the Window of Tolerance — within which higher-order processing is accessible. Outside this window in either direction:

- **Hyperarousal** (above the window): reactivity, emotional flooding, over-communication, pursuit behavior, difficulty listening
- **Hypoarousal** (below the window): flatness, disengagement, withdrawal, cognitive slowing, emotional numbness

Within the window: the person can think, relate, regulate, repair, and problem-solve.

### Key Finding for RPA

Regulated presence — a person consistently functioning within their own window — is the precondition for relational effectiveness. A dysregulated manager cannot regulate their team. This is not metaphorical; it describes a specific neurobiological transmission process (Siegel's concept of interpersonal neurobiology: minds are shaped by minds in relationship).

### The Four Behavioral Dimensions as Window States

| RPA Dimension | Window Relationship |
|---|---|
| Withdrawal | Chronic hypoarousal under relational pressure |
| Escalation | Chronic hyperarousal under relational pressure |
| Disorganization | Cycling between hyper and hypo without stabilization |
| Collaborative Capacity | Functional within the window under activation |

Development work — the coaching component — is fundamentally about expanding and stabilizing each leader's window in the specific conditions that trigger their pattern.

**Conversational form:** *"There's a functional zone every person operates within — when you're in it, you can think clearly and relate well. When you're outside it — either shut down or amped up — the relational behaviors the job requires aren't accessible. The assessment identifies where each manager goes under pressure. The coaching is about expanding that window."*

## Stream 3 — Cognitive Neuroscience in Organizations

**Anchor researcher:** David Rock — Co-founder, NeuroLeadership Institute; *Your Brain at Work* (2009); *SCARF: A Brain-Based Model for Collaborating With and Influencing Others* (2008, *NeuroLeadership Journal*)

### Core Framework: SCARF

Rock and colleagues identified five social domains that the brain processes using the same neural pathways as physical threat or reward (Rock & Cox, 2012; Lieberman & Eisenberger, 2009 — supporting fMRI research). Threat in any domain activates an away response; reward activates a toward response.

| Domain | Threat Examples in Hospitality Management | Away Response |
|---|---|---|
| **Status** | Being corrected in front of peers; passed over for a role; public criticism from owner | Withdrawal, defensiveness, counter-attack |
| **Certainty** | Shifting expectations; unpredictable owner decisions; unclear performance standards | Anxiety-driven escalation; over-communication |
| **Autonomy** | Micromanagement; decisions reversed without explanation | Passive non-compliance; surface agreement without follow-through |
| **Relatedness** | Exclusion from owner's inner circle; team conflict unresolved; perceived favoritism | Social withdrawal; coalition-building; exit |
| **Fairness** | Inconsistent standards applied across properties or employees; perceived special treatment | Moral outrage; collective grievance; disengagement |

### Key Finding for RPA

Most management behavior that drives employee turnover, conflict, and disengagement activates threat across one or more SCARF domains — not because managers intend harm, but because they are operating from their own dysregulated state and activating the workforce as a result.

The specific power of the SCARF model for RPA: it is observable and measurable in management behavior. The 360 instrument and narrative interview are designed to identify which SCARF domains a leader's behavior most chronically activates in others.

**Conversational form:** *"There's very well-replicated brain research showing that when people feel threatened socially — status, fairness, not knowing what to expect — the same pathways fire as a physical threat. Your managers aren't trying to trigger their teams. They're operating from their own stress, and it lands on the workforce as threat. That's what the assessment is designed to see."*

## Stream 4 — Self-Determination Theory

**Anchor researchers:** Edward Deci & Richard Ryan — Professors, University of Rochester; foundational SDT papers from 1985 onward; *Self-Determination and Intrinsic Motivation in Human Behavior* (1985)

### Core Framework: Three Basic Psychological Needs

SDT proposes three universal psychological needs whose satisfaction predicts motivated, well-functioning behavior across cultures and settings:

- **Autonomy** — acting from genuine volition, not external pressure
- **Competence** — experiencing effectiveness and mastery
- **Relatedness** — feeling meaningfully connected and cared for

When managers satisfy these needs in their employees, employees function with autonomous motivation — they do the work because it matters to them, not because they are monitored. When managers thwart these needs, employees shift to controlled motivation or amotivation — which predicts lower performance, higher burnout, and higher turnover.

### The Training Evidence

The critical finding for RPA: needs-supportive manager behavior is **trainable**, and training produces measurable effects within weeks.

| Study | Sample | Training | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| Deci, Connell & Ryan (1989) | Xerox managers and employees | Manager autonomy-support training | Managers showed significantly more autonomy-supportive style; employees reported higher trust and job satisfaction vs. controls |
| Baard, Deci & Ryan (2004) | Corporate employees, two organizations | Correlational | Need satisfaction from managers predicted performance ratings, well-being, and job satisfaction independently |
| Hardré & Reeve (2009) | Manager training study | Needs-supportive training program | Measurable motivation and engagement gains in direct reports within weeks |
| Su & Reeve (2011) | Meta-analysis | Multiple interventions | Consistent positive effects of autonomy-support training across work and educational settings |
| Slemp et al. (2018) | Meta-analysis, 99 studies | Leader autonomy support | Strongly predicted employee vitality, engagement, and job satisfaction; moderate-to-large effect sizes |
| Jungert et al. (2022) | *International Journal of Training and Development* | One-day workshop | Measurable reduction in subordinate-rated needs-thwarting behavior within 6 weeks |
| ImmersyveHealth (RCT) | Nurse managers, healthcare | SDT-based manager training | 83% reduction in burnout; 92% retention improvement |
| motivationWorks Fortune 500 RCT | Corporate managers | SDT-consistent manager training | Significant increases in employee need satisfaction, autonomous motivation, and work engagement at 5-week follow-up |

### Trainable Behaviors

The three SDT behavior clusters are specific, observable, and trainable — not personality traits:

**Autonomy support:** Providing a meaningful rationale for directives. Offering choices within constraints. Using non-controlling language. Acknowledging employee perspectives before redirecting.

**Competence support:** Delivering clear, informational (not evaluative) feedback. Setting achievable goals with structured support. Identifying strengths before correcting deficits.

**Relatedness support:** Regular one-on-one contact. Supporting team cohesion. Sharing appropriate information and personal experience.

**Conversational form:** *"There's a specific body of research — decades of it, multiple meta-analyses — showing that when managers are trained in specific behaviors that meet employees' basic psychological needs, you get measurable improvements in engagement and retention within weeks. Not years. Weeks. And the behaviors are specific — not 'be a better leader.' Concrete things a manager can actually do differently on a Tuesday morning."*

## Stream 5 — Constructive-Developmental Theory & Change Science

### 5A. Kegan & Lahey — Immunity to Change

**Robert Kegan & Lisa Laskow Lahey** — Harvard Graduate School of Education; *Immunity to Change* (2009)

**Core claim:** Most change failure in adult development is not skill deficit or motivation deficit. It is immunity to change — an unconscious system of competing commitments and big assumptions that make the current behavior feel necessary for survival, even when the person genuinely wants to change.

**Application to OM development:** A manager who says they want to give direct feedback but consistently avoids it is not lazy or resistant. They hold a competing commitment — perhaps to being liked, to avoiding confrontation, to preserving a specific relationship — that overrides the stated goal. Coaching that only deposits skill does not surface the competing commitment. The immunity map process does.

**RPA Application:** The individual coaching arc includes immunity-to-change mapping for leaders whose stated commitment and actual behavior are significantly misaligned. This is not a clinical intervention — it is a structured reflective process that surfaces the assumption layer beneath the pattern.

### 5B. Prochaska — Transtheoretical Model (Stages of Change)

**James Prochaska, Ph.D.** — University of Rhode Island; *Changing for Good* (1994)

| Stage | Description | Appropriate Intervention |
|---|---|---|
| **Pre-contemplation** | Does not see behavior as a problem; attributes outcomes to others | Personalized feedback linking behavior to specific outcomes; raise awareness without confronting |
| **Contemplation** | Sees the problem; weighing costs and benefits of change; ambivalent | Decisional balance; explore ambivalence; evoke personal reasons |
| **Preparation** | Committed to change; developing a plan | Behavioral goal-setting; initial experiments; build confidence |
| **Action** | Actively practicing new behavior | Skill consolidation; relapse prevention; accountability |
| **Maintenance** | Sustained change; preventing relapse | Ongoing support; recognition of progress; identity consolidation |

**Application:** Stage-mismatched intervention is a primary failure mode in management development. Giving action-stage tools to a pre-contemplation-stage leader produces compliance performance without behavioral change. The narrative interview and initial coaching sessions are stage-assessment opportunities before any development work begins.

### 5C. Miller & Rollnick — Motivational Interviewing

**William Miller & Stephen Rollnick** — *Motivational Interviewing: Helping People Change* (3rd ed., 2012)

**Core claim:** Ambivalence is a normal feature of change, not a sign of resistance. The practitioner's job is to evoke the person's own motivation for change — not to convince, argue, or prescribe.

**OARS skill set:** Open questions; Affirming; Reflecting; Summarizing — the relational posture of the coaching relationship throughout the engagement.

**Application:** The spirit of MI governs every touch point in the coaching arc. Particularly important in delivering the behavioral pattern profile: the leader's reaction to their profile is the first coaching session. How Andrew holds that conversation determines whether the leader integrates or defends.

### 5D. Kolb — Experiential Learning Theory

**David Kolb** — *Experiential Learning: Experience as the Source of Learning and Development* (1984)

**Core claim:** Learning that transfers to behavior requires a full cycle: concrete experience → reflective observation → abstract conceptualization → active experimentation. Skipping the experiential or reflective stages produces knowledge without behavioral change.

**Application:** The facilitated observation session (Layer 1 assessment) and team facilitation sequence (Layer 3 intervention) are designed on Kolb's cycle. Leaders experience genuine relational tension, observe their own responses, conceptualize what happened in behavioral terms, and experiment with different responses in subsequent sessions.

## Stream 6 — Organizational Culture & Identity

### The Three-Level Culture Model (Schein)

**Edgar Schein** — MIT Sloan School of Management; *Organizational Culture and Leadership* (1985, 4th ed. 2010)

Culture operates at three levels:
1. **Artifacts** — visible behaviors, structures, procedures; easy to observe, difficult to decipher
2. **Espoused values** — strategies, goals, stated philosophies; what the organization says it believes
3. **Underlying assumptions** — unconscious, taken-for-granted beliefs; the actual driver of behavior

**Key finding:** Most culture programs fail because they target Levels 1–2 and never reach the assumption level. Identity formation — the condition for sustainable culture — only occurs at Level 3.

**RPA Application:** The narrative interview specifically targets the assumption layer. How a leader speaks about relational events reveals assumptions about authority, accountability, and self-efficacy that no survey or training program surfaces.

### Identity Formation as the Mechanism (Ashforth & Mael; Kusluvan)

**Ashforth & Mael (1989)** — Applied Social Identity Theory to organizations. Employees derive self-concept from organizational membership. Identification is stronger when the organization is distinctive and worth belonging to.

**Kusluvan et al. (2010)** — *Cornell Hospitality Quarterly* comprehensive review. Culture is the dominant predictor of turnover and service quality in hospitality — more than compensation. Workers who identify with the property show 40–60% lower turnover rates. The structural turnover problem in hospitality is an identity and belonging deficit.

**Hochschild (1983)** — *The Managed Heart.* When organizations require emotional performance without enabling genuine alignment, workers experience identity conflict and burnout. High-retention properties produce authentic warmth because workers find genuine meaning in service; low-retention properties demand performance and get managed surfaces.

**Bowen & Schneider (2014)** — Employee identity perceptions of their role independently predict guest satisfaction scores, controlling for training and procedures. Identity is a causal variable.

### The Unit Manager as Culture Transmission Mechanism

**Tracey & Hinkin (2008)** — Culture programs that do not explicitly invest in supervisory modeling fail within 18 months. The unit manager is the proximate identity transmitter in dispersed operations — not the brand, not the owner, not corporate.

**Van Maanen & Schein (1979)** — Formal, serial socialization (veteran-to-newcomer) produces stronger identification. Investiture tactics — affirming the newcomer rather than stripping their identity — produce higher commitment.

**Cable & Parsons (2001)** — Institutionalized socialization tactics predicted person-organization value alignment over time. The mechanism is repeated exposure and veteran modeling, not policy documents.

## Stream 7 — Trauma-Informed Practice & Psychological Safety

### Psychological Safety (Edmondson)

**Amy Edmondson, Ph.D.** — Novartis Professor of Leadership and Management, Harvard Business School; *The Fearless Organization* (2018); seminal study: *Psychological Safety and Learning Behavior in Work Teams* (1999, *Administrative Science Quarterly*)

**Core finding:** Psychological safety — the shared belief that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking — is the strongest predictor of team learning and performance, consistently across industries. In Edmondson's Google-sponsored Project Aristotle study (2012), psychological safety was the number-one differentiator between high- and low-performing teams — above all other variables.

**Operational definition:** Team members can speak up, ask questions, make mistakes, and disagree without fear of humiliation, punishment, or exclusion.

**Application:** The relational patterns RPA targets are precisely the patterns that destroy psychological safety at the unit level. A withdrawal-pattern OM creates safety collapse through absence. An escalation-pattern OM creates it through intensity. A disorganized OM creates it through unpredictability. The aggregate pattern map delivered to the sponsor translates RPA findings directly into the psychological safety language many owners already recognize.

### Trauma-Informed Practice (Bloom/SAMHSA)

**Sandra Bloom, M.D.** — *Creating Sanctuary* (1997); Andrus Children's Center; SAMHSA's Concept of Trauma and Guidance for a Trauma-Informed Approach (2014)

**Core principles relevant to RPA:**
- Safety: physical, psychological, social, and moral safety as the precondition for behavior change
- Trustworthiness: consistency between stated values and actual behavior; transparency in decisions
- Peer support: relational connection as a healing and stabilizing resource
- Collaboration: shared decision-making; power-with rather than power-over
- Empowerment: building on strengths; amplifying voice and choice
- Cultural considerations: recognizing how history and culture shape relational experience

**Application:** These principles govern how the engagement itself is structured — the confidentiality design, the voluntary participation in coaching, the way profiles are delivered, the relational posture Andrew maintains throughout. The engagement is not trauma therapy; but the structural conditions that allow people to engage honestly with difficult feedback are the same conditions these principles describe.

**van der Kolk (2014)** — *The Body Keeps the Score.* Relevant as background: behavioral patterns are stored somatically, not just cognitively. This is why behavioral change requires experiential practice in activation conditions, not just cognitive insight. Supports the rationale for the facilitated observation session design.

# Part 2 — Case Rationale

## The Presenting Problem — What the Evidence Predicts

A multi-property hospitality operation with 70–80% annual frontline turnover, persistent management inconsistency, and employee behavior patterns described as entitlement, conflict, and absenteeism is exhibiting a predictable evidence-based profile:

**What the research predicts is true about this operation:**

1. The unit manager is the proximate cause of most frontline turnover — not compensation, not the brand, not the property (Tracey & Hinkin; Kusluvan)
2. Frontline workers are experiencing chronic SCARF-domain threat activation from management behavior — producing the withdrawal, escalation, and disorganization visible as "entitlement" (Rock)
3. The workforce lacks identity-based belonging to the organization — they are performing roles, not carrying culture (Schein; Ashforth & Mael; Hochschild)
4. Management's behavioral patterns under pressure are not skill deficits — they are pattern activations that training cannot reach (Perry; Siegel)
5. Psychological safety at the unit level is compromised — learning, feedback, and correction cannot operate effectively in its absence (Edmondson)

None of these conclusions require assessment to predict. The evidence predicts them from the symptom profile. Assessment confirms the specific pattern distribution and activation profile per leader — which is what makes development targeted rather than generic.

---

## Why Standard Approaches Have Failed

The evidence also predicts why what has already been tried has not worked:

| Approach | Why It Fails | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Manager training (generic) | Deposits skill in the cortex; does not transfer to behavior under activation | Perry; Siegel |
| HR policy updates | Targets Level 1 artifacts; does not reach assumption-level culture | Schein |
| Compensation adjustments | Addresses controlled motivation, not autonomous motivation; treats a relational problem as a transactional one | Deci & Ryan; Kusluvan |
| Exit interviews | Captures what people say about why they left; misses the relational experience that preceded it | Hochschild; Tracey & Hinkin |
| Leadership development (self-report based) | Self-report divergence from behavioral reality is itself a diagnostic signal, not a data source | RPA 360 design |

---

## What the Evidence Supports as Effective Intervention

Synthesizing across all seven streams, effective intervention for this problem profile requires:

**1. Assessment before prescription** — Pattern identification precedes development planning. Generic training without assessment is treating the symptom profile, not the specific pattern distribution. (Perry; Siegel; Prochaska)

**2. Triangulated behavioral assessment** — Self-report alone is insufficient; the divergence between self-report and behavioral data is diagnostic. Multi-method, multi-rater assessment is required. (RPA 360 design; Siegel)

**3. Stage-matched individual development** — Leaders in pre-contemplation require different intervention than leaders in preparation. Stage mismatch produces compliance performance without behavior change. (Prochaska; Miller & Rollnick)

**4. Needs-supportive behavior training** — Specific, trainable behaviors in autonomy, competence, and relatedness support produce measurable improvement in employee need satisfaction within weeks. This is the behavioral translation layer of the assessment findings. (Deci & Ryan; Jungert 2022; Slemp 2018)

**5. Structural conditions** — The engagement environment must itself model the safety conditions that make honest engagement possible. Confidentiality design, voluntary participation, and the relational posture of the practitioner are structural variables, not incidentals. (Edmondson; Bloom/SAMHSA)

**6. The unit manager as the intervention point** — Any intervention that does not explicitly target OM relational behavior as the primary lever will fail to change frontline outcomes. (Tracey & Hinkin; Kusluvan)

## The Literature Gap RPA Fills

No existing provider or framework integrates these seven evidence streams in a mid-market organizational consulting engagement:

- SDT-based manager training exists but does not address neurobiological pattern formation or use behavioral assessment to target development
- Trauma-informed practice addresses the relational environment mechanism but has no organizational management development application
- NeuroLeadership (Rock) addresses cognitive science in organizations but does not provide an assessment and coaching methodology
- Immunity to Change (Kegan/Lahey) addresses the assumption layer but is not designed for the OM population or hospitality context
- Generic management consulting uses outcomes language but does not reach the behavioral pattern level

RPA occupies the intersection. The integration is the differentiated value.
