# A001 — Conversation Readiness Guide

*Pre-meeting internalization reference. Not a script — a readiness map. Know the pivots cold so the conversation stays in the relational register rather than dropping into problem-solving mode when the owner goes somewhere unexpected.*

## Pivot 1 — Cost and Pricing

**Signal phrases:**
*"What does something like this cost?" / "Give me a ballpark." / "I need to know what we're talking about financially."*

**Ready answer:**
"Phase 1 — the assessment and recommendation phase — is $28K. If you decide to move to full implementation, that runs $87K to $136K depending on scope. I'll give you a specific number once we know what we're actually building.

Here's the frame that matters: at your scale, you're likely replacing somewhere between 280 and 300 people a year. At $3,000 to $5,000 per replacement, that's $840K to $1.5 million annually in replacement cost alone — before productivity loss and guest experience. The engagement pays for itself at a fraction of a percentage point of turnover reduction."

**Credibility bridge:**
The way the pricing is structured is scope-matched — Phase 1 is fixed; implementation phases flex with what the operation actually needs. The number you get will be real, not a negotiating position.

**Red-flag guard:**
- Don't apologize for the price or minimize it — it lands as insecurity
- Don't offer a discount or "work something out" before he's pushed back
- Don't show all three tiers unless he asks — lead with Option C only
- Don't introduce the ROI math before he's named the cost as a concern — let him raise it

## Pivot 2 — What Are You Doing With My Managers?

**Signal phrases:**
*"What exactly does this look like for my OMs?" / "I don't want my managers feeling observed or studied." / "What are you going to be asking them?"*

**Ready answer:**
"There's a specific confidentiality structure, and it protects you as much as it protects them. What I gather in assessment stays with each leader. What you receive is an aggregate picture — how the team functions collectively, what conditions are producing what you're seeing — but not individual data. Your managers know that before we start.

The reason that matters practically: managers who know their data goes to the owner perform for the evaluation. Managers who know their data is theirs give me accurate information. The confidentiality structure is what makes the assessment worth anything."

**Credibility bridge:**
This is standard architecture in organizational assessment work. Confidentiality isn't protective bureaucracy — it's what produces the accurate data you actually need.

**Red-flag guard:**
- Don't frame it as "I won't tell you anything about your managers" — sounds like you're hiding something; frame it as the structure that produces accuracy
- Don't describe the full assessment process in detail — it sounds bureaucratic and extends the meeting into territory that belongs in Phase 1 contracting
- Don't mention specific patterns or clinical-sounding language here

## Pivot 3 — Timeline and Results

**Signal phrases:**
*"How long before I see a difference?" / "I need results within 6 months." / "What's the timeline on this?"*

**Ready answer:**
"Two layers. The first things you'll notice are behavioral — your managers showing up differently in difficult situations, particularly when something goes wrong on a shift. Those signals typically appear within 60 to 90 days of well-implemented development work. The turnover data follows that, usually at 6 to 12 months.

A culture that holds without you maintaining it manually — where the norms are self-transmitting to new employees — that takes 18 months to 3 years. I'm not going to tell you that's fast, because it's not. What's faster is not doing it — and you've already seen what that costs.

Phase 1 itself — assessment and recommendations — is 13 weeks."

**Credibility bridge:**
The behavioral change research is consistent on timelines. Rushing them produces surface change that performs while you're watching and reverts when you're not. What I'm building with you changes the system, not the performance review score.

**Red-flag guard:**
- Don't give unrealistic timelines to close — it builds false expectation that creates a credibility problem later
- Don't say "it depends" without immediately specifying what it depends on
- Don't be vague about Phase 1 — 13 weeks is specific and honest; say it

## Pivot 4 — I've Tried Things Before

**Signal phrases:**
*"We've done management training — it didn't stick." / "I brought someone in a few years ago and nothing changed." / "My managers went through a program and I didn't see any difference."*

**Ready answer:**
"That's the most common experience — and the research explains exactly why it happens. Standard training tells people what to do differently. But when they walk back into the same environment, the same patterns return within 30 to 90 days. The training changed the knowledge. It didn't change the conditions that produce the behavior.

That's actually the same thing you identified in that conversation — the environment drives behavior, not the character of your people. Standard training doesn't touch the environment. That's the structural difference here."

**Credibility bridge:**
There's meta-analytic data on this — across hundreds of training transfer studies, single-event training reverts without structural follow-through and without changing what's above the manager. That's not a failure of the training; it's a failure of the model.

**Red-flag guard:**
- Don't name or critique any prior provider — it implicates his judgment in hiring them
- Don't claim immunity to the same failure: "this is guaranteed to work" is less credible than the honest version
- Don't rush past his statement — acknowledge it before responding: "That's actually the most common experience..." not "Yes, but what I do is different."

## Pivot 5 — How Are You Different From a Trainer or HR Consultant?

**Signal phrases:**
*"Is this like a training program?" / "What's different from what an HR consultant does?" / "We already have an HR person."*

**Ready answer:**
"Trainers deliver content to groups. I don't do that. I work with individual leaders — assessment, private development, and structural recommendations for the operation. The group component is facilitation, not content delivery.

HR manages compliance, process, and personnel administration. What I work on is the relational and behavioral patterns HR can't reach — how your managers actually function in difficult situations, and what the environment is producing.

I'm also not operational management. I'm an advisor. I build the framework and guide the development work. Your team implements."

**Credibility bridge:**
The distinction matters most for your OMs. They need to know I'm not their HR person and not their evaluator. That clarity is what makes them willing to be candid.

**Red-flag guard:**
- Don't position negatively against HR if he has an HR person he values — frame the distinction as complementary, not competitive
- Don't imply that prior training was wasted — it wasn't worthless, it just didn't address the root level
- Three sentences max on this — if you over-explain the role, it sounds defensive

## Pivot 6 — Are My Managers the Problem?

**Signal phrases:**
*"So are you saying my OMs are causing this?" / "I've had these managers for years — are they the issue?" / "This is starting to sound like it's about my management team."*

**Ready answer:**
"No — not in the way you mean it. Your managers are functioning the way their environment shaped them to function. The same conditions that produced what you're seeing in your frontline staff shaped your OMs. They're not the cause — they're the transmission mechanism. Which also means they're the lever.

It's the same reframe from that conversation — behavior is environmental. It applies at every level of the organization. The OMs aren't the problem to fix; they're the most important part of what we build."

**Credibility bridge:**
The hospitality research on this is consistent — the unit manager is the proximate culture carrier in dispersed operations. They can only transmit what they've experienced. That's the development target.

**Red-flag guard:**
- Don't fully remove OM accountability — "it's all the environment" is too easy and not credible; the point is that change requires support, not just willpower
- Don't volunteer this pivot — only deliver it if he raises the concern directly
- Don't name specific OMs or speculate about individual patterns before the assessment

## Pivot 7 — What Do I Actually Get?

**Signal phrases:**
*"What do I have at the end of this?" / "What exactly are you producing?" / "How do I know what I'm paying for?"*

**Ready answer:**
"Three things from Phase 1. A behavioral profile of each leader in the engagement — what their patterns are, what activates them, and where their development edge is. An aggregate pattern map of your management team — how the team functions collectively and what structural conditions are contributing. And a set of structural recommendations for your operation — specific changes to how you're running the environment that are driving what you're seeing.

Those three give you a clear picture of what you're actually dealing with and a specific path forward. At that point, you decide whether you move to implementation."

**Credibility bridge:**
The assessment is built to produce information you can act on — not a document. Everything in the output connects to a specific development or structural decision.

**Red-flag guard:**
- Don't call them "reports" — operational owners want outcomes, not documents; say "profile," "map," "recommendations"
- Don't describe the assessment methodology in detail here — that belongs in Phase 1 contracting
- Don't present all three options unless he asks; keep the focus on Phase 1 output

## Credibility Reference Block

*How to name researchers without lecturing. Rule: plain language first, always. Only attribute if it adds genuine weight to the point. Never lead with the name.*

| Concept                                                | Plain language first                                                                                                                                                                                                                                   | Optional attribution                                                                                                                                         |
| ------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Regulated brain = learning; threatened brain = pattern | "Under relational threat, the parts of the brain that training targets are the first to go offline — judgment, perspective-taking, accountability delivery. That's not a character failure. It's a physiological sequence."                            | "A developmental neuroscientist named Bruce Perry documented this across decades of research on how the brain responds under stress."                        |
| Training transfer failure                              | "Meta-analytic data across hundreds of training studies shows that single-event training reverts within 30 to 90 days without structural follow-through."                                                                                              | "The research on training transfer is pretty unambiguous on this."                                                                                           |
| Unit manager as culture carrier                        | "In dispersed hotel operations, the unit manager is the culture — not the brand, not the handbook. What they model is what transmits."                                                                                                                 | "Hospitality-specific research confirms this — culture programs that don't invest in supervisory modeling fail within 18 months."                            |
| Psychological safety                                   | "Teams where people feel safe enough to speak up, flag problems, and admit mistakes consistently outperform teams where they don't. That's not intuitive management philosophy — it's one of the most replicated findings in organizational research." | "Amy Edmondson at Harvard has built 25 years of research on this."                                                                                           |
| Turnover as identity deficit                           | "The structural turnover problem in hospitality is fundamentally a belonging and identity deficit — not a compensation problem. Workers who identify with the culture stay at 40 to 60 percent lower rates than those who don't."                      | "That's from large-scale hospitality workforce research — the compensation levers that owners reach for first are not what the data shows is driving exits." |

**Three rules:**
1. Translate first — then optionally attribute
2. Never say "studies show" — too vague; say "the data shows" or "the evidence is consistent"
3. Never use research to end a conversation — use it to open one: "What do you see in your operation that fits that pattern?"

## What NOT to Say

| Temptation | Why it backfires | Say this instead |
|---|---|---|
| "I've worked with hundreds of organizations like yours" | Claim you can't back up; undermines the honest pull-back | Let the credibility talking points carry the weight |
| "Trust me, this works" | Self-orientation red flag — sounds like selling, not advising | "Here's what the data shows and why it applies to your operation" |
| "Your managers probably don't know what they're doing" | Implicates his hiring and supervision decisions | "Your managers are functioning the way the environment shaped them to function" |
| "This is guaranteed to reduce your turnover" | No outcome is guaranteed; false certainty produces future credibility damage | "Here's what organizations at your scale typically experience when this is done well" |
| "The consultants you tried got it wrong" | Attacks his judgment in hiring them | "Standard approaches miss this because of how they're designed, not who delivers them" |
| "I'll take care of all of this" | Overpromises scope; creates dependency frame | "I'll build the framework and guide the work — your team implements" |
| "This won't take long" | Dishonest; 13 weeks minimum for Phase 1, 18 months for culture embedding | "Phase 1 is 13 weeks. I'll be honest with you about what the full arc looks like." |
| "Let me tell you about [researcher]'s work on this..." | Switches the register from conversation to lecture | Translate to plain language first; only name the researcher if it adds weight after the point lands |
| "You're probably spending more than you realize on turnover" | Presumptuous; makes him feel diagnosed before he's spoken | Ask: "Do you know what turnover is costing you annually?" — let him surface the number |
| "Let me propose..." | Introduces the engagement before he's asked for it | Wait for him to surface "what now" — then respond to his question, not your agenda |
| "I'm not a therapist" | True but sounds defensive; brings clinical framing into a conversation it wasn't in | "I'm an organizational consultant — behavioral and operational. This is about what managers do, not who they are." |
