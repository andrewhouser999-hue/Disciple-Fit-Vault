**Scope:** Practices whose *absence* demonstrably prevents psychological safety from forming or holding in a chronically dysregulated hospitality operation. Not a "nice to have" list — the research establishes that each addresses a mechanism no other practice can reach.

**Timeframe:** Meaningful safety levels observable in employee behavior: 6–12 months minimum. Full cultural embedding (norms self-transmit to new employees): 2–3 years.

**Type conventions:** (T) = trainable behavioral cluster · (S) = structural/design intervention · (C) = cultural norm requiring sustained practice

## Prerequisites — Must Be True Before Other Practices Can Hold

These are not interventions to schedule. They are conditions that either exist or don't. If they don't exist, every practice below is compromised.

| # | Prerequisite | Why it's load-bearing |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Owner behavior change** | Owner behavior is the ongoing permission structure for all OM behavior. If the owner models threat, blame, and control, OM training reverts within 30–90 days regardless of quality. This is not one factor among many — it is the single largest barrier to sustainable safety-building in owner-operated organizations. *(Burke & Hutchins 2007 MA; Tracey & Hinkin 2008)* |
| 2 | **Manager regulation capacity baseline** | Behavioral training requires prefrontal access to execute. A chronically activated manager cannot reliably deliver inclusive behaviors under the pressure conditions that matter most. Train regulated managers — the ROI is multiplicative. Train dysregulated managers — the ROI is near zero. *(Porges 2011; Iodice et al. 2022)* |
| 3 | **Elimination of active punishment of voice** | You cannot build a safety ceiling in an environment still actively destroying the floor. Before safety-building can begin, the known punishment behaviors must be removed from the management repertoire. *(Detert & Edmondson 2011)* |
| 4 | **Shared language for safety, emotion, and error** | Without a shared vocabulary — whether SELF, the three check-in questions, or simplified polyvagal language — safety concepts remain implicit and unaddressable. *(Bloom 2013; Porges 2011)* |

## Level A — Owner / Senior Leadership

The owner's behavior is not an antecedent to the program — it *is* the program at this level. These three practices belong to the owner, not to the OMs.

| Practice | Type | Evidence | What it does | Why non-negotiable |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Owner behavioral modeling of safety practices** | (C) | Tracey/Hinkin Long; Burke/Hutchins MA | Owner visibly exhibits the same inclusive, non-blame-attributing, inquiry-first behaviors expected of OMs — in every observed interaction, not only in stated values | Owner behavior that models threat in practice while training safety in theory produces OM cynicism, not OM change |
| **Structural protection of OM regulation capacity** | (S) | JD-R (Bakker/Demerouti MA); effort-recovery theory (Meijman/Mulder) | Role design, scheduling, and performance pressure management that do not chronically deplete OMs' regulatory capacity | An owner who sends OMs to regulation training while maintaining structures that keep OMs in chronic sympathetic activation is producing mutually canceling interventions |
| **Explicit safety-first framing from owner** | (C) | Edmondson Long; SAMHSA CF | Owner publicly names psychological safety as an organizational priority, names what will no longer be tolerated, and commits to observable behavioral change | This metacommunicative act is the only direct IVT-disconfirmation intervention available at the cultural level; without it, employees have no reason to update their implicit beliefs that "it's always unsafe here" |

## Level B — Manager / Supervisor Daily Behaviors

These are what the OMs and shift supervisors *do* — the operational mechanism through which safety is built or destroyed each shift. The first item is a prerequisite for all the others.

| Practice | Type | Evidence | What it does | Why non-negotiable |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Regulated presence (co-regulation)** | (T+C) | Porges 2011 CF; Dana 2018/2020; Iodice et al. 2022 QE | Manager enters interactions in ventral vagal state — calm, present, non-scanning — which co-regulates the employee's nervous system into the state required for learning and voice | A dysregulated manager co-dysregulates the team regardless of the behavioral techniques they've been trained in; the nervous system state communicated non-verbally overrides all behavioral content |
| **Leader inclusiveness: openness, accessibility, availability** | (T) | Nembhard/Edmondson 2006 Long; Frazier et al. 2017 MA r=.36 | Manager visibly invites and appreciates contribution; reduces the transactional cost of approaching them; creates structured moments for input | Strongest and most consistent antecedent of psychological safety in the literature; disproportionately impactful in high-power-distance contexts (hospitality frontline); non-redundant with SDT — addresses interpersonal risk calculation that SDT does not reach |
| **Regulate-relate-reason interaction sequencing** | (T) | Perry NMT CF/Long | Manager sequences all significant interactions — performance conversations, conflict resolution, feedback delivery — regulate first (reduce threat state), relate second (establish relational contact), reason third (content, problem-solving, behavioral direction) | Without correct sequencing, every performance conversation and feedback delivery systematically fails for dysregulated employees; Reason-before-Regulate is not a style preference problem — it is a neurobiological error |
| **EMC: inquiry-first error response** | (T+C) | Frese/Keith 2015 Review; Van Dyck et al. 2005 XS replication | When an error occurs, the manager's first move is inquiry ("walk me through what happened") — not blame, not consequence — before any evaluation; personal error disclosure modeling | Error response is the highest-stakes safety test in a service environment; each error event is a data point that the entire floor observes and uses to calibrate their implicit safety assessment — the audience effect multiplies the impact of every response |
| **Task-level, informational feedback delivery** | (T) | Kluger/DeNisi 1996 MA (k=607); Deci/Ryan SDT | Feedback targets specific, observable, recent behaviors; separates observation from evaluation; begins with behavioral description before any evaluative label; corrective feedback always private, appreciative feedback can be public | 38% of feedback interventions *harm* performance; self-level negative feedback is an active safety-destroying act; in high-turnover environments where no relational trust buffer exists, the structure of feedback must be safe on its face, not safe by virtue of relationship |
| **Servant leadership orientation: emotional healing + humility** | (C+T) | Eva et al. 2019 MA/systematic review (285 articles); van Dierendonck/Nuijten 2011 | Manager operates from the fundamental orientation that their job is follower flourishing, not goal extraction; actively recognizes emotional depletion and creates conditions for recovery; models accurate self-limitation; credits the team | Without this integrating orientation, all specific behavioral practices can be deployed in a controlling-instrumental way that defeats their safety function; emotional healing is the highest-leverage servant leadership behavior in an emotional labor context |

## Level C — Structural / Operational Design

Structures that embed safety into how the organization runs — independent of individual leader presence. In a high-turnover environment, this is essential: the leader who built safety will be replaced. The structure must survive them.

| Practice | Type | Evidence | What it does | Hospitality note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Daily pre-shift Community Meeting (3-question check-in)** | (S) | Bloom/Sanctuary Model Long; EWOP 2025 standup Long | Pre-shift opens with three questions: (1) How are you feeling today? (2) What is your goal for today? (3) Who can you ask for help? — then operational content follows | Shift-based work creates daily relationship discontinuity; the 12-hour gap between shifts resets the safety climate; this is the structural equivalent of regulate-relate-reason applied organizationally each day |
| **Post-shift brief debrief (5–8 min)** | (S) | Edmondson 1999 Long (N=51 teams); Frese/Keith EMC | Structured closing: one learning question ("what went well, what was hard, what one thing could we change?") — serves the EMC learning function, regulation-recovery function, and safety function simultaneously | Full team debrief may not be operationally feasible with rolling departures; adapt to brief rolling check-outs (supervisor acknowledges each departing employee, one question) |
| **Bi-weekly 1-on-1 meetings as safety infrastructure** | (S) | INSEAD/Sandoz/Novartis RCT (N≈7,000 employees) | Supervisor asks each employee what matters to them and where they need support; private setting removes audience threat from voice-testing | High-turnover adaptation: shorter but higher-frequency; brief pre/post-shift contacts serve the same function as a longer scheduled meeting; the structural commitment to consistent individual contact is the essential element |
| **Supervisory accountability: upward feedback + structured discussion** | (S) | Walker/Smither 1999 Long/QE (5-year); Heslin/Latham 2004 QE; Burke/Hutchins MA | Regular, structured measurement of safety and management behavior, with results fed back to managers and *required discussion* with their reports | The combination of feedback + discussion is what produces improvement; feedback alone without discussion produces less improvement; the required discussion is not optional |
| **Meeting norms: invitation, round-robin, silence tolerance, challenge-welcome** | (S+T) | Edmondson Long; Detert/Edmondson 2011 | Explicit invitation to contribute at the start of group interactions; round-robin opening before open discussion; 3–5 seconds of silence after questions; named permission to disagree | Protects against the first-speaker effect (dominant voices suppressing lower-status voice); normalizes contribution from frontline employees who have the highest power distance from leadership |
| **Formal voice channels with functional follow-through** | (S) | Detert/Edmondson 2011 XS/CF | Anonymous reporting mechanisms, suggestion processes, or designated escalation paths that demonstrably produce responses | Non-functional formal channels are worse than none — they generate expectation and then fail to deliver, confirming the IVT that "nothing changes when I speak up"; functional follow-through is mandatory |
| **Psychological safety measurement and feedback loop** | (S) | Frazier et al. 2017 MA; Walker/Smither 1999 | Regular administration of a validated psychological safety instrument (e.g., Edmondson 7-item team scale) at the property level, results shared with managers | Makes the invisible visible; creates the data loop that sustains manager behavior improvement over time; without measurement, there is no accountability signal and no way to verify whether anything is working |

## Sequencing

The research establishes a causal sequence. Skipping levels does not accelerate the timeline — it produces the most common failure mode (see below).

```
1. PREREQUISITES
   Owner behavior change
   Manager regulation capacity
   Eliminate active voice punishment
   Shared language

        ↓

2. LEVEL A — OWNER BEHAVIORS
   Modeling · Structural protection · Explicit framing

        ↓

3. LEVEL B — MANAGER BEHAVIORS
   Regulated presence → Leader inclusiveness → Regulate-relate-reason
   → EMC error response → Task-level feedback → Servant orientation

        ↓

4. LEVEL C — STRUCTURAL MECHANISMS
   Daily meeting · Post-shift debrief · 1-on-1s
   · Accountability structures · Voice channels · Measurement

        ↓

5. CULTURAL EMBEDDING
   IVT disconfirmation through sustained consistency
   Peer norm transmission · Shared language deepens
   (18 months – 3 years)
```

## The Most Common Failure Mode

**Training managers in safety practices without changing what's above them.**

The mechanism: OM receives training, returns to an environment where the owner still models threat and blame, performance pressure structures keep the OM in chronic sympathetic activation, and the workforce IVTs continue to be confirmed by the historical organizational narrative. Behavioral reversion within 30–90 days — not because the OM failed, but because the system that produced the unsafe culture is still fully operational.

The research on this is unambiguous: the first move in any safety-building initiative in a chronically dysregulated owner-operated hospitality organization is the **owner's public and behavioral commitment** — not manager training.
