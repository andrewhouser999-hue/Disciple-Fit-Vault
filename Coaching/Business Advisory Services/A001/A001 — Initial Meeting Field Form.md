> "Our time together will be successful if you leave with a clearer picture of what's driving what you're already seeing — not my interpretation of it, yours. My job today is to ask the right questions. Yours is to tell me what's actually true, not what sounds good. If we do that, we'll both know by the end of this conversation whether what I do is the right fit for your situation."

# A001 — Initial Meeting Field Form

*During-meeting reference. Check off, note briefly. Full debrief → [[A001 — Engagement Intake]] after the meeting.*

---

> [!note] The Goal of This Meeting
> Not to diagnose his operation. Not to present your credentials. Not to close.
>
> **The goal is for him to see something about his situation that he couldn't see before you sat down.** Not because you showed it to him — because your questions created the conditions for him to see it himself. If you accomplish that, everything else follows: curiosity, OM involvement, the ask. If you don't, everything else is salesmanship.
>
> His implicit pattern is part of what needs to become visible. Not named, not diagnosed — visible. He arrives thinking about his management team. He should leave wondering about himself.

> [!warning] Facilitation Discipline — Read This First
> - **Ask → quiet → specific.** When he generalizes, return to the incident: *"Walk me through the last time."*
> - **Reflect before adding.** Show him what you heard before introducing anything new.
> - **Hold the solution space.** Do not introduce what you do until the problem space is fully mapped.
>
> Every question in this document is decent. None of them matter if you don't hold this discipline.

---

## Pre-Meeting

- [ ] Two or three specific moments from the coffee shop exchange — written down
- [ ] Decided what you will NOT accomplish today
- [ ] Role boundary language is yours, not improvised
- [ ] ★ Next-step offer ready: specific ask, specific day
- [ ] Engagement Intake open and ready — capture immediately after

---

## Phase 1 — Calibrate

- [ ] ★ **"Where has your thinking gone since we talked?"**

  *Notes:*

> [!tip] What This Question Does
> 1. Re-anchors the shared starting point — the coffee shop insight is the relational foundation of this meeting.
> 2. Tells you whether he's moved toward or away from the root-cause frame.
> 3. Positions you as someone who cares about his thinking, not just his data.
>
> **If he's moved deeper into the root-cause frame:** compress pain picture, push sooner toward Phase 5.
> **If he's normalized again** ("it's just a busy season"): spend more time in Phase 2 before moving to structure.

- [ ] Let him finish completely — no agenda-driving in the first five minutes

---

## Phase 2 — Behavioral Specifics

> [!note] The Discipline Here
> Your job is to help him describe his situation at a level of specificity he hasn't reached before. Most owners have the broad strokes — turnover is high, managers don't handle conflict well, culture varies by property. They rarely have the behavioral incident layer.
>
> Every time you bring him back to the specific incident, you're doing two things: getting more accurate data, and demonstrating the diagnostic posture — you're not summarizing his experience, you're helping him see it more clearly. That's a credibility signal no credential can buy.

- [ ] ★ **"Walk me through the last time a supervisor had to address a recurring performance problem. How did that play out?"**

  *Notes:*

> [!tip] Listen For
> - "They came to me" → supervisor escalated upward; classic withdrawal from accountability
> - "We let it go and then had to let them go" → delayed action with no corrective feedback loop; termination was a surprise to the employee
> - "I usually have to get involved" → owner is carrying accountability delivery that belongs with the supervisor; this is operational cost that can be quantified
> - Vague or general answer → the owner doesn't have visibility into how supervisors handle this; no feedback mechanism exists at the supervisor level
>
> *If vague → "What did the supervisor actually say to the employee — do you know?"*

- [ ] **"What happens when a guest complaint hits — walk me through the management chain."**

  *Notes:*

> [!tip] Listen For
> - Who handles the complaint: manager or supervisor → who carries the relational weight under pressure
> - What happens to the employee involved → does the employee feel supported or exposed? This is a direct psychological safety signal
> - Whether the manager stays regulated or shifts register → escalation, blame attribution, defensiveness
> - "We follow brand standards" → compliance frame; the human relational sequence may or may not be happening under the script
> - "The manager takes the hit" → owner-level pattern of absorbing accountability; exhausting and culturally disempowering
>
> *If compliance-frame answer → "What does the employee experience in that sequence — from the moment the complaint happens?"*

- [ ] **"If I walked into three of your properties unannounced and asked employees what it's like to work here — would I hear the same answer at all three?"**

  *Notes:*

> [!tip] Listen For
> - Hesitation before answering → culture is inconsistent and he knows it
> - Confident "yes" → probe it: *"What would they say?"* — if vague, consistency is assumed not verified
> - "It depends on the GM" → acknowledge it directly: *"That's actually the most important thing you could say — the GM is the proximate culture carrier. Tell me more about what that looks like across yours."*
> - Names a specific property as different → use that property as a concrete reference point for the rest of the conversation

> [!warning] FLEX — Deploy Here, Not Earlier
> Wait until he has just described the behavioral breakdown in specific detail — after one of the questions above — then drop:
>
> **"Given what you're describing — do you know what that's actually costing you, across all properties, annually?"**
>
> *Notes:*
>
> If no number: *"At your scale, roughly how many people are you replacing per year?"* — let him do the math. Don't run it for him.
>
> If *"that's just hospitality"*: this is the highest-leverage opening in the conversation. The reframe from normalized assumption to diagnosable problem is where engagement energy lives. Do not rush past it.
>
> **Why it's a flex, not an opener:** Leading with this question signals that your diagnostic arc starts with financial case-building, not with listening to him. He already has the root-cause frame from the coffee shop. He doesn't need the ROI case installed — he needs to be heard first. After he's described the behavioral reality in specific detail, the cost question lands as a natural sharpening move. Before that, it reads as a setup.

## Phase 3 — Reflect the Pattern

> [!note] Why This Step Cannot Be Skipped
> After two or three behavioral incidents, you have enough to reflect. This is the trust-deepening move. You're not analyzing — you're showing him what you heard in his own language.
>
> When he confirms — and he will, because you're reflecting his own description — he hears his situation summarized by someone who understood it. That's a qualitatively different experience than being interviewed. It's also diagnostic: his reaction to your summary tells you whether his theory of the situation is settled or whether he's still uncertain about what's actually happening.

- [ ] ★ Reflect back in his language, his specific examples:

  **"Here's what I'm hearing across what you've described — [X, Y, Z]. Does that capture it, or is there something I'm missing?"**

  *Notes:*

This synthesis correctly covers six distinct PS-gap signals — upward accountability routing, no early-warning system, supervisors promoted without relational preparation, culture as person-dependent rather than structural, no GM culture accountability mechanism, and character attribution over structural attribution. Each maps directly to a specific question in the stack.

> [!warning] Hold the Solution Space
> The *"or is there something I'm missing"* is not a throwaway — it signals that his picture of the situation is the authoritative one, not yours. Wait for his confirmation. Do not introduce what you do here.

## Phase 4 — Structural Layer

> [!note] When to Deploy This Phase
> These questions move from what's happening to what the operation is producing. By this phase, the owner should begin to see the system — not just the people. Do not lead with these if Phase 2 is still producing. The structural layer earns its weight only after he's named the behavioral problem himself.

- [ ] **"When someone gets promoted into a supervisory role — what does their first 90 days look like?"**

  *Notes:*

> [!tip] Listen For
> - "They figure it out" → no development; cultural replication by osmosis
> - "We train them on systems" → task competence addressed; relational readiness not addressed
> - "They shadow me" or "they shadow the GM" → development exists but is informal; whatever patterns the GM carries are what the supervisor inherits
> - "The ones who are good just get it" → character attribution applied to leadership readiness; the environment-drives-behavior frame has not yet extended to supervisors
>
> *Follow-up: "What's the hardest thing for a new supervisor to figure out on their own?"*

- [ ] **"How do you hold your GMs accountable to a culture standard — not a performance standard, but how the place actually feels to work in?"**

  *Notes:*

> [!tip] Listen For
> - Confusion at the distinction → culture has never been separated from performance; most common gap
> - "They know what I expect" → informal, subjective, non-accountable
> - Answer drifts to metrics (turnover rate, guest scores, RevPAR) → culture measured through lagging operational indicators, not leading behavioral ones
> - "I walk the properties" → owner-level visibility substituting for structural accountability; doesn't scale across 11 properties
>
> *Follow-up: "When a property's culture is off — how do you know, and what's your move?"*

- [ ] **"How do you know when a good employee is struggling — before they decide to leave?"**

  *Notes:*

> [!tip] Listen For
> - "We find out when they don't show up" → no early warning; maximum surprise, minimum intervention window
> - "The supervisor tells me" → signal depends on the supervisor's own disclosure pattern; supervisors with withdrawal patterns will not surface problems they haven't addressed
> - "I can usually tell" → owner-level intuition filling a structural gap; doesn't scale across 11 properties
>
> *Follow-up: "What would it look like if a manager were seeing the early signs but not surfacing them?"*

> [!tip] Bridge Question — Surfaces His Causal Frame
> *"When you see the same behavior repeated across multiple people in the same role — do you read that as a people problem, a system problem, or something else?"*
>
> This tells you how he's currently framing causation. **If he says "people problem" — that's the moment you name the structural layer without arguing.** Don't correct him; follow the answer and name what you see: *"That's the most common read. What I usually find is that when the same pattern shows up across multiple people in the same role, the role itself — or how the operation surrounds that role — is doing most of the work."*

## Phase 5 — The Footprint

> [!note] The Most Important Phase
> He's described behavioral failures in his management team. You've reflected the pattern back. Now you're asking about a specific moment that almost always surfaces his own implicit contribution to the system.
>
> Most consultants either skip this phase or fumble it. The skip looks like: great reflection, now let me tell you about my solution. The fumble looks like: *"I wonder if your own management style is contributing to this."* Both end the diagnostic depth.
>
> **Do not name his pattern. Do not frame this as "your contribution."** Ask about a specific moment and let him follow the thread. His pattern — whether he absorbs accountability upward, routes around OMs, models the behaviors he wants changed or doesn't — will surface in his answers. Your job is to hold the space for him to see it, not to show it to him.

- [ ] **"When one of your OMs doesn't address something you expected them to handle — what do you do?"**

  *Notes:*

> [!tip] Listen For
> - Does he absorb accountability upward — handles it himself?
> - Does he route around the OM without a direct conversation?
> - Does he have a direct conversation with the OM about the gap?
> - Does he know what the OM was experiencing before he stepped in?
>
> The OM's behavior toward their team is often a mirror of what the OM experiences from the owner. File this observation internally. Do not name it here.

- [ ] **"What do you wish they'd handled differently in that moment?"**

  *Notes:*

> [!tip] Listen For
> His answer is his behavioral target for his OMs — and often mirrors something he either models or doesn't model for them. If he wants them to stay in the difficult conversation, hold this question internally: *does he stay in the difficult conversation with them?* Do not point it out. It surfaces in Phase 1 assessment.

> [!warning] The Most Important Move in the Meeting
> If he pauses, trails off, or begins *"I suppose I..."* — say only:
>
> **"Say more about that."**
>
> Do not finish his thought. Do not explain what you're seeing. Do not help him get there faster. Let him follow the thread. If he goes there, you've reached something real. If he doesn't, the meeting hasn't earned it yet — that's fine. It will surface in Phase 1 assessment with his OMs.

- [ ] **"What does it cost you personally when this is at its worst?"**

  *Notes:*

> [!warning] Gating Condition — Hard Stop
> Only ask if he has already disclosed something personal without being prompted — named frustration, exhaustion, something he's carrying. If the disclosure came from him first, you're in the right register. If it hasn't come yet, skip directly to the Curiosity Bridge.
>
> Asked prematurely, this lands as intrusive. Asked at the right moment, it builds more trust faster than any credential.

---

## Curiosity Bridge

> [!note] Where He Is Right Now
> He's now sitting in a more precise picture of his situation than he arrived with. He's described specific behavioral failures. He's heard his own pattern reflected back. He may have caught a glimpse of his own footprint. He doesn't have a solution. **That's exactly where you want him.**
>
> The bridge is a question, not a pitch. Do not introduce what you do. Surface what he believes would need to be true for change to happen. His answer is the entry point.

- [ ] ★ **"What do you think would need to be true for this to actually change — not improve slightly, actually change?"**

  *Notes:*

> [!tip] Two Response Paths
> **If he names what he's tried and why it didn't hold:**
> *"That's the pattern with most interventions at this level — they address the behavior without reaching what's producing it. That's the layer where my work sits."*
>
> **If he's still in the character frame** (*"I need managers who actually care"*):
> *"In my experience, the behavior is usually the right diagnosis — but what produces the behavior is one level down from that. That's where the assessment goes."*
>
> Either answer gives you the entry point to the solution without presenting the solution.

---

## Role & Scope

*Introduce after the curiosity bridge — not before.*

- [ ] ★ Frame as care for him, not disclaimer: *"I want to be clear about how I work — mostly because it matters when you're preparing your managers. I'm an advisor and implementation orchestrator, not operational management, not running programs. Your managers need to know they're not being supervised by an outside person."*

- [ ] State what you need: *"I need candid access to what's actually happening — not the polished version. And I need you willing to hear findings that might be uncomfortable."*

- [ ] Do not define full engagement scope today

---

## Transition

- [ ] ★ **OM Plant — do not propose; create the condition:**

  **"What do you think your OMs would say if I asked them the same question you just answered — about what it costs them personally?"**

  *Notes:*

> [!note] Why This Works
> Don't propose OM involvement. Create the condition for him to propose it.
>
> Let him speculate. His speculation reveals either: (1) he knows his OMs are carrying something, or (2) he's assumed they're fine and this question creates the first crack in that assumption.
>
> Then: *"That's exactly what I'd want to know before designing anything. Because the pattern you're describing isn't just a management behavior problem — it's a system. Your OMs are the transmission point in that system. What they're experiencing matters as much as what you're observing."*
>
> **Stop there. Let him propose the OMs. Not you.**

- [ ] Wait for him to surface "what now" before answering it

- [ ] ★ Summarize the diagnostic picture first: **"Here's what I've heard — does this capture it?"**

  *Notes:*

- [ ] Offer the next step, not a full engagement: *"I'll send you a short summary and a proposed next step — not a full proposal, just the shape of how I'd approach this. You review it and we talk again."*

- [ ] **Pricing:** Hold unless he raises it.
  If he raises it → *"Typically [X–Y] depending on scope. I'll give you a specific number once we know what we're actually building."*

---

## Close

- [ ] Summarize in two or three sentences: **"Have I captured that?"**

- [ ] ★ Specific next step, specific day: *"I'll send you a one-pager by [day]. Take a look and let's find time to talk before you meet with your managers."*

- [ ] Close in the same relational register you opened

## If He Asks "What Do You Recommend as Next Steps?"

*The ask is an invitation, not a close. He's asking what's next — not asking for a full proposal. Match the ask.*

- [ ] ★ **Say this:**

  > "Here's what I'd recommend — and I'll put this in writing for you before we meet again. Before either of us commits to a full engagement, I want to give you an accurate picture of what your management environment is actually producing. Not a guess — a real diagnostic. Narrative interviews with your key leaders, a 360 from their teams, and a direct observation session. At the end of that, you get a clear picture of the patterns at work, what's driving them, and a specific set of structural recommendations. You then decide whether you want to move into implementation or handle it internally.
  >
  > That phase runs 13 weeks. I'll send you a one-page summary of exactly what it includes and what it costs. Look it over, and let's talk again before you have any conversation with your managers about what we're doing."

- [ ] Then stop. Do not pivot to Options B or A. Do not give the $28K number unprompted unless he asks.

- [ ] **If he asks the price immediately:**

  > "That phase is $28,000. And based on what you've described — even at conservative turnover numbers — it pays for itself if it moves the needle by a fraction of a percent. I'll put that math in what I send you."
  >
  > *"Before we wrap up — how are you thinking about bringing your operations managers into this conversation? I want to make sure they hear it in a way that sets things up well."*
