## Core Premise — Why Training Didn't Hold

**Signal phrases:**
*"I've already put money into training." / "We tried that. It didn't stick." / "My managers know what they should be doing."*

**Short answer:**
"Your investment wasn't wrong — it just couldn't reach the level where the problem runs. Training reaches what managers know. It doesn't reach what overrides what they know when pressure hits. That's a different layer entirely, and it requires a different approach."

**Developed answer:**
"Bruce Perry's research on how the brain processes stress shows that skills learned in calm, classroom conditions don't transfer to behavior under relational pressure. It's not a motivation problem. When a manager is in a high-stakes moment — a difficult conversation, an employee conflict, a public failure — their lower brain structures activate first, and the cortical skills they learned in training go offline. That's why the training looked like it worked in the room and didn't hold on the floor. The problem was never what they knew. It was what ran underneath."

**What training couldn't reach:**
Training addresses the explicit layer — skills, scripts, knowledge. The pattern driving accountability breakdowns, conflict, and turnover operates at the implicit layer: behavioral sequences that fire automatically, below conscious access, under relational activation. No amount of training reaches that layer. What reaches it is a multi-method assessment that sees the pattern, and a development process calibrated to change it.

**Research anchor:**
*Bruce Perry, M.D. — "Cortical skills trained in calm conditions don't transfer to activation-state behavior. The lower brain structures dominate under threat."*

## Component 1 — Narrative Interview

**Signal phrases:**
*"What does that conversation actually look like?" / "You're going to interview my managers?" / "What are you asking them?"*

**Short answer:**
"It's a 45-minute structured conversation about specific incidents — not a performance review, not self-assessment. I'm asking each leader to walk me through a time when feedback didn't land, a relationship with a supervisor that was difficult, a moment when accountability broke down. I'm not listening to the story. I'm listening to how they tell it."

**Developed answer:**
"The diagnostic value is in three things: coherence — does the account hold together or does it collapse into vagueness and blame? Affect — does the person stay regulated while recounting a high-stakes event, or do they go flat or get activated? Self-other balance — do they account for their own part, or does the account consistently point outward? These patterns in the telling show you what's running underneath — more accurately than any survey, because they can't be managed in the moment."

**What training couldn't reach:**
Training assumed the problem was skill or knowledge. The interview finds something else: managers often have the skills and still can't access them under relational pressure. The conversation shows you exactly where regulation fails — which is where the pattern lives.

**Research anchor:**
*Bruce Perry, M.D. — "Cortical skills go offline under activation. The interview shows which relational conditions trigger that sequence for each leader."*

## Component 2 — Multi-Rater 360

**Signal phrases:**
*"A 360? I've done those before — they don't tell you much." / "You're asking my employees to rate their managers?" / "What's different about yours?"*

**Short answer:**
"Most 360s measure personality or generic leadership competency — and you're right, they don't tell you much. This one is built differently. Every item is a specific, observable behavior under pressure. Not 'is this person a good communicator' — 'when something goes wrong, does this person stay approachable or do they go somewhere else?' That's measurable, and it's not subject to interpretation."

**Developed answer:**
"What makes the multi-rater data valuable is what you do with the gap between how a leader rates themselves and how three groups of raters — their supervisor, their peers, their direct reports — rate them. If a leader scores themselves high on accountability delivery and their direct reports score them low, that's not a perception problem. That's the pattern I most need to see. The pattern most worth addressing is often the least visible to the person running it — which is exactly why self-report alone doesn't get you there."

**What training couldn't reach:**
Training assumed managers could self-assess accurately. The 360 finds that the behavioral pattern activating the workforce is often invisible to the manager experiencing it — not because they're dishonest, but because it runs below conscious access. Three independent rater groups catch what one source misses.

**Research anchor:**
*David Rock, NeuroLeadership Institute — "Social threat activates the same neural pathways as physical danger. Managers activate SCARF threat in others without knowing it."*

## Component 3 — Behavioral Observation

**Signal phrases:**
*"You want to watch them work?" / "What does that look like practically?" / "Is that going to make everyone uncomfortable?"*

**Short answer:**
"I run a structured half-day session with the management team — facilitated, not observed from the corner. I design scenarios that create real relational tension: a peer challenge, a difficult decision under time pressure, a repair moment. I'm watching what each leader does when pressure is actually present — not what they say they do, and not what their team reports about them."

**Developed answer:**
"The reason this is irreplaceable: most patterns are invisible until pressure is applied. A leader who avoids accountability can present well in a one-on-one conversation with me and completely go quiet three weeks later when a real problem surfaces. The interview tells me what they say about it. The 360 tells me what their team observes. The observation tells me what actually happens — in real time, in conditions I control. And by the time we run the observation, I've already built a profile from the first two sources, so I'm not watching generally. I know exactly what I'm looking for."

**What training couldn't reach:**
Training assumed calm-state learning transfers to pressure-state behavior. Observation finds that it doesn't — and that what a leader does in a genuine relational pressure situation cannot be performed or managed. This is the validity check on everything else.

**Research anchor:**
*Ekman & Friesen — "Involuntary behavioral signals under stress are more diagnostic than deliberate ones. What people do under genuine pressure cannot be managed."*

## Why Three Sources

**Signal phrases:**
*"Why do you need all three of those?" / "Isn't that a lot of process for an interview?" / "Can't you just pick the most useful one?"*

**Short answer:**
"Each source catches something the others miss. The interview shows how they construct their own experience. The 360 shows how the pattern lands on others. The observation shows what happens under actual pressure. Pull any one out and there's a blind spot — and in my experience, the blind spot is exactly where the real pattern is."

**Developed answer:**
"Think about it this way: self-report tells you what a person believes about themselves. Multi-rater data tells you what others observe. Direct observation gives you a behavioral sample under pressure. These three sources don't agree by default — and the gaps between them are the most diagnostic information I collect. A leader who scores well on the 360 but falls apart in observation is showing me something their team can't name and they can't see. That convergence — or divergence — between three independent sources is what makes the profile credible enough to deliver and specific enough to act on."

## Component 4 — Individual Behavioral Pattern Profile

**Signal phrases:**
*"So what do I actually get from all this assessment?" / "What's in the profile?" / "How specific does it get?"*

**Short answer:**
"Each leader gets a profile built from all three data sources. It identifies their primary behavioral pattern — which of four dimensions shows up when pressure hits — their activation threshold, what triggers it, what it costs operationally, and what development for them specifically looks like. Not generic. Not 'this person should communicate better.' Specific to how this leader actually functions."

**Developed answer:**
"The four dimensions are all behavioral — withdrawal under pressure, escalation, disorganization (cycling between both), and collaborative capacity. Every leader lands somewhere on this map. The profile also identifies where the leader is developmentally — whether they can see the pattern, whether they're ready to work on it, whether they're in a pre-contemplation state or actively trying to change. That readiness determines the coaching approach entirely. Giving action-stage tools to a pre-contemplation leader produces compliance without change. The profile prevents that mismatch."

**What training couldn't reach:**
Training gave the same program to every leader. The profile establishes that every leader's pattern is different — different trigger, different cost, different development path. What's effective for an escalation-pattern leader will actively backfire with a withdrawal-pattern leader.

**Research anchor:**
*Prochaska (Univ. of Rhode Island) — "Intervention mismatched to readiness stage produces compliance without behavioral change. The stage determines the method."*

## Component 5 — Coaching Arc

**Signal phrases:**
*"How is coaching different from what we've already tried?" / "What happens in the sessions?" / "How long does this take?"*

**Short answer:**
"12 sessions, roughly monthly, built around each leader's specific pattern. This is not training. Training deposits information. The coaching arc surfaces the thing that overrides the information — the competing commitment running underneath the pattern that makes the current behavior feel necessary even when the leader genuinely wants to change."

**Developed answer:**
"Kegan and Lahey at Harvard spent decades studying why adults don't change even when they want to. Their finding: most change failure isn't skill deficit or lack of motivation. It's immunity to change — an unconscious competing commitment the person holds that overrides the stated goal. A manager who says they want to give more direct feedback actually holds an equally strong commitment to not being the person who makes things worse — or to not losing a relationship they depend on. Both are real. Training adds to the stated commitment. Coaching surfaces the competing one. You can't address what you can't see."

**What training couldn't reach:**
Training operates at the stated-commitment level — what the leader says they want to do. The coaching arc operates at the competing-commitment level — what unconsciously overrides that under pressure. The distinction is the difference between a leader who leaves training knowing what to do and a leader who does it differently when the pressure is real and no one is watching.

**Research anchor:**
*Kegan & Lahey (Harvard GSE) — "Immunity to change: an unconscious competing commitment overrides the stated goal. Coaching that doesn't surface it produces compliance without change."*

## Component 6 — Team Facilitation

**Signal phrases:**
*"What's the team piece?" / "Why do you need group sessions if you're already coaching them individually?" / "Are my managers going to be uncomfortable with each other?"*

**Short answer:**
"Three structured sessions with the management team together. The individual coaching changes what each leader does in a coaching conversation. The team facilitation puts them in the room under real relational pressure — peer challenge, authority challenge, repair — and gives them a place to practice in the environment that actually activates the patterns. Individual change without team practice reverts."

**Developed answer:**
"Amy Edmondson at Harvard has more research on team psychological safety than almost anyone, and her finding across industries is consistent: psychological safety is a team-level phenomenon. You can't build it in individual coaching. A leader who develops new capacity individually re-enters a team environment that hasn't changed — and the team pulls them back. The facilitation sessions are where the individual work gets tested against real group dynamics, with someone in the room who can intervene when old patterns emerge."

**What training couldn't reach:**
Training addressed individual skill. Team facilitation addresses the shared environment — what the team creates together. That's a different level of change, and the research is clear that individual development without group-level reinforcement reverts.

**Research anchor:**
*Amy Edmondson (Harvard Business School) — "Psychological safety is the #1 predictor of team performance across industries. It is a team-level phenomenon — not an individual one."*

## Component 7 — Structural Recommendations

**Signal phrases:**
*"What are structural recommendations?" / "What does 'what makes it hold' mean?" / "Are you changing how we run things?"*

**Short answer:**
"Structural recommendations are the operational changes that reinforce what changes in the leaders — how GMs are held to culture standards, not just performance metrics; how onboarding is designed so belonging is built, not just job competency; what early warning systems look like before someone decides to leave. The behavioral work changes what your leaders do. The structural work changes the conditions they do it in."

**Developed answer:**
"Edgar Schein at MIT spent a career studying why culture programs fail. His conclusion: they target what people say and do, and never reach the underlying assumptions — or the structural conditions — that actually drive behavior. Tracey and Hinkin's research on hospitality specifically found that culture interventions focused only on individual development fail within 18 months unless the management systems and structural conditions reinforce the change. The structural recommendations are what converts individual development into a culture shift."

**What training couldn't reach:**
Training changed what leaders knew. It had no mechanism for changing the systems and conditions those leaders work within. Without structural reinforcement, even leaders who change individually return to an environment that pulls against everything they've built.

**Research anchor:**
*Tracey & Hinkin — "Culture programs without structural reinforcement fail within 18 months. The unit manager is the proximate culture transmitter — the system has to support what the manager is being asked to do."*

## The Integration Question — Why All of It?

**Signal phrases:**
*"Do you need all of this? Can we just start with the coaching?" / "Why not just do the 360 and see what it says?" / "This seems like a lot."*

**Short answer:**
"Each piece was designed to catch what the others miss. The assessment identifies the pattern. The coaching changes it at the level where it runs. The team facilitation makes the change relational, not just individual. The structural piece makes it operational, not just developmental. Pull any one out and you're back to the same failure mode you've already experienced."

**Developed answer:**
"Your training investment didn't fail because it was wrong. It failed because it addressed one layer and left the others intact. The pattern it was trying to change kept running because the assessment hadn't identified it precisely, the coaching hadn't surfaced the competing commitment behind it, the team hadn't built the shared environment to sustain different behavior, and the systems hadn't changed to reinforce it. That's four failure points. This approach closes all four. No single piece does that."
