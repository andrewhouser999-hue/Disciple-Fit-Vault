# A001 — Psychological Safety Review — Discussion Reference

**Style:** Written in the manner of Amy C. Edmondson — Harvard Business School, *The Fearless Organization* (2018), originator of the psychological safety construct in organizational research.

## A Review of the Relational Performance Advisory Approach to Psychological Safety in Multi-Property Hospitality Operations

The persistent failure of management development in high-turnover service organizations is not a mystery. The research is consistent: training that deposits knowledge into calm managers does not transfer to behavior under pressure. Organizations invest in technique, then watch it disappear under the first difficult shift. They interpret the reversion as a motivation problem or a hiring problem, and the cycle continues.

What the Relational Performance Advisory approach recognizes — and what most organizational interventions miss — is that the relevant variable is not the behavior. It is the *state* the manager is in when the behavior is required.

---

## The Mechanism This Approach Targets

Psychological safety, as the research consistently demonstrates, is not a program outcome. It is the cumulative product of micro-decisions made by managers in small moments — the error responded to without blame, the dissenting voice invited back into the conversation, the accountability conversation delivered without shame attached. What determines whether managers make those decisions correctly, under pressure, with real employees in front of them, is their capacity for regulation. A dysregulated manager cannot functionally deliver safe behaviors. This is not a character assessment — it is a neurobiological description of how threat processing works (Perry; Siegel). When managers operate outside their window of functional arousal, the skills they've been trained in are precisely what goes offline.

The RPA approach begins here. Before any behavioral training, before any facilitation, before structural recommendations are delivered, it asks: *what is the regulatory state of this management team, and what is causing it?* The multi-modal assessment — narrative interview, multi-rater 360, facilitated observation — is designed to answer that question behaviorally and specifically, with no clinical inference required.

This is a meaningful departure from standard practice. Most organizational interventions assume regulatory capacity and train behavior on top of it. When the capacity isn't there, the training fails. This approach identifies the capacity gap first, then develops toward it.

---

## What Full Adoption Actually Unlocks

The architecture works in a specific sequence, and the sequence matters causally, not just logically. The research is clear that you cannot build a safety ceiling while the floor is still being destroyed (Detert & Edmondson, 2011). Owner behavior is not one input among several — it is the ongoing permission structure for everything below it. If the owner models blame, control, and threat-attributing responses, manager training reverts within thirty to ninety days. This is not speculation; it is one of the most replicated findings in organizational culture research (Tracey & Hinkin, 2008; Burke & Hutchins, 2007).

**What full management adoption at every level actually changes:**

**Owner behavioral commitment**
When the owner publicly commits to psychological safety as an operational priority — and then demonstrates it behaviorally — the implicit validity test employees have been running changes its baseline. Employees who have learned, through accumulated experience, that this organization is unsafe do not update that belief because a memo is sent. They update it because the authority figure's actual behavior begins to disconfirm what they expected. This is IVT disconfirmation — the slow, behavioral process by which the organization's historical narrative about safety is rewritten in real time. It requires sustained consistency, not declaration.

**Manager regulated presence**
When operations managers develop regulated presence — the trained capacity to enter interactions from a functional arousal state rather than a defensive or collapsed one — the neurobiological transmission process runs in the safe direction. Regulated managers co-regulate their teams. The mechanism is not metaphorical: the nervous system state communicated through tone, posture, pacing, and relational availability is processed by employees before any verbal content is assessed for safety. An OM who has done the internal developmental work changes the emotional climate of every interaction they enter. Their team begins, over weeks and months, to experience something they may not have had words for: that bringing a problem here does not cost them anything except the effort to say it.

**Leader inclusiveness**
When leader inclusiveness behaviors are practiced consistently — genuinely inviting input, tolerating silence after questions, catching the marginal signal in the room before it disappears — the transactional cost of voice drops. Employees who have learned to pre-screen what they say based on what will land safely begin to un-learn that filter. This does not happen quickly. But it happens.

**Inquiry-first error response**
When error is met with inquiry instead of blame — *walk me through what happened* before any evaluative move — the most important safety test in a service environment is passed. Every error event is observed by the entire floor and used to calibrate the team's implicit safety assessment. The audience effect means that one well-handled error in front of five employees does more for psychological safety than a training session on how to give feedback.

## The Structural Layer That Sustains What Behavior Creates

Individual manager development alone does not produce culture. Culture embeds through structural mechanisms that outlast the individual who created them.

- **Daily pre-shift check-in** — Three questions before operational content. Applies regulate-relate-reason at the organizational level; addresses the daily discontinuity that shift-based work creates.
- **Bi-weekly one-on-ones** — Removes the audience threat from voice-testing. The structural commitment to consistent individual contact is the essential element.
- **Supervisory accountability with required discussion** — Not feedback alone. The combination of measurement + required discussion with reports is what the research supports; feedback without discussion produces less improvement (Walker & Smither, 1999).
- **Formal voice channels with functional follow-through** — Non-functional channels are worse than none. They generate expectation and then fail to deliver, confirming the employee's belief that nothing changes when they speak up.
- **Psychological safety measurement loop** — Makes the invisible visible; creates the accountability signal that sustains manager behavior change when coaching ends.

In a high-turnover environment, these structures are not supplemental — they are mandatory. The manager who built safety will leave. The structure must survive them.

---

## The Honest Assessment of What Full Adoption Produces

Given full management engagement at every level — owner behavioral commitment, coachable operations managers, structurally implemented practices — this approach creates the conditions under which psychological safety forms through the mechanisms the research identifies as causal.

- It addresses prerequisites before practices.
- It sequences behavior change against regulatory capacity.
- It installs structural mechanisms that sustain safety after individual development ends.
- It measures what it claims to affect.

**What this approach will not produce is rapid cultural transformation.** The timelines are not pessimistic — they reflect the mechanism:

- **6–12 months:** Meaningful safety levels observable in employee voice behavior, error reporting, and retention trends.
- **18 months – 3 years:** Full cultural embedding — where safety norms self-transmit to new employees through the existing culture, not through deliberate practice.

Implicit validity tests built over years of organizational experience do not update on a quarterly timeline. What they do update on is evidence. Specifically: the evidence that the environment has changed. RPA, fully implemented, produces that evidence daily.

## Verdict

**Solid.** The design logic is causally coherent, the sequence is research-supported, and the mechanism targeted is the right one. The conditional — full adoption at all levels — is doing real work in this analysis. The most common failure mode in safety-building initiatives is not the approach. It is partial implementation: manager training without owner behavior change, or structural installation without the regulatory development that makes structures meaningful. RPA's design accounts for this explicitly, and the prerequisite architecture is its strongest feature.

**What to watch:** The distance between the approach as designed and the approach as delivered depends entirely on whether the owner can be developed. The owner is both the highest-leverage variable and the one least subject to formal coaching authority. If the owner's behavior does not change, the rest of the architecture performs at reduced effectiveness regardless of OM development quality.
