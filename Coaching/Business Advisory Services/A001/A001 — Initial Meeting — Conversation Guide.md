# A001 — Initial Meeting — Conversation Guide

*Pre-meeting internalization. Read the night before or the morning of. This is not the in-room checklist — that is [[A001 — Initial Meeting Field Form]]. This document is for internalizing who Kirt is, what already happened between you, and what to say when you sit down.*

---

## Who Kirt Is — What to Hold Going In

> [!note] Three Things He Told You Before This Meeting
> **1. He's moved past wealth — he wants simplicity and meaning.**
> This is a life-stage reorientation, not a passing comment. He's achieved the external markers and found them hollow. ROI framing, turnover cost math, and payback period language are speaking to a motivation he's explicitly told you is no longer driving him. His primary motivation is significance, not efficiency.
>
> **2. He feels closer to his OMs than his own family.**
> His investment in his OMs is not professional — it is relational and personal. Any finding that reveals something troubling about an OM will be felt the way discovering something troubling about a family member is felt. He disclosed this to a stranger in a first conversation. He is emotionally open, not guarded.
>
> **3. His email reply moved the frame from problem to vision.**
> Your email was about the mechanism — implicit patterns, the loop, why correction doesn't hold. His reply was: *"Teach, educate, nurture, and create culture at the source...within. Not just on the surface. Create a generation that can carry these values forward as future leaders."*
> He took your problem language and translated it into legacy language. He's already past "is the problem real." He's at "what does building the solution actually look like."

> [!warning] The Risk Walking In
> You have prepared thoroughly. The preparation is valuable for later meetings. For this one, attachment to your preparation is a liability.
>
> The most valuable asset you have is the organic quality of what already happened at the coffee shop. He didn't schedule this meeting because he evaluated your credentials. He scheduled it because someone saw something he'd been living with and named it accurately. The moment he senses you've shifted from that person into a consultant executing a process — he'll feel it.
>
> **Walk in as the same person he met at the coffee shop. Nothing more.**

---

## When You Sit Down

> [!note] Before You Say Anything
> Let him settle. Don't fill the first thirty seconds. If there's a moment of comfortable silence while you're getting coffee or getting seated, let it be. Don't open with "so great to see you" or "thanks for making time." Just be present. He will feel the difference between someone who is comfortable in silence and someone who is managing the air.

When the moment opens:

---

**"Something you wrote back stayed with me — 'create a generation that can carry these values forward as future leaders.' That's not the language of someone trying to fix a turnover problem. I want to understand what that actually means to you."**

---

Then stop. Fully stop.

> [!tip] What You're Listening For
> Whether the generational vision is deeply held or was an inspired moment in the email. Whether he goes to his OMs specifically. Whether he goes personal. Wherever he goes — follow it. Don't redirect, don't structure it, don't move toward the framework yet.
>
> He's telling you what this is actually about for him.

---

## During His Response

Reflect his specific words back — not paraphrases. If he says *"I want these people to actually become something"* — don't say *"so growth matters to you."* Say: **"Become something — say more about that."**

One follow-up at a time. Not a sequence. When he names something specific, get curious about that specific thing before moving on.

> [!tip] If He References His OMs
> Let him describe them. Don't analyze what he says. Just follow:
>
> **"What do you see in them right now?"**
>
> His answer will tell you more about the operation — and about his own relational investment in these people — than any structured diagnostic question.

---

## Connecting Vision to Mechanism

After he's expressed the vision — when there's a natural breath — this is the move. Not a pitch. A grounding.

---

**"What you're describing isn't what most organizations build. Most build training. Training reaches what people can consciously process and decide to change. What you're describing has to reach the level where these patterns actually live — below that. That's a different design. And it's buildable. But it requires starting in a specific place."**

*Pause. Let that land.*

**"In your operation, it starts with your OMs. What you build into them is what reaches the floor. They're the transmission point for everything you're describing."**

---

> [!tip] After This — Watch and Follow
> Don't elaborate yet. He may immediately go to his OMs — who they are, what he sees, what he wishes were different. Let him. You're not presenting the engagement design. You're letting him arrive at the shape of it through his own thinking.

---

## The OM Moment

When he's talking about his OMs, or when the moment opens naturally:

---

**"When you picture your OMs right now — what do you see? What are they doing well, and where's the gap between who they are and who you'd want them to be as the leaders of what you're describing?"**

---

> [!note] What This Question Does
> Three things simultaneously: surfaces his picture of his OMs, opens the developmental frame, and — if the meeting goes deep enough — eventually leads to the question of what he has built into them and what he models for them.
>
> Don't rush to that last layer. It surfaces on its own if you hold the space. His OMs' behavior toward their teams is often a mirror of what the OMs experience from him. File that observation internally. Do not name it in this meeting.

---

## If He Goes Personal

He may. His email has the energy of someone who found language for something he's been carrying. If he discloses something about the weight of it — the exhaustion, what it costs him, what he wants his legacy to be — don't pivot back to the operation. Stay there.

**"What does it look like for you when this is at its worst?"**

Or, given his meaning-seeking frame:

**"When you picture having actually built what you're describing — what changes for you personally?"**

> [!warning] Gating Condition
> Either question only if the meeting earns it. If he's stayed operational throughout, skip it. If he's gone personal, follow him. Asked prematurely, this lands as intrusive. Asked at the right moment, it builds more trust faster than any credential.

---

## The Close

Don't propose a full engagement. Don't pull out numbers. When the conversation has run its natural arc and there's a sense of arrival:

---

**"Here's what I'd recommend. Before either of us commits to anything, let me give you a real picture of where your operation actually is — not a guess, a genuine diagnostic. From there, you decide whether you want to move into building what you're describing or take it another direction."**

Then specifically:

**"That starts with a structured conversation — with you, and then with your OMs. I'll tell you what I'm seeing as we go. Within 13 weeks you have a clear picture of exactly what's running beneath the surface and what would need to shift to build what you're describing."**

Then:

**"Before we wrap — how are you thinking about bringing your OMs into this conversation? I want to make sure they hear about it in a way that sets things up well."**

---

> [!tip] If He Asks About Price
> **"That phase is $28,000. And based on what you've described — even at conservative turnover numbers — it pays for itself if it moves the needle by a fraction of a percent. I'll put that math in what I send you."**
>
> Then move directly back to the OM framing question. Don't linger on the number.

---

## The One Thing to Hold Throughout

> [!note] The Through-Line
> You're not presenting. You're not running a discovery protocol. You're continuing a conversation that already opened something real.
>
> The through-line from the coffee shop to the email to this meeting is a single thread — a man who named something true, a man who recognized it, and now a man who wants to know what building something true actually requires.
>
> Stay on that thread. Everything else follows.
