# A001 — Initial Meeting — Owner Questions & Credibility

*Pre-meeting internalization. Read the night before or the morning of — alongside [[A001 — Initial Meeting — Conversation Guide]]. This document prepares you for what he asks. The Conversation Guide prepares you for what you ask. Both need to be in your body before you sit down, not in your hands during the meeting.*

---

> [!note] The Organizing Frame — Maister's Trust Equation
> **Trust = (Credibility + Reliability + Intimacy) / Self-Orientation**
>
> The denominator is what kills most consultant first meetings. Every credential you mention, every time you move toward your framework rather than his situation, every time you extend your answer beyond what was asked — you are increasing self-orientation and decreasing trust.
>
> His email reply was "YES!" He is not coming in skeptical. He is coming in largely convinced and curious. That means the credential-defense posture — the one that answers every question by trying to prove competence — is the wrong register. He doesn't need to be persuaded. He needs to be grounded in what building this actually requires, and he needs to trust that the person across from him is honest enough to tell him the truth about it.
>
> **Sources:** Maister, Green & Galford — *The Trusted Advisor*; Aronson — Pratfall Effect; Schein — *Humble Inquiry*; McAdams — Narrative Identity; Pink — *To Sell Is Human*

> [!warning] The Single Most Dangerous Pattern
> **Self-orientation.** Every credential you mention, every time you lead with your framework rather than his situation, every time you extend your answer beyond what was asked — you signal that you are more interested in being impressive than in being useful.
>
> The counter-move, consistently: **answer the question, then ask one.** Keep returning attention to him. The more curious you are about his situation and the less attached you appear to demonstrating your expertise, the more expert you seem.
>
> Schein called it humble inquiry. Maister called it low self-orientation. The practical version: every time you feel the urge to add one more sentence proving something — don't.

## Q1 — "Tell me about yourself" / "What exactly do you do?"

> [!note] What He's Actually Asking
> *Is this person real? Is what happened at the coffee shop a fluke, or does it reflect how they actually think and work?*
>
> This is the question most people handle worst. The instinct is to list credentials. The credential list increases self-orientation, decreases intimacy, and leaves the listener doing math about whether any of it applies to them. It answers the wrong question.
>
> **Framework:** Narrative Identity (McAdams) — people connect to stories, not CVs. The most trust-building answer has: a turning point, a reason this work matters to you, and a fast pivot back to them.

**90 seconds. Three moves. Then ask a question.**

*Move 1 — The nature of the work (20 seconds):*

> "I work with business owners who are watching something they built start to degrade — and can't figure out why the standard fixes aren't holding. The reason they're not holding is almost always the same: the intervention is operating at the explicit level, and the problem is living one level below that."

*Move 2 — The human element (20 seconds):*

> "I came to this through my own experience of that gap — watching people try to change and not being able to, including myself. That's what pointed me toward the research underneath it and eventually toward building something that could actually reach it."

*Move 3 — The pivot back to him (10 seconds):*

> "But what matters is whether any of it is useful for your specific situation — which is what I want to understand today."

**Then ask a question. Do not continue talking.**

> [!warning] Hard Stop
> Do not list credentials here. Do not mention Schwab, FOX 42, CPCC, or any specific credential unprompted. Those come later, and only if he asks or the conversation requires them. Leading with a credential list is a self-orientation move. It answers the wrong question and signals the wrong posture.

## Q2 — "What would this actually look like in my operation?"

> [!note] What He's Actually Asking
> *I said yes to a concept. Now I need to see the concrete picture. Is this real or theoretical?*
>
> His "YES!" was to the vision. Now he's a business owner who needs to understand what it looks like in practice. This question will come — probably early.
>
> **The discipline:** Don't present the full methodology. That's a lecture, and lectures increase self-orientation. Describe what the experience is from *his* position and from his OMs' position — not from yours.

**Answer from his position and his OMs' position — not yours.**

> "Here's what it looks like from your end: I come in before anything starts and spend real time understanding what's actually happening — not what you think is happening, what the data shows is happening. That means structured conversations with your key leaders, a 360 from their teams, and a direct observation session where I watch them function in real situations.
>
> At the end of that, you get a specific picture — not a general report, a picture of the patterns actually running in your operation, what's producing them, and what would need to shift. You then decide whether you want to move into building what you described, or handle it another way.
>
> From your managers' position: they're having a structured conversation with someone who's not going to report what they say to you. What they tell me stays with me. What you get is a picture of the team as a system — not a scorecard on individuals."

Then:

> "Does that match what you were imagining, or is it different than you pictured?"

## Q3 — "Have you done this in hospitality before?"

> [!note] What He's Actually Asking
> *Do you understand my world, or are you going to learn on my dime?*
>
> **The Pratfall Effect (Aronson):** People trust competent individuals *more* after they admit a limitation than if they presented as flawless. The key sequence: establish competence first, then disclose the limitation. Lead with the limitation and it reads as lack of confidence. Follow it with overcompensation and it reads as defensiveness.
>
> This is the question the Credibility Talking Points document flags as the pratfall moment. Handle it wrong and you lose credibility. Handle it right and you build more trust than any credential could.

**Sequence: establish competence → disclose the limitation → explain why it matters less than expected → hold.**

> "I haven't run this specific engagement in a hotel before. I'll tell you why I'm telling you rather than hoping you don't ask — and also why I think it matters less than you might expect.
>
> The mechanism I'm working with — how implicit patterns form, how they get activated in a management chain, what it takes to actually reach them — that's not industry-specific. I've worked with it across enough different environments to know it's consistent. What's industry-specific is the context: the guest relationship, the dispersed property structure, the labor profile. I've done my homework on that. But I won't pretend I've run this in a hotel, because I haven't.
>
> What I can tell you is what I've built it on — [two or three specific anchors from your actual experience]. And I can tell you that in every context I've worked in, the pattern underneath the surface behavior is more consistent than the industry is different."

**Then hold. Do not add to it. Let it land.**

> [!tip] The Anchors to Have Ready
> When you say "what I've built it on" — know which two or three you're reaching for before you sit down. From the Credibility Talking Points: the training outcome (75 new hires, 30% performance gain), the business results track (Schwab #13 nationally; Houser Consulting avg 30% revenue gain), and the personal transformation piece (CPCC, personal attachment reorganization). Choose based on which feels most relevant to what he's expressed. Do not list all of them.

## Q4 — "What do my managers experience in this?"

> [!note] What He's Actually Asking
> He is closer to his OMs than his own family. This question is **protective**, not evaluative. He wants to know they are safe. Treat it as a logistics question and you miss what's underneath it. Treat it as the relational question it actually is and you deepen trust significantly.

> "Your managers have a structured conversation — private, about two hours — where they're asked about real situations they've navigated: accountability conversations, difficult moments, relationships that didn't go the way they wanted. It's not an interrogation. It's closer to an interview with someone who's genuinely curious about what they're dealing with.
>
> They also receive a 360 from their own teams and participate in a direct observation session together.
>
> At the end, each of them gets their own profile — specific to their pattern, not a generic report. That's theirs. What you receive is a picture of the team as a system.
>
> The reason the confidentiality structure is designed the way it is — where I don't report back individually on any of them — is that it protects them. It also means what they tell me is actually true, rather than managed. That's what makes the assessment worth anything."

Then:

> "I want to make sure they hear about this in a way that doesn't threaten what you've already built with them. How are you thinking about framing this when you bring it to them?"

> [!tip] Why That Last Question Matters
> It hands him agency over something he cares about deeply. It also surfaces the D3 dynamic naturally — how he plans to introduce this to his OMs tells you a great deal about how he relates to them and what he expects them to do with his direction.

## Q5 — "How long does this take? What does it cost?"

> [!note] What He's Actually Asking
> *Let me calibrate the scope of commitment before I go further.*
>
> These often come together. Answer them cleanly and directly — don't soften or qualify. He's a business owner. He can handle a number. What he can't handle is a consultant who hedges on price as if it were a negotiating position.

> "The assessment phase — what I'd recommend starting with — runs 13 weeks. That's the full picture: narrative interviews, 360 instrumentation, facilitated observation, individual profiles for your leaders, the team aggregate, and structural recommendations. That phase is $28,000.
>
> From there, you decide whether you want to move into the development work — coaching with individual leaders, team facilitation, and implementation support. That's a longer arc and a different investment. But we don't go there until you've seen what the assessment produces and decided it's worth building on."

> [!warning] Hold on the Full Option Architecture
> Do not offer Options B or A unless he asks directly. He is not comparing packages — he is deciding whether to trust you. Presenting a menu of options at this stage signals that you're selling, not advising. If he asks about a longer engagement, answer it directly and then return to Option C as the right starting point.

## Q6 — "What got you into this?" / "Why do you care about this?"

> [!note] What He's Actually Asking
> This is a meaning-seeking question from a meaning-seeking person. He said he no longer cares about wealth. He wants simplicity. He is curious whether the person across from him is operating from the same register. This is the intimacy variable in the Trust Equation. Answer it from that register — not the professional one.
>
> **Brief. Real. Not rehearsed.**

> "I lived on the other side of this. I spent years watching my own patterns run in my relationships and my work — and trying the explicit-level fixes that didn't hold. When I finally understood what was actually happening below that level, it changed things in a way that correction never had. That's what pointed me toward the research, and eventually toward building something that could go there for other people."

**Then stop. Do not add to it.**

## The Question He Won't Ask — Surface It Anyway

> [!note] Why This Move Matters
> Near the end of the meeting, before you close. He won't ask you to say this. Say it anyway. It is the move that separates advisors from salespeople — and he is perceptive enough to know the difference. Done right, it builds more trust than anything else in the conversation.

> "I want to be honest with you about something. What you're describing — building something generational in your operation — that's a real ambition and it's achievable. It also requires a kind of honesty about the people closest to you in this operation. When the assessment produces findings about your OMs, some of those findings may be uncomfortable. I want to make sure you're going into this willing to hear what's actually true, not just what confirms what you already think."

> [!tip] What This Signals
> - You are not selling him a good outcome. You are telling him the truth about what the process requires.
> - You are demonstrating that you've thought about what's hard about this — not just what's possible.
> - You are treating him as someone who can handle honesty — which is the deepest form of respect in an advisory relationship.
> - You are differentiating yourself from every consultant who has ever made a comfortable promise in a first meeting.

## Credential Architecture — When and How to Use It

> [!note] The Rule
> Credentials come in response to questions, not as part of your opening. They are anchors, not advertisements. Two or three at most in any response. Always followed by a question or a return to his situation.

| Credential | When It's Relevant | How to Frame It |
|---|---|---|
| CPCC (CTI) | If he asks about formal training or coaching background | "Certified at the advanced level through CTI — that's the relational-skills track, not the goal-setting track." |
| Personal transformation | If he asks why you care, or if the intimacy question opens | Brief and real. See Q6 above. |
| 75 new hires / 30% performance gain | If he questions whether you can actually move people | "I've trained groups of 75 new hires and documented 30% performance gains. The mechanism is the same — environment and relationship produce behavior." |
| 40+ businesses / avg 30% revenue gain | If he questions business results | State it once. Don't elaborate. Let the number carry it. |
| Schwab #13 nationally | If he questions commercial track record | One sentence. Move on. |
| Hasn't run this in hospitality | When he asks — use the pratfall sequence above | Never volunteer it preemptively. Answer it when asked. |
