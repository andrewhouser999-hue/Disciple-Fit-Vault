# A001 — First Meeting Question Guide

*Pre-meeting study reference. Read this before the meeting; use the [[A001 — Initial Meeting Field Form]] in the meeting. The guide explains the diagnostic logic behind each question — what it actually surfaces, what the question communicates about you, and what to listen for in the response.*

*11 questions across 4 layers + 1 meta-question. Designed for a 60-minute meeting with warm entry. You will not use all of them — read the room and follow what opens. The layers give you a diagnostic arc; the question count gives you options.*

*Pairs with: [[A001 — Conversation Readiness Guide]] (what to do when he pivots) · [[A001 — Initial Meeting Field Form]] (in-meeting checklist)*

---

## 1-Minute Pitch

*For rehearsal or transition into the role/scope phase. ~150 words at a conversational pace — approximately 60 seconds.*

When we talked at the coffee shop, I helped you see what your operations team had been trying to solve for hours — by looking at root cause instead of behavior. That's what I do at scale.

I come in and assess your management team: structured interviews, behavioral observation, a multi-rater 360. Each leader gets an individual profile — specific to their pattern, not a generic report. What you get is a clear picture of *why* accountability breaks down, why turnover runs at the rate it does, and what your managers actually need to shift — not just perform differently in a training room.

From there, I run a coaching arc with each manager and a team facilitation process that moves the whole group. You get structural recommendations at the close — the systems adjustments that make the behavior changes hold.

Starting point is $28K for the full assessment phase. Most operations recover that in the first quarter through retention alone.

## Layer 1 — Pain Picture

*Goal: Help the owner quantify and scope what he's already seeing. Not introducing the framework yet — helping him see his own situation more clearly. Two questions. Both operational, both specific to his multi-property context.*

---

### Q1 — Turnover Cost

**"Do you know what turnover is actually costing you — across all your properties, annually?"**

**What it surfaces:**
Whether he has quantified the problem or normalized it. Owners who can answer are already in the diagnostic frame. Owners who can't — "that's just hospitality" or "I don't track that" — have the biggest opening in front of them. Either answer gives you the next move.

**Credibility signal:**
You think in costs, not concepts. This is not a consultant's question — it's an owner's question. It signals that you understand the business problem before you understand the relational problem.

**What to listen for:**
- "I don't track that" or "I don't have exact numbers" → he has normalized turnover as a cost of doing business; the ROI math in your Conversation Readiness Guide is the immediate follow-up
- A turnover percentage without a dollar figure → he tracks the rate but hasn't translated it to cost; help him do it: "At your scale, roughly how many people is that per year?"
- A specific dollar figure → he's already calculated it and it's painful; acknowledge the weight of it before moving on
- "That's just how this industry works" → this is the highest-leverage opening in the conversation; the reframe from normalized assumption to diagnosable problem is where engagement energy lives

**Follow-up probe if surface:**
"What does that translate to in number of people replaced per year?"

---

### Q2 — Culture Consistency Across Properties

**"If I walked into three of your properties unannounced and asked employees what it's like to work here — would I hear the same answer at all three?"**

**What it surfaces:**
Culture consistency vs. fragmentation. In an 11-property operation, culture is almost always property-specific — it tracks with GM personality and management style, not brand standards. This question names that dynamic without diagnosing it.

**Credibility signal:**
Multi-property thinking. You understand that dispersed operations produce uneven cultures by default. Most consultants don't ask this — they ask about "the company culture" as if it were monolithic. This question communicates that you know how these operations actually work.

**What to listen for:**
- Hesitation before answering → culture is inconsistent and he knows it; he may be about to name his best and worst properties
- Confident "yes" → probe it: "What would they say?" — if the answer is vague, the consistency is assumed, not verified
- "It depends on the GM" → this is exactly right and exactly the problem; he's named the transmission mechanism; acknowledge it: "That's actually the most important thing you could say — the GM is the proximate culture carrier. Tell me more about what that looks like across yours."
- Names a specific property as different → that property is the diagnostic reference; use it as a concrete example for the rest of the conversation

**Follow-up probe if surface:**
"What's the most different from what you'd want? What does it look like there?"

## Layer 2 — Management Behavior Gap

*Goal: Surface the gap between what managers are supposed to do and what they actually do — in specific incidents, not general patterns. Three questions. Each targets a specific mechanism: accountability delivery, developmental readiness of new supervisors, and regulated response under pressure.*

---

### Q3 — Accountability Delivery

**"Walk me through the last time a supervisor had to address a recurring performance problem. How did that play out?"**

**What it surfaces:**
The actual accountability delivery sequence — or the absence of one. This is the most diagnostic question in Layer 2. It reveals withdrawal patterns (supervisor avoided, escalated to the owner, or let it go), escalation patterns (the conversation became a confrontation), disorganization (inconsistent, unclear, or reversed course), or functional accountability delivery (rare, worth noting).

**Credibility signal:**
You work from incidents, not abstractions. Asking about "the last time" rather than "how do supervisors generally handle" signals that you diagnose from specific events — which is how the assessment work will operate.

**What to listen for:**
- "They came to me" → supervisor escalated upward; classic withdrawal from accountability
- "We let it go for a while and then had to let them go" → delayed action with no corrective feedback loop; the termination was a surprise to the employee
- "They handled it" → probe how: "What did that conversation look like?" — most "they handled it" answers reveal either avoidance or a single blunt confrontation
- "I usually have to get involved" → owner is carrying accountability delivery that belongs with the supervisor; this is operational cost that can be quantified
- Vague or general answer → the owner doesn't have visibility into how supervisors handle this; there is no feedback mechanism at the supervisor level

**Follow-up probe if surface:**
"What did the supervisor actually say to the employee — do you know?"

---

### Q4 — Supervisory Development Pipeline

**"When someone gets promoted into a supervisory role — what does their first 90 days look like?"**

**What it surfaces:**
Whether management development is deliberate or absent. In most hospitality operations, high-performing hourly employees are promoted because they're good at the job — not because they're ready to manage people. The first 90 days reveal whether the operation has a development architecture or whether new supervisors inherit the patterns of whoever supervised them.

**Credibility signal:**
You know where the transmission break happens. The promotion-to-supervisor gap is the primary point at which management culture replicates itself — poorly or well. Asking about it specifically signals that you understand how patterns perpetuate across generations of staff.

**What to listen for:**
- "They figure it out" → no development; cultural replication by osmosis
- "We train them on systems" → task competence addressed; relational readiness not addressed
- "They shadow me" or "they shadow the GM" → development exists but is informal; whatever patterns the GM carries are what the supervisor inherits
- Any reference to structured relational or behavioral development → this is unusual and worth probing; it's either working or it's more theoretical than real
- "The ones who are good just get it" → character attribution applied to leadership readiness; the owner believes some people have it and some don't — the environment-drives-behavior frame has not yet extended to supervisors

**Follow-up probe if surface:**
"What's the hardest thing for a new supervisor to figure out on their own?"

---

### Q5 — Regulated Response Under Pressure

**"What happens when a guest complaint hits — walk me through the management chain."**

**What it surfaces:**
SCARF activation and regulated response under the highest-visibility pressure in hospitality. A guest complaint is the most emotionally loaded operational event in a hotel — it happens in front of witnesses, it involves a real threat to the property's reputation, and it tests whether the manager can regulate while under scrutiny. The response sequence reveals more about the management culture than any other single event type.

**Credibility signal:**
Hospitality-specific. A consultant who understands that guest complaint response is a relational pressure point — not just a service recovery process — signals industry fluency without stating it. Most management consultants ask about "difficult conversations." You're asking about the specific high-stakes moment that defines how this industry's culture gets expressed.

**What to listen for:**
- Who handles the complaint: manager or supervisor → who carries the relational weight under pressure
- What happens to the employee involved → does the employee feel supported or exposed after the complaint? This is a direct psychological safety signal
- Whether the manager stays regulated or shifts register → escalation, blame attribution, defensiveness
- "We follow brand standards" → compliance frame; the human relational sequence may or may not be happening under the script
- "The manager takes the hit" → owner-level pattern of absorbing accountability that belongs at the property level; exhausting and culturally disempowering

**Follow-up probe if surface:**
"What does the employee experience in that sequence — from the moment the complaint happens?"

## Layer 3 — Structural Contributors

*Goal: Move from management behavior to the structural conditions that produce it. By Layer 3, the owner should begin to see the system — not just the people. Four questions. Each targets a specific structural gap: onboarding, early warning, culture accountability, and the departure signal.*

---

### Q6 — Onboarding Depth

**"What does a new employee experience in their first two weeks — beyond the job training itself?"**

**What it surfaces:**
Whether onboarding is relational or purely procedural. The highest turnover concentration in hospitality is the first 30-90 days. New employees who learn the PMS and the service script but don't form a relational connection to the team, the manager, or the culture leave before they become loyal. This question separates task onboarding from belonging infrastructure.

**Credibility signal:**
You know where the dropout happens and why. The framing "beyond the job training itself" signals that you understand the distinction — it communicates that you've diagnosed this before.

**What to listen for:**
- Answer stays in the job training register → onboarding is purely procedural; belonging is not designed
- "We pair them with a buddy" → social connection exists but is informal; whether the buddy is a culture carrier or a culture liability is unknown
- No mention of the GM or owner meeting the new hire → leadership is not visible in onboarding; new employees don't know who they're working for
- "We do 30/60/90 check-ins" → development architecture exists; rare; probe what's actually asked in those check-ins
- "They either stick or they don't by week three" → selection-by-attrition model; the operation has accepted early dropout as a filter rather than a failure

**Follow-up probe if surface:**
"Does the new employee meet you — or the GM — in the first two weeks?"

---

### Q7 — Early Warning System

**"How do you know when a new hire is struggling — before they decide to leave?"**

**What it surfaces:**
Whether the operation has any early detection mechanism for disengagement, or whether everything comes as a surprise. Turnover is always a lagging indicator — the decision to leave happens weeks or months before the departure. An owner who finds out when someone doesn't show up has no early warning system. An owner who hears from managers has a warning system that's only as functional as those managers' own willingness to surface problems.

**Credibility signal:**
You know that the departure is not the event — it's the conclusion of a process that had earlier signals. This framing shifts the diagnostic frame from reactive (why did they leave?) to prospective (where are the early indicators?).

**What to listen for:**
- "We find out when they don't show up" → no early warning; maximum surprise, minimum intervention window
- "The supervisor tells me" → signal depends on the supervisor's own disclosure pattern; supervisors with withdrawal patterns will not surface problems they haven't addressed
- "We do check-ins" → probe: "Who does them and what do they actually ask?" — most check-ins are operational, not relational
- Genuine uncertainty → the owner doesn't know; this is honest and opens the conversation about what a detection system could look like
- "I can usually tell" → owner-level intuition filling a structural gap; doesn't scale across 11 properties

**Follow-up probe if surface:**
"What would it look like if a manager were seeing the early signs but not surfacing them?"

---

### Q8 — Culture Accountability

**"How do you hold your GMs accountable to a culture standard — not a performance standard, but how the place actually feels to work in?"**

**What it surfaces:**
Whether culture is designed and measured or assumed and informal. In most multi-property operations, GM accountability is structured around financial performance and brand compliance. Culture is treated as an informal consequence of the GM's personality — not as a designed and measurable output. This question names the distinction explicitly.

**Credibility signal:**
The distinction between performance and culture standards signals that you understand culture as a designed output, not a background condition. Most owners have never separated these. The question itself is instructive — it communicates the frame without explaining it.

**What to listen for:**
- Confusion at the distinction → culture has never been separated from performance; this is one of the most common gaps
- "They know what I expect" → informal, subjective, non-accountable; this means culture is only as consistent as the owner's visibility and the GM's interpretive accuracy
- Answer drifts to performance metrics → turnover rate, guest scores, RevPAR → culture is being measured through lagging operational indicators, not leading behavioral ones
- "I walk the properties" → owner-level visibility substituting for structural accountability; doesn't scale; creates dependence on the owner being present
- Any mention of employee surveys or feedback mechanisms → rare; probe whether the data actually reaches the GM with expectations attached

**Follow-up probe if surface:**
"When a property's culture is off — how do you know, and what's your move?"

---

### Q9 — Departure Signal

**"When your best people leave — what do you typically see as the trigger?"**

**What it surfaces:**
His current theory of departure. This is a diagnostic read on where he is in the causal frame. Character attribution ("they get better offers," "younger workers aren't loyal") tells you his theory is still at the surface level. Relational attribution ("they lose confidence in management," "they feel overlooked") tells you the coffee-shop reframe has extended further than he may have realized. This question also surfaces whether exit interview data exists and what it actually shows.

**Credibility signal:**
You care about the people who leave — specifically the best ones. Most operators focus on turnover volume. Asking about the best people signals that you understand retention quality, not just retention rate.

**What to listen for:**
- "Better offers, better pay" → compensation attribution; probe: "Is it always pay, or is pay the stated reason?" Most honest owners know the stated reason is not always the actual reason
- "They just weren't committed" → character attribution; the coffee-shop frame has not yet extended to departures
- "The ones who leave are usually the ones I'd want to keep" → he's noticing selectivity in departure; this is a significant diagnostic opening
- "They get frustrated with management" → relational attribution already present; he's close to the root-cause frame
- No clear pattern → exit interview data either doesn't exist or doesn't surface honest answers (which is itself a psychological safety indicator)

**Follow-up probe if surface:**
"Do you notice any pattern in which properties they leave from — or which supervisors they leave under?"

## Layer 4 — Pressure Test

*Goal: Test whether the owner's insight holds under operational specificity, and surface what he's most personally invested in changing. Two questions. The first is behavioral and operational. The second goes to the personal cost — the intimacy move that most consultants never make.*

---

### Q10 — Behavioral Target

**"If you could change one thing about how your management team functions — specifically what they do in the room when something goes wrong — what would it be?"**

**What it surfaces:**
His actual behavioral target. The phrase "specifically what they do in the room" is load-bearing — it forces behavioral precision rather than dispositional aspiration. "I want them to care more" tells you he's still in the character frame. "I want them to stay in the conversation instead of shutting down" tells you he can see the behavioral pattern and has language for it.

**Credibility signal:**
You work at the behavioral level, not the character level. "What they do in the room" is not a soft question — it's precise. It signals that your work is observable, measurable, and specific.

**What to listen for:**
- Character language: "I want them to care," "I want them to be more committed" → still in dispositional frame; the root-cause reframe hasn't fully extended to management behavior
- Behavioral language: "I want them to stay calm," "I want them to address problems instead of coming to me" → he can see the behavior; he's a natural fit for the pattern-specific coaching approach
- Specific incident reference → even better; if he anchors the answer in a specific event, you have the diagnostic starting point for the entire engagement
- "I want them to be more like [specific OM or staff member]" → he has a behavioral benchmark in the operation; name what that person does differently

**Follow-up probe if surface:**
"What does it look like when one of them does handle it well — what specifically do you see them do?"

---

### Q11 — Personal Cost

**"What does it cost you personally when this is at its worst?"**

**What it surfaces:**
The emotional driver of the engagement and the depth of the owner's personal investment. Most owners carry organizational dysfunction at a personal level — they're absorbing accountability that should sit at the property level, they're in relationships with OMs that are draining rather than generative, or they're watching something they built degrade. This question opens the relational register in a way no operational question can.

**Credibility signal:**
You're interested in him as a person, not just as a client. This is the intimacy move in Maister's Trust Equation — and it's the move most consultants never make because it requires genuine curiosity. If it's performed, it lands as manipulation. If it's genuine, it builds trust faster than any credential.

**What to listen for:**
- Purely operational answer: "I spend all my time putting out fires," "I can't focus on growth" → stay at the operational register; some owners are not ready to go personal in the first meeting
- Personal disclosure: named frustration, exhaustion, feeling like he's carrying this alone → this is a deep engagement signal; the problem has a human cost to him; his motivation through difficulty is real
- Deflection with humor → note it; he registered the question's depth and redirected; this is worth returning to at a different moment
- Brief pause before answering → he's going somewhere real; give him the space

**Important:** Only ask this if the meeting is in a genuine relational register by this point. If it's still transactional or evaluative, this question lands as intrusive rather than connecting. Read the room before using it.

## Meta-Question

### Q-Meta — The Unasked Question

**"Is there something about what's happening in your operation that you haven't been asked about yet — something that feels significant but hasn't come up?"**

**What it surfaces:**
What the owner has been holding back — the real presenting problem beneath the stated one, the thing he doesn't think a consultant would understand, or the situation he's been managing privately that no one else knows. This question produces more diagnostic information per minute than any structured question, because it hands him complete control over what comes next.

**Credibility signal:**
Genuine inquiry. You're not running a diagnostic track — you're following his situation wherever it actually is. This is the behavioral demonstration of Schein's Humble Inquiry: you don't know what you haven't heard yet, and you're asking.

**When to use it:**
Near the end of the diagnostic deepening phase, before transitioning to role/scope. Not as a throwaway close — as a deliberate invitation. Leave 3-5 seconds of silence after asking it.

**What to listen for:**
- Silence before answering → he's considering something real; wait
- "No, I think you've covered it" → he may be satisfied, or he may be managing what he discloses; either way, move to role/scope
- A new disclosure that reframes everything that preceded it → this is the highest-value outcome; the real problem surfaces; treat it as the primary diagnostic material
- A question he asks you → he's testing whether you can handle what he's about to say; answer it directly and return the question to him

## Selection Rationale

These 11 questions were selected from a larger candidate set using three filters:

**1. Diagnostic yield.** Each question surfaces information about the mechanism — not just the symptoms. Questions that produce only operational data (turnover rate, property count) were cut in favor of questions that surface how the environment is producing behavioral outcomes.

**2. Credibility signal.** Each question communicates something about how Andrew works — specifically, that he works from incidents (not patterns), from the owner's language (not the framework), and from multi-level operational knowledge (not generic HR framing). Questions that sounded generic or over-consultative were cut.

**3. Warm-entry fit.** Because the owner already shares the causal frame, questions that are designed to install the root-cause reframe were cut. The reframe is already present. These questions deepen and specify it.

## Cut List

*Questions considered and cut — with rationale. Preserved here for future use in different engagement contexts or as second-meeting questions.*

| Question | Why Cut |
|---|---|
| "What does a manager do when an employee is struggling — not in violation, but not performing?" | Superseded by Q3 (accountability delivery); Q3 produces richer data from a specific incident rather than a general pattern |
| "When a manager assigns a task, how often do they explain why it matters to the employee?" | Too narrow for a first meeting; belongs in the needs-supportive training phase, not diagnostic entry |
| "What's your current process for documenting verbal warnings?" | Operational/legal register; useful for due diligence but creates an HR frame in a conversation that should stay in a relational register |
| "Since that conversation, has anything happened that confirmed what you were seeing?" | Useful warm-entry bridge, but better as a natural follow-up to Q1 or Q2 than as a standalone structured question |
| "Who else in your organization sees this the way you do?" | Risks sounding like Andrew is checking for political allies before committing; creates evaluative register; belongs in Phase 1 scoping, not first meeting |
| "What would you want to be different in 12 months?" | Desired-state question; useful in a second meeting when scope is being defined; premature here — he hasn't yet seen the full diagnostic picture |
| "How do your OMs relate to each other?" | Interpersonal dynamics between OMs are important but too specific for first-meeting depth; surfaces naturally in the assessment phase |

## Staffing & Manager Development Question Set

*Supplementary diagnostic set. Organized as pre-meeting prep + in-meeting deployment. Use after the core 11-question arc has established the diagnostic frame — this set goes deeper on employee behavior patterns, structural contributors, and what the owner has invested in his management team.*

---

### Pre-Meeting

**Know cold before deploying this set:**

- **The behavioral/structural distinction.** Behavioral problems = what employees do. Structural problems = what the operation does that produces or sustains those behaviors. Know which layer you're in at any given moment — he will move between them freely.
- **Most owners conflate the two.** He may answer a structural question with a behavioral frame or vice versa. Don't correct him — follow the answer and re-route from there.
- **Development investment is almost always thinner than the owner believes.** He may cite a training from two years ago. Hold space for the gap between what was attempted and what actually changed behavior.
- **Section C lands after Sections A and B.** The manager development questions earn their credibility only after he's named the problem himself. Don't use Section C cold.

**What you're trying to learn:**
- Whether behavior problems concentrate somewhere — role, property, shift, tenure — or are system-wide
- Whether his structural practices are generating the behaviors he's complaining about
- How much real development investment his managers have received and whether it changed anything
- Whether he can distinguish between a people problem and a system problem

---

### In-Meeting

*Navigate by what opens. A → B → C is the natural arc, but follow his language. Section C is the most credibility-generating set — use it if you get there.*

---

#### Section A — Behavioral Problems

*Goal: Identify what the behaviors actually are, where they concentrate, and what patterns exist.*

**A1.** Walk me through a bad week. What behaviors are you seeing that you shouldn't have to deal with?

**A2.** Which ones cost you the most — financially, operationally, or just managerially?

**A3.** Are these spread evenly, or do they concentrate somewhere — specific properties, departments, shifts, tenure brackets?

**A4.** Do problem employees show up that way from day one, or does something change? When does it usually start?

**A5.** When a behavior blows up, is there usually a story behind it — or does it come out of nowhere?

**A6.** Are your best employees and your worst behaving differently from each other, or are you seeing the same patterns across the board?

**A7.** Are guests seeing it? What's showing up in reviews or complaints that traces back to staff behavior?

---

#### Section B — Structural Problems

*Goal: Identify the systems, practices, or gaps that are generating or sustaining the behaviors.*

**B1.** What does someone experience in their first 30 days? Walk me through what actually happens — not the policy, what actually happens.

**B2.** When a supervisor handles a behavior problem, what does that look like? Is there a consistent approach, or does it depend on who's managing that day?

**B3.** How consistent are your managers across properties? Same problem, same response — or is it all over the map?

**B4.** Do your employees know what's expected of them beyond the job tasks? Not the handbook — do they actually know?

**B5.** When someone crosses a line, what's the real response? Walk me through what happens from the moment you find out.

**B6.** Is there a visible path forward for someone who wants to grow with you — or does advancement feel random to them?

**B7.** How do you find out when something's going wrong — does it come to you, or are you hunting for it?

**Bridge (A → B or B → C if needed):** *"When you see the same behavior repeated across multiple people in the same role — do you read that as a people problem, a system problem, or something else?"* This tells you how he's currently framing causation and sets up Section C.

---

#### Section C — Manager Development & Investment

*Goal: Identify what he's actually built into his managers — formal and informal — and whether it landed.*

**C1.** When you promote someone to a management role, what prepares them for it? Is there a defined process, or do they mostly learn on the job?

**C2.** Have you ever brought in outside training for your managers — leadership, communication, conflict, anything? What have you tried?

**C3.** Is there a management style or philosophy you've tried to instill — something you'd say: *"This is how we lead here?"*

**C4.** What does a good manager look like in your operation? If you picture your best one — what are they doing that the others aren't?

**C5.** When a manager struggles, what do you do with that? Is there a development path, or does it usually end in a personnel decision?

**C6.** Do your managers know what to do when an employee's personal life is bleeding into their work? Not policy — do they have any actual tools for that conversation?

**C7.** Have you invested differently in managers at different properties, or has development been consistent across the chain?

**C8.** After any training you've done — did behavior change, or did people go back to their defaults within a few weeks?

**Bridge (if training didn't stick):** *"When it didn't hold, where did it break down — the managers not applying it, or the environment not supporting it?"* This names the structural vs. skill gap and frames exactly where your work sits.
