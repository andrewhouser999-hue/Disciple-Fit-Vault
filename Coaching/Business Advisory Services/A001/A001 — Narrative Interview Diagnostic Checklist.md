## Section 1 — Narrative Coherence

*Coherence is the capacity to tell a clear, connected account of relational experience. The diagnostic frame is adapted from Main & Goldwyn's Adult Attachment Interview narrative coding, applied here in non-clinical form. Track throughout all three domains.*

### Coherence Strengths — Check all observed

- [ ] Specific episodes produced without extended prompting
- [ ] Account holds together across time — beginning, middle, end without collapse
- [ ] Others appear in the account with interior experience (they had feelings, intentions, perspectives)
- [ ] Leader includes themselves as an agent — decisions made, words said, actions taken
- [ ] Proportional affect — emotional tone matches the weight of the events described
- [ ] Repairs and resolution appear in the account, not just rupture
- [ ] Acknowledges complexity without becoming disoriented by it

### Incoherence Signals — Check all observed; note domain where signal appeared

| # | Signal | Definition | Observed | Domain | Brief note |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Vagueness under probing** | Cannot produce a specific example even after explicit redirect; describes approach instead of event | | | |
| 2 | **Contradiction without acknowledgment** | States one position, then contradicts it in the same account without noticing or resolving the contradiction | | | |
| 3 | **Idealization** | Global positive statements about self or others without specific supporting memory ("He was a great leader" — but cannot recall a specific example) | | | |
| 4 | **Blame externalization** | All causality located in others; self is absent as a variable or agent in the account | | | |
| 5 | **Account brevity collapse** | Narrative becomes progressively shorter under probing; trails off rather than deepens; "I don't know" increases | | | |
| 6 | **Sudden topic shift** | Moves away from loaded content before it resolves; pivots without acknowledging the shift | | | |
| 7 | **Pronoun or tense disorientation** | Loss of narrative thread — confusion about who did what, when; present and past collapse without signal | | | |
| 8 | **Affect-content mismatch** | Emotional register disconnected from content weight — laughing while describing serious harm; distress over minor events | | | |
| 9 | **Unresolved intrusion** | Grief, anger, or loss from a past relationship intrudes inappropriately into the account of a current situation | | | |

### Coherence Rating — Circle one

**LOW** — Multiple incoherence signals; account does not hold together under probing; specificity unavailable

**MODERATE** — Some incoherence signals in one or two domains; coherence degrades under activation but holds at baseline

**HIGH** — Account is specific, connected, and holds together across all three domains; incoherence signals absent or minor

## Section 2 — Affect Regulation

*Track against Siegel's Window of Tolerance. The diagnostic question is not whether the leader shows emotion — it is whether their affect is proportional to the content and whether they remain accessible while activated. Track by domain.*

### Regulation Vocabulary

**Within window** — Proportional affect; accessible and responsive to probing; slight affect present but does not dominate; can shift topic without residue

**Above window (hyperarousal)** — Affect escalates beyond what the content warrants; emotional intensity increases with probing; voice rises or quickens; pursues the point beyond what the question invited; seeks validation; hard to interrupt

**Below window (hypoarousal)** — Flatness; affect absent even at loaded content; voice monotone; engagement decreases under activation; answers become shorter; appears to remove themselves from the room

**Disorganized** — Rapid cycling between hyper and hypo within the same domain; no stabilization point; emotional responses appear unpredictable relative to content; simultaneous activation and withdrawal signals

## Section 3 — Self-Other Balance

*The capacity to integrate self and others as simultaneous agents with interior lives — each having intentions, feelings, and perspectives. Collapse in either direction is diagnostically significant.*

### Designations

**Other-collapsed** — Story told entirely from the leader's perspective; others appear as objects, obstacles, or background. No evidence that the leader considers what others might be experiencing. Perspective-taking question produces confusion or deflection.

**Integrated** — Self and others both present as agents. The leader can describe their own interior experience AND represent how others likely experienced the situation. Complexity held without collapse.

**Self-collapsed** — Leader is absent from their own story. Things happen to them. Passive construction dominates. They describe others' behavior in detail but cannot locate their own decisions, words, or agency.

### Tracking

**Opening posture (pre-interview):** Other-collapsed / Integrated / Self-collapsed

**D1 — Accountability Delivery:** Other-collapsed / Integrated / Self-collapsed

**D2 — Feedback That Didn't Land:** Other-collapsed / Integrated / Self-collapsed

**D3 — Difficult Supervisor Relationship:** Other-collapsed / Integrated / Self-collapsed

## Section 6 — Prochaska Change Readiness

*Observe the ratio of change talk (reasons to change, intentions, ability statements) to sustain talk (reasons to stay the same, minimization, counter-arguments). Stage is an inference, not a declaration — hold it lightly.*

### Change Talk Signals (toward movement)
- [ ] Acknowledges that their own behavior contributes to the problem
- [ ] Expresses dissatisfaction with current outcomes in their own words
- [ ] Names something they wish they did differently
- [ ] Asks what effective managers do — curiosity about an alternative
- [ ] Expresses belief that change is possible for them specifically
- [ ] Has already attempted something different — describes an experiment

### Sustain Talk Signals (away from movement)
- [ ] Attributes all outcomes to factors outside their control
- [ ] Minimizes the significance of the problem described
- [ ] Defends current approach when probed
- [ ] Counter-argues when Andrew reflects a behavioral observation
- [ ] Expresses hopelessness about change in others ("the employees are just that way")
- [ ] No acknowledgment of personal contribution to relational outcomes

### Stage Indicators

**Pre-contemplation** — Does not see their own behavior as a variable. Sustain talk dominant. Attributes outcomes to employees, the owner, the property, or circumstances. Engagement with self-referential probing is minimal or defensive.

**Contemplation** — Sees a problem but ambivalent. Change talk and sustain talk roughly balanced. Expresses both "I know I need to handle that differently" and "but also it's really hard when..." within the same interview. Genuine openness coexists with genuine resistance.

**Preparation** — Change talk dominant. Has identified something they want to do differently. May have already tried something. Asks what effective behavior looks like. Receptive to behavioral goal framing.

**Action** — Has already changed something and can describe it specifically. Seeking reinforcement and refinement, not permission.

**Stage estimate:** Pre-contemplation / Contemplation / Preparation / Action

## Section 7 — Kegan Developmental Stage

*Observe the leader's relationship to external authority and internal self-authorship. Stage markers are observable in how they narrate accountability, disagreement, and their own positions under pressure. This is a rough indicator — treat as a hypothesis, not a classification.*

### Stage 3 — Socialized Mind
The leader's identity is substantially defined by external relationships and what significant others think of them. They know what their values are — but when pressed, those values bend to relational pressure.

**Observable markers:**
- [ ] Strong investment in being seen positively by the owner — references to owner's opinion appear frequently
- [ ] Difficulty holding a position that conflicts with what a respected authority believes
- [ ] Accountability delivery feels threatening to relationship, not just uncomfortable
- [ ] Others' approval visible as a motivational driver ("I wanted them to understand I was trying")
- [ ] Self-evaluation depends heavily on how others responded — "if they were upset, then I must have done something wrong"

### Stage 3/4 — Transitional
Beginning to author their own positions while still substantially dependent on external validation. Can hold a self-determined perspective in low-stakes contexts; loses it under relational pressure.

**Observable markers:**
- [ ] Can express disagreement with authority but describes it as difficult or costly
- [ ] Beginning to distinguish between what they think and what others expect
- [ ] Self-evaluation is sometimes internal ("I knew I did the right thing even if...") but reverts under pressure
- [ ] Asks questions about what Andrew thinks — seeks external calibration, then appears to weigh it

### Stage 4 — Self-Authoring Mind
The leader generates their own standards and evaluates experience against those standards, independent of whether others agree. Does not collapse into self-blame or defensiveness when questioned — can hold complexity.

**Observable markers:**
- [ ] Holds position when interviewer probes differently — does not immediately revise under question pressure
- [ ] Self-evaluation is primarily internal; references to others' opinions are present but not dominant
- [ ] Perspective-taking question answered with genuine reflection — not performance
- [ ] Accountability narratives include their own contribution without prompting and without self-collapse
- [ ] Disagreement in D3 described as resolved through their own reasoning, not just accommodation

**Developmental stage estimate:** Stage 3 / Stage 3–4 Transition / Stage 4
