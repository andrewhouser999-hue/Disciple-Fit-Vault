## Six Approach Advisories

These are A001-specific. They apply to all correspondence and pre-engagement communication with this owner.

---

**Advisory 1 — Protect the coffee shop register.**

The informal warmth of that exchange is the primary relational asset. Every communication should feel like a continuation of that conversation — curious, unhurried, following his track. The moment a communication feels like a sales setup, the owner will retrospectively reinterpret the coffee shop exchange as having been instrumental. That reframe collapses both the relational capital and the credibility simultaneously. Never reference the prior exchange as leverage ("you already know this works"). Reference it as genuine continuity.

---

**Advisory 2 — Operational framing only.**

His vocabulary: properties, turnover, frontline staff, GMs, supervisors, shift coverage, guest experience, costs. Use his words. Avoid: dysregulation, trauma-informed, attachment, psychological safety (in clinical-sounding framing), framework, methodology, evidence base. The mechanism is real and the research is solid — but in correspondence, translate everything to operational cost and behavioral outcome. He will respond to "this is driving turnover and here's why" far more than "the neurobiological literature explains this pattern."

---

**Advisory 3 — Tease the mechanism; don't explain it.**

In teaser communications, name what the research says about what he identified — enough to create curiosity — without explaining the full mechanism. The pull toward the meeting should come from his own curiosity, not from you having explained enough that he feels he already understands it. The goal of a teaser is to produce: *"I want to hear more about that."* Not: *"Interesting — I'll think about it."*

---

**Advisory 4 — Concrete close in every communication.**

Mildly anxious attachment + operational mindset = ambiguous closes produce anxiety, not patience. Every correspondence ends with one specific ask or one specific next step. *"I'll send you a summary by Thursday"* beats *"I'll be in touch."* *"60 minutes, your schedule, sometime in the next two weeks"* beats *"Let me know if you want to talk."*

---

**Advisory 5 — One ask per communication.**

Don't stack requests. Don't ask him to review something AND schedule a meeting AND consider including his OMs in a single message. One clear request. If there's more to communicate, send two short messages rather than one long one.

---

**Advisory 6 — 48-hour window after any teaser.**

After any teaser communication (text or email), the window of peak engagement is 48 hours. If there's no response, one brief follow-up within that window is appropriate. After 72 hours without response, the moment has cooled and follow-up begins to feel like pressure. See [[#Delivery and Follow-Up Protocol]] for the full sequence.

## Outreach Templates

### Text Message — Scheduling the First Meeting

*Tone: warm, brief, one ask. References the prior exchange without making it a setup.*

---

> Hey [name] — I've been thinking about what you described at [coffee shop/location]. I did some reading on it and I think there's more to unpack than we had time for. Would you be open to 60 minutes sometime in the next couple of weeks? I'll work around your schedule.

---

**Notes:**
- Send text before email — matches the informal origin of the relationship
- "I did some reading on it" signals you took his insight seriously without claiming expertise
- "More to unpack than we had time for" positions the meeting as a continuation, not a pitch
- No price, no service description, no methodology — that's for the meeting

---

### Email — Scheduling the First Meeting

*Tone: slightly more developed than the text. One additional sentence of substance. Still a teaser, not a proposal.*

---

**Subject:** Following up — what you described at [location]

[Name],

What you put together in that conversation about your team — the entitlement, the environment piece — that's not just intuition. The research on what actually drives that behavior in hospitality operations is more specific than most operators know, and the implications for your portfolio are worth 60 minutes of your time.

I'd like to sit down and take that conversation further. No pitch, no presentation — just the kind of conversation we had, with more ground to cover. Sometime in the next couple of weeks works well on my end.

What does your schedule look like?

Andrew

---

**Notes:**
- "That's not just intuition" validates the insight without flattering him — it signals there's substance behind what he identified
- "The research is more specific than most operators know" — creates information asymmetry without explaining it; this is the teaser move
- "No pitch, no presentation" is a direct self-orientation signal; it reduces the sales-setup read
- Short paragraphs; no bullet points; reads like a person, not a consultant

---

### Follow-Up Text (48-Hour Window)

*If no response to text or email within 48 hours:*

---

> Hey [name] — just wanted to make sure my note landed. No rush — whenever works for you.

---

**Notes:**
- One sentence only
- "No rush" reduces pressure for mildly anxious attachment profile — he may be busy, not uninterested
- Do not re-pitch, do not restate the teaser, do not ask again in the same message

## Delivery and Follow-Up Protocol

**Step 1 — Text first.**
Send the text message. Personal, brief, matches the informal origin. Wait 2–4 hours.

**Step 2 — Email same day.**
Send the email within 2–4 hours of the text. The text primes; the email gives him something to respond to in writing if that's easier for him.

**Step 3 — 48-hour window.**
If no response within 48 hours, send the follow-up text. One time. Keep it brief.

**Step 4 — If still no response after 72 hours.**
Do not follow up again immediately. Wait 5–7 days, then one more brief text: *"Hey [name] — still open to connecting whenever the timing works for you."* This is the final outreach in this sequence. Do not triple-follow-up.

**Step 5 — When he responds.**
If he responds positively, confirm the specific time within the same conversation. Don't leave the scheduling open-ended. *"Tuesday the 10th at 10am works — [location or call link]?"*

**Step 6 — Confirmation message (24 hours before the meeting).**
One brief confirmation: *"Looking forward to tomorrow. [Time, location]."* No agenda, no preparation instructions.

## First Meeting Structure

*High-level sequence for the 60-minute initial meeting. For full detail, use [[A001 — Initial Meeting Field Form]] (in-meeting) and [[A001 — First Meeting Question Guide]] (pre-meeting study). For pivot readiness, use [[A001 — Conversation Readiness Guide]].*

| Phase | Approx. Time | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Opening** | 5–10 min | Reference coffee shop exchange → ask where his thinking has gone since then → let him set pace |
| **Diagnostic deepening** | 25–30 min | 4-layer question sequence: pain picture → management behavior gap → structural contributors → pressure test |
| **Role and scope** | 10 min | Introduce role clarity as care for him (not disclaimer) → state what you need from him |
| **Transition** | 5–10 min | Summarize diagnostic picture → wait for him to surface "what now" → offer next step, not full engagement |
| **Close** | 5 min | Confirm next step with specific day → surface OM framing question |

**Non-negotiables for this meeting:**
- Do not open with methodology, credentials, or service description
- Do not introduce pricing unless he raises it
- Do not define full engagement scope
- Do not skip the OM framing question at close

## OM-in-the-Room Protocol

*What to do if the owner brings one or both OMs to the initial meeting without advance notice.*

**Why this requires a protocol:**
The initial meeting is designed for the owner alone. The warm-entry opening, the diagnostic deepening, the credibility establishment, and the role/scope framing all assume a private conversation. If OMs are present:
- The opening move (reference the coffee shop, where has your thinking gone) becomes awkward and potentially exclusionary to the OMs
- The diagnostic questions will produce self-protective answers — OMs are not going to disclose management gaps in front of the owner
- The SCARF threat profile for OMs (high threat on Status, Certainty, Autonomy, Fairness) activates immediately in a room where the owner is clearly primed and aligned with an outside person they haven't met

**How to read the situation:**
- Planned vs. surprise: did the owner mention bringing OMs in advance? If yes, he's chosen to include them and the opening must honor that. If no, he brought them without thinking through the implications — a brief redirecting conversation is appropriate.
- Posture of the OMs: curious and relaxed, or guarded and scanning? This tells you whether threat is already activated.

**If OMs are present and the owner has not flagged it in advance:**

After initial greetings and brief relational warm-up, before moving into the structured conversation:

*"[Name] — I want to make sure we use this time well. The conversation I was hoping to have with you today goes into some specifics about the operation that I think work better one-on-one first — before we bring the management team in. I'd love to find a brief time with you separately, and then a separate conversation with [OM names] that's designed specifically for them. Does that work?"*

If he pushes back or wants to proceed with OMs present:

*"That's completely your call. If you want to proceed together, I'm happy to do that — I'll just adjust how we approach it. What I'd want your [OMs] to know from the start is what this is and what it isn't, so we're all starting from the same place."*

Then open with the OM threat-reduction framing from the Conversation Readiness Guide before any diagnostic questions.

**If OMs are present and it was planned:**

Open with the OM threat-reduction language immediately after greetings:

*"Before we get into anything — I want to be clear about what I am and am not here to do, because I'd want to know that too if someone walked into my operation. I'm not here to evaluate how any of you manage. I'm here to help [owner name] understand what the environment is producing and build something that makes your jobs easier. Your experience of how this place actually works is the most important information I have access to."*

Then proceed with a modified meeting structure — the diagnostic questions shift toward what OMs can answer honestly in group context (structural contributors, industry patterns) rather than management behavior gaps.

## What NOT to Say

*Consolidated for all correspondence and pre-meeting communication. Phrases, framings, and approaches specific to this engagement that would damage the warm entry or undermine credibility.*

| Don't Say | Why | Say This Instead |
|---|---|---|
| "I've been helping organizations like yours for years" | Can't back it up fully in this context; undermines the honest pull-back credibility move | Let the credibility talking points carry the weight — [[A001 — Credibility Talking Points]] |
| "I have a process that would work really well for you" | Positions a pre-formed solution before diagnosis; self-orientation signal | "I'd like to hear more about what you're seeing before I say anything about what I'd do with it" |
| "Your managers are probably the problem" | Implicates his hiring decisions; positions OMs as subjects before they're partners | "The environment shapes management behavior the same way it shapes frontline behavior" |
| "What you experienced in that coffee shop conversation changed my thinking too" | Makes the prior interaction mutual in a way that sounds performed | "That conversation stuck with me — not because of anything I said, but because of how quickly you connected it to your operation" |
| "I think you're really onto something" | Hollow validation; operational owners don't need to be told their intuitions are good — they need information | Translate his insight to mechanism: "What you identified has a specific name in the research" |
| "This is a complex problem" | Vague; sounds like setup for a long engagement; operational owners hear "expensive and uncertain" | "Here's the specific driver, here's what changes when you address it" |
| "Let me send you some information about what I do" | Converts the relationship from warm entry to cold prospect management | "I'd like to sit down and take the conversation further — 60 minutes, your schedule" |
| "I just need to know more before I can say what this would look like" | Too early for this hedge; sounds like stalling | Ask the diagnostic questions; don't flag your uncertainty before you've heard the situation |
| "I'm not a therapist" | True but sounds defensive; introduces clinical framing the conversation wasn't in | "I work at the level of behavior and environment — not clinical intervention" (only if it comes up) |
| "This is going to require a long-term commitment" | Sounds like pressure before he's even seen what the work produces | "Phase 1 is 13 weeks. At the end you decide whether you want to continue." |
