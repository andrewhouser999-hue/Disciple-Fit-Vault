# Relational Performance Advisory — Sponsor Debrief Guide

**What this is:** Practitioner guide for the Phase 4 sponsor debrief — the meeting where Andrew delivers aggregate findings and structural recommendations to the owner/sponsor. This meeting carries two risks that must be managed throughout: (1) the owner pressing for individual leader data Andrew cannot provide, and (2) the owner under-reacting to aggregate findings that don't have names attached to them. The guide addresses both.

**Session duration:** 75–90 minutes.

**Timing:** After all individual leader debriefs are complete. Do not conduct the sponsor debrief before leaders have received their own profiles — they are entitled to their findings first.

**What the owner receives in this meeting:**
- Team Aggregate Pattern Map
- Structural recommendations
- Proposed next phase

**What the owner does not receive:**
- Individual leader profiles
- Narrative interview content
- Individual 360 data
- Anything disclosed in individual coaching sessions

---

## Pre-Meeting Preparation

**Complete the Team Aggregate Pattern Map before this meeting.** It is the primary document — have it ready to present.

**Prepare structural recommendations.** These are organizational design and management practice recommendations drawn from the aggregate picture — not from individual profiles. They should address: feedback cadence, conflict escalation protocols, accountability structures, any patterns in the aggregate that point to systemic contributors.

**Anticipate owner questions about specific leaders.** In a 4–8 leader cohort, the owner almost certainly has opinions about specific people. Prepare the boundary-holding script. The response to "what about [name]?" is not improvised.

**Know the option transition pitch.** If this engagement was Option C (diagnostic entry), this meeting is the natural conversion point to Option B or A. Prepare the transition language — connect aggregate findings directly to what the full intervention would address.

---

## Phase 1 — Opening (5 minutes)

**Frame what the owner is about to receive:**
> "What I'm going to share today is the team-level picture — what the data shows about your management team as a whole, what structural factors are contributing to what you're seeing, and what I'd recommend for the next phase. Individual findings stay with the individual leaders — that's the confidentiality structure we established at the start, and it's what made people willing to be honest in the assessment. What I can tell you is that the team picture is genuinely useful."

This framing sets the expectation before the owner experiences the boundary as a withholding. Naming it proactively is less threatening than having it enforced reactively.

---

## Phase 2 — Presenting the Team Aggregate Pattern Map (25–30 minutes)

**Hand the document to the owner. Give 60 seconds of reading time before beginning.**

**Walk through Section 1 (Team Pattern Distribution) using operational language:**
> "The most prevalent pattern across your management team is [withdrawal / escalation / disorganization]. That means when pressure rises — a service problem, a difficult staffing situation, a conflict with a direct report — the default response for most of your managers is [describe in operational terms: avoidance, over-reaction, inconsistency]."

**Connect pattern to the business problem the owner named at the start of the engagement.** This is the most important move in the meeting. The owner hired Andrew because of a specific problem — turnover, culture instability, service inconsistency. Connect the aggregate finding to that problem directly:
> "You told me at the start that your biggest concern was [stated problem]. Here's what the data shows is underneath it: [pattern in operational terms]. This isn't a motivation problem or a skills gap. It's a relational pattern under pressure — and that's something that can actually be developed."

**Section 2 (Team Activation Threshold) — keep brief:**
> "Your team's collective threshold is [low/moderate/high]. What that means in practice is [brief operational translation]."

**Section 3 (Structural Observations) — this often produces the most owner engagement.** These are systemic factors, not individual failures:
> "A few things at the structural level are contributing to what you're seeing. [Walk through 2–3 structural observations.] These aren't things any individual leader can fix on their own — they require changes at the organizational level."

---

## Phase 3 — Presenting Structural Recommendations (15–20 minutes)

Deliver recommendations as a written list (Section 4 of the Aggregate Map). Walk through each one briefly — not an exhaustive explanation, a clear statement of what it is and why:
> "Recommendation 1: [name it]. Right now, [current state]. The data suggests [what it's costing]. The change is [specific structural shift]."

**Invite reaction after each recommendation:**
> "Does that match what you're seeing?"

Owner engagement here is diagnostic. Strong agreement = the recommendation is credible. Pushback = either a legitimate operational constraint or a Status-protective response. Listen for which it is before responding.

**If the owner pushes back on a recommendation:**
> "Tell me what gets in the way of that."

Understand the constraint before defending the recommendation. Some structural recommendations won't be implemented — that's real and shouldn't be argued. The goal is that the owner understands the cost of not implementing, not that every recommendation gets adopted.

---

## Phase 4 — Handling Questions About Individual Leaders

The owner will ask. Prepare for it.

**The boundary-holding script:**
> "I'm not able to share individual profiles without written consent from the leader. What I can tell you is that each leader is working through a coaching arc that's specifically designed around what their profile showed. If a leader decides they want to share their profile with you, that's entirely their decision — and I'll support that if they want to do it.
>
> What I can tell you at the aggregate level is [reframe the question in aggregate terms — e.g., if the owner asks about a specific GM's accountability, describe the accountability pattern across the team]."

**Hold this boundary without exception and without apology.** If the owner presses:
> "I understand the frustration — you're responsible for these properties and you want to know what you're working with. The reason I can't share individual data is the same reason the data is good: people were honest because they trusted it would stay confidential. If I break that once, the process doesn't work for anyone — including you."

**Do not soften this into ambiguity.** A half-held boundary is worse than a firm one — it signals that the boundary might move with enough pressure.

---

## Phase 5 — Next Phase and Transition (15 minutes)

**Connect aggregate findings to the intervention proposal:**
> "The aggregate picture points directly to what the next phase would address. [Describe what the coaching arc and team facilitation would target, in operational terms.] You'd see the impact first in [specific operational area] — that's where the pattern is costing you the most."

**If this was an Option C engagement, introduce the transition:**
> "Option C was designed to answer the diagnostic question — what are the actual patterns, and what's driving them? We've answered that. The next question is what to do about it. That's what Option [B/A] addresses."

**Be specific about expected timeline and outcomes:**
> "Over [X months], here's what changes: [specific behavioral and operational outcomes tied to what the owner originally identified as the problem]. At Month 12, I'll give you a measurement report showing pattern movement against baseline."

**If the owner is not ready to decide:**
> "You don't have to decide today. Take the aggregate map, look at the structural recommendations, and let's talk in [timeframe]. What I'd ask is that we don't let too much time pass — the leaders who went through the assessment are expecting a next step."

---

## Closing (5 minutes)

**Name exactly what happens next:**
- If advancing to next phase: confirm scope, timeline, fees, and first session date
- If owner is deciding: set a specific follow-up date — not "when you're ready"
- Either way: confirm Month 12 checkpoint is on the calendar

**Acknowledge the owner's investment in the process:**
> "You made a real commitment here — not just financially, but in the time your team put into this. What the data shows is that this organization has leaders who are willing to be honest and do hard work. That's not a given. It's a real asset."

This is not flattery — it is true, and it reframes the aggregate findings from "your team has problems" to "your team has capacity."

---

## If the Owner Is Disappointed by the Aggregate Findings

Some owners expect the assessment to confirm what they already believe about specific leaders. When the findings are more complex or more systemic than expected, disappointment is possible.

**Response:**
> "I know this might not be the picture you were expecting. What the data shows is that this isn't primarily a 'bad apples' problem — it's a pattern that most of your management team shares to some degree. That's actually better news: it means the intervention doesn't depend on replacing people. It means developing the people you have."

If the owner continues to push for individual accountability data:
Hold the boundary. Offer the structural recommendations as the actionable takeaway. The aggregate picture is the product — it is not a consolation prize for not getting individual data.
