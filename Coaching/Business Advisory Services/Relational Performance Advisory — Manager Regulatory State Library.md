# Manager Regulatory State Library
*Relational Performance Advisory — Practitioner Reference*

**Companion prompt:** [[Relational Performance Advisory — Manager State Identification Prompt]]

**Purpose:** Reference library of regulatory, attachment, and stress-state patterns commonly active in business managers and leaders. Used to identify what is *below* the behavior — the state driving it — and name it in language the manager can recognize and hear. Output language should be operational throughout: behavioral, observable, non-clinical.

**Evidence anchors:** Perry (neurosequential model), Siegel (interpersonal neurobiology / Window of Tolerance), Porges (polyvagal theory), Rock (SCARF), Kegan & Lahey (immunity to change), Argyris & Schön (theory-in-use), van der Kolk (stress-state physiology), Bowlby/Main (attachment theory).

---

## Attachment States

| State | Operational Description |
|---|---|
| **Chronic distrust** | Default assumption that others will fail, betray, or disappoint. Pre-dates the management role. Manifests as vigilance before evidence, skepticism of good faith, and relational tests people don't know they're taking. |
| **Relational hypervigilance** | Constant scanning for threat cues in people's tone, silence, expression, or hesitation. High false-positive rate — neutral signals read as negative. Exhausting to be around; people learn to manage their own presentation carefully. |
| **Closeness aversion** | Discomfort with warmth, dependency, or vulnerability. Labeled "professional distance" or "high standards." Creates a relational temperature others feel as coldness, disapproval, or inaccessibility. |
| **Anxious attachment in authority** | Needs ongoing confirmation of respect, standing, or performance. Interprets ambiguity as disapproval. Overworks or over-explains in response to perceived distance from superiors. |
| **Approval hunger masked as control** | High standards and detailed oversight used to manage how others perceive them, not just the quality of work. The real fear is evaluation; the visible behavior is control. |

## Regulatory States

| State | Operational Description |
|---|---|
| **Emotional flooding** | Cognitive access narrows under relational threat. Can't think clearly when challenged, evaluated, or publicly questioned. Goes to pre-set responses (defensiveness, dismissal, attack) rather than reflective ones. |
| **Chronic low-grade dysregulation** | Persistent baseline tension, irritability, or edge that others adapt around. The manager doesn't notice it because it's their normal. Others organize their behavior around it without naming it. |
| **Threat-detection bias** | Nervous system trained — by role pressure, performance history, or earlier experience — to find danger. Neutral inputs register as threatening. The baseline is vigilance, not ease. |
| **Burnout plateau** | Emotional shutdown after prolonged overactivation. Looks like composure, decisiveness, or efficiency. Actual access to relational range, warmth, or curiosity is significantly reduced. |
| **Hypervigilance to performance** | Constant internal monitoring for failure, inadequacy, or exposure. The monitoring itself is costly — cognitive load is always divided between the task and self-evaluation. |
| **Chronic cortisol elevation** | Prolonged stress response that hasn't resolved. Body and mind operate in defended mode even when the immediate threat isn't present. Recovery time is longer; thresholds are lower. |

## Shutdown / Dissociation-Adjacent States

| State | Operational Description |
|---|---|
| **Functional absence** | Physically present, relationally unavailable. Checks out in high-stakes conversations — gives formulaic answers, doesn't track the person, processes the content without the relational texture. |
| **Emotional constriction** | Expressive range narrows to control or flat neutrality. Others can't read them. The team fills that ambiguity with anxiety. Decisions feel opaque; feedback feels like a wall. |
| **Relational depersonalization** | People become functions or problems rather than persons. Warmth disappears. The manager still executes the role but the relational container — the thing that makes accountability land rather than sting — is gone. |
| **Going through the motions** | Role performance without relational presence. Meetings happen, check-ins happen, the words are right — but nothing connects. People feel managed, not known. |

## Stress-Response Patterns

*These are non-clinical descriptions of the fight / flight / freeze / fawn survival-response spectrum as they appear in management contexts.*

| State | Operational Description |
|---|---|
| **Fight response** | Control, aggression, micromanagement, dominance, escalation. The most visible pattern in management. Often mistaken for high standards, accountability culture, or decisive leadership. |
| **Flight response** | Avoidance of hard conversations, overwork as escape, relational withdrawal, physical or emotional absence from conflict. The schedule fills; the person disappears. |
| **Freeze response** | Indecision, stonewalling, paralysis under pressure. Team waits; nothing moves. Mistaken for deliberation, thoughtfulness, or strategic patience. |
| **Fawn response** | Appeasing upward (compliant to superiors), displacing tension downward onto the team. Alignment theater with leadership; pressure or volatility directed at those below. |
| **Shame-based reactivity** | Error or feedback registers as exposure or attack rather than information. Response is disproportionate — withdrawal, counterattack, subject change, over-apology, or blame displacement. |
| **Rage-to-shame cycling** | Explosive response followed by withdrawal, over-apology, or subject change. The cycle is the pattern. People learn the cycle and manage around it rather than addressing the trigger. |

## Cognitive and Belief States

| State | Operational Description |
|---|---|
| **Status threat activation** | Perceived loss of rank, respect, or authority triggers outsized response. The SCARF Status domain is the most sensitive; challenge to standing registers as threat to self. |
| **Narrative rigidity** | A fixed story about people, situations, or self that interprets all new data to confirm itself. Evidence to the contrary is dismissed, reframed, or attributed to the other person. |
| **Attribution error** | Problems explained by others' deficiency; own contribution to the dynamic is invisible. The pattern is consistent — it's always the team, the circumstances, the person. |
| **Certainty demand** | Low tolerance for ambiguity. Manifests as pressure, false decisiveness, urgency that isn't warranted, or dismissal of options before they're evaluated. |
| **Perfectionism as control** | Standards weaponized to maintain power or avoid vulnerability. The standard is the shield — no one can get close if the work is always insufficient. |
| **Catastrophic forecasting** | Small threat signals amplified to worst-case. Operates from anticipated failure. Decisions are made in the shadow of what hasn't happened yet. |
| **Self-protective distancing** | Relational withdrawal deployed preemptively to avoid exposure, disappointment, or evaluation. Disguised as professionalism, efficiency, or appropriate role boundaries. |

## Practitioner Notes

**Language discipline:** Name the state in terms the manager can recognize — not in clinical language. "You get loud when you feel disrespected" rather than "hyperarousal." "You check out when the conversation threatens you" rather than "dissociation."

**Connection sequence:** State → internal experience → belief → behavior → team impact. The manager usually enters at behavior (what they do) or team impact (what others say). Work backward to state and belief. That's where the leverage is.

**What to watch for in conversation:** The state the manager is describing in others is often the state they're in. The complaint about the team's defensiveness is sometimes a description of their own.

**Scope boundary:** Andrew is a non-clinical organizational consultant. These descriptions support pattern identification and coaching language — not diagnosis. Any referral threshold (clinical indicators, trauma that requires therapeutic containment) follows the RPA Processes & Procedures referral protocol.
