# Relational Performance Advisory — Narrative Interview Practitioner Skills Reference

## The Governing Principle

The data in the Diagnostic Checklist comes from **emergent narrative behavior** — not from self-report, not from question answers, not from what the leader says about themselves. Technique creates the conditions. The leader's natural pattern does the rest.

The primary job is to create conditions where natural patterns surface, then observe them without contaminating them.

---

## Core Technique: Press-for-Specificity Protocol

The entire checklist depends on this. Every coherence signal is only visible when you probe. If you accept general claims, you get managed self-presentation.

**The discipline:** any time a leader makes a general claim, press once for a specific episode.

Not "can you tell me more?" — that is soft. The language is directional:

> *"Can you give me a specific example — a particular time that happened?"*
> *"What did you actually say in that moment?"*
> *"Walk me through what happened — beginning to end."*

You are not helping them find the story. You are testing whether a specific memory exists at all. Vagueness under this probe is data. Deflection is data. Sudden topic shift is data.

Press once clearly. Then wait. Silence is not a problem to solve.

---

## Minimal Probe Set

Use five phrases and almost nothing else throughout all three domains. Variation introduces your language into the narrative and contaminates what you observe.

1. *"Tell me more about that."*
2. *"What did you say — or do — in that moment?"*
3. *"What do you think was going on for them?"* (perspective-taking probe — use throughout, not only at the direct question)
4. *"What happened after that?"*
5. *"How did it resolve — or did it?"*

**What to avoid:** normalizing language ("that makes sense," "a lot of managers feel that way"), reframing language ("so it sounds like you were trying to..."), summary language ("so what I'm hearing is..."). These are coaching moves. In a diagnostic interview they contaminate data by regulating the leader toward you.

---

## Activation Sequencing Within Each Domain

Each domain has a natural activation arc. Where coherence or regulation degrades tells you where the leader's floor is. Move through four layers in sequence:

1. **Situational** — "Tell me about a specific time you had to hold someone accountable."
2. **Relational** — "What did you actually say? What did they say back?"
3. **Interior** — "What was going on for you in that moment — what were you thinking or feeling?"
4. **Perspective-taking** — "What do you think was happening for them?"

Some leaders produce clean situational narrative but degrade at the relational layer (can't produce specific language). Some hold through relational but collapse at interior (affect absent, goes blank). Some hold through layers 1–3 but cannot access layer 4.

The layer where degradation begins is diagnostic. Don't skip layers to move faster.

---

## Domain Separation

The domain-by-domain comparisons in Sections 2–4 of the checklist are only valid if the domains stay distinct. A leader who is within-window in D1 and above-window in D3 is telling you something specific about authority relationships. You lose this if domains bleed together.

**Transition language:** explicit, neutral, full-stop before opening next domain.

> *"I want to shift to something different now. Can you tell me about a time you received feedback that didn't quite land — feedback you either pushed back on or just didn't act on?"*

Hard close the previous domain before opening the next. Do not let D3 material surface in D1 time.

---

## "Shows" vs. "Tells" — the most important distinction

The checklist is built entirely on **shows** data — how someone speaks, not what they say about themselves.

A leader who tells you "I'm direct — hard conversations don't bother me" while producing passive construction, no specific language recall, and account brevity collapse under probing is **showing** you the withdrawal pattern. The verbal self-description is noise. The structural behavior is signal.

Pre-interview reminder: *I am not here for their self-assessment. I am watching how they narrate.*

---

## Managing the Articulate Leader — Backward Probing

Some leaders produce smooth, detailed, well-organized narrative easily. Fluency is not coherence. A prepared narrative is not an episodic memory.

**Backward probing** — take a specific claim and ask what preceded it:

> *"You said you went back and had a direct conversation with her. What had the preceding few days looked like — before you decided to do that?"*

Genuine episodic memory can produce the context. A rehearsed narrative often cannot. Vagueness or pivot at the "what came before" question is data — you're testing whether the memory exists across the full timeline, not just at the resolved moment.

---

## Differentiating "I Don't Know"

The checklist marks "I don't know" increases as an account brevity collapse signal. But the responses are not equivalent.

| Type | What it looks like | Diagnostic meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **Genuine memory absence** | Flat, non-defensive; "It was a while ago, I honestly don't recall" | Low signal |
| **Defensive withdrawal** | Slight affect shift; topic change follows; shorter answers afterward | Incoherence signal |
| **Disorientation** | Confusion at the question itself; can't orient to what's being asked | Window activation signal |
| **Authentic complexity** | "I genuinely don't know what was driving that — I've thought about it" | HIGH coherence signal |

**Differentiating probe:** after an "I don't know," wait without filling the silence. Defensive withdrawal typically escalates or shifts topic. Authentic complexity often continues working the question unprompted.

## The Perspective-Taking Question

*"What do you think they would say about you in that relationship?"*

This is the most diagnostically loaded question in the interview. The four responses (Engaged / Thin / Refused / Overloaded) tell you about self-other balance more directly than anything else.

Ask it cold, as stated, in at least D2 and D3. Do not soften it. Do not explain why you're asking. Ask it, then go quiet. The immediate response before they compose themselves is the data.

---

## The D3 Live Dynamic (AOI-Specific — High Priority)

D3 asks about a difficult supervisor relationship. In AOI, the supervisor **is the owner** — the person who hired Andrew.

The leader knows, or can reasonably infer, that Andrew is operating on behalf of the owner. This means **D3 is not just a historical question — it is a live relational event happening in the room.** The leader is being asked about their relationship with the owner by someone who reports to the owner.

**Threat:** D3 data will be more defended than in other contexts. Idealization signal is especially likely here.

**Opportunity:** D3 regulation can be observed in real time, not just historically. How the leader navigates the interview itself — affect around supervisor-adjacent topics, seeking validation, going flat — is live D3 data.

**Required discipline:** Maintain strict neutrality on the owner throughout. No alignment signals. No owner-adjacent commentary. No response that suggests Andrew will report back specific content. Any ownership signal collapses the domain.

*This framing issue needs a specific protocol before the first AOI interview runs.*

---

## Andrew's Own Pattern in the Interview

Andrew's relational pattern will show up in how he conducts the interview. This is quality control on the data, not incidental self-awareness.

Concrete risks:
- High SCARF **status** sensitivity → accommodation of high-status leaders: softer probes, faster topic moves, less willingness to hold silence after a press
- High **relatedness** sensitivity → over-repairing silences; rescuing degrading narratives before degradation completes
- **Withdrawal tendencies** → avoiding probing in the domains where the leader is most activated — exactly where the data is

**Discipline:** before the interview, identify which domain or leader type is most likely to activate your own pattern. Pre-commit to the probe sequence regardless. Note in Section 8 afterward whether your own state may have affected the data.

---

## Interview Opening and Closing as Diagnostic Windows

**Opening:** The first 60–90 seconds before any domain question — how the leader enters, their presenting affect, initial framing — is the only uncontaminated baseline window before probing begins. Note it briefly before asking anything. This feeds the "Opening posture" field in Section 3.

**Closing:** After formal domains conclude, leaders often produce the most unguarded material — either because tension drops or because something that surfaced during the interview hasn't resolved. The verbatim-phrase captures in Section 8 often come from the edges. Stay attentive through the full close.

---

## Verbatim Capture — Practical Technique

Section 8 requires 3–5 diagnostically significant verbatim phrases. These are impossible to recall accurately 30 minutes post-interview without in-session capture.

Keep a narrow column in your notes specifically for direct quotes. Write during natural pauses — not while the leader is speaking. Abbreviate aggressively; reconstruct immediately after the interview ends, before synthesis.

**Phrases worth capturing:** not polished self-summary statements — the ones that appeared under probing. The first response to a specificity press. The language used when account brevity began to collapse. The exact words in the perspective-taking response. These are diagnostically load-bearing in a way prepared answers are not.

---

## Pre-Interview Mode Setting

Name your mode explicitly to yourself before entering: *this is diagnostic, not coaching.* Brief leaders on this too:

> *"I'm going to be asking questions and mostly listening. I won't be coaching today."*

---

## Coaching Mode Contamination — What to Avoid

Coaching instincts active during a diagnostic interview:

- **Affirming** → signals what earns approval → leader produces more of it
- **Normalizing** → reduces tension → but mild tension is what makes Window of Tolerance observation possible
- **Reframing** → fills in narrative coherence for the leader → destroys the vagueness data

The signals in Sections 1 and 2 are only visible if you do not manage the leader's experience during the interview.

---

## Three Failure Modes

**Failure Mode 1 — Leader gives you a performance, not a narrative.** Happens when rapport is too warm pre-interview, the leader is high-status and accustomed to managing upward, or questions don't press. Symptom: smooth, polished, all positive. Fix: press-for-specificity harder, earlier. A leader who produces smooth generals under probing is showing you something — mark it.

**Failure Mode 2 — You over-activate a SCARF domain in the interview itself.** If questions feel evaluative or opaque, activation is a reaction to Andrew, not the leader's natural pattern. Symptom: affect escalates on the first accountability question and stays there. Fix: neutral framing on D1 entry; emphasize this is not a performance review.

**Failure Mode 3 — You collapse the 30-minute synthesis window.** Section 8 must be completed before reviewing other leaders' data. Back-to-back interviews with end-of-day debrief introduces contamination. This is a scheduling discipline, not a technique question.

---

## Pre-Internalization Requirement

You cannot fill out the Diagnostic Checklist in real time while running an interview. Internalize these cold before the first interview:

- 9 incoherence signals (Section 1)
- 4 window positions (Section 2)
- 3 self-other designations (Section 3)
- 4 behavioral pattern types with key signals (Section 4)
- 5 SCARF domains and their interview-context signals (Section 5)
- Change talk vs. sustain talk distinction and stage indicators (Section 6)
- Stage 3 / 3-4 / Stage 4 observable markers (Section 7)

Use the checklist for notation during the interview (quick checkmarks, one-word notes) and synthesis within the 30-minute window. Not as a reading reference during the interview.
