# RPA Evidence Base — Researcher Reference

Quick-reference for every researcher cited across the RPA evidence base. Format per entry: who they are, the story that shaped the work, what it produced, when, who uses it, and why it matters to RPA.

---

## Neuroscience & Nervous System

**Bruce Perry** — Child psychiatrist; founder, ChildTrauma Academy (Houston)
*The Neurosequential Model of Therapeutics (NMT); co-author, What Happened to You? (2021, with Oprah Winfrey)*
Spent 40 years treating traumatized children and kept watching therapists fail — because they started at the wrong level of the brain. Insight: you can't do talk therapy with a brainstem in survival mode. Built NMT to sequence interventions bottom-up: regulate → relate → reason.
**Used by:** trauma clinicians, school systems, child welfare. **Business backing:** RPA's sequencing logic — safety and regulation must precede skill-building or performance feedback.

---

**Daniel Siegel** — Clinical professor of psychiatry, UCLA; founder, Mindsight Institute; MD Harvard
*The Developing Mind (1999); Interpersonal Neurobiology (IPNB)*
Frustrated that medical training ignored the mind, Siegel synthesized neuroscience, attachment theory, and mindfulness into a new field. Introduced the *Window of Tolerance* — the arousal zone where people can think, connect, and respond flexibly. Outside it, cognition and collaboration degrade.
**Used by:** therapists, educators, leadership developers. **Business backing:** explains why employees under chronic threat can't receive feedback or training until the relational environment changes.

---

**Stephen Porges** — Distinguished University Scientist, Indiana University; former brain-body center director, UIC
*Polyvagal Theory (introduced in 1994 presidential address to Society for Psychophysiological Research)*
Spent decades on vagus nerve research. Reframed the autonomic nervous system as three hierarchical circuits: ventral vagal (safety/connection), sympathetic (fight/flight), dorsal vagal (shutdown). Safety is not a thought — it's a bodily state (*neuroception*) registered below conscious awareness.
**Used by:** trauma therapists, somatic practitioners, organizational consultants. **Business backing:** a manager's tone, pacing, and facial expression are physiological cues that determine whether employees can engage — the nervous system reads the room before the mind does. *Note: some anatomical claims are contested by neuroscientists; clinical application remains widely supported.*

---

**Antonio Damasio** — University Professor, USC; director, Brain and Creativity Institute; MD University of Lisbon
*Descartes' Error (1994); Somatic Marker Hypothesis*
Studied patients with vmPFC brain damage at University of Iowa for 30 years. His famous patient "Elliot" had perfect IQ after tumor removal but couldn't decide which pen to use. Damasio's discovery: without emotional input, decision-making collapses. Emotions are *required* for rationality, not opposed to it.
**Used by:** behavioral economists, leadership researchers, decision scientists. **Business backing:** every HR and service decision runs through affective channels — "keep emotion out of business" is neurologically false.

---

**Bessel van der Kolk** — Psychiatrist; Professor, Boston University; president, Trauma Research Foundation
*The Body Keeps the Score (2014)*
Grew up under Nazi occupation in the Netherlands; his father was a wartime forced laborer. Began studying Vietnam veterans with PTSD in the 1970s — contributed to PTSD's DSM-III inclusion. *The Body Keeps the Score* spent 328+ weeks on the NYT bestseller list.
**Used by:** trauma therapists, social workers, organizational practitioners. **Business backing:** employees who have experienced chronic workplace threat don't reset when conditions improve — body-level regulatory interventions (co-regulation, tone, pacing) are operational necessities, not soft extras. *Note: a 2025 BJPsych Bulletin review found insufficient support for some specific mechanistic claims.*

## SCARF & Threat Response

**David Rock** — CEO, NeuroLeadership Institute; author, Your Brain at Work (2009)
*SCARF Model (2008)*
Spent the 1990s as an executive coach increasingly frustrated that coaching models couldn't explain why certain conversations derailed people. Partnered with neuroscientists, coined "NeuroLeadership" in 2007, and synthesized the SCARF framework as a practitioner tool.
**Used by:** HR practitioners, leadership developers, executive coaches worldwide. **Business backing:** gives Andrew a five-domain diagnostic (Status, Certainty, Autonomy, Relatedness, Fairness) for reading which specific social threat is driving defensive behavior. *Practitioner framework, not a primary research model.*

## Self-Determination Theory

**Edward Deci** — Professor Emeritus, University of Rochester (died February 2026, age 83)
*Self-Determination Theory (with Ryan); SOMA cube studies (1971)*
His 1971 dissertation found that paying students to solve puzzles made them *less* likely to work on them voluntarily afterward — the opposite of what everyone assumed. Faced significant resistance. Spent 50 years at Rochester with Ryan building SDT into one of psychology's most-cited frameworks.
**Used by:** organizational psychologists, educators, HR strategists. **Business backing:** the theoretical engine behind RPA's claim that management behavior — not incentive structure alone — drives sustainable performance.

---

**Richard Ryan** — Professor, Australian Catholic University; co-founder, motivationWorks; formerly University of Rochester
*SDT motivation continuum; sub-theories including Cognitive Evaluation Theory and Organismic Integration Theory*
Met Deci at Rochester in 1977 in a conversation both described as life-changing. Spent 40+ years elaborating the motivation continuum — from amotivation through external regulation to integrated motivation — which explains why compliance differs from commitment.
**Used by:** Same as Deci; Ryan continues active research and consulting. **Business backing:** the continuum diagnoses *where* a manager's team sits and *what specifically* keeps them in compliance rather than commitment mode.

---

**Marylène Gagné** — John Curtin Distinguished Professor, Curtin University (Perth); PhD from Rochester under Ryan
*Oxford Handbook of Work Engagement, Motivation, and SDT (editor); Nature Reviews Psychology 2022 workplace SDT synthesis*
Trained directly under Ryan, then dedicated her career to translating SDT from social psychology into organizational settings — knowledge workers, volunteers, cross-cultural contexts.
**Used by:** HR researchers, management consultants. **Business backing:** maps SDT to hospitality specifically — service work is relational and complex; controlled motivation (surveillance, bonus-only) systematically undermines it.

---

**Anja Van den Broeck** — Full Professor, KU Leuven (Belgium)
*Meta-analysis: 99 studies, N > 36,000 (2016, Organizational Psychology Review)*
Built her career on large-scale SDT synthesis. Her key finding: need *thwarting* (active blocking) is distinctly harmful beyond just the absence of satisfaction — a distinction that changes intervention design.
**Used by:** SDT researchers and HR strategists. **Business backing:** the large-sample organizational proof Andrew cites when owners ask "how do you know this?"

---

**Gavin Slemp** — Associate Professor, Centre for Wellbeing Science, University of Melbourne
*SDT meta-analyses on supportive leadership behaviors and engagement*
Concentrated on producing synthesis research — pulling together SDT findings to confirm that need-supportive leadership behaviors produce robust, cross-cultural outcomes. Particularly useful for framing *manager behavior* as the proximal cause of need satisfaction or frustration.
**Used by:** organizational researchers, leadership developers. **Business backing:** supports RPA's claim that the manager — not the organization's structure — is the first-order lever.

## Psychological Safety

**Amy Edmondson** — Novartis Professor of Leadership and Management, Harvard Business School; BS Engineering/Design + MA Psychology + PhD Organizational Behavior, all Harvard
*"Psychological Safety and Learning Behavior in Work Teams" (Administrative Science Quarterly, 1999)*
As a doctoral student, she expected better nursing teams to make fewer errors. Her data came back flipped — better teams reported *more* errors. She followed the finding instead of discarding it: better teams reported more because they felt safe enough to. Google's Project Aristotle (2012–2015) independently confirmed psychological safety as the top predictor of team performance across 180 teams.
**Used by:** every major consulting firm, Google, healthcare systems, military. **Business backing:** the primary academic authority behind RPA's psychological safety construct — manager behavior either enables or shuts down organizational learning loops.

---

**M. Lance Frazier** — Organizational psychology researcher, Old Dominion University
*Psychological safety meta-analysis, Personnel Psychology (2017); 136 samples, 22,000+ individuals*
Synthesized the psychological safety literature quantitatively. Confirmed consistent relationships with learning behavior, performance, creativity, and voice — and identified the primary antecedents: supportive leadership, coaching, openness, trust.
**Used by:** researchers and practitioners needing quantitative proof of concept. **Business backing:** psychological safety is an organizationally tractable variable — it can be built, and the levers are leadership behaviors.

---

**Ingrid Nembhard** — Professor, Wharton School (Health Care Management)
*"Making It Safe: The Effects of Leader Inclusiveness and Professional Status on Psychological Safety" (Journal of Organizational Behavior, 2006 — won Best Paper in Positive Organizational Scholarship)*
Studied why interprofessional healthcare teams (doctors, nurses, pharmacists) failed to surface problems across status lines. Identified that specific leader behaviors — *leader inclusiveness* (inviting and appreciating others' contributions) — could overcome status barriers.
**Used by:** healthcare organizations, high-status-differential teams. **Business backing:** translates directly to hospitality — executive chef to line cook to housekeeping. Leader inclusiveness is a teachable behavior set Andrew operationalizes.

---

**Abraham Carmeli** — Professor of Strategy and Management, Tel Aviv University
*Relational quality → psychological safety → learning from failures; multiple studies 2000s–2020s*
Built a body of work showing that high-quality interpersonal relationships inside organizations create the psychological safety that enables learning, creativity, and performance — a causal chain: inclusive leadership → psychological safety → employee creative involvement.
**Used by:** strategy and OB researchers. **Business backing:** relational quality at the management level is a strategic lever for organizational learning capacity, not HR "culture work."

## Implicit Beliefs & Double-Loop Learning

**Chris Argyris** — James Bryant Conant Professor Emeritus, Harvard (Education and Organizational Behavior); died 2013, age 90
*Theory-in-Use; Double-Loop Learning; Left-Hand Column technique; co-author with Schön*
WWII Army Signal Corps vet, studied under Kurt Lewin. Observed that organizations hire capable people then systematically prevent their learning through *defensive routines* — behaviors designed to avoid embarrassment that simultaneously prevent problem-solving. Worked with Ford, IBM, State Department, dozens of executive teams over 60+ years documenting this with devastating precision.
**Used by:** OD consultants, executive coaches, organizational learning researchers. **Business backing:** central to RPA's diagnosis — owners and managers create problems through governing *assumptions*, not bad intentions. Training that doesn't reach the assumption level doesn't produce durable change.

---

**Donald Schön** — Professor, MIT Urban Studies and Planning; died 1997
*The Reflective Practitioner (1983); co-author with Argyris on Organizational Learning*
Sociologist of knowledge who partnered with Argyris at MIT. His independent question: how do expert professionals actually think in practice versus how they say they do? Answer: professional mastery is artistry — *reflection-in-action* (thinking while doing), not just technique application.
**Used by:** professional education, consulting, organizational learning. **Business backing:** justifies RPA's observe-and-debrief methodology as professional development — and explains why one-time workshops don't produce lasting change.

---

**Robert Kegan** — William and Miriam Meehan Professor Emeritus, Harvard Graduate School of Education; co-founder, Minds at Work
*The Evolving Self (1982); Immunity to Change (2009, with Lahey)*
Spent his career at Harvard asking: how does the structure of adult meaning-making change over time? Built a five-stage developmental model. His key insight for organizations: most change efforts fail because they require self-authoring capacity from people still operating from a socialized mind.
**Used by:** executive coaches, leadership developers, organizational change practitioners. **Business backing:** explains why owners and managers who intellectually agree with a change don't implement it — the problem is developmental, not motivational.

---

**Lisa Lahey** — Faculty, Harvard Graduate School of Education; founder, Minds at Work
*Immunity to Change (2009, with Kegan); How the Way We Talk Can Change the Way We Work (2001)*
Brought the practitioner's lens to Kegan's theory. Where Kegan described the developmental architecture, Lahey built the diagnostic system — specifically the four-column ITC map that surfaces hidden competing commitments and the big assumptions underneath them.
**Used by:** executive coaches, organizational development. **Business backing:** the ITC process is a direct RPA tool for owner-level work — surfacing and testing the big assumptions that block the owner's own improvement goals.

---

**Peter Gollwitzer** — Professor of Social Psychology, NYU; Professor Emeritus, University of Konstanz; 2019 Wilhelm Wundt-William James Award
*Implementation Intentions (meta-analysis, Psychological Bulletin, 1999)*
Spent his career studying the gap between intention and action. His research: "if-then" plans that specify *when, where, and how* to act delegate behavioral initiation to situational cues — making goal-directed behavior automatic when the cue arrives. Dramatically increases follow-through rates.
**Used by:** behavior change practitioners, health psychologists, coaches. **Business backing:** rationale for why RPA coaching builds specific action plans tied to contextual cues (shift start, pre-service meeting) rather than relying on general intention.

---

**Anthony Greenwald** — Professor of Psychology, University of Washington; 2024 BBVA Frontiers of Knowledge Award
**Mahzarin Banaji** — Richard Clarke Cabot Professor of Social Ethics, Harvard; born India; PhD from Ohio State under Greenwald
*Implicit Association Test (IAT) — launched online 1998; "Implicit Social Cognition" (1995 Psychological Review)*
Greenwald was studying attitude measurement gaps; Banaji was his graduate student. Together they developed a computerized reaction-time measure of automatic associations — the IAT — which has been taken 14+ million times. It revealed that implicit attitudes toward race, gender, and age diverge significantly from stated beliefs.
**Used by:** diversity researchers, social psychologists, organizational practitioners. **Business backing:** grounds RPA's "not bad people" frame — manager behavior is partly driven by automatic associations, not conscious intention. *Note: IAT is better for population-level phenomena than individual behavioral prediction.*

## Somatic / Body-Based

**Peter Levine** — Founder, Somatic Experiencing International; PhD Biophysics (UC Berkeley) + PhD Psychology
*Waking the Tiger (1997); Somatic Experiencing (SE)*
Developed SE over 45 years from a single question that came to him in a clinical session: why don't wild animals develop chronic trauma symptoms despite regular predator encounters? Tracked the answer through ethology, stress physiology, and indigenous healing traditions. The body mobilizes for survival; if that discharge is incomplete (freeze response), the activated energy stays lodged.
**Used by:** trauma therapists, somatic practitioners. **Business backing:** contributes to the body-based regulatory framework underlying RPA's co-regulation rationale, especially for workplaces with histories of chronic interpersonal threat.

---

**Pat Ogden** — Founder, Sensorimotor Psychotherapy Institute; co-founder, Hakomi Institute; 45+ years clinical experience
*Trauma and the Body (2006); Sensorimotor Psychotherapy*
In the early 1970s, worked as a yoga/dance teacher in a psychiatric hospital, observing that patients' disconnection from their bodies tracked closely with psychological difficulty — long before PTSD was in the DSM. Co-founded the Hakomi Institute in 1981, SPI the same year.
**Used by:** trauma therapists, somatic clinicians. **Business backing:** reinforces the rationale that regulatory approaches must include body-level awareness — relevant to training facilitators in co-regulation rather than cognitive reframing alone.

---

**Peter Fonagy** — Professor, University College London; Chief Executive, Anna Freud Centre; CBE 2024; born Hungary, emigrated alone to England at age 14
*Mentalization-Based Treatment (MBT); Reflective Functioning Scale (RFS); "Earned Secure Attachment"*
His own adolescent struggle — arriving in England delinquent and failing, recovered through education and mentorship — shaped his research question: what allows a person with an adverse attachment history to achieve psychological security? Answer: *mentalization* — the capacity to understand behavior as driven by mental states — grown through relationships with reflective others.
**Used by:** attachment researchers, clinical psychologists, developmental scientists. **Business backing:** supports RPA's change-is-possible premise — earned security is real, reflective capacity can grow, and the consulting relationship is itself a change mechanism.

---

**Mary Main** — Professor Emerita of Psychology, UC Berkeley; Mary Ainsworth's graduate student; died January 2023
*Adult Attachment Interview (AAI); Disorganized Attachment classification (1986)*
Trained directly under Ainsworth, then moved to Berkeley. Watching attachment videos for years, she identified a fourth pattern — *disorganized attachment* — in infants whose caregivers were simultaneously frightening and needed. Then built the AAI: an interview that classifies adult attachment not on what was experienced but on how coherently it is *narrated*.
**Used by:** attachment researchers, clinical psychologists. **Business backing:** narrative coherence (not childhood content) predicts security — meaning reflective capacity, not history, determines relational functioning. Supports the change narrative Andrew brings to owners.

---

**Eugene Gendlin** — Professor Emeritus of Philosophy, University of Chicago; born Vienna 1926, fled Nazi Europe as a child; died 2017
*Focusing (1978); The "Felt Sense"*
Worked alongside Carl Rogers in the 1950s–60s researching what distinguished clients who made lasting change. The answer wasn't the therapist's technique — it was whether the client could access a pre-verbal, bodily sense of the problem. He named it the "felt sense" and built Focusing as a teachable method to access it.
**Used by:** therapists, body-based practitioners, reflective practice educators. **Business backing:** the capacity to sense — before articulating — what is happening in a difficult situation is a foundation of reflective leadership Andrew builds toward.

## Leadership Behavior & Power

**Dacher Keltner** — Professor of Psychology, UC Berkeley; founding director, Greater Good Science Center
*The Power Paradox (2016); multiple lab studies on power and empathy erosion (1990s–present)*
Started his career studying the evolutionary origins of compassion, asking whether cooperation (not dominance) was humanity's primary social strategy. Then discovered the paradox: people gain power through prosocial traits, then lose those traits once power is held. His lab documented this behaviorally — powerful subjects cheated at dice 3x as often, took food from others, interrupted more, missed social cues.
**Used by:** leadership researchers, executive coaches, organizational consultants. **Business backing:** scientific foundation for Andrew's claim that manager empathy erosion is a function of role, not character — structural and treatable, not personal failing.

---

**Paul Ekman** — Professor Emeritus, UCSF; ranked 59th most eminent psychologist of the 20th century; died November 2025
*Facial Action Coding System (FACS, 1978, with Friesen); Micro-expression research*
Did fieldwork with the Fore people of Papua New Guinea in the late 1960s — an isolated group who recognized American emotional expressions without ever having seen Western media. This proved emotional expressions are universal and innate, not cultural. Later, reviewing psychotherapy films in slow motion, discovered *micro-expressions* — facial expressions lasting 1/25–1/5 of a second that reveal concealed emotions.
**Used by:** law enforcement, clinicians, deception researchers. **Business backing:** contempt — a specific FACS-coded expression — is measurable in management interactions and predictive of the relational damage Gottman's research documents.

---

**John Gottman** — Professor Emeritus of Psychology, University of Washington; co-founder, Gottman Institute; PhD Wisconsin-Madison
*The Four Horsemen of Relationship Breakdown; 40 years of "Love Lab" research on 3,000+ couples; divorce prediction accuracy 93.6%*
Observed couples in a laboratory apartment while physiologically monitoring them. After 40 years, could predict divorce within 6 years with 93.6% accuracy using behavioral coding alone. The single most lethal predictor: contempt — disgust + anger, communicating "I am above you" — which suppresses the recipient's immune system and predicts relational dissolution.
**Used by:** couples therapists, relationship researchers, organizational practitioners. **Business backing:** contemptuous management micro-behaviors (eye rolls, dismissiveness, condescension) are measurable, physiologically harmful, and predictive of disengagement and turnover — Andrew's primary behavioral diagnostic at the interaction level.

---

**Jim Detert** — John L. Colley Professor, Darden School of Business, University of Virginia; formerly Cornell
*"Why Employees Are Afraid to Speak" (HBR, 2007); "Implicit Voice Theories" (AMJ, 2011 — Best Article award; co-authored with Edmondson)*
Started from frustration: organizations consistently waste employee knowledge — the line worker who knows the machine is failing stays silent; the nurse who sees the risk says nothing. With Edmondson, identified that this silence is strategic calculation based on automatic belief patterns, not apathy.
**Used by:** organizational behavior researchers, HR practitioners. **Business backing:** asking employees to "just speak up" is ineffective — the suppression is automatic. RPA's intervention must change the conditions that trigger the implicit calculation.

---

**Brené Brown** — Huffington Foundation Endowed Chair, University of Houston; Visiting Professor, UT Austin
*Daring Greatly (2012); Dare to Lead (2018)*
Spent 20 years studying shame and vulnerability in a social work research context. In 2010, a TEDx talk went viral (60M+ views, top-5 TED of all time). She didn't change her work — she exposed it honestly. *Dare to Lead* was built from two years of interviews with 150+ global executives examining what prevents courageous leadership.
**Used by:** leadership developers, executives, HR practitioners worldwide. **Business backing:** "armored leadership" — self-protective behaviors (perfectionism, cynicism, aggression) adopted in response to vulnerability — is the field-tested language for what RPA is treating. *Qualitative research; strength is construct richness, not effect sizes.*

---

**John Bargh** — Professor of Psychology, Yale; director, ACME Lab; Guggenheim Fellowship; ~200 research papers
*Automaticity research; "The Unbearable Automaticity of Being" (American Psychologist, 1999)*
Trained under Robert Zajonc at Michigan, who studied affect and cognition at the most basic behavioral levels. Bargh extended this into a career-long investigation of how much behavior people assume is consciously chosen is actually triggered by environmental cues with no conscious intent.
**Used by:** social psychologists, behavior change practitioners. **Business backing:** management behavior in high-stakes service moments is largely automatic — shaped by habit scripts and unconscious priming — not deliberate choice. Deliberate intervention (reflection, implementation intentions, coaching) is required to interrupt it. *Note: some specific priming effects haven't replicated; the general automaticity principle is robust.*

---

**Christine Porath** — Tenured Professor, Georgetown McDonough School of Business; PhD UNC Chapel Hill
*Mastering Civility (2016); 20+ years polling tens of thousands of workers on incivility*
Her first job out of Holy Cross was her dream job launching a sports academy — the workplace was rife with rudeness. She quit, got her PhD, and spent 20 years measuring the cost. Her father was hospitalized from stress caused by a decade under two toxic bosses. That anchored her research question: what is the measurable cost of disrespect?
**Used by:** HR departments, management trainers, organizational consultants. **Business backing:** incivility — even subtle, often unintentional rudeness — measurably impairs cognitive performance (~30%), suppresses immune function, and drives turnover. Provides Andrew's cost data for why management tone matters operationally.

## Organizational Culture & Identity

**Edgar Schein** — Professor Emeritus, MIT Sloan (joined 1956); died January 2023; PhD Social Psychology, Harvard
*Organizational Culture and Leadership (1985); Process Consultation (1969); Humble Inquiry (2013)*
Widely recognized as the father of organizational culture. His early research on organizational socialization drew partly on observations of thought reform in Chinese Communist prisons. His final major concept — *Humble Inquiry* — came from 67 years of watching a "culture of telling" destroy organizational learning conditions.
**Used by:** OD consultants, culture-change practitioners, leadership developers worldwide. **Business backing:** Schein's three-level culture model (artifacts → espoused values → basic underlying assumptions) is Andrew's primary diagnostic map for why stated values and actual behavior diverge — and why surface training fails.

---

**Blake Ashforth** — Horace Steele Arizona Heritage Chair, W. P. Carey School, ASU; PhD University of Toronto
**Fred Mael** — Ashforth's collaborator; career moved into applied/government consulting
*"Social Identity Theory and the Organization" (Academy of Management Review, 1989) — 5,700+ citations*
Ashforth built his career on how organizations and employees mutually shape each other — how organizational membership satisfies identity needs and drives behavior beyond contractual compliance. The 1989 Ashforth-Mael paper applied social identity theory to organizations systematically for the first time.
**Used by:** OB researchers, HR strategists, culture practitioners. **Business backing:** culture is not just "how we do things here" — it is a system through which identity is conferred. Disrespect or exclusion is not just a morale issue; it is an identity violation with proportionate behavioral consequences.

---

**Stuart Albert & David Whetten** — Albert: Professor Emeritus, Carlson School, University of Minnesota; Whetten: Professor, BYU Marriott School
*"Organizational Identity" (Research in Organizational Behavior, 1985) — launched the field*
A single 1985 conceptual collaboration that proposed organizational identity as a formal construct with three defining criteria: what is *central*, *distinctive*, and *enduring* about an organization. Whetten later published "Albert and Whetten Revisited" (2006) clarifying and defending the framework. Albert subsequently moved into research on timing; the paper was their defining joint contribution.
**Used by:** strategy researchers, OD practitioners. **Business backing:** the CDE framework is Andrew's definitional tool when helping owners articulate what their operation actually stands for — the identity questions that must be answered before strategic alignment is possible.

---

**Arlie Hochschild** — Professor Emerita of Sociology, UC Berkeley
*The Managed Heart (1983) — won Charles Cooley Award, American Sociological Association*
Conducted fieldwork with Delta Air Lines flight attendants and bill collectors — observing that these workers were required not just to perform tasks but to actively manage and display emotions as part of their labor. Named this *emotional labor* and distinguished *surface acting* (faking it) from *deep acting* (genuinely inducing the required feeling).
**Used by:** service industry researchers, HR practitioners, burnout researchers. **Business backing:** foundational construct for understanding hospitality work as fundamentally emotional — burnout in service workers follows from this recognition. The entire RPA practice domain is built on it.

---

**Salih Kusluvan** — Professor, Faculty of Tourism, Istanbul Medeniyet University
*Managing Employee Attitudes and Behaviors in the Tourism and Hospitality Industry (edited volume, 2003)*
Built a research program examining the structural HR challenges unique to hospitality — high turnover, emotional labor, seasonal work, status issues, the gap between career expectations and reality. His 2003 edited volume became a primary industry reference.
**Used by:** hospitality management researchers, industry HR strategists. **Business backing:** provides industry-specific grounding that justifies RPA's hospitality specialization — not generic OB applied to hotels, but research *from* hotel contexts.

---

**Benjamin Schneider** — Professor Emeritus, University of Maryland; **David Bowen** — Herberger Chair, Thunderbird School of Global Management
*Attraction-Selection-Attrition (ASA) Model (Schneider, 1987); Service Climate research (Bowen & Schneider)*
Schneider's foundational insight: "The people make the place." Organizations become homogeneous over time as they attract, select, and retain people who fit — while those who don't fit leave. Bowen and Schneider extended this to *service climate* — the shared employee perception of what the organization actually values in service delivery.
**Used by:** HR strategists, service industry researchers, organizational consultants. **Business backing:** you can't mandate a service culture — you have to build the climate conditions (HR practices, leadership behavior, reward systems) that make service behavior the natural output of daily work.

---

**John Van Maanen** — Erwin Schell Professor of Organization Studies, MIT Sloan; one of the founders of ethnographic organizational research
*"Toward a Theory of Organizational Socialization" (with Schein, 1979); "People Processing" (1978)*
An organizational anthropologist who spent years doing fieldwork with police departments and Disneyland employees. Systematized the socialization tactics organizations use (intentionally or not) to shape how newcomers understand their role and identity.
**Used by:** OB researchers, HR practitioners designing onboarding. **Business backing:** onboarding is not administrative — it is the most critical window for transmitting organizational climate. Poorly designed socialization produces role confusion, misalignment, and early turnover.

---

**Daniel Cable** — Professor of Organizational Behaviour, London Business School; PhD Cornell; 41,000+ citing researchers
*Person-organization fit research; "Breaking Them In or Eliciting Their Best?" (Administrative Science Quarterly, 2013, with Gino & Staats)*
Began studying P-O fit, then asked the deeper question: do organizations socialize newcomers in ways that suppress or elicit their authentic selves? His 2013 study found onboarding that invites newcomers to express their best selves produces higher retention and satisfaction than conformity-based onboarding.
**Used by:** HR practitioners, onboarding designers. **Business backing:** directly applicable to hospitality — the first weeks set the motivational trajectory. Onboarding redesign is an early, high-leverage RPA recommendation.

---

**J. Bruce Tracey & Timothy Hinkin** — Both faculty, Cornell Nolan School of Hotel Administration; career-long hospitality researchers
*Transformational leadership in hotels (1994, 1996); Turnover Cost Evaluator methodology; Cornell Hospitality Quarterly*
Decades of producing applied research specifically in hospitality management contexts — leadership behavior, training effectiveness, and turnover cost calculation. Their turnover cost methodology produces calculations far higher than operators typically assume.
**Used by:** hospitality operators, hotel HR departments, Cornell-adjacent consultants. **Business backing:** hospitality-specific citations for leadership behavior and turnover cost — not extrapolated from general management research, but from hotel settings.

## Consulting & Contracting

**Peter Block** — Author, OD consultant; began career at Esso 1963; BS University of Kansas, MS Yale; OD Network 2008 Lifetime Achievement Award
*Flawless Consulting (1981, now 4th edition); The Answer to How Is Yes (2002)*
Co-founded Block Petrella Weisbord consulting in the early 1970s. *Flawless Consulting* came from 40 years of watching consultants fail — not from bad advice but from poor contracting: unclear roles, too much accommodation, insufficient honesty about risk. The book codified what excellent consulting contracting actually looks like.
**Used by:** OD consultants, internal HR business partners, external advisors. **Business backing:** Block's contracting model is directly operationalizable in Andrew's practice — especially the entry conversation, scope negotiation, and explicit agreement on what Andrew will and will not do.

---

**David Maister** — Retired; HBS faculty 1976–1985; then 24 years consulting professional service firms; DBA Harvard; born London 1947
*The Trusted Advisor (2000, with Green & Galford); Managing the Professional Service Firm (1993)*
Left academia in 1985 to consult directly with law firms, consulting firms, and accounting firms on strategy and client relationships. The Trust Equation emerged from that practice work — a formula for understanding what actually builds trust in professional relationships.
**Used by:** professional service firms, consultants, advisors of all kinds. **Business backing:** the Trust Equation (Credibility + Reliability + Intimacy / Self-Orientation) is Andrew's self-assessment tool and his frame for explaining why owners' current advisors, despite high credibility, may have low impact.

## Change Science & Coaching

**James Prochaska** — Professor Emeritus, University of Rhode Island; died July 2023; PI on $80M+ in research grants; PhD Wayne State
*Transtheoretical Model (TTM); Stages of Change (developed late 1970s)*
His breakthrough came from comparison — not from a clinical theory. He systematically studied 18 leading psychotherapy systems to find what they shared. The Stages of Change model emerged from studying smokers who quit without formal treatment: they moved through a predictable internal sequence that mismatch with the intervention was causing most change failures.
**Used by:** health behavior researchers, addiction counselors, coaches, organizational change consultants. **Business backing:** before designing any management intervention, diagnose which stage the owner/manager is actually in. Action-stage techniques applied to someone in contemplation produce resistance, not change.

---

**William Miller** — Emeritus Distinguished Professor of Psychology and Psychiatry, University of New Mexico; 600+ articles, 50 books; PhD Oregon
*Motivational Interviewing (first articulated 1982; book with Rollnick, 1991)*
Observed in the mid-1970s that counselors expressing accurate empathy produced better addiction outcomes than those with better protocols. On sabbatical in Bergen, Norway in 1982, Norwegian psychologists kept stopping him mid-session to ask *why* he was responding the way he was — forcing him to articulate an implicit model he hadn't consciously named. He wrote it down. MI was born.
**Used by:** addiction counselors, healthcare providers, coaches, social workers worldwide. **Business backing:** MI is the conversational methodology underlying Andrew's consulting and coaching conversations — especially with ambivalent owners or resistant managers.

---

**Stephen Rollnick** — Honorary Distinguished Professor, Cardiff University School of Medicine; grew up in Cape Town; retired from academic career
*Motivational Interviewing (co-developer with Miller)*
Worked 16 years as an NHS psychologist before becoming a teacher and researcher. His fellowship to Sydney placed his office next to Miller's by coincidence. Rollnick's specific contribution: adapting MI to healthcare settings where time is constrained — demonstrating it works in short, bounded professional interactions, not just extended therapy.

## Burnout & JD-R

**Christina Maslach** — Professor Emerita of Psychology, UC Berkeley; AB Harvard-Radcliffe, PhD Stanford; instrumental in stopping the Stanford Prison Experiment
*Maslach Burnout Inventory (MBI); burnout as a workplace phenomenon (late 1970s–present); WHO ICD-11 recognition 2019*
Began studying burnout in the late 1970s when the concept wasn't recognized as legitimate — conducting fieldwork with doctors, nurses, social workers, teachers, police. Developed the MBI to measure what she observed: emotional exhaustion, depersonalization, and reduced personal accomplishment. The WHO formally recognized burnout in ICD-11 in 2019 citing her work.
**Used by:** occupational health researchers, HR departments, public health systems worldwide. **Business backing:** hospitality has structurally high burnout risk. Maslach's six-mismatch framework (workload, control, reward, community, fairness, values) gives Andrew a diagnostic that is both research-grounded and immediately legible to operators.

---

**Arnold Bakker** — Professor, Erasmus University Rotterdam; past president, EAWOP; among the world's most-cited organizational psychologists
**Evangelia Demerouti** — Professor, Eindhoven University of Technology; PhD Oldenburg
*Job Demands-Resources (JD-R) Model (Journal of Applied Psychology, 2001) — ~4,000 citations*
Met at a small Utrecht research group in 1997 when Demerouti presented preliminary burnout prediction work. Their collaboration produced JD-R: all work characteristics are either *demands* (requiring effort with costs) or *resources* (enabling goals, reducing demands, stimulating growth). Two parallel processes: high demands → energy depletion → burnout; high resources → motivation → engagement.
**Used by:** HR departments, occupational health researchers, organizational consultants worldwide. **Business backing:** universal model for analyzing hospitality job design — identifying which demands are excessive and which resources (supervisor support, autonomy, feedback, development) are absent, and targeting intervention accordingly.

---

**Wilmar Schaufeli** — Professor Emeritus, Utrecht University; Distinguished Research Professor, Leuven; PhD Groningen; 300+ articles
*Work Engagement as positive antipode to burnout; Utrecht Work Engagement Scale (UWES)*
Began as a burnout researcher — collaborated with Maslach on early burnout scale work. Over time, shifted toward the positive: what does engagement look like and how is it built? Established with Bakker that work engagement (vigor, dedication, absorption) is qualitatively different from "not burned out" — and that resources are its primary driver.
**Used by:** occupational health researchers, HR practitioners. **Business backing:** gives Andrew the positive target state — not just "reduce burnout" but "build engagement" — and the resource-focused pathway to get there.

## Error Management

**Michael Frese** — Distinguished Professor, Asia School of Business; Professor Emeritus, Leuphana University; ranked #1 management researcher in Germany and Malaysia (2023)
*Error Management Culture (EMC) research; Personal Initiative; Entrepreneurship*

## Shame & Guilt

**June Price Tangney** — Professor of Psychology, George Mason University; PhD UCLA; Distinguished Lifetime Career Award, International Society for Self and Identity
*Shame and Guilt (with Ronda Dearing, 2002); decades of empirical moral emotion research*
Dedicated her career to systematically distinguishing shame from guilt — two constructs common language conflates. Research funded by NICHD and the John Templeton Foundation.
Key finding: shame ("I am bad") produces desire to hide or escape, associates with anger and aggression, and paralyzes behavior change. Guilt ("I did something bad") evaluates a specific behavior, produces desire to make amends, and is associated with more constructive outcomes.
**Used by:** clinical psychologists, organizational coaches, HR practitioners. **Business backing:** shame-inducing management feedback (common in high-accountability service cultures) produces the opposite of desired change. Shifting toward behavior-specific feedback is a concrete, evidence-based target.

## Training Transfer

## Feedback

**Avraham Kluger** — Hebrew University of Jerusalem, School of Business Administration
**Angelo DeNisi** — Management scholar, Tulane University
*"Effects of Feedback Interventions on Performance" — meta-analysis of 607 effect sizes from 23,663 observations (Psychological Bulletin, 1996)*
Undertook the most comprehensive review of the feedback literature ever attempted. What they found contradicted the foundational HR assumption: more than one-third of feedback interventions *decreased* performance.
Key finding: what determines feedback's effect is where it redirects attention. Task-level feedback → performance improves. Self-level feedback (ego or identity focus) → performance typically declines.
**Used by:** HR and performance management practitioners, I/O psychologists. **Business backing:** challenges the assumption that more performance review automatically helps — and explains why poorly designed feedback processes in hotels systematically undermine the performance they're meant to improve.
