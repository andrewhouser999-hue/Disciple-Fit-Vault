# Relational Performance Advisory — Delivery Infrastructure

## Service Definition

Relational Performance Advisory is an organizational consulting engagement that identifies how personnel relational patterns under pressure drive measurable business outcomes — retention, service quality, leadership effectiveness, and culture stability.

The diagnostic premise: performance problems in hospitality organizations are frequently relational in origin. People leave managers, not companies. Service breaks down at the point of relational stress. Culture instability traces to leadership patterns that activate and disorganize rather than regulate and stabilize.

Standard HR approaches reach this at the explicit level — surveys, self-assessments, exit interviews — and consistently miss the behavioral layer where the actual patterns operate. Relational Performance Advisory is designed to reach that layer.

**What this is not:**
- Clinical assessment or psychological diagnosis
- Employee wellness or EAP programming
- Generic management training
- Covenant Identity Coaching (a separate practice; its truths may inform this work but the practices are distinct)

---

## Engagement Architecture

Three sequential layers:

```
Layer 1: OBSERVATION
Narrative Interview + Multi-Rater Behavioral Assessment
↓
Layer 2: INTERPRETATION
Behavioral Pattern Profile — four dimensions, behavioral language, no clinical labels
↓
Layer 3: INTERVENTION
Psychoeducation → Retrospective Review → Anticipatory Coaching → Facilitated Observation
Individual leader development + Team facilitation + Structural practice recommendations
```

No single method is sufficient. Facilitated observation is an intervention-phase tool — it becomes productive only after leaders have language and self-awareness to debrief against what they experienced.

## Layer 1: Observation Protocols

### Narrative Interview Protocol

A structured one-on-one conversation with key leaders (GMs, OMs, department heads) about significant relational events in their work history. Not a performance review. Not a personality assessment.

**What is being tracked:**
- Narrative coherence: does the account hold together, or does it collapse into vagueness, blame externalization, or emotional flooding?
- Affect regulation: does the person remain regulated while recounting high-stakes relational events?
- Self-other balance: does the account integrate both self and others as agents, or does it collapse in one direction?

**Sample domains:**
- "Tell me about a time a team member's performance created a serious problem. Walk me through what happened."
- "Describe a situation where you had to deliver feedback that didn't land well."
- "Tell me about a working relationship with a supervisor that was difficult. What was going on?"

The diagnostic value is in *how* the person speaks about these events — not the content of the events themselves.

**Logistics:** 45–60 minutes per leader. Conducted before any other assessment method to avoid priming.

---

### Multi-Rater Behavioral Assessment

A structured 360 built around observable relational behaviors under pressure — not personality traits or generic leadership competencies. Raters: supervisor, peers, direct reports (minimum 3 raters per category).

 "360 reviews" is too broad a term. What the framework specifies is directional upward feedback — employees evaluating manager behaviors — combined with a required discussion of those results. A traditional 360 (peers, superiors, subordinates simultaneously) is a different instrument with different dynamics. The measurement tool referenced is Edmondson's validated 7-item psychological safety scale at the team level, not a manager competency 360.

**Behavior clusters:**

| Cluster | Sample Item |
|---|---|
| Accountability delivery | "This person addresses performance problems directly and in a timely way." |
| Regulation under pressure | "This person remains consistent and approachable when things go wrong." |
| Feedback receptivity | "This person listens to concerns without becoming defensive." |
| Relational repair | "When conflict occurs, this person moves toward resolution rather than avoidance." |
| Authority clarity | "This person's expectations and decisions are consistent and predictable." |

**Output:** Convergent behavioral profile per leader. Where self-rating diverges significantly from rater consensus, that gap is itself diagnostic — it indicates limited self-awareness about relational impact.

**Logistics:** Administered after narrative interviews are complete. 15–20 minutes per rater. 4–6 week data collection window.

## Layer 2: Interpretation Framework

### Four Behavioral Dimensions

All pattern description stays in behavioral, observable language. No clinical labels are used with clients or in client-facing deliverables.

| Dimension | What It Looks Like | Organizational Cost |
|---|---|---|
| **Relational withdrawal under pressure** | Goes quiet in conflict; avoids accountability conversations; delegates difficult feedback; exits emotionally or physically under stress | Subordinates receive no feedback; problems fester; high performers leave first |
| **Relational pursuit/escalation** | Over-communicates when anxious; seeks reassurance from supervisors; personalizes disagreement; escalates when not responded to | Team walks on eggshells; decisions stall; OMs become emotional management loads for the owner |
| **Authority disorganization** | Inconsistent with supervisors — cycles between submission and defiance; unclear on role boundaries; performs compliance while resisting accountability | Unpredictable leadership; role confusion downstream; culture instability at the property level |
| **Collaborative capacity** | Regulates under pressure; delivers feedback directly; repairs ruptures without drama; takes accountability without collapsing | Benchmarks the above three; identifies development-ready leaders |

### Pattern Mapping Process

Each leader receives a profile across the four dimensions, drawn from triangulated observation data. The profile identifies:

1. **Primary pattern** — dominant dimension under activation
2. **Secondary pattern** — secondary behavioral tendency
3. **Activation threshold** — what triggers pattern activation (low / moderate / high stress)
4. **Regulatory resources** — what the leader already does well that can be built on

### Deliverable: Behavioral Pattern Profile

One per leader. 2–3 pages. Includes:
- Behavioral pattern summary (four dimensions rated Low / Moderate / High presence)
- Key observations from each assessment method
- Activation profile
- Development implications
- Recommended intervention track

## Layer 3: Intervention Framework

### Individual Leader Development

Coaching engagement targeting the specific pattern dimensions identified in the profile. Structured around behavioral change goals — not psychological insight for its own sake.

**Entry point:** Post-debrief session where the leader reviews their profile and identifies 2–3 behavioral targets.

**Format:** Bi-weekly 60-minute sessions. Duration: 3–6 months depending on pattern severity and leader readiness.

**Four-stage sequence:**

**Stage 1 — Psychoeducation (build language)**
Introduce the behavioral framework in plain language. The goal is not insight — it is vocabulary. A leader who can name what is happening in themselves can begin to track it. Learning that patterns are formed, predictable, and changeable (not character defects) reduces shame and increases engagement. This stage functions like bibliotherapy: language and concepts that clarify what the leader has already been experiencing.

**Stage 2 — Retrospective Review (surface pattern)**
Work through specific past episodes — situations the leader identifies as difficult, unresolved, or out of character. The goal is to map the pattern in their own story. Retrospective review yields richer diagnostic data than observation alone because the leader narrates how they interpreted the event — which reveals blind spots, self-other balance, and activation triggers that real-time observation would miss.

**Stage 3 — Anticipatory Coaching (apply forward)**
Identify upcoming situations — a difficult conversation, a performance issue, a team conflict on the horizon — and work through them in advance. What is likely to activate? What does the pattern predict they will do? What would a different response look like? This stage converts self-awareness into a behavioral experiment before the situation occurs.

**Stage 4 — Facilitated Observation (debrief against experience)**
Once the leader has language and pattern awareness, structured observation becomes productive. A designed session — positioned as a leadership team alignment workshop — creates activation conditions. Relational patterns become visible under genuine tension. After the session, the leader debriefs what was observed against what they experienced. Without the foundation of Stages 1–3, observation is surveillance. With it, observation becomes confirmation and refinement.

**Observation design:**
- Introduce genuine relational tension: a case scenario with no clean answer, requiring negotiation among leaders
- Introduce authority challenge: facilitator challenges assumptions, disagrees with consensus, redirects dominant voices
- Track who withdraws, who escalates, who disorganizes, who regulates and collaborates
- Observation targets: response to peer challenge, response to authority challenge, repair behavior at tension peak

**Logistics:** Half-day session. Observation notes taken in real time; synthesized in post-session debrief with each leader individually.

**Focus areas per pattern:**
- *Withdrawal:* building accountability delivery skills; increasing regulatory capacity during conflict; staying relationally present under stress
- *Escalation:* regulating relational anxiety; building tolerance for non-response; direct communication without pursuit
- *Disorganization:* clarifying role identity; building consistent authority posture; supervisor relationship repair

---

### Team Facilitation Sequence

Structured facilitation addressing collective relational pattern dynamics — not generic team-building.

**Session 1:** Introduce the four behavioral dimensions as a shared language. No individual profiles shared in the group context.

**Session 2:** Apply the framework to a real team challenge — a recent conflict, a retention problem, a service breakdown. Map what happened in behavioral terms.

**Session 3:** Build relational agreements — specific behavioral commitments the team makes to each other, grounded in their identified pattern dynamics.

**Spacing:** Monthly. Each session 2–3 hours.

---

### Structural Practice Recommendations

HR and management practice changes that reduce unnecessary activation and build relational infrastructure. Delivered as a written recommendations report to the owner/sponsor.

**Common recommendation domains:**
- *Feedback cadence and structure:* regularize what is now episodic and high-stakes
- *Conflict escalation protocol:* clear path reduces disorganization at the OM level
- *Onboarding relational design:* how new employees are received shapes early pattern activation
- *OM accountability structure:* how OMs are held accountable models the accountability culture they create downstream
- *Separation and exit practices:* how people leave communicates relational safety to those who remain

**Psychological Safety Essential Practices**
Structural recommendations are anchored to the 11 evidence-based essential practices. Each addresses a mechanism no other practice can reach. Sequencing is causal — prerequisites must hold before practices above them can take effect.

*Prerequisites — must be in place before other practices can hold:*
1. Owner behavior change
2. Manager regulation capacity baseline
3. Elimination of active voice punishment

*Manager behaviors:*
4. Leader inclusiveness (openness, accessibility, availability)
5. EMC inquiry-first error response
6. Task-level, informational feedback delivery
7. Servant leadership orientation

*Structural mechanisms:*
8. Daily pre-shift Community Meeting (3-question check-in)
9. Bi-weekly 1-on-1s as safety infrastructure
10. Supervisory accountability (upward feedback + required discussion)
11. Psychological safety measurement and feedback loop

## Engagement Flow

### Phase 1 — Discovery (Weeks 1–2)
- Owner/sponsor conversation: presenting problem, organizational context, scope agreement
- Identify key leaders for assessment (typically GMs and OMs — 4–8 leaders for Anant)
- Establish data confidentiality agreements

### Phase 2 — Assessment (Weeks 3–8)
- Narrative interviews conducted (weeks 3–5)
- Multi-rater 360 administered and collected (weeks 4–8)

### Phase 3 — Interpretation (Weeks 11–12)
- Behavioral Pattern Profiles built per leader
- Team aggregate pattern map built
- Structural recommendations drafted

### Phase 4 — Debrief (Week 13)
- Individual leader debriefs (confidential; leader receives their own profile)
- Sponsor debrief (aggregate team pattern map + structural recommendations; individual profiles not shared without leader consent)

### Phase 5 — Intervention (Months 4–9)
- Individual leader development coaching (bi-weekly): psychoeducation → retrospective review → anticipatory coaching → facilitated observation
- Facilitated observation session conducted after leaders have established language and self-awareness (typically mid-phase)
- Team facilitation sequence (monthly)
- Structural recommendations implementation support (as needed)

### Phase 6 — Checkpoint (Month 12)
- Abbreviated re-assessment: narrative interview + abbreviated 360
- Pattern movement measured against baseline
- Report to sponsor: behavioral outcomes, recommendations for continued development

## Scope Boundary

**This engagement does not:**
- Provide psychological diagnosis or clinical assessment of any kind
- Address mental health conditions, trauma history, or clinical attachment disorders
- Replace therapy, EAP services, or psychiatric care
- Operate as Covenant Identity Coaching (a separate faith-based practice)

**Referral threshold:** If a leader's pattern profile indicates clinical-level dysregulation — significant dissociation under stress, apparent trauma activation, or substance involvement — the recommendation is referral to a licensed clinician. The engagement continues with remaining participants.

## Client-Facing Language

**Introducing the service:**
> "Most performance problems in hospitality — retention, service inconsistency, management breakdowns — trace back to how people relate under pressure. When things get hard, people fall into predictable patterns. Some withdraw. Some escalate. Some become inconsistent in ways that confuse their teams. Standard training doesn't reach that level. What we do is identify those patterns specifically, so the development work is targeted — not generic."

**Describing the assessment:**
> "We use two methods together: a structured conversation with each leader, and behavioral data from the people around them. The combination gives us a reliable read on what's actually happening — not just what people say about themselves."

**Describing the output:**
> "Each leader gets a behavioral profile — specific patterns, specific impact, specific development targets. You get an aggregate picture of your management team and a set of structural recommendations for how to build systems that support better relational functioning across properties."
