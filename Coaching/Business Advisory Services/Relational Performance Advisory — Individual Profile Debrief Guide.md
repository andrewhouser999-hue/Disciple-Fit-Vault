# Relational Performance Advisory — Individual Profile Debrief Guide

**What this is:** Practitioner guide for delivering the Behavioral Pattern Profile to the leader in a private 1:1 session. This is the highest-threat session in the entire engagement. The leader is receiving behavioral observations about themselves drawn from how other people described them. Status threat is acute throughout. The session also serves as the coaching entry point — it ends with the leader identifying their first behavioral target.

**Session duration:** 75–90 minutes. Do not schedule less than 75 minutes — rushing the debrief produces defensive shutdown.

**Format:** In-person strongly preferred. Video acceptable. Phone is not appropriate — non-verbal regulation tracking is essential throughout.

**Confidentiality reminder before beginning:** No one else has seen or will see this profile without the leader's written consent.

---

## Pre-Session Preparation

*Complete before the session. The debrief requires Andrew to hold the diagnostic picture in memory while managing the leader's real-time regulation — preparation is what makes that possible.*

**Review the completed profile:**
- Identify the one finding most likely to activate Status threat. This is usually the dimension rated Highest presence or a significant self-rating divergence.
- Identify the one regulatory resource that is genuinely strong. You will name this early.
- Identify the leader's most sensitive SCARF domain (from Section 2). This calibrates your pacing and language throughout.
- Identify the Prochaska stage (from Section 3). This determines how much change talk to expect and how to hold ambivalence.

**Prepare the divergence delivery (if flagged clusters exist):**
Do not improvise the divergence conversation. Pre-plan: which cluster, what the numbers are, and the exact question you will ask to open it. See the divergence protocol below.

**Have the leader's copy of the profile printed or on a shared screen.** Do not email it before the session — the leader should receive it for the first time in the room with you present.

---

## Phase 1 — Opening and Framing (10 minutes)

**Settle before delivering the profile.** A few minutes of low-stakes conversation before moving to the document — not as small talk, but to establish regulation. Observe the leader's baseline state before you introduce the high-stakes content.

**Framing statement — deliver before handing over the profile:**
> "What I'm about to share with you is the most useful output of the entire assessment process — and it's also the most personal. Everything in here traces to specific things I observed or that raters described. Nothing is interpretation without evidence behind it.
>
> This is yours. It doesn't go to [owner name]. It doesn't go anywhere without your written permission. The way I'd like to do this is to walk through it together — I'll read sections aloud, and I want you to tell me as we go what lands as accurate and what doesn't. You know yourself better than any instrument does. My job is to give you a mirror, not a verdict."

**Hand over the profile.**

Give the leader 60–90 seconds to look at it before you begin. This is not wasted time — it lets them process the structure, which reduces the surprise of each section as you arrive at it.

---

## Phase 2 — Delivering Section 1: Behavioral Pattern Summary (25–30 minutes)

**Start with the ratings table.** Walk through the four dimensions — not evaluatively, but descriptively:
> "You can see we rated four dimensions. These aren't personality types — they're behavioral patterns that show up under pressure. Everyone has some of each. What matters is the degree and the context."

**Deliver each dimension paragraph by reading it aloud.** After each paragraph, pause and ask:
> "Does that match what you know about yourself?"

This question is the most important facilitation move in the debrief. It invites the leader into the profile rather than delivering it to them. A leader who says "yes, that's me" is building self-awareness in real time. A leader who says "not really" gives you information about self-awareness gaps — and opens a productive conversation.

**When the leader says "yes, that's accurate":**
Reflect and deepen: *"Where do you see it show up most?"* or *"What does it cost you when it activates?"* Don't linger — the goal is engagement, not processing.

**When the leader says "no, that doesn't seem right":**
Do not defend the finding. Stay curious:
> "Tell me what you'd push back on."

Listen fully. Then:
> "Here's what I was tracking when I wrote that — [cite specific observation from NI or 360 data]. Does that change it at all?"

If the leader still disagrees: hold the finding as a hypothesis, not a verdict. Note it. The coaching arc will either confirm or disconfirm it. Never argue a leader into accepting a profile finding — a forced acceptance is not self-awareness.

**The Collaborative Capacity paragraph delivers last among the four.** This is intentional. The leader has been receiving pattern findings for 20+ minutes. Ending Section 1 with genuine strength — specifically described — restores equilibrium before moving into activation and development implications.

---

## Phase 3 — Delivering Section 2: Activation Profile (15 minutes)

**Activation threshold tends to land easier than the pattern paragraphs.** Leaders generally recognize their own threshold accurately. This section often produces the first moment of genuine relief — "you see what I'm actually dealing with."

**Deliver the activation threshold rating with context:**
> "You're at a moderate threshold — which means it takes real pressure to activate the pattern. That's an asset. Routine friction doesn't knock you off. The challenge is that when genuine pressure comes, it comes fast."

Adjust language to match the leader's actual threshold.

**Walk through the SCARF profile.** Name the most active domains and ask for confirmation:
> "Status showed up as your most active domain. When authority or recognition feels threatened, that's when the pattern activates most reliably. Does that track?"

**Regulatory resources — name them specifically and genuinely.** Not as consolation, as fact:
> "One consistent finding across the data: you initiate repair. After a difficult interaction, you go back. That's not common. It's a real asset for the coaching arc."

---

## Phase 4 — Delivering Section 3: Development Implications (15 minutes)

**Lead with the operational cost in language the leader already uses.** Not: "your withdrawal pattern costs you relationally." Instead: "what the data shows is that performance problems are sitting longer than they should before you address them — and by the time they surface, they're harder to fix."

**The development outcome statement is the motivational frame.** Deliver it as possibility, not obligation:
> "Here's what changes when you build this: you catch problems earlier, they resolve faster, and your direct reports don't have to read your mind about what's expected."

**Walk through the three behavioral targets.** These should be specific and observable — the leader should be able to picture them happening.

**Do not move to the coaching entry question until you've confirmed the leader understands what each target means.** Vague agreement with a behavioral target produces no change.

---

## Phase 5 — Divergence Findings (10 minutes, if flagged clusters exist)

*Skip this phase if no clusters met the ≥2.0 divergence threshold. If flagged, deliver after Section 3 — not before. Trust and engagement need to be established before introducing the highest-threat finding.*

**Do not lead with the numbers.** Lead with a question:
> "Before I show you the 360 data directly — what do you think the people around you would say about your [cluster name]? If your direct reports filled out a rating, what number do you think they'd give you?"

Let the leader answer. Then:
> "The actual mean was [X]. What's your reaction to that?"

**If the leader rated themselves significantly higher than raters (positive divergence):**
The gap is not a character finding — it is an information finding. Frame it that way:
> "What this tells us isn't that you're not doing the right things — it's that what you're doing isn't consistently visible to the people around you. That's a different problem, and it's solvable."

**If the leader rated themselves significantly lower (negative divergence):**
> "The data is more favorable than you gave yourself credit for. What do you make of that?"

Explore self-critical patterns without pathologizing.

**Hold this data lightly.** One divergence finding does not override the triangulated profile. Present it as one data point, not a verdict.

---

## Phase 6 — Coaching Entry (10 minutes)

**The transition:**
> "Everything we've been looking at points toward the same place — there are two or three specific behavioral targets where development would have the highest impact. I want to ask you one question before we talk about what that looks like."

**The coaching entry question:**
> "Of these three targets — [name them briefly] — which one do you feel most ready to work on?"

This question does two things: it gives the leader agency (Autonomy protection) and it surfaces Prochaska stage data. A leader in contemplation will hedge. A leader in preparation or action will identify a target readily and start generating ideas.

**If the leader hedges or deflects:**
Return to MI. Don't push toward action readiness that isn't there:
> "What would need to be different for you to feel ready?"

**Close the coaching entry:**
> "Here's what I'd suggest: we take this target and build one specific experiment around it — something you can try in the next two weeks with a specific person in a specific situation. That's how this works. Not insight, action."

---

## Closing and Next Steps (5 minutes)

**Name exactly what happens next and when:**
> "I'll follow up [timeline] to schedule our first coaching session. We'll start with this target. Between now and then, the only thing I'd ask you to do is notice when the pattern activates — don't try to change it yet. Just notice."

**Profile consent:**
> "This profile is yours. If you ever want to share it with [owner name], that's your decision to make. I'll have a consent form if you decide to do that. There's no pressure either way."

**Specific acknowledgment:** Name one thing from the session — not generic, specific.

## Predictable Leader Responses

| Response | What it signals | Facilitation move |
|---|---|---|
| "That's not me at all" | Low self-awareness or genuine inaccuracy | Cite specific observation; if still rejected, hold as hypothesis; do not argue |
| "I already knew all this" | Intellectualization; awareness without change | "Knowing it and changing it are different. What gets in the way of changing it?" |
| "Is this bad?" | Status threat; seeking evaluation | "It's information. Everyone has a primary pattern. The question is whether it's costing you anything." |
| "What do the others look like?" | Curiosity or deflection; either is fine | "I can't share individual profiles. What I can tell you is that everyone has a pattern — yours isn't unusual." |
| Silence | Processing; possible flooding | Slow down. Hold silence. Do not fill it. |
| Tears or visible distress | Activation beyond the session container | Name it gently: "This is landing hard. That makes sense." Pause. Check: "Do you want to keep going, or do you need a minute?" |
| "Can I share this with my spouse / a friend?" | Positive engagement | "It's yours — share it however you want. I'd just ask that it doesn't go to anyone at [company name] without your written consent to me first." |
