# Relational Performance Advisory — Narrative Interview Facilitation Guide

**What this is:** Practitioner facilitation guide for conducting the narrative interview. Covers the full sequence from pre-interview preparation through the immediate post-interview routine — with facilitation moves, probing language, predictable divergences, and regulation management.

**Paired document:** Use this alongside [[Anant Operations — Narrative Interview Diagnostic Checklist]]. This guide tells you how to conduct the interview. The checklist tells you what to observe and how to score it. They run simultaneously — the checklist is the diagnostic lens you hold while working the facilitation moves in this guide.

**When to use which:**
- *Before and during the interview:* this guide
- *During the interview (observation layer):* checklist Sections 1–7 (active, in real time)
- *Immediately after:* checklist Section 8 (synthesis — complete before reviewing notes from other leaders)

**Total interview time:** 45–60 minutes. The sequence below maps to 55 minutes. Compress by shortening probing depth in Domain 2 or 3 if time is short — do not compress the opening or the close.

---

## Pre-Interview Preparation

*Complete before the leader arrives — 10 minutes.*

**Review:**
- The leader's role, property, and tenure. Know these before they sit down — asking basic logistics questions after the interview has started signals lack of preparation and activates Status threat.
- The individual confidentiality agreement. Have it signed before any content begins.
- The checklist. You are not filling it in yet — but reviewing it now loads the diagnostic categories. You want them in the background, not retrieved mid-conversation.

**Set yourself:**
- Your own regulation first. You will be tracking two people's states simultaneously for 55 minutes. If you are activated coming in — distracted, fatigued, preoccupied — that degrades your observational accuracy. Two minutes of deliberate settling before the leader enters is not optional.
- One internal orientation question: *"What am I about to learn about this person?"* Not "What am I about to assess?" Curiosity produces better data than evaluation.

**Physical setup:**
- Notepad visible on the table. Do not wait until the interview starts to produce it.
- No audio recording device present unless written consent has been obtained in advance.
- Private room. Door closed. No glass wall with foot traffic.

---

## Phase 1 — Opening and Consent *(5 minutes)*

The opening is doing two jobs: establishing the confidentiality frame and calibrating the leader's initial regulation level. Do not rush it.

**Seat them. Let there be a moment before you begin.**
Brief discomfort before you speak is better than launching immediately — it signals that this conversation operates at a different pace than a management meeting.

**Deliver the consent statement — slowly, making eye contact:**
> "Before we start, I want to be clear about how this works. What you share with me in this conversation is confidential. It will inform a behavioral profile that I'll share with you privately — not with [owner name] or anyone else on the team. The only thing [owner name] will see is an overall picture of the team, without anything attributed to you individually. Does that work for you?"

Wait for a real response. A quick "yeah sure" is not the same as a considered "yes." If the leader seems uncertain, slow down: "Any questions about how that works before we start?"

**Have them sign the confidentiality agreement before proceeding.**

**Disclose note-taking:**
> "I'm going to take some notes as we talk so I can be accurate in the profile. I'll be capturing specific things you say — not a summary. That okay?"

**Frame the interview:**
> "What I'm going to ask you about today are specific situations — real events, not your general approach or philosophy. I'm more interested in what actually happened than in how you think things should work. There are no right answers. The only thing that's useful to me is what's true for you."

This framing does three things: it signals that rehearsed competency answers won't serve them, it reduces performance pressure, and it invites specificity from the start.

**→ Checklist: begin active observation now. Sections 1–7 are live from this moment.**

*First observation: how does the leader settle after consent? Quickly — or do they remain watchful? What SCARF domains appear active in the first two minutes?*

## Phase 2 — Domain 1: Accountability Delivery *(15 minutes)*

**Primary question:**
> "Tell me about a specific situation where you had to address someone's performance directly — walk me through what happened."

**If they generalize** ("I usually try to...") — redirect immediately:
> "Give me a specific time. One situation."

**If they choose a minor example** — accept it, work it fully, then ask for another:
> "Tell me about one that had more at stake."

**Probing sequence once the story is running:**
- *"What did you actually say to them?"* — pulls for specific language, not recalled intent
- *"What were you thinking before you went into that conversation?"* — pre-activation data
- *"How did they respond?"* — surfaces self-other balance
- *"What happened after?"* — repair or avoidance signal
- *"Is that typical of how those conversations go for you?"* — pattern invitation

**Follow the affect:**
When energy shifts — voice drops, pace quickens, the account becomes vivid — don't move on. Slow down:
> "Say more about that."
Or simply stay quiet. Silence here is a probing move, not a gap to fill.

**→ Checklist: Sections 1 (coherence), 2 (regulation), 3 (self-other balance), 4A/4B (behavioral pattern), 5 (SCARF activation)**

---

## Phase 3 — Domain 2: Feedback That Didn't Land *(12 minutes)*

**Primary question:**
> "Think of a time you gave someone feedback and it didn't land the way you intended. What happened?"

This domain generates feedback receptivity data and self-other balance data simultaneously. Watch for whether the leader locates the failure in the delivery (their side) or in the recipient (other side).

**Probing sequence:**
- *"What did you intend for them to hear?"*
- *"What do you think they actually heard?"*
- *"What did you do next?"*
- *"Do you know how they walked away from that conversation?"*
- *"What would you do differently?"* — only ask this if the leader hasn't offered it; don't ask it as a closing formality

**If the leader says the feedback did land eventually:**
> "Walk me through how it shifted."

**→ Checklist: Sections 1 (coherence), 3 (self-other balance), 4 (behavioral pattern), 6 (change readiness — first signal)**

---

## Phase 4 — Domain 3: Difficult Supervisor Relationship *(12 minutes)*

This is the highest-yield domain for coherence and regulation data. Authority relationship narratives activate pattern most reliably. Pace yourself — the leader may need more time to enter this one.

**Primary question:**
> "Tell me about the most difficult supervisor relationship you've had. What made it difficult?"

**If they struggle to identify one** ("I've been pretty lucky with my managers"):
> "Think about a time when what you needed from a supervisor wasn't what you were getting. What were you needing?"

**Probing sequence:**
- *"How did you handle it?"*
- *"What did you want from them that you weren't getting?"*
- *"How did it resolve — or did it?"*
- *"What do you think they would say about you in that relationship?"*

The last question is the most diagnostically loaded. It requires the leader to take the supervisor's perspective — to represent themselves as the other person saw them. Coherence and self-other balance both become visible here.

**If the leader refuses the perspective-taking question** ("I have no idea what they'd say" or dismisses it quickly) — note it, don't press. The refusal is data.

**→ Checklist: Sections 1 (coherence — highest activation here), 2 (regulation), 3 (self-other balance), 4C (disorganization pattern), 7 (developmental stage)**

## Probing Move Library

Deploy these across all three domains. Do not overuse any single move — variety prevents the interview from feeling like an interrogation.

**Deepening** — when affect or significance appears:
- *"Say more about that."*
- *"What was that like?"*
- *"Stay with that for a second."*

**Specificity** — when the account is too general:
- *"Give me a specific example of that."*
- *"Walk me through that moment."*
- *"What did you actually say?"*

**Self-invitation** — when the leader is absent from their own story:
- *"What were you thinking at that moment?"*
- *"What did you decide?"*
- *"What did you do with that?"*

**Perspective-taking** — to probe self-other balance:
- *"What do you think they were seeing from their side?"*
- *"How do you think they experienced that?"*
- *"What would they say happened?"*

**Pattern invitation** — once a story is complete:
- *"Is that how it typically goes for you in that kind of situation?"*
- *"Do you recognize that pattern in yourself?"*

**Affect acknowledgment** — when emotional weight is visible:
- *"That sounds like it carried some weight."* [pause — do not fill it]
- *"That was clearly significant."* [pause]

These are not therapeutic reflections — they are observational acknowledgments that give the leader permission to go deeper if they have more to say.

## Predictable Divergences

**Leader generalizes instead of telling a story**
Redirect immediately, every time. Don't let a general answer stand — once you accept one, the pattern sets for the interview.
> "Give me a specific situation. One real example."

**Leader gives their management philosophy instead of an event**
Same redirect:
> "I want to hear about a specific time that actually happened."

**Leader shuts down at a high-stakes domain**
Don't push into a closed door. Move to a different domain — return to the loaded one later when trust has deepened:
> "Let me ask you about something different. We can come back to that."

**Leader floods — affect escalates beyond the session's container**
Slow everything down. Lower your own voice. Reduce the pace:
> "Let's take a second. [pause] There's clearly something significant there."
If flooding doesn't resolve within 2–3 minutes: name it and redirect — see scope enforcement in [[Anant Operations — Processes & Procedures]].

**Leader asks what you're writing**
Answer honestly:
> "Specific things you say — phrases and words. It helps me be accurate in the profile rather than working from my memory."
Offer to show them the notepad if they seem genuinely uneasy. Transparency here builds more trust than withholding.

**Leader seeks evaluation — "Did I handle that right?"**
Do not evaluate. Reflect back:
> "What do you think?"
Or redirect to the story:
> "Tell me what happened next."
Evaluation from you collapses their self-authoring and taints the data.

**Leader re-raises the confidentiality question mid-interview**
Re-anchor without impatience — this is a trust signal, not an interruption:
> "Same answer as at the start. This stays with me. [Owner name] sees the team picture, not your individual profile."

**Leader discloses material outside coaching scope**
Use the redirect script from [[Anant Operations — Processes & Procedures]]. Do not explore it. Return to the behavioral domain.

## Regulation Management

**Andrew's regulation is primary.** You cannot track the leader's Window of Tolerance while dysregulated yourself. The pull to fix a flooding leader, to rescue an avoidant one, or to mirror an escalating one is natural — and all three degrade the interview. Notice the pull. Do not act from it.

**The Window of Tolerance as your operating frame:**
- Leader slightly outside their comfort zone = productive. This is where genuine disclosure happens.
- Leader at their window's edge = slow down, use an affect acknowledgment, lower your pace.
- Leader outside their window = stop. Regulate before proceeding. Do not push for diagnostic data from a dysregulated person — it isn't accurate data, and it damages trust.

**Calibrate with your management experience.** A high-activation response to a genuinely high-stakes story is proportional. Disproportionality — affect that exceeds what the content warrants — is the diagnostic signal. Your management background tells you which is which.

## The Close *(5 minutes)*

**The final question — ask this before reorienting to logistics:**
> "Is there anything about how you work with people — or what drives you as a manager — that you think is important for me to understand, that we haven't covered?"

Wait. Do not fill the silence. This question often produces the most significant disclosure of the interview — either a moment of genuine self-awareness that confirms or complicates what you observed, or a notable absence of it.

**Reorientation — after the final question:**
> "Here's what happens next. I'll put together a behavioral profile based on this conversation and two other assessment processes. I'll share that with you privately — before anyone else sees anything. The timeline is [X weeks]. Any questions about the process?"

**Specific acknowledgment:**
Name something concrete they said — not a generic "thank you for your time." 
> "I appreciate you walking me through the [situation]. That gave me a clear picture."

This is not flattery. It is a specific acknowledgment that signals you were actually listening, which matters for the next phase of the engagement.

**→ Checklist: complete Section 6 (change readiness) and Section 7 (developmental stage) while reorienting to logistics — these are your last live observations.**

## Immediate Post-Interview Routine *(within 30 minutes)*

**Do these in order before reviewing other interviews or any other material:**

1. **Complete checklist Section 8 — Synthesis.** Primary pattern, confidence level, activation threshold, regulatory resources, referral flag, coaching entry point. Write the one-sentence summary last.

2. **Record exact language.** Review your notes and flag 3–5 phrases that were diagnostically significant. Transcribe them verbatim if you paraphrased during the interview.

3. **Rate the three core dimensions:**
   - Narrative coherence: Low / Moderate / High
   - Affect regulation level and which domain produced the most activation
   - Self-other balance designation

4. **Note any scope flags** — in the Flags field only. No clinical specifics.

5. **Write the cross-reference note** — which observations will feed which sections of the Behavioral Pattern Profile.

Do not let the immediate post-interview routine run past 24 hours. After that, memory has degraded and pattern drift across interviews has begun.
