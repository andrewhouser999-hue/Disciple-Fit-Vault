# Implicit Layer — Manager Behaviors Undermining Psychological Safety (Observer Use)

**Use case:** Observer/diagnostician — what to look for in the room, why these signals are trustworthy, and how to interpret what you detect.
**Depth:** Level 1 (mechanism — why the signals are reliable) + Level 2 (diagnostic — what to observe and what it means). Level 3 (what changes implicit beliefs) is flagged but not developed here.

**Core claim this brief establishes:** Implicit beliefs and heart intent are not hidden. They show up in micromovements, nonverbal signals, and automatic behavioral outputs that the people in the room can read — often before the manager registers what they have transmitted. The involuntary nature of these signals is what makes them reliable. A deliberate behavior can be masked; an automatic one cannot.

---

## Part 1: The Implicit Layer — Origin, Mechanism, and Why It Produces Involuntary Behavior

### What "Implicit" Means at the Belief Level

Greenwald & Banaji (1995, *Psychological Review*, 102(1), 4–27) defined implicit attitudes as "introspectively unidentified (or inaccurately identified) traces of past experience that mediate favorable or unfavorable feeling, thought, or action toward social objects." The critical distinction from explicit attitudes: implicit attitudes are not accessible to introspection — the person holding them cannot accurately report them and they frequently diverge from stated values.

Bargh (1994, "The Four Horsemen of Automaticity," *Handbook of Social Cognition*, 2nd ed.) established the mechanism: implicit attitudes activate behavioral schemas automatically — without conscious intention, goal, or awareness. The sequence:

1. A social stimulus is encountered (a challenge to the manager's position, an unexpected question, input from a lower-status person)
2. The implicit evaluation of that stimulus activates automatically
3. A behavioral schema associated with that evaluation activates
4. The behavior occurs — before conscious deliberation can intervene

The manager's stated belief ("I want input") and operating belief ("I am the one who should know") diverge at exactly this point. The operating belief governs the automatic behavioral output. The stated belief governs the deliberate response that follows — often a half-second or more later.

**Argyris & Schön — Theory-in-Use vs. Espoused Theory:**
Argyris & Schön (1974, *Theory in Practice*, Jossey-Bass; 1978, *Organizational Learning*, Addison-Wesley) drew the foundational distinction:

- **Espoused theory:** stated values, principles, and predicted behavior — what the manager says they believe and how they say they would act
- **Theory-in-use:** the governing values and action strategies that actually drive behavior, inferred from observing behavior — not from asking

The gap is not hypocrisy. Argyris documented that people are typically unaware of their theory-in-use. When asked to predict their behavior in a hypothetical situation, they describe their espoused theory. When they encounter the actual situation, theory-in-use governs — and it is often substantially different.

For the observer: the theory-in-use is visible in behavior. It is what you are reading in the room. The manager's self-report is an account of their espoused theory. These are different data sources and they frequently conflict. Trust the behavior.

Argyris also identified "defensive routines" — automatic patterns that protect people from experiencing the discomfort that awareness of the gap would produce. These routines are self-sealing: they prevent the manager from registering that there is a gap to close. The observer can see what the defensive routine is protecting; the manager cannot.

**Kegan & Lahey — Hidden Competing Commitments:**
Kegan & Lahey (2009, *Immunity to Change*, Harvard Business Review Press) identified the specific belief architecture that sustains the gap. A hidden competing commitment is an unconscious goal that competes with the stated commitment and produces behaviors that undermine it.

Common competing commitments in the psychological safety context:

| Stated Commitment | Hidden Competing Commitment | Big Assumption Driving It |
|---|---|---|
| "I want candor and input" | To not appear incompetent | "If I don't have the answer, I will lose credibility" |
| "I value diverse perspectives" | To maintain authority position | "If I allow a challenge, it will undermine my standing with the team" |
| "I encourage questions" | To efficiency and closure | "If we open this up, we'll never get through the agenda" |
| "I want honest feedback" | To certainty and predictability | "If uncertainty is visible, people will lose confidence in me" |

These commitments are implicit — the manager does not reason from them consciously. They function as pre-reflective rules that activate when the relevant stimulus is encountered. The behavior they produce is what the observer sees.

### The Neurobiological Mechanism: Why Implicit Beliefs Produce Involuntary Behavior

**Bargh's Automaticity (Bargh & Chartrand, 1999, *American Psychologist*, 54(7), 462–479):**
Most social behavior is driven by automatic processes activated by environmental cues — without conscious deliberation. Bargh, Chen & Burrows (1996, *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 71(2), 230–244) demonstrated through behavioral priming experiments that implicit evaluations produce behavioral outputs that participants are unaware of and cannot account for when asked.

Applied to management: the manager primed by an implicit evaluation — "this person is challenging me," "this input is beneath consideration," "I need to reassert my position" — produces behavioral signals automatically. The behavior the employee reads is not the output of a decision. It is the output of an automatic activation sequence. The deliberate verbal response follows — often a half-second later, often carefully constructed — but the implicit signal has already transmitted.

**Damasio's Somatic Marker Hypothesis (1994, *Descartes' Error*, Putnam):**
Damasio demonstrated that implicit evaluations are stored as bodily states — "somatic markers." When a stimulus associated with a previous emotional experience is encountered, the body responds first: postural changes, facial muscle activation, autonomic shifts in heart rate, skin conductance, and vocal tension. These responses occur before conscious appraisal of the stimulus.

For the observer: what you are reading in the room is the somatic marker manifesting in the body before the deliberate response is constructed. The slight facial configuration of contempt, the micro-shift in body orientation, the vocal tone change — these are somatic markers, not choices. The manager will then construct a deliberate response — often careful and neutral in content — but the somatic marker has already transmitted. You are reading the first signal, not the managed one.

**Porges' Neuroception and Co-Regulation (2011, *The Polyvagal Theory*, Norton):**
Porges' concept of neuroception describes the nervous system's continuous, sub-conscious surveillance of the environment for safety or threat cues — faster than conscious perception. Co-regulation is the mechanism by which one nervous system influences another through proximity and social engagement signals: prosody (vocal rhythm, tone, pitch variation), facial expression, and body posture.

Applied to management: when a manager is in an implicit state of contempt, dismissal, or status defense — even while producing carefully controlled words — their autonomic state transmits through co-regulation signals. Employees' nervous systems receive this transmission and shift toward threat/defense responses before they have consciously registered what they detected. This is the mechanism by which implicit states produce neuroception of psychological threat.

The key point for the observer: the manager cannot suppress the co-regulation signal while the implicit state is active. Only a change in the actual internal state changes the signal. This makes it more reliable than deliberate behavior.

### The Asymmetry: Manager Transmission vs. Employee Reception

Fiske et al. (2002, *Psychological Science*, 13(4)) established the status-attention asymmetry: lower-power individuals attend to higher-power individuals with significantly greater vigilance than the reverse. This is not choice but an evolved survival mechanism — in hierarchical social structures, dominant-individual behavior is the primary variable governing lower-status individuals' access to resources, safety, and belonging.

Keltner, Gruenfeld & Anderson (2003, *Psychological Review*, 110(2), 265–284) documented the inverse: high-power individuals process others less carefully — reduced attention to others' emotional signals, reduced perspective-taking. The result: employees read manager implicit state signals with greater accuracy than the manager reads their own behavior. The manager is the last to know what they are transmitting.

**Conditions that heighten employee sensitivity:**
- Uncertainty: in ambiguous situations, employees monitor manager signals more intensively for cues about what is safe
- History of punished voice: if speaking up has previously produced negative consequences, employees become hypervigilant to any cue that the current moment resembles previous dangerous moments
- Chronic organizational threat states: employees already operating with elevated threat sensitivity have hair-trigger neuroception responses to manager implicit state signals

For the observer: you are entering an environment where employees have already built detailed models of what the manager's different implicit states look like. They have been watching continuously. Your task is to verify and name what they already know — and to give it the analytical frame that makes it actionable.

## Part 2: Detert & Edmondson — Implicit Voice Theories (Expanded)

### What Implicit Voice Theories Are

Detert & Edmondson (2011, *Academy of Management Journal*, 54(3), 461–488) conducted qualitative research across multiple organizations, identifying implicit voice theories (IVTs) as learned, largely unconscious beliefs about the personal risks and benefits of speaking up at work. They form through:

- Direct experience: speaking up and receiving positive or negative consequences
- Vicarious experience: observing what happens to others who speak up
- Organizational and cultural socialization messages about hierarchy and one's place

IVTs operate as automatic filters — rapid, pre-reflective assessments that run before the employee consciously deliberates about whether to speak. They are highly attuned to specific signals the manager sends in the moment. The manager's implicit belief state is the primary input into the employee's IVT activation.

This brief focuses on the **manager side** — the implicit beliefs that produce the signals employees read. The employee IVTs are the downstream consequence.

### The Manager's Implicit Belief Architecture

**"My role is to have the answers"**
- Theory-in-use: authority is established and maintained through expertise display. Being corrected or out-performed by a subordinate represents a structural threat.
- Observable signal: subtle dismissal or minimization of input that would require acknowledging a gap in the manager's knowledge. The facial expression of contempt or dismissal at the exact moment the employee's suggestion is received — before the verbal response. Rapid redirection to the manager's own position without fully receiving the input.

**"Hierarchy governs who has standing to challenge"**
- Theory-in-use: ideas should be weighted by source position. Input from lower-status individuals requires more evidence to be taken seriously.
- Observable signal: differential engagement — more eye contact, leaning in, follow-up questions for higher-status voices; briefer acknowledgment, faster topic movement for lower-status voices. The differential is automatic and therefore consistent across interactions. Employees read the pattern immediately.

**"Meetings are for execution, not deliberation"**
- Theory-in-use: the purpose of gathering is to align on what has already been decided. Questions represent inefficiency or misalignment.
- Observable signal: impatience signals when questions or alternative perspectives extend beyond the expected timeline — checking time, subtle postural shift toward closure, reduced eye contact, vocal prosody shifting toward finality when challenged.

**"Uncertainty should not be displayed"**
- Theory-in-use: leaders who display uncertainty lose credibility and control. Performing certainty is a leadership requirement.
- Observable signal: the manager performs certainty even when uncertain — and simultaneously communicates discomfort with others' expression of uncertainty. Dismissal or rapid reframing of "I don't know" responses from employees. Discomfort expression in the face when uncertainty is named.

### Observable Behavioral Signatures — The Micro Level

**Verbal micro-dismissals:**
- Tone shift from engaged to flat at the moment of receiving unwanted input — the vocal quality changes before the words are chosen
- Responses beginning with adversative conjunctions ("but," "however," "right, but") that signal the previous contribution has been received as an obstacle, not an input
- Restatement of the employee's idea followed by a slight modification that re-centers the manager's original position — attributing the combined idea to the manager's framing without acknowledgment
- Questions framed as challenges: "Do you really think that would work?" vs. "Tell me more about that" — the prosody communicates evaluation, not inquiry

**Nonverbal response patterns:**
- Body orientation: subtle rotation away from the speaker at the moment of receiving challenging input — as little as 5–10 degrees of torso shift, documented in social interaction research as a disengagement signal
- Eye contact: reduced duration or gaze aversion in the moments immediately following input that conflicts with the manager's position
- Facial configuration: the microexpression of contempt (unilateral lip corner raise, often lasting less than 200ms) appearing at the moment of receiving input the manager implicitly evaluates as beneath consideration — before the deliberate response is constructed
- Eyebrow micromovement: brief furrowing at the moment of challenge, registering the challenge as threat

**Temporal signals — the observer's primary tool:**
- Response latency: dismissal is faster than engagement. A brief, rapid acknowledgment-and-move-on signals that less processing occurred — which signals less value was assigned. Extended pauses before responding signal that the input required actual consideration.
- Acknowledgment absence: moving to the next agenda item or next speaker without any acknowledgment of the previous contribution — the most powerful implicit signal that the input did not register as significant. Its power is in its invisibility to the manager.
- Interruption timing: not all interruptions are equal. Interrupting at the point of an employee's key claim — before the thought is complete — signals that the claim was already categorized and dismissed before it was fully received.
- Attribution lag: ideas raised by lower-status members that reappear later in the discussion attributed to higher-status members — often without conscious intent. The observer tracks attribution to notice the pattern.

**Status-marking behaviors:**
- Idea attribution: citing an idea later in the meeting as having originated with a higher-status person when it was first raised by a lower-status person — automatic, not deliberate
- Selective follow-up: asking clarifying questions of some contributors and not others — whose input is treated as requiring more understanding vs. whose is treated as already understood (and therefore not seriously considered)
- Differential address: consistently directing discussion back through the manager rather than allowing direct dialogue between team members — implicitly signaling that all input is mediated through and evaluated by the manager's position

## Part 3: Brown — Armored Leadership (Expanded)

### What Armor Means at the Implicit Belief Level

Brown's research (2018, *Dare to Lead*, drawing on a 20-year qualitative research program on vulnerability, shame, and courage) identifies armored leadership as behavioral patterns driven by implicit beliefs about what vulnerability means — and what it costs.

**The implicit belief architecture of armor:**

| Armor Pattern | Underlying Implicit Belief | Competing Commitment |
|---|---|---|
| Certainty performance | "Uncertainty = incompetence" | To never being caught not knowing |
| Perfectionism display | "Errors reveal fundamental inadequacy" | To maintaining an error-free appearance |
| Cynicism as default | "Hope and enthusiasm are naïve" | To being seen as experienced and unsurprisable |
| Emotional distance | "Emotional response = weakness" | To projecting control at all times |
| Credit protection | "Sharing credit dilutes my value" | To being the identifiable source of results |

These beliefs are implicit — the armored leader does not consciously reason from them. They function as pre-reflective rules that activate automatically when the relevant stimulus is encountered: a challenge, an admission of uncertainty, an employee's display of enthusiasm, a request for vulnerability.

Armor is a threat response. Sympathetic nervous system activation in response to perceived threat (to status, to identity, to belonging) produces the behavioral outputs Brown categorizes as armor. This makes armor involuntary in the same way all threat responses are involuntary — it is a survival response, not a deliberate strategy. The manager does not choose to armor up in the moment; their nervous system does it before the choice is available.

### The Armored Leader's Micro-Behavioral Signature

**At the moment of receiving a challenge or correction:**
- The micro-lag: a brief pause, then a response that opens with the manager's position rather than engagement with the input — the pause is the moment of armor activation; what follows is the armor output
- Vocal tone shift from conversational to authoritative/declarative — prosody moves from question-receiving mode to conclusion-stating mode
- Physical stillness in some leaders — a parasympathetic counter-response that reads as coldness or control; others show a slight forward lean with increased speech pace — sympathetic activation producing urgency to close the gap the challenge has opened

**Certainty performance — signature behaviors:**
- Immediate reframing of the challenging input as a misunderstanding or incomplete picture — the manager explains why the employee doesn't have the full context rather than engaging with the substance of the challenge
- Facial compression: a subtle tightening around the eyes and jaw at the moment of receiving information that contradicts the manager's stated position
- Volume or pace increase in the response — the manager speaks louder or faster when defending a position that has been challenged, even mildly

**Perfectionism armor — most relevant to psychological safety:**
- Rapid reframing of errors as "learning opportunities" without dwelling in the acknowledgment — employees read this as error-intolerance despite the verbal framing; the pace of the pivot is the signal
- Facial discomfort at the moment of hearing about mistakes — appearing before the deliberate "thanks for telling me" response is produced
- Performance appraisal questions with prosody of evaluation rather than inquiry — the vocal pattern communicates that a correct answer is expected, not an honest one

**Cynicism armor:**
- The slight smile at the moment of hearing new ideas or expressions of enthusiasm — not warm, but slightly elevated at one corner, reading as "I've seen this before"
- Brevity: cynicism produces short responses that signal the input has already been categorized and doesn't require engagement
- Tone of light mockery framed as humor — depotentiating the contribution through a comment that the armored leader experiences as collegial banter and the employee experiences as diminishment

### What Employees Read and How They Respond

Brown's research and the clinical literature she draws on indicate that employees are highly accurate readers of armored leadership signals — particularly those carrying shame implications. Shame sensitivity is adaptive: humans are wired to detect social signals that precede exclusion or diminishment.

Team conditioning to armored leadership occurs rapidly. Brown's research suggests that teams develop behavioral norms in response within weeks of a significant armor-activation incident. The conditioned response pattern: performance of competence rather than honest reporting, reduction in questions, reduction in error disclosure, increase in image management. The team learns what armor communicates before it can consciously name what it learned.

## Part 4: Additional Sources — The Implicit → Micro-Behavior Pathway

### Paul Ekman — Microexpressions

Ekman developed the Facial Action Coding System (FACS) and documented microexpressions — involuntary facial expressions revealing implicit emotional states before conscious management, lasting 1/25 to 1/5 of a second (Ekman & Friesen, 1978, *Facial Action Coding System*; Ekman, 2003, *Emotions Revealed*, Times Books).

**Implicit states most relevant to psychological safety and their facial signatures:**

| Implicit State | Facial Signature | What It Communicates |
|---|---|---|
| Contempt | Unilateral lip corner raise (asymmetric — the only one) | Superiority + moral disapproval; "you are beneath consideration" |
| Disgust | Nose wrinkle + upper lip raise | Rejection; the input is offensive to standards |
| Disbelief | Inner brow raise + slight lid compression | Skepticism; "I don't credit this" |
| Anger (suppressed) | Lip press + jaw tension | Controlled threat response to challenge |
| Fear of exposure | Brief upper eyelid raise + momentary frozen expression | The manager is caught in uncertainty and armoring |

**The temporal observation window:**
Microexpressions appear in the first 200 milliseconds of the response — before the deliberate expression is deployed. Ekman & O'Sullivan (1991, *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 61(6), 913–920) demonstrated that observers who know to attend to this window detect implicit states more reliably than observers attending to the deliberate response. For the field observer: watch the first moment of reception, not the constructed response.

**Observer accuracy:**
Naive observers detect microexpressions above chance. Motivated observers — those with reason to read accurately — show significantly higher accuracy. Employees in subordinate positions, reading upward with high motivation, achieve reliability that approaches trained observer levels for the specific manager they have been watching over time.

### John Gottman — Contempt as Implicit State with Observable Markers

Gottman's longitudinal research (1999, *The Seven Principles for Making Marriage Work*; Gottman & Levenson, 2000, *Journal of Marriage and Family*) identified contempt as the single most predictive marker of relationship dissolution among the "Four Horsemen" (criticism, contempt, defensiveness, stonewalling).

**Definition:** Contempt = superiority + moral disapproval toward the other person. It communicates "I am above you and you are defective" — not "what you did was wrong" (criticism) but "you are wrong at the level of what you are."

**Observable markers in the management context:**
- Eye rolling — the clearest contempt signal; registers the other person's contribution as not worth engaging
- Sneering — lip configuration of contempt combined with dismissive vocal tone
- Sarcasm — verbal contempt; the gap between literal meaning and implied meaning communicates that the target is too limited to deserve direct engagement
- Light mockery framed as humor — functionally identical to Brown's cynicism armor signal; the shared laughter of the group comes at the target's expense
- The contempt microexpression (Ekman's unilateral lip corner raise) at the moment of receiving the employee's contribution

**Why contempt is uniquely damaging:**
Porath & Erez (2007, *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 92(5), 1307–1316) found that incivility carrying a contempt signal produces significantly greater cognitive and performance deficits than equivalent criticism without contempt. Criticism attacks behavior; contempt attacks standing. The team present at the moment of contempt registers that this person's standing has been publicly diminished — which communicates to every member what the cost of being in that position might be.

**For the observer:** Contempt signals in a manager's response to an employee's contribution are the highest-priority observation to log. They are the most reliable implicit state indicators and carry the most significant downstream consequences for team psychological safety.

### Dacher Keltner — Power and Automatic Empathy Erosion

Keltner's research (2016, *The Power Paradox*, Penguin; Keltner, Gruenfeld & Anderson, 2003, *Psychological Review*, 110(2), 265–284) documents a consistent effect: as individuals gain power, their automatic processing of others' emotional states changes:

- Reduced spontaneous perspective-taking
- Reduced attention to others' emotional signals
- Increased impulsive, dominant behavior
- Reduced self-monitoring accuracy

**The neurological basis:** Power activates a behavioral approach system (Carver & White, 1994; Depue & Collins, 1999) that orients toward reward and disinhibits approach behavior. The high-power individual becomes less wired — literally — to process others in the moment.

**Micro-behavioral expressions of power-eroded empathy:**
- Reduced mirroring: lower-power individuals spontaneously mirror the emotional expressions of higher-power others; higher-power individuals do not reciprocally mirror. A manager who doesn't mirror an employee's emotional expression communicates (automatically) that the employee's state is not being registered. Absence of mirroring is observable and consistent.
- Interruption: Keltner's research documents increasing interruption rates with power acquisition — not as deliberate dominance assertion but as automatic disinhibition of approach behavior
- Reduced gaze at emotional information: eye-tracking studies show high-power individuals spend less time looking at faces and emotional cues — they are attending to task and environment, not people

**Critical for the observer:** These behaviors are not deliberate. The manager with eroded empathy is not choosing to dismiss; their nervous system is not attending. But the employee cannot distinguish inattention from contempt in terms of its neuroceptive impact — both register as diminishment. The observer's job is not to determine intent but to note the signal and its effect.

**Self-monitoring:** Power acquisition reduces the accuracy of self-assessment of social behavior. High-power individuals consistently rate their own behavior as more collaborative, open, and fair than observers and direct reports rate it (Atwater & Yammarino, 1992, *Personnel Psychology*, 45(1), 141–164; 360-degree feedback literature broadly). Self-report from the manager is systematically biased; behavioral observation is more reliable.

### Christine Porath — Incivility as Unintentional Implicit Signal

Porath (Porath & Erez, 2007; Porath & Pearce, 2009; Porath, 2016, *Mastering Civility*, Grand Central Publishing) documented that the majority of workplace incivility is unintentional — the person delivering it does not typically register having behaved rudely. The implicit evaluation (this person's time is less valuable; this input doesn't require full engagement) produces the behavior without the manager registering it.

**Smallest-unit incivility behaviors:**
- Checking a phone or watch while someone is speaking — the implicit signal: what is on the screen is more relevant than what this person is saying
- Dismissive tone without dismissive content — words are neutral, prosody communicates dismissal; employees read the channel divergence
- Absence of eye contact during a subordinate's contribution
- Responding with delayed, minimal acknowledgment before moving on — the briefness of the acknowledgment is the data, not its presence
- Addressing someone by the wrong name or failing to connect a current contribution to what they said previously — communicates that their input was not retained

**Cognitive performance effects:**
Porath & Erez (2007) found that even a single episode of mild incivility toward a target reduced cognitive task performance by approximately 30% and creative output by approximately 45% in subsequent tasks. Mechanism: incivility activates a threat response that consumes working memory resources — the cognitive capacity required for the kind of engagement that psychological safety enables is degraded.

**The bystander effect (Porath & Pearce, 2009):**
Witnesses to incivility show nearly identical cognitive performance deficits as targets. A single episode of micro-incivility directed at one employee degrades the thinking capacity of everyone present. For the observer: you are watching not just the target's response but the room's response — the shift in engagement, the reduced contribution, the increase in careful behavior from everyone who witnessed it.

### John Bargh — Automaticity of Social Behavior

Bargh & Chartrand (1999, *American Psychologist*, 54(7), 462–479): most social behavior is driven by automatic processes initiated by features of the social environment without conscious deliberation. Every stimulus encountered is automatically and immediately evaluated (good/bad, safe/threatening, relevant/irrelevant) before conscious appraisal and this automatic evaluation shapes all subsequent deliberate processing.

**What this means for the gap between intent and impact:**
The manager who genuinely does not intend to undermine is not lying. Their stated intentions are sincere. But their sincere intentions govern the deliberate verbal response, which follows the automatic behavioral output by a half-second or more. Employees are reading the automatic output — the first signal, the somatic marker expression, the micro-dismissal — not the deliberate response. The gap between intent and impact in the implicit layer is not a gap between intention and execution. It is a structural gap between what the automatic nervous system produces and what the conscious mind intended.

## Part 5: Discernibility as the Central Finding

### Why Involuntary Signals Are More Readable Than Deliberate Ones

The involuntary nature of implicit-layer signals is precisely what makes them reliable for the observer. Deliberate behaviors can be managed, performed, and masked. A manager can choose words carefully, rehearse a response, produce a facial expression of engagement that does not reflect their internal state. But the somatic marker fires before the deliberate response is constructed. The microexpression appears in the first 200 milliseconds — before the managed expression is deployed. The vocal prosody shift occurs at the moment of stimulus evaluation, not at the moment of deliberate communication.

**Research basis:** Ekman & O'Sullivan (1991) demonstrated that microexpressions consistently reveal the emotional state being suppressed and appear in a predictable temporal relationship to the subsequent managed expression. The micro-expression is the first expression; the managed expression follows. Attending to the first 200 milliseconds of the response window is the observational discipline.

**Subliminal emotional processing:** Whalen et al. (1998, *Science*, 281(5380), 1083–1086) demonstrated using backward-masked facial expressions that the amygdala responds to threatening facial expressions presented below the threshold of conscious awareness. Applied to organizational contexts: employees' threat responses to manager implicit state signals may activate before they can consciously identify what they detected. The employee feels unsafe before they can explain why — and cannot articulate a specific incident when asked. This is why employees often describe implicit-layer undermining as "something about the atmosphere" or "a feeling" rather than pointing to specific events.

### The Status Asymmetry in Signal Detection

Fiske (1993, *Psychological Review*, 100(4), 621–628) established that attention is distributed asymmetrically by status: lower-status individuals attend to higher-status individuals significantly more than the reverse. The evolutionary logic: in hierarchical structures, dominant-individual behavior is the primary variable governing lower-status individuals' resource access, safety, and belonging. Vigilance to those signals is adaptive.

**For the observer:** Employees have been attending to the manager's implicit state signals continuously, over time, and have built detailed behavioral models of what the manager's different states look like. They know what each implicit state precedes. An observer entering for a session will see less total data than employees have already registered — but the observer has the conceptual framework to interpret what they see and to name what employees have been experiencing without language for it.

### The Gap Between Manager Self-Perception and Employee Reception

Atwater & Yammarino (1992, *Personnel Psychology*, 45(1), 141–164) and the broader 360-degree feedback literature consistently find that managers rate their own behavior as more positive, inclusive, and open than direct reports rate it — and the gap increases with management level. This is not defensiveness; it is the structural consequence of the self-monitoring deficit that power produces (Keltner et al., 2003).

**Self-report as a diagnostic tool:** Unreliable in the implicit layer for two reasons. First, the manager's self-perception is systematically biased by the power-self-monitoring gap. Second, what the observer is trying to access is definitionally below conscious awareness — the manager cannot accurately introspect what is driving their automatic behavior. Behavioral observation is the only reliable data source for the implicit layer.

## Part 6: Observer Protocol — What to Attend To in the Room

Synthesizing from the research above, the observer's attention should be structured around five disciplines:

**1. The temporal layer — first response, first 200 milliseconds**
Before the manager constructs their deliberate response, the somatic marker and the microexpression have already transmitted. The first moment of reception — the facial configuration, the body shift, the prosodic change — is the most reliable data. It is what cannot be managed.

**2. Asymmetries — differential treatment is automatic and therefore consistent**
The manager's implicit evaluations of different contributors produce consistent differentials in how they are engaged. Track: who gets follow-up questions vs. who gets minimal acknowledgment; who gets eye contact vs. who does not; whose ideas get attributed vs. whose are absorbed without attribution. Single instances are noise. Consistent patterns are signal.

**3. Channel divergence — verbal vs. nonverbal vs. prosodic**
When the verbal content is positive but the prosody is flat, or the words are neutral but the body has shifted away, or the face shows a microexpression that contradicts the spoken response — the implicit layer is visible in the gap between channels. The deliberate channel (words) and the automatic channels (tone, body, face) diverge when the implicit state conflicts with the intended message.

**4. Absence of behaviors**
Implicit dismissal shows up as much in what is absent as what is present: acknowledgment withheld, mirroring not occurring, eye contact not made, follow-up questions not asked, contributions not referenced later. Absence requires active tracking — it does not call attention to itself.

**5. Room response — the bystander effect in real time**
After a micro-incivility or contempt signal directed at one team member, observe the room: does contribution rate drop? Does careful, hedged language increase? Do others become more physically still? The room's behavioral shift following an implicit-layer event is data about the cumulative psychological safety state of the team.

**What the observer is NOT doing:** Inferring intent. The implicit layer research establishes that intent is irrelevant to the signal — the signal is produced automatically regardless of what the manager intends. Documenting behavior and its observable effects is the task. Attribution of motivation belongs to the coaching conversation, not the observation session.
