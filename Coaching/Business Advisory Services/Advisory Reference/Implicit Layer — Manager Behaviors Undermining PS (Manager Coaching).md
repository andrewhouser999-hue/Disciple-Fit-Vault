# Implicit Layer — Manager Behaviors Undermining Psychological Safety (Manager Coaching Use)

**Use case:** Manager coaching — helping a manager access their own implicit belief architecture and see the connection between their operating beliefs and the behaviors that undermine psychological safety.
**Depth:** Level 1 (mechanism — depersonalizes the behavior; opens the coaching conversation) + Level 2 (the manager's mirror — giving the manager language and a map for what they are doing and why). Level 3 (what changes implicit beliefs) is flagged throughout but not developed — it is the next prompt.

**The fundamental framing difference from the observer use:**
The observer use asks: *what does Andrew see in the room?*
The coaching use asks: *what can the manager learn to see about themselves — and how does the research equip the coaching conversation to get there?*

The mechanism (Level 1) serves a different function here. In observer use, it establishes why the signals are trustworthy. In coaching use, it is the primary entry point — the neurobiological framing that removes blame, prevents shame-activation, and makes the implicit material examinable rather than defensive. The coaching conversation cannot access the belief layer if it first triggers the armor that protects it.

## Part 1: The Mechanism as Coaching Entry Point — Depersonalization

### Why Level 1 Comes First in Coaching

In the observer context, the mechanism grounds the reliability of behavioral observation. In the coaching context, the mechanism is the frame that makes honest examination possible at all. Before a manager can look at their own implicit layer, they need a way to hold what they find that does not activate shame or defensiveness — because shame and defensiveness are themselves armor responses that will close off access to exactly what the coaching is trying to reach.

Brown (2018, *Dare to Lead*) and Brown's grounding in Tangney & Dearing (2002, *Shame and Guilt*, Guilford Press) establish the distinction between shame and guilt that is critical here: **guilt** = "I did something wrong" (behavior-focused, promotes repair); **shame** = "I am wrong" (self-focused, promotes concealment and defense). Coaching that leads with behavioral confrontation ("here is what you did that undermined safety") risks activating shame — and the manager who is in shame is in the identical neurological state as the manager who is armoring against a challenge in a team meeting. The armor goes up. Access closes.

The neurobiological mechanism reframes the conversation: **"This is how human nervous systems work — including yours"** rather than "here is what you did wrong." It locates the behavior in a universal physiological process rather than a character flaw. This is not therapeutic softening; it is strategic. The research supports it as the framing that keeps the coaching conversation open.

### The Depersonalization Research

**Bargh's automaticity (1994; Bargh & Chartrand, 1999, *American Psychologist*, 54(7), 462–479):**
The automatic nature of the behavior means the manager genuinely did not choose it in the moment. This is not an excuse — it is an accurate account of how the behavior was produced. Coaching that treats automatic behavior as if it were a deliberate choice will produce a manager who defends their intentions rather than examines their beliefs. Coaching that accurately frames the behavior as automatic opens the question: *what implicit evaluation activated this?*

The coaching entry language that follows from Bargh: "The behavior happened faster than a decision. The question isn't why you decided to respond that way — it's what your nervous system was evaluating at that moment." This language is clinically accurate and non-blaming. It shifts the manager's attention from defending intent to examining implicit evaluation — which is where the coaching work lives.

**Damasio's somatic marker hypothesis (1994, *Descartes' Error*, Putnam):**
The somatic marker is felt before it is expressed. The manager who dismisses a contribution with a tone shift or a body reorientation was experiencing something in their body in the moment before the behavior — a subtle signal of threat, discomfort, or evaluation — that produced the automatic response. This is the coaching access point: not the behavior the employee observed, but the internal experience the manager had immediately before it.

Damasio's framework gives the coaching conversation a specific question: *"What were you noticing internally at that moment — just before you responded?"* This question accesses the somatic marker — the felt sense of the implicit evaluation — before the manager's deliberate narrative about the interaction covers it over. If asked "why did you respond that way?", the manager will answer from their espoused theory. If asked "what were you noticing in your body or your reaction in that moment?", they are more likely to access the actual implicit state.

**Porges' co-regulation (2011, *The Polyvagal Theory*, Norton):**
The manager's implicit state transmitted to direct reports through co-regulation signals. The coaching parallel: the coach's regulated state is the primary condition for the manager being able to access implicit material. A coaching conversation conducted in a tone of evaluation, urgency, or subtle disapproval will activate the same threat response in the manager that the manager's implicit state activated in their employees. The coach must regulate first — and maintain that regulated state throughout — as the precondition for the manager's access to the implicit layer.

Porges' neuroception framework also explains why the coach's nonverbals matter as much as their words. The manager's nervous system is continuously evaluating whether the coaching environment is safe enough for the kind of exposure that implicit layer examination requires. This surveillance is sub-conscious and automatic — meaning the manager is not consciously deciding whether it feels safe. Their nervous system is already deciding before the manager forms an opinion. The coach's responsibility is to maintain the signals of safety that keep that decision favorable.

### The Shame-Armor Dynamic — The Primary Coaching Risk

When confronted with evidence of behavior that contradicts their self-image as a good manager, most leaders activate what Argyris & Schön (1974, *Theory in Practice*, Jossey-Bass) called a defensive routine: an automatic pattern that protects the person from experiencing the discomfort of the gap between their espoused theory ("I value input") and their theory-in-use ("I dismiss input that challenges my position"). The defensive routine is itself automatic — it is not a choice to defend.

The forms defensive routines take in coaching:
- **Explanation:** "What actually happened was..." — the manager recounts the situation in a way that makes their behavior rational and appropriate
- **Deflection:** "The real issue is..." — attention moves to the employee's behavior, the organizational context, the time pressure
- **Compliance performance:** "You're right, I should do better" — the manager accepts the feedback verbally in a way that closes the conversation without accessing the belief behind the behavior
- **Minimization:** "That was just one moment" — the behavior is isolated from the pattern it belongs to

Each of these prevents access to the implicit belief layer. None of them are dishonest — they are automatic protective responses. The coaching task is not to push through them but to design the conversation so the defensive routine doesn't activate in the first place. The neurobiological mechanism framing, delivered early and non-judgmentally, is the primary design tool.

## Part 2: The Manager's Internal Experience — The Coaching Access Point

### The Felt Sense of the Implicit State

The somatic marker is not only expressed as behavior — it is experienced internally by the manager as a felt sense before it becomes behavior. This internal experience is the data that coaching accesses. The manager cannot change what they cannot perceive; they cannot perceive what has never been brought into awareness; and bringing it into awareness requires accessing the moment-level internal experience, not the retrospective narrative about it.

Gendlin (1978, *Focusing*, Everest House; 1996, *Focusing-Oriented Psychotherapy*, Guilford Press) developed the concept of the "felt sense" — the bodily sense of a situation that contains more information than can be put into words. A felt sense is experienced as a physical sensation (tightness, warmth, a kind of heaviness or lightness) and carries the implicit evaluation of the situation before that evaluation is articulated. Gendlin's "focusing" process is a method for accessing and staying with the felt sense until it yields its content.

The RPA application is not the full focusing process — that belongs in clinical and developmental coaching contexts. But the concept of the felt sense is clinically accurate and practically useful: the manager's implicit state is accessible as a body-level experience. The question "what were you noticing — even briefly — in your body or your reaction in that moment before you responded?" is not metaphorical. It is pointing to a real, detectable internal event that preceded the automatic behavior. The manager who can access this — even partially — has begun to see the implicit layer from the inside.

**What the felt sense of common implicit states tends to feel like:**
- Contempt/superiority: a slight internal contraction, a subtle sense of elevation relative to the other person, sometimes described as a kind of stillness or disengagement
- Status threat: a rapid tightening, often in the chest or jaw; a sense of urgency to reestablish position; sometimes experienced as mild irritation or the impulse to interrupt
- Dismissal: a kind of flattening — the internal engagement drops; the input registers as not requiring real attention; described sometimes as "going elsewhere" mentally while the person is still speaking
- Uncertainty performance: the discomfort of not knowing, immediately followed by the impulse to perform certainty; experienced as a brief flash of exposure followed by a covering movement

These are not universal — individual somatic marker patterns vary. But the coaching question is always the same: *what was happening internally, in the moment, just before the behavior?*

### The Left-Hand Column — Argyris's Tool for Surfacing Theory-in-Use

Argyris & Schön (1974; Argyris, 1990, *Overcoming Organizational Defenses*, Allyn & Bacon) developed the left-hand column technique as a structured method for surfacing theory-in-use without triggering defensive routines. The exercise:

1. The manager takes a real interaction — a meeting, a conversation — and writes what was actually said in the right column
2. In the left column, they write what they were thinking and feeling during the interaction but did not say
3. The left column is examined: what does it reveal about the manager's actual governing values in that moment?

The left column bypasses the espoused theory because the manager is not being asked to describe their values — they are being asked to recall their thoughts. The thoughts are a more direct trace of the theory-in-use than any value-statement the manager could offer. What the manager was thinking but not saying in a meeting is a direct window into the implicit belief architecture that was governing their behavior.

The technique is most effective when the manager selects a high-stakes or emotionally activated interaction — one where the gap between espoused and operating beliefs is likely to be widest. The coaching question that follows: *"What do your left-column thoughts tell you about what you were actually trying to protect or maintain in that moment?"*

## Part 3: Detert & Edmondson — The Manager's Belief Map

### The IVT Framework as a Coaching Mirror

Detert & Edmondson's implicit voice theories (2011, *Academy of Management Journal*, 54(3), 461–488) research focused primarily on the employee side — how employees' implicit beliefs about the costs of speaking up determine their voice behavior. For the coaching use, the parallel framework applies to the manager: what implicit beliefs does this manager hold about their role, their authority, and what speaking up means — and how do those beliefs produce the automatic behaviors that confirm employees' IVTs?

The coaching mirror function: when a manager hears the IVT framework applied to their own belief architecture, it provides a map that makes something previously invisible legible. The four implicit belief patterns from the observer brief translate into coaching questions:

| Implicit Belief | Mirror Question |
|---|---|
| "My role is to have the answers" | "What do you notice when an employee raises something you hadn't considered? What happens in you?" |
| "Hierarchy governs who has standing" | "When you look back at that meeting, whose input did you follow up on — and whose did you let pass? What determined that?" |
| "Meetings are for execution" | "What were you aware of as the conversation extended? What were you tracking?" |
| "Uncertainty should not be displayed" | "When was the last time you said 'I don't know' in front of your team? What felt like it was at stake?" |

These questions are designed to bypass the espoused theory by asking the manager to recall specific moments rather than state their values. Recalled experience is harder to manage than stated belief — the implicit layer is more visible in what the manager remembers feeling than in what they say they believe.

### The Pattern Recognition Conversation

One of the coaching challenges in the implicit layer is that the manager typically cannot see the pattern because they are inside it. Each individual instance of implicit-layer behavior feels situationally justified. The pattern only becomes visible when the instances are held together.

The coaching technique: after collecting several incidents (through the left-hand column exercise, through observation session data, through self-report), the coach reflects the pattern back without interpretation: *"I've noticed that in each of these situations — [instance 1], [instance 2], [instance 3] — something similar happened. Here's what I'm noticing. What do you see?"*

Presenting the pattern and inviting the manager's own interpretation respects the Argyris principle that confronting defensive routines directly triggers them. The manager who discovers their own pattern — even with the coach's facilitation — has a different relationship to it than the manager who is told what their pattern is.

## Part 4: Kegan & Lahey — The Immunity to Change Process as Coaching Methodology

Kegan & Lahey's Immunity to Change (ITC) framework (2009, *Immunity to Change*, Harvard Business Review Press) is the most fully developed coaching methodology for the implicit belief layer. It was designed specifically to address the gap between genuine intention to change and the automatic behaviors that undermine that intention — which is precisely the structure of the problem this research brief addresses.

### The Four-Column Map

The ITC process produces a four-column map that makes the hidden competing commitment and Big Assumption visible:

**Column 1 — Improvement Goal:** The stated commitment. For a manager working on psychological safety: "I am committed to creating an environment where people feel safe to speak up, raise concerns, and admit mistakes."

**Column 2 — Behaviors Working Against the Goal:** What the manager actually does that undermines Column 1. Not labeled as failure — observed as data. Examples from the implicit layer research: rapid dismissal of input; tone that signals the manager has already decided; brevity of acknowledgment that communicates the contribution didn't register; absence of follow-up on ideas raised by lower-status members.

**Column 3 — Hidden Competing Commitment:** The unconscious goal that the Column 2 behaviors are serving. This is the discovery the coaching is working toward. For psychological safety: "I am also committed to not being seen as uncertain or uninformed," or "I am committed to maintaining clear positional authority," or "I am committed to efficiency and not being slowed down by extended deliberation." The competing commitment explains the behavior — it is not irrationality but competing rationality.

**Column 4 — Big Assumption:** The implicit belief that makes the competing commitment feel necessary. "If I don't have the answer, people will lose confidence in me and my authority will erode." "If I allow a direct challenge to stand in front of the team, my leadership position will be undermined." "If meetings become genuinely deliberative, I will lose control of outcomes." These Big Assumptions are typically operating as if they are simply true — the manager has not consciously examined them; they are the unquestioned premise that the competing commitment is built on.

### Working on the Big Assumption

The ITC process does not challenge the Big Assumption directly — confrontation triggers defensive routines. Instead, Kegan & Lahey use a process of **assumption testing**: the manager designs small, low-risk experiments to test whether the Big Assumption is actually true.

For the coaching context: a manager whose Big Assumption is "if I don't have the answer, I will lose credibility" might experiment with saying "I don't know — what do others think?" in a single low-stakes meeting and observing what actually happens. The assumption predicts loss of credibility. The experiment may produce data that contradicts the assumption — the team responds with engagement rather than lost confidence. This contradiction is the entry point for assumption revision.

The ITC process is documented as effective in organizational settings: Kegan & Lahey (2009) report program evaluation data across multiple organizations, and the methodology is used in executive coaching contexts with documented outcomes. *Note: the primary evidence base is program evaluation and case studies rather than RCTs — the claim of effectiveness is well-supported but not at the level of experimental evidence.*

**What the ITC process accesses that behavioral coaching does not:** The Big Assumption is the level at which lasting change is possible. Coaching that addresses Column 2 behaviors (telling the manager to do differently) without addressing the Column 4 Big Assumption will produce compliance behavior that is maintained by deliberate effort and erodes under pressure — because the underlying belief structure is still organizing toward the competing commitment. The ITC process is designed to address the belief level directly.

### The Competing Commitment as the Coaching Pivot

The moment of discovering the competing commitment is the pivot point of the coaching. Before the competing commitment is named, the manager experiences their Column 2 behaviors as failures — as evidence that they are not as good as they intend to be. After the competing commitment is named, the same behaviors are revealed as logical — they are serving a real (if implicit) goal. The manager is not failing to change; they are succeeding at protecting something. The coaching question then becomes: *"Is what this competing commitment is protecting worth the cost it's creating?"* This is a question the manager can answer honestly because it is framed as a genuine choice, not a moral failure.

## Part 5: Brown — Armor in the Coaching Room

### How Armored Leadership Manifests in the Coaching Relationship

The armor that a leader deploys in team meetings to protect against implicit threat will also activate in the coaching conversation when the coaching approaches the same implicit material. Brown's armored leadership patterns produce specific dynamics in the coaching room:

**Certainty performance armor in coaching:**
The manager who cannot display uncertainty in front of their team will have the same impulse in coaching when the coach asks them to examine what they don't know about themselves. Signs: the manager arrives at answers very quickly (before genuine reflection has occurred); gives well-formed explanations for their behavior that close down inquiry; demonstrates comfort with the conceptual framework and discomfort with the experiential questions. The armor function: keeping the coaching at the level of ideas prevents access to the felt sense where the implicit belief actually lives.

*Coaching response: slow the conversation down. Stay with the question. "I notice you moved to an answer quickly — I'm not sure we've found it yet. Let's stay here a little longer."*

**Perfectionism armor in coaching:**
The manager who is highly self-critical may appear to be doing deep work while actually using self-criticism as a way to manage the exposure. "I know I'm terrible at this" is armor — it gets to the negative conclusion first, preempting the coach from getting there. Self-blame is a form of control.

*Coaching response: distinguish self-criticism from self-examination. "What you're describing sounds like a verdict about yourself. I'm less interested in the verdict than in understanding what was happening for you in that moment."*

**Compliance performance in coaching:**
The manager who says "you're right, I need to do better" and closes down the conversation has produced what feels like progress — agreement — without any access to the implicit layer. This is the most common coaching trap and the hardest to navigate because it looks like successful coaching from the outside. The indicator: the manager can tell you what they should do but cannot tell you what they were experiencing internally when they did what they did.

*Coaching response: return to the specific moment. "I believe you that you want to do this differently. Before we get to how, I want to understand what was happening at that moment. Walk me back through it."*

### The Conditions for Examining Armor

Brown's research identifies what conditions allow a leader to begin examining their armor:

1. **The coach is not armor-activating:** The coaching conversation must not carry signals of evaluation, impatience, or disappointment — because these are exactly the signals that activate the armor being examined. A coach who subtly communicates that the manager should already know this, or that the behavior is obviously wrong, or that progress is too slow will produce the armor patterns they are trying to help the manager see.

2. **The manager experiences the coaching relationship as safe:** Brown's research on vulnerability identifies that people can only examine their armor in relationships where they have evidence that the relationship can hold what they reveal. This is built gradually, before the coaching approaches the implicit layer. Rushing to the implicit material before the coaching relationship has sufficient trust will produce compliance performance, not genuine access.

3. **The implicit material is named without judgment:** "What I'm noticing is..." rather than "What you're doing is..." The observation is offered as data for the manager's consideration, not as an indictment. The manager is always the one who decides what is true about their experience — the coach offers observations and questions, not conclusions.

4. **There is an explicit frame that separates behavior from character:** Brown's distinction between shame and guilt is applied explicitly: "We're not looking at this because something is wrong with you. We're looking at it because your nervous system is doing something automatically that your intention doesn't want — and understanding that gap is where the work lives."

## Part 6: Additional Sources — Coaching Application

### Gottman — Contempt in the Self-Diagnostic Context

The manager coaching application of Gottman's contempt research (1999, *The Seven Principles for Making Marriage Work*) is different from the observer application. In observer use, contempt markers are things Andrew reads in the room. In coaching use, the question is: can a manager learn to recognize their own contempt signals — and what does that recognition produce?

Gottman's research on couples identifies contempt as the emotional state most associated with felt superiority. The internal experience of contempt is the sense of looking down — a combination of mild disgust and a sense of being above the situation or the other person. This is accessible as a felt sense. The coaching question: *"Have there been moments in meetings where you've felt — even briefly — like you were above the conversation? Like the input wasn't worth taking seriously?"*

Most managers will not initially name this experience as contempt. They will name it as frustration, impatience, or "knowing what the right answer is." The coaching task is not to label it for them but to stay with their description until the implicit evaluation underlying it becomes visible: what was the implicit judgment about the person or the contribution that produced that feeling?

Gottman's finding that contempt is the most corrosive relational state — because it attacks standing, not behavior — is coaching-relevant because it gives the manager a concrete way to understand the cost. "This isn't about rudeness or tone. This is about a signal that communicates to the team that this person's standing is lower than others. And everyone in the room registers that signal, including people who weren't its target."

### Keltner — Coaching the Power-Degraded Self-Monitor

Keltner's research (2016, *The Power Paradox*; Keltner, Gruenfeld & Anderson, 2003, *Psychological Review*, 110(2)) establishes that power acquisition structurally degrades the self-monitoring capacity that would allow a manager to register what they are transmitting. This creates a specific coaching challenge: the manager cannot self-report accurately on their own behavior because the neurological capacity for accurate self-report has been partially eroded by the power they hold.

**Coaching implications:**
- Do not rely on the manager's self-report as the primary data source. Behavioral observation data (Andrew's observation notes, 360 feedback, specific incident analysis) is more reliable than the manager's account of what they did or how they typically behave.
- The gap between the manager's self-perception and others' experience is itself data for the coaching conversation: *"I want to show you something interesting. Here's how you described what happened in that meeting. Here's what three members of your team described. What do you make of the difference?"*
- The goal is not to win the argument about who is right. The goal is to produce enough dissonance between self-perception and external data that the manager becomes curious about the gap rather than defensive about it.

Keltner also identifies that the empathy erosion produced by power is partially reversible — sustained attention to others' perspectives, practices that reduce social distance (eating with teams, informal contact), and deliberate perspective-taking exercises can restore some of the automatic empathy capacity that power degrades. This is Level 3 territory (what changes implicit beliefs) — flagged here but not developed.

### The Research on Coaching Effectiveness in the Implicit Layer

The coaching literature on implicit belief change is thinner than the organizational psychology literature on the beliefs themselves. Key findings:

**Ely, Ibarra & Kolb (2011, *Harvard Business Review*, "Taking Gender into Account"):** Leadership development programs that focus on behavioral skills without addressing the identity-level beliefs that produce behavior show short-term behavior change that does not sustain under pressure. The implicit belief layer is not changed by behavioral coaching alone. This is not specific to gender — the mechanism generalizes.

**Stober & Grant (2006, *Evidence-Based Coaching Handbook*, Wiley):** The coaching relationship quality is the primary predictor of coaching outcome across methodologies — more predictive than the specific technique used. This supports the Brown finding that the coaching container is the precondition for implicit layer work, not a warm-up to the real intervention.

**Note on evidence level:** Research specifically on coaching interventions targeting the implicit belief layer in organizational leaders is limited. The ITC framework (Kegan & Lahey) is supported by program evaluation data. The broader claims about what produces lasting behavioral change rest on the combination of implicit cognition research, psychotherapy outcome research, and organizational behavior literature — not on RCTs of coaching specifically. This is an honest limitation to flag.

## Part 7: What Progress Looks Like — and the Level 3 Flag

### Behavioral Indicators of Belief-Level Shift vs. Compliance Performance

The coaching risk is producing compliance behavior — the manager who knows what to do and does it deliberately — without producing belief-level change. Compliance behavior is maintained by effort and erodes under pressure, fatigue, or high-stakes situations. Belief-level change produces behavior that is consistent across conditions — including conditions where deliberate effort is unavailable.

**Signs of compliance performance (not belief-level change):**
- The manager does the desired behavior in contexts where they are being observed or where it is salient as a goal — and reverts to the prior pattern in contexts where it isn't
- The manager can describe the right behavior fluently but shows discomfort or reluctance when actually doing it — the behavior feels performed rather than natural
- The manager's behavior improves in low-stakes situations and regresses in high-stakes ones — the implicit belief takes over when pressure is highest and deliberate effort is most depleted

**Signs of belief-level shift:**
- The new behavior occurs in conditions where the manager wasn't thinking about it — consistency without deliberate effort is the indicator
- The manager begins to notice their own implicit states in the moment — the gap between stimulus and automatic response increases even slightly; they can catch the beginning of the pattern before it completes
- The manager's internal narrative about the situation changes: they begin to describe team members' challenges differently — as input rather than obstacle, as information rather than threat
- The manager begins to be curious about instances where they reverted rather than defensive about them — the instances become data rather than evidence of failure

### The Level 3 Flag

What actually produces belief-level change is the central unanswered question of this work. The research available:

- **Argyris** on surfacing defensive routines without triggering them — necessary but not sufficient; awareness does not automatically produce change
- **Kegan & Lahey** on the ITC assumption-testing process — the most developed intervention methodology for the implicit belief layer; the Big Assumption testing sequence is specifically designed to produce belief revision
- **Siegel** (2010, *Mindsight*, Bantam) on reflective function and interpersonal neurobiology — the capacity to observe one's own mental states from a slight distance (mindsight) as the neurological mechanism by which implicit material becomes accessible to modification
- **Somatic approaches** (Levine, Ogden, Siegel) — whether implicit beliefs stored as somatic markers can be effectively addressed through cognitive means alone, or whether the belief change process requires somatic access, is an open question in the literature

## Appendix: Manager Behaviors Undermining Psychological Safety — Research Taxonomy

*Retained from pre-prompt research synthesis. Covers the explicit/behavioral layer of undermining — the foundation from which the implicit layer work above was derived. Useful for coaching when naming patterns in plain terms before going to the implicit belief layer underneath them.*

### Tier 1 — Empirical Anchor Studies

**Edmondson & Detert — Implicit Voice Theories (2011)**
*Academy of Management Journal*, 54(3), 461–488. Identifies manager behaviors that *confirm* employees' ingrained beliefs that speaking up is dangerous: visible frustration when challenged, status-based dismissal (idea evaluated by who said it, not what it says), performance of certainty (projecting infallibility), and failing to follow through when input is given. These behaviors don't create the fear — they validate pre-existing learned beliefs that the environment is unsafe.

**Nembhard & Edmondson — Leader Inclusiveness (2006)**
*Administrative Science Quarterly*, 51(4), 941–971. Defines "leader inclusiveness" (invitation + appreciation for voice) as the protective factor — the reverse behaviors are the undermining factors: not asking, not following up, not acknowledging contributions, allowing status hierarchies to govern who speaks.

**Edmondson — Learning vs. Performance Orientation (1999, 2002)**
Manager framing of outcomes as performance-only (results, not process; success, not learning) directly suppresses candor. Managers who treat errors as evidence of incompetence rather than data create environments where concealment is rational. Cannon & Edmondson (2001, *Journal of Organizational Behavior*) specifically isolated how manager emotional reactions to mistakes shape team reporting behavior.

**Tepper — Abusive Supervision (2000)**
*Academy of Management Journal*, 43(2), 178–190. Landmark study on the continuum from undermining to active harm. Documents downstream costs (turnover, health outcomes, performance) of hostile verbal/nonverbal behavior. Establishes that "subtle" undermining (sarcasm, public embarrassment, withholding credit) sits on the same behavioral continuum as overt abuse — not a separate category.

**Manzoni & Barsoux — The Set-Up-to-Fail Syndrome (2002)**
Harvard Business School Press. How managers, through differential treatment of lower-trust employees (over-supervision, reduced task complexity, diminished communication), create self-fulfilling prophecies that systematically exclude certain employees from the conditions psychological safety requires. The mechanism is often unconscious — managers believe they are managing performance, not undermining safety.

**Kahn — Psychological Safety — Original Concept (1990)**
*Academy of Management Journal*, 33(4), 692–724. Identified three manager behaviors that threaten safety conditions: interpersonal threat (punishment, ridicule), unpredictability (emotional volatility, inconsistent application of rules), and demonstrating that candor has no upside (input not used, feedback not acknowledged).

### Tier 2 — Applied Taxonomies with Research Backing

**Liz Wiseman — Multipliers vs. Diminishers (2010)**
Research across 150 leaders in 35 organizations. Five "Diminisher" archetypes with specific behavioral signatures that shut down voice, thinking, and contribution:
- *The Know-It-All* — broadcasts expertise, crowds out others' ideas
- *The Micromanager* — controls decisions, signals distrust
- *The Tyrant* — creates anxiety that suppresses experimentation
- *The Decision Maker* — makes unilateral calls without genuine input
- *The Rescuer* — solves problems before others can engage, eliminating contribution opportunity

What makes this useful for coaching: it's not about intent. Diminishers often believe they're being helpful. The undermining is structural in how they position themselves relative to others.

**Timothy Clark — 4 Stages of Psychological Safety (2020)**
Field research across multiple organizations. Organizes undermining behaviors by the stage they damage:
- *Inclusion safety* — exclusion from information, communication, or decisions signals the person doesn't belong
- *Learner safety* — punishment or ridicule of mistakes, questions, or uncertainty eliminates conditions for growth
- *Contributor safety* — micromanagement, idea dismissal, and credit theft suppress active contribution
- *Challenger safety* — status defense, retaliation for dissent, and rewarding compliance over candor shut down the highest-stakes voice

The stage model shows the sequence of erosion — managers often preserve inclusion while actively destroying contributor and challenger safety.

**Rock — SCARF Threat Triggers in Management (2008, 2009)**
*NeuroLeadership Journal*. Maps specific manager behaviors to five threat domains:
- *Status:* public correction, withholding credit, comparative evaluation, unsolicited feedback
- *Certainty:* sudden changes without rationale, vague expectations, inconsistent feedback
- *Autonomy:* micromanagement, removing decision rights without explanation
- *Relatedness:* exclusion from meetings or communications, visible favoritism, cold or dismissive tone
- *Fairness:* inconsistent rule application, opaque criteria for advancement or recognition

---

### Tier 3 — Practitioner Research (High Credibility, Lower Study Rigor)

**Brené Brown — Armored Leadership (2018)**
*Dare to Lead*. Based on 20 years of qualitative research. Armored leadership behaviors that undermine safety: perfectionism modeled from the top, cynicism as a default response to new ideas, using power-over rather than power-with, avoiding vulnerability in ways that make it unsafe for others to be vulnerable.

**Kim Scott — Radical Candor (2017)**
Research-informed framework. Relevant undermining behaviors: *ruinous empathy* (withholding honest feedback to avoid discomfort — signals the relationship can't handle truth), *obnoxious aggression* (directness without care — produces compliance and concealment), and *manipulative insincerity* (vague, uncommitted feedback that communicates disregard).

---

### Eight Behavior Categories — Cross-Source Synthesis

Across all of the above, the undermining behaviors cluster into eight categories:

1. **Mistake punishment** — overt or implicit; emotional reaction to error signals concealment is safer than disclosure
2. **Certainty performance** — projecting infallibility; never admitting mistakes or uncertainty
3. **Status-based dismissal** — evaluating ideas by source rather than substance
4. **Emotional volatility** — unpredictable reactions create chronic threat states (Kahn's "unpredictability" condition)
5. **Voice theater** — soliciting input with no intention to use it; performative participation
6. **Differential supervision** — over-managing lower-trust employees (Manzoni/Barsoux set-up-to-fail)
7. **Meeting domination** — interrupting, not soliciting input, allowing hierarchy to govern who speaks
8. **Brilliant jerk tolerance** — allowing high-performing individuals to undermine others' safety without consequence (Sutton, *The No Asshole Rule*, 2007)

### Literature Gap — Note for RPA Design

The empirical literature is thinner on subtle and unconscious undermining — the sigh, the slight eye roll, the question that isn't asked. The qualitative work (Detert & Edmondson; Brown) captures this, but the experimental/quantitative literature is less developed here than on overt behavior. This is the territory the implicit layer research above addresses directly: the mechanism by which implicit beliefs produce involuntary micro-behaviors that are discernible — and why the thinness of the explicit-behavior literature does not mean these signals are hard to read. It means they have not yet been the primary target of the quantitative research program.
