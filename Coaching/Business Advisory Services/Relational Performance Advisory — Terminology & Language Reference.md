## Section 1 — Neurobiological Foundations

*The science of how the brain processes stress. This section explains why training doesn't transfer to behavior under pressure — the foundational mechanism behind all of RPA.*

---

### CLIENT-READY

---

#### Window of Tolerance
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Functional zone, regulatory capacity band, the zone where thinking is available
The functional range of nervous system arousal within which a person can think clearly, relate effectively, and access trained skills. Above it (hyperarousal): reactivity, intensity, pursuit. Below it (hypoarousal): flatness, shutdown, withdrawal. Under pressure, managers activate outside this window — which is when the pattern takes over and trained behavior goes offline.
**Say it like this:** *"Every person has a zone they operate in. When they're in it, they can think and relate. When they're outside it — either checked out or ramped up — the behaviors the job requires aren't available. That's not a character problem. It's a stress-response sequence."*
**See also:** Hyperarousal · Hypoarousal · Dysregulation · Activation Threshold · Perry (neurosequential model)

---

#### Dysregulation
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Outside the window, operating from threat, activated state, pattern-running
The state of being outside the Window of Tolerance — either above (escalation, reactivity) or below (withdrawal, flatness). In this state, higher-order thinking is partially or fully offline. The manager is not choosing to behave poorly; the lower brain structures are dominating. This is the core mechanism explaining why training deposits knowledge managers can't access when they most need it.
**Say it like this:** *"When the pressure is real, some managers go quiet — others ramp up. Both are forms of dysregulation. The relational skills we want them to use aren't accessible in that state. The coaching work is about expanding the zone where they can function under pressure."*
**See also:** Window of Tolerance · Hyperarousal · Hypoarousal · Activation Threshold · Pattern Activation

---

#### Hyperarousal
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Above the window, over-activation, fight/flight activation, revved up
State of nervous system activation above the Window of Tolerance. Observable as reactivity, emotional intensity, over-communication, pursuit behavior, difficulty listening. In management contexts: escalation pattern, intensifying when anxious, personalizing disagreement. Often reads as personality or temperament — it is a stress-state.
**Say it like this:** *"When this manager gets anxious, they turn up the volume — over-communicating, pursuing, sometimes pushing hard. That's not who they are; that's where they go when the pressure hits a certain level."*
**See also:** Window of Tolerance · Dysregulation · Escalation Pattern · Sympathetic Activation

---

#### Hypoarousal
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Below the window, shutdown, checked out, emotionally unavailable, going quiet
State of nervous system deactivation below the Window of Tolerance. Observable as flatness, disengagement, withdrawal, cognitive slowing, absence of emotional response. In management contexts: withdrawal pattern, avoiding accountability conversations, going quiet under conflict. Often misread as indifference or incompetence.
**Say it like this:** *"When things get hard, this manager goes quiet — avoids the conversation, delegates upward, or just disappears from the dynamic. That's not indifference. That's where they go under relational threat."*
**See also:** Window of Tolerance · Dysregulation · Withdrawal Pattern · Dorsal Vagal Shutdown

---

#### Co-Regulation
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Nervous system mirroring, relational regulation, calm is contagious
The process by which one person's regulated nervous system influences another's through non-verbal channels — facial expression, tone, pace, posture. A regulated manager transmits safety; a dysregulated manager transmits threat — neither is deliberate. This is the mechanism by which a manager's own state becomes the team's relational environment.
**Say it like this:** *"Your managers' nervous systems are running the room before they say a word. When a manager walks in regulated — calm, present, approachable — the team's nervous systems respond. When a manager walks in activated, the team feels it. That's not metaphor. That's a documented transmission mechanism."*
**See also:** Regulated Presence · Psychological Safety · Dysregulation · Interpersonal Neurobiology

---

### PRACTITIONER ONLY

---

#### Sequential Brain Processing / Hierarchical Brain Activation
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Bottom-up processing, neurosequential model logic, brainstem-first
Perry's core finding: the brain processes experience from lower structures (brainstem/limbic) to higher (cortex), not the other way. Under threat, lower structures dominate — cortical functions go offline. This is the neurobiological explanation for why skills trained in calm conditions don't transfer to behavior under activation. Not pathology — normal design.
**See also:** Neurosequential Model · Window of Tolerance · Dysregulation · Perry

---

#### Neurosequential Model (NMT)
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Regulate-relate-reason sequence, sequential development model
Bruce Perry's structural framework describing the order in which brain systems must be engaged for effective functioning: regulation first (brainstem), then relationship (limbic), then reasoning (cortex). Interventions that lead with reasoning (most management training) skip the first two steps and fail under activation. The RPA coaching sequence follows this order.
**See also:** Sequential Brain Processing · Regulate-Relate-Reason · Perry · Window of Tolerance

---

#### Interpersonal Neurobiology (IPNB)
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Minds shaped by relationship, relational brain science
Daniel Siegel's integrative framework: the brain develops and changes through relationships; mental states are transmitted between people; the mind is both internal and relational. Foundational to RPA's premise that manager relational patterns shape the team's functioning — not as metaphor but as a documented neurobiological process.
**See also:** Co-Regulation · Window of Tolerance · Mindsight · Siegel

---

#### Polyvagal Theory
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Autonomic nervous system hierarchy, three-circuit model
Stephen Porges' framework: three hierarchically organized autonomic circuits govern our social and defensive responses. Ventral vagal = social engagement (safe, connected). Sympathetic = mobilization (fight/flight). Dorsal vagal = immobilization (shutdown, collapse). The system defaults to the highest available circuit; when safety cues are absent, it drops to lower ones. Explains why organizational threat environments disable learning and collaboration.
**See also:** Ventral Vagal · Sympathetic Activation · Dorsal Vagal Shutdown · Neuroception · Co-Regulation

---

#### Neuroception
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Automatic safety scanning, below-conscious threat detection, unconscious risk assessment
Porges' term for the nervous system's automatic, subcortical evaluation of the environment for safety or danger — occurring before conscious awareness. Neuroception determines which circuit activates. The implication: employees and managers are evaluating safety in every interaction before conscious processing begins. A manager's tone, posture, and facial expression are read neurologically before their words land.
**See also:** Polyvagal Theory · Co-Regulation · SCARF · Psychological Safety

---

#### Ventral Vagal Activation / Social Engagement System
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Safe/social state, calm engaged mode
The newest branch of the vagus nerve (evolutionarily); active when the person feels safe. Enables facial expression, prosodic voice, attentive listening, and collaborative engagement. This is the circuit that must be active for learning, psychological safety, and genuine accountability conversations to be possible. Most training delivers content assuming this circuit is active — it often isn't.
**See also:** Polyvagal Theory · Dorsal Vagal Shutdown · Sympathetic Activation · Co-Regulation

---

#### Sympathetic Activation
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Fight/flight response, mobilization, activated state
The autonomic response when social engagement system fails to restore safety — heart rate rises, executive function degrades, defensive behavior produces. Under this activation, the manager's system is preparing for threat response, not collaborative problem-solving. Produces the escalation/hyperarousal behaviors visible in management settings.
**See also:** Polyvagal Theory · Hyperarousal · Escalation Pattern · Activation Threshold

---

#### Dorsal Vagal Shutdown
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Immobilization, collapse, freeze, functional absence
The oldest autonomic circuit (evolutionarily) — activates when fight/flight is unavailable or fails. Produces shutdown: flatness, dissociation, emotional numbness, inability to respond. In management contexts, this is what produces the withdrawal pattern at its most pronounced — not avoidance but actual functional absence. Often misread as indifference.
**See also:** Polyvagal Theory · Hypoarousal · Withdrawal Pattern · Dissociation-Adjacent States

---

#### Somatic Marker / Somatic Encoding
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Body-level belief storage, physiologically encoded belief, gut sense
Damasio's hypothesis: beliefs aren't stored only cognitively — they're encoded as bodily states associated with specific stimuli. A manager's threat response to a particular type of conversation is partly a somatic memory, not just a cognitive pattern. This explains why purely cognitive coaching approaches sometimes hit a ceiling; the belief is in the body as well as the mind.
**See also:** Implicit Belief · Big Assumption · Somatic Experiencing · Body Keeps the Score

---

#### Broca's Area Inhibition / Language Shutdown Under Threat
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Speech production shutdown, language access loss under stress
Van der Kolk's finding: under intense threat activation, Broca's area (responsible for speech and language production) goes offline. The person genuinely cannot articulate their experience coherently — not because they don't want to, but because the neural circuit for language is inhibited. Explains why employees in high-threat environments can't voice concerns even when asked directly.
**See also:** Threat Activation · Dysregulation · Psychological Safety · Voice Behavior

---

#### Sensitization Over Time
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Lowered activation threshold, hair-trigger response system, becoming more reactive
Van der Kolk's mechanism: repeated threat activation lowers the threshold for future activation. A workforce chronically exposed to management dysregulation doesn't just have isolated bad days — the baseline for threat detection progressively lowers. What originally required significant provocation eventually requires very little. This is how organizational culture becomes toxic over time even without a single dramatic incident.
**See also:** Activation Threshold · Chronic Cortisol Elevation · SCARF · Threat Activation

---

#### Chronic Cortisol Elevation
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Prolonged stress response, defended mode, stress that hasn't resolved
When the stress response activates repeatedly without resolution, cortisol remains elevated. The body and mind operate in a defended mode even when immediate threat is absent — recovery time decreases, the activation threshold lowers. In organizational terms: the workforce that can't relax even on a good day. The stress isn't today's event; it's accumulated and hasn't discharged.
**See also:** Sensitization Over Time · Dysregulation · Activation Threshold · Window of Tolerance

## Section 2 — Threat, Safety, and Psychological Safety

*The relational conditions that either enable or destroy productive behavior. SCARF identifies the threat domains managers activate; Psychological Safety defines the team-level outcome RPA is designed to build.*

---

### CLIENT-READY

---

#### Psychological Safety
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Interpersonal safety, safe to speak up, environment where honesty is possible
Amy Edmondson's foundational construct: the shared belief among team members that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking. Consistently the strongest predictor of team learning and performance across industries and cultures. When psychological safety is low, employees conceal errors, suppress voice, and manage impressions rather than problems. The relational patterns RPA targets are precisely the patterns that destroy it.
**Say it like this:** *"The research on what actually makes teams perform is pretty clear — the #1 factor isn't skill, experience, or process. It's whether people feel safe enough to be honest. To raise a problem, admit they don't know, disagree with a decision. When managers' patterns destroy that environment, performance follows."*
**See also:** SCARF · Leader Inclusiveness · Voice Behavior · Co-Regulation · Edmondson

---

#### Status (SCARF Domain)
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Rank, respect, standing, recognition
The SCARF domain: the perception of one's relative standing and importance. Perceived loss of status triggers outsized threat responses. In management contexts: public correction, being passed over, inconsistent recognition, favoritism. When employees feel their status is chronically threatened, they stop volunteering, stop taking risks, and start managing how they look.
**Say it like this:** *"People need to feel respected in their role. When they're corrected in front of others, ignored, or treated inconsistently — their brain reads that as a threat. The defensive behaviors follow automatically."*
**See also:** SCARF · Threat Activation · Psychological Safety · Fairness (SCARF)

---

#### Certainty (SCARF Domain)
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Predictability, clear expectations, knowing what to expect
The SCARF domain: the ability to predict what happens next. The brain consumes significant resources managing uncertainty — when it can't, threat circuits activate. Shifting expectations, unclear performance standards, unpredictable owner decisions all generate certainty threat. Anxious behavior in employees is often certainty restoration behavior: over-communicating, checking, seeking reassurance.
**Say it like this:** *"When people don't know what to expect from their manager or their job, the brain treats that as a threat — the same way it treats physical danger. A lot of what looks like problem behavior is actually people trying to restore some predictability."*
**See also:** SCARF · Autonomy (SCARF) · Threat Activation · Escalation Pattern

---

#### Autonomy (SCARF Domain)
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Control, agency, having a say, ownership
The SCARF domain: the sense of control over one's environment and decisions. Micromanagement, reversed decisions without explanation, and removal of discretion all trigger autonomy threat. Response: surface compliance while actually resisting, passive non-compliance, doing the minimum required. Distinct from SDT autonomy (which is about intrinsic motivation) — SCARF autonomy is about perceived control.
**Say it like this:** *"People need to feel like they have some control over their work. When that's removed — through micromanagement or having decisions reversed without explanation — you get surface agreement and private resistance. They say yes and do something else."*
**See also:** SCARF · SDT Autonomy · Controlled Motivation · Passive Non-Compliance

---

#### Relatedness (SCARF Domain)
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Belonging, inclusion, connection, being part of the team
The SCARF domain: the sense of social connection and inclusion. Social exclusion and isolation activate threat circuits at the same level as physical threat. In management contexts: cliques, favoritism, unresolved team conflict, OM withdrawal from team connection. Exit behavior in hospitality often traces to relatedness threat — people leaving a team, not a company.
**Say it like this:** *"A lot of your turnover is relatedness. People don't leave jobs — they leave teams. When they feel excluded, overlooked, or like they don't matter to the people around them, they're gone — even if the pay is fine."*
**See also:** SCARF · Social Pain · Psychological Safety · Identity Formation · Retention

---

#### Fairness (SCARF Domain)
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Equity, consistent standards, being treated the same as everyone else
The SCARF domain: the perception of equitable treatment. Inconsistent application of standards, visible favoritism, and "different rules for different people" all generate intense fairness threat. The response is often collective — fairness violations spread quickly and produce moral outrage, coalition-building, and disengagement at a team level.
**Say it like this:** *"Fairness violations travel fast. The moment people see one employee treated differently without a clear reason, the whole team's trust is affected. Consistent standards aren't bureaucratic — they're the thing that keeps the relational environment intact."*
**See also:** SCARF · Status (SCARF) · Psychological Safety · Culture Accountability

---

#### Emotional Labor
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Managing emotions at work, performing vs. feeling, emotional performance
Hochschild's concept: when an organization requires specific emotional displays as part of the job (warmth, enthusiasm, patience), workers choose between surface acting (performing the emotion without feeling it) and deep acting (genuinely inducing the feeling). Surface acting predicts burnout and inauthenticity. High-retention hospitality properties produce genuine warmth because workers find real meaning in service — low-retention ones demand the performance.
**Say it like this:** *"Your front-line people are being asked to perform warmth every shift — whether they feel it or not. When the management environment makes that feel hollow or unsafe, the performance shows. Guests pick it up. The research is clear: genuine service follows from a genuine culture, not a script."*
**See also:** Burnout · Relatedness (SCARF) · Identity Formation · Psychological Safety · Hochschild

---

#### Culture Carrier / Culture Transmission
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Who actually shapes the culture people experience, the proximate transmitter
The unit manager — GM, department head, or supervisor — is the proximate transmitter of organizational culture in dispersed operations. Not the owner. Not the brand. Not corporate training. What the new hire experiences as "how things work here" is determined almost entirely by the behavior of the person they directly report to. Programs that bypass the supervisory level don't reach the culture.
**Say it like this:** *"The culture your employees experience isn't the culture you intend. It's the culture your managers carry. Whatever patterns your managers have — regulated or dysregulated, accountable or avoidant — that's what gets transmitted to every person they supervise."*
**See also:** Socialization Tactics · Identity Formation · Management Development · Tracey & Hinkin

---

#### Threat Activation / Being Triggered
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Being triggered, activated state, operating from threat
The activation of threat-response systems in response to SCARF domain violations — status loss, unpredictability, loss of control, exclusion, or unfairness. Produces away-responses (defensive behavior, withdrawal, aggression) rather than toward-responses (engagement, openness). Managers activate threat in their workforce without knowing it, through their own dysregulated patterns.
**Say it like this:** *"Your managers aren't trying to threaten their teams. They're operating from their own stress, and it lands on the workforce as threat. The way a manager delivers feedback, runs a meeting, or responds when something goes wrong either creates safety or activates people. That's what we're measuring."*
**See also:** SCARF · Dysregulation · Psychological Safety · Away Response

---

### PRACTITIONER ONLY

---

#### SCARF Model
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Five social threat domains, organizational threat framework, Rock's threat/reward model
David Rock's framework identifying five social domains (Status, Certainty, Autonomy, Relatedness, Fairness) that activate the brain's threat/reward circuitry identically to physical threat — confirmed by fMRI research (Lieberman & Eisenberger). The model gives a structured vocabulary for mapping how specific manager behaviors activate specific threat responses. Andrew uses SCARF internally to analyze what a manager's pattern is activating in their team.
**See also:** Status · Certainty · Autonomy · Relatedness · Fairness · Threat Activation · Rock

---

#### Away Response / Toward Response
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Threat response vs. reward response, pulling back vs. stepping forward
Rock's distinction: SCARF threats trigger away-responses (withdrawal, defensiveness, reduced cognitive function); SCARF rewards trigger toward-responses (engagement, openness, expanded thinking). Most management behavior that drives turnover activates away-responses — not because managers intend it, but because they're activating SCARF domains in others while in their own dysregulated state.
**See also:** SCARF · Threat Activation · Dysregulation · Psychological Safety

---

#### Implicit Voice Theory / IVTs
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Beliefs about when speaking up is safe, voice-safety assumptions, tacit speaking-up rules
Detert & Edmondson's construct: widely-held, stable beliefs about when speaking to authority is safe or dangerous — formed over a lifetime, largely independent of current conditions. These are the reason employees don't speak up even in environments that explicitly invite it. The manager's expressed openness doesn't override the employee's implicit belief about what happens when they actually speak. Understanding IVTs explains why "open door" policies often produce no useful feedback.
**See also:** Psychological Safety · Voice Behavior · Leader Inclusiveness · Psychological Safety

---

#### Social Pain / Social Exclusion as Pain
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Relational injury, exclusion as damage, belonging deprivation
Eisenberger & Lieberman's fMRI research: social exclusion activates the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex — the same region that processes physical pain. Organizational exclusion and incivility are not merely uncomfortable; they produce genuine neurobiological harm. This is the empirical foundation for treating fairness and relatedness violations as serious operational problems, not interpersonal preferences.
**See also:** Relatedness (SCARF) · Psychological Safety · Incivility Research · SCARF

---

#### Trauma-Informed Practice
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Safety-first organizational design, relational safety framework
Framework applying trauma-informed principles to organizational design and management practice. Six SAMHSA principles: Safety, Trustworthiness, Peer support, Collaboration, Empowerment, Cultural humility. In RPA, this means designing the management development process to avoid re-activating defensive patterns unnecessarily — the coaching relationship itself must provide regulated presence and safety.
**See also:** Sanctuary Model · Psychological Safety · Co-Regulation · SAMHSA

---

#### Sanctuary Model / S.E.L.F. Framework
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Creating sanctuary, structured relational safety system
Sandra Bloom's organizational change intervention translating trauma-informed principles into daily operational structure. S.E.L.F. = Safety, Emotional Management, Loss, Future — four domains addressed in significant interactions. The community meeting (pre-shift check-in) is the most directly applicable practice for hospitality. Andrew uses Sanctuary Model concepts to design the structural recommendations component.
**See also:** Trauma-Informed Practice · Community Meeting · S.E.L.F. · Bloom

## Section 3 — Motivation and Psychological Needs

*Self-Determination Theory (SDT) explains what produces genuine motivation vs. surface compliance. Critical for designing the intervention and understanding why employees disengage even when they like the job.*

---

### CLIENT-READY

---

#### Autonomy (SDT Need)
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Volition, acting from genuine choice, self-initiated action
One of three universal basic psychological needs (Deci & Ryan). The experience of acting from genuine choice rather than external compulsion. When managers provide rationale, offer meaningful choice within constraints, and minimize control/surveillance, autonomy is satisfied. When managers micromanage, reverse decisions without explanation, or use pressure-based accountability, autonomy is frustrated — producing passive non-compliance or surface agreement.
**Say it like this:** *"People perform differently when they're choosing to do something versus when they feel forced. The specific behaviors that satisfy that — giving people a real reason, offering choices within limits, not hovering — are trainable. And the research shows effects within weeks."*
**See also:** Competence (SDT) · Relatedness (SDT) · Autonomy-Supportive Management · Autonomous Motivation · Need Frustration

---

#### Competence (SDT Need)
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Effectiveness, mastery, feeling capable
One of three basic psychological needs. The experience of effectiveness and mastery in interactions with the environment. Satisfied by: clear informational (not evaluative) feedback, achievable goals with structured support, strength identification before correction. Frustrated by: vague feedback, unattainable standards, correction without support. When competence need is chronically frustrated, effort drops — because effort produces no felt effectiveness.
**Say it like this:** *"People stop trying when they consistently feel ineffective — when feedback is either absent or only critical, when expectations keep shifting, when there's no sense of getting better. Specific manager behaviors address that. Not 'be encouraging' — concrete things that help people feel capable."*
**See also:** Autonomy (SDT) · Relatedness (SDT) · Competence-Supportive Management · Need Satisfaction

---

#### Relatedness (SDT Need)
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Connection, belonging, being genuinely cared for
One of three basic psychological needs. The experience of meaningful connection to others and feeling valued. Satisfied by: regular one-on-one contact, genuine interest in the person beyond their role, team cohesion support, appropriate personal sharing. Frustrated by: transactional management, indifference to the person's experience, isolation. Relatedness need frustration is consistently the strongest predictor of early departure in hospitality.
**Say it like this:** *"The single strongest predictor of early departure in your industry is whether people feel their manager actually cares about them as a person. Not as an employee. As a person. That's not soft — it's one of the most replicated findings in organizational research."*
**See also:** Autonomy (SDT) · Competence (SDT) · Relatedness-Supportive Management · Retention · Socialization

---

#### Autonomy-Supportive Management
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Choice-giving, rationale-providing, non-controlling management
The set of specific management behaviors that satisfy the autonomy need: providing a meaningful rationale for directives, offering genuine choices within constraints, using non-controlling language, acknowledging employee perspectives before redirecting. These behaviors are trainable (multiple RCTs show effects within weeks), observable, and distinct from personality style. Autonomy support produces autonomous motivation; its absence produces compliance or withdrawal.
**Say it like this:** *"The behaviors are specific. It's not 'be a better leader.' It's: tell people why a directive matters, give them options where you can, and hear their reaction before redirecting. Managers can learn those things in a short window — and the data shows the team responds within weeks."*
**See also:** Autonomy (SDT) · Competence-Supportive Management · Relatedness-Supportive Management · Need Satisfaction

---

#### Intrinsic Motivation
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Doing it because it matters, genuine interest, internal drive
Highest-quality form of motivation: behavior performed because the activity is inherently valued. Produces persistence, higher performance quality, and resilience under difficulty. Develops when autonomy, competence, and relatedness needs are consistently satisfied. Critically: intrinsic motivation cannot be directly installed by management — it grows when the conditions are right. Management's job is to build those conditions.
**Say it like this:** *"You can't make someone care. But you can build an environment where caring becomes natural. That's what the SDT research shows — needs-satisfaction creates the conditions where intrinsic motivation develops. It's not magic. It's design."*
**See also:** Autonomous Motivation · Controlled Motivation · Need Satisfaction · SDT

---

### PRACTITIONER ONLY

---

#### Self-Determination Theory (SDT)
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Basic psychological needs theory, motivation science framework
Deci & Ryan's macro-theory of human motivation. Core claim: three universal psychological needs (autonomy, competence, relatedness) whose satisfaction predicts autonomous motivation, well-being, and growth; whose frustration predicts controlled motivation, ill-being, and maladaptive defenses. Most empirically supported theory of human motivation; replicated across cultures, industries, and age groups. Foundational to the SDT-based manager behavior clusters in RPA.
**See also:** Autonomy (SDT) · Competence (SDT) · Relatedness (SDT) · Autonomous Motivation · Need Frustration

---

#### Autonomous Motivation / Autonomous Regulatory Style
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Self-determined motivation, internalized motivation, choosing to
SDT motivational continuum: behavior experienced as self-initiated. Includes intrinsic (inherent interest), integrated (aligned with identity), and identified (personally valued) types. Predicts persistence, quality performance, and well-being. The development goal for RPA management development — not compliance-level behavior change but autonomous-level commitment.
**See also:** Intrinsic Motivation · Controlled Motivation · Need Satisfaction · SDT

---

#### Controlled Motivation / Controlled Regulatory Style
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Compelled motivation, doing it to avoid consequences, external pressure
SDT motivational continuum: behavior driven by external reward/punishment or internal pressure (guilt, shame). Produces lower performance quality, fragility under unmonitored conditions, burnout, and higher turnover. Most management accountability systems produce controlled motivation — which is why behavior holds while someone is watching and reverts when they're not.
**See also:** Autonomous Motivation · Need Frustration · External Regulation · SDT

---

#### Need Frustration
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Needs blocked, active need undermining, conditions that cut against people
SDT construct: the active blocking or undermining of autonomy, competence, or relatedness needs — distinct from merely unsatisfied needs. Need frustration independently predicts psychological ill-being, rigidity, and maladaptive defenses (withdrawal, aggression, passivity). The environments generating RPA's target problems — high turnover, conflict, disengagement — are typically need-frustrating, not merely need-neutral.
**See also:** Need Satisfaction · SDT · SCARF · Dysregulation

---

## Section 4 — Change Science and Learning

*Why adults don't change even when they want to, and what actually produces lasting behavioral change. This section explains why training fails and what the RPA coaching arc does instead.*

---

### CLIENT-READY

---

#### Competing Commitment
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Hidden priority, unconscious goal, the thing that overrides the stated goal
Kegan & Lahey's construct: alongside every stated commitment to change, a person holds a competing commitment — equally real, usually unconscious — that overrides the stated goal under pressure. A manager who says they want to give direct feedback may hold a competing commitment to not being the person who makes things worse. Both are real. Training addresses the stated commitment; the competing one runs untouched.
**Say it like this:** *"Your managers don't lack the right intentions. They hold competing ones. A manager who avoids accountability isn't lazy — they're protecting something equally important to them. Until coaching surfaces that, you can train the behavior all day and it won't stick."*
**See also:** Immunity to Change · Big Assumption · Stages of Change · Defensive Routine

---

#### Stages of Change / Readiness to Change
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Where someone is in the change process, developmental readiness
Prochaska's framework: change readiness progresses through stages — Pre-Contemplation (doesn't see it as their problem), Contemplation (sees it, ambivalent), Preparation (committed, building a plan), Action (actively practicing), Maintenance (sustaining). Giving action-stage tools to a pre-contemplation leader produces compliance performance without change. The RPA assessment identifies each leader's current stage so the coaching matches it.
**Say it like this:** *"One of the primary reasons development programs don't hold is that the same tools get given to everyone, regardless of where they are. A manager who doesn't yet see the pattern needs completely different work than a manager who sees it clearly but doesn't know how to change it."*
**See also:** Pre-Contemplation · Contemplation · Action Stage · Competing Commitment · Coaching Arc

---

#### Pre-Contemplation
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Not seeing it as their issue, externalizing causes, blaming the environment
Prochaska's first stage: the person does not see their behavior as a problem; outcomes are attributed to others, circumstances, or the organization. In management contexts: "My team doesn't perform because they're not motivated" (without examining what produced that). The appropriate intervention is personalized feedback linking behavior to specific outcomes — not skill training, not confrontation.
**Say it like this:** *"Some of your managers genuinely don't see how their behavior is producing the outcomes you're seeing. That's not dishonesty — it's a stage. The intervention for that stage is different from anything you've tried before."*
**See also:** Stages of Change · Contemplation · Competing Commitment · Self-Awareness Gap

---

### PRACTITIONER ONLY

---

#### Immunity to Change / ITC Process
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** The system that makes current behavior feel necessary, competing commitment system
Kegan & Lahey's methodology: most change failure is not skill deficit or motivation deficit — it is immunity to change, an unconscious system of competing commitments and big assumptions that make the current behavior feel necessary for survival. The four-column map surfaces this system: stated commitment → undermining behaviors → competing commitment → big assumption. Andrew uses this in the coaching arc for leaders whose stated commitment and actual behavior are significantly misaligned.
**See also:** Competing Commitment · Big Assumption · Behavioral Experiment · Stages of Change · Kegan

---

#### Big Assumption
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Core belief driving the competing commitment, the assumption beneath the assumption
In ITC process: the foundational belief that makes the competing commitment necessary. Example: competing commitment = "don't be the person who makes things worse." Big assumption = "If I deliver hard feedback, I will damage the relationship permanently." Big assumptions are treated as facts rather than hypotheses; they've never been tested. The coaching process designs behavioral experiments to test them safely.
**See also:** Immunity to Change · Competing Commitment · Behavioral Experiment · Theory-in-Use

---

#### Behavioral Experiment / Big Assumption Experiment
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Testing assumptions, safe-stakes trial, trying something different to see what happens
A designed behavioral test in which the leader acts against their competing commitment in controlled, low-stakes conditions to generate disconfirming evidence about the big assumption. The goal is not to prove the assumption wrong — it is to generate genuine data about what actually happens. Multiple experiments over time accumulate evidence that the assumption is not a fact.
**See also:** Big Assumption · Immunity to Change · Experiential Learning · Kolb

---

#### Motivational Interviewing (MI)
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Evoking change motivation, ambivalence-respecting conversation, collaborative change approach
Miller & Rollnick's framework: change motivation is evoked, not installed. The practitioner's role is to elicit the person's own reasons for change — not to convince, argue, or prescribe. Ambivalence is treated as normal, not resistance. The OARS skill set (Open questions, Affirming, Reflecting, Summarizing) governs the relational posture of every RPA coaching session. The spirit: partnership, acceptance, evocation, compassion.
**See also:** OARS · Stages of Change · Competing Commitment · Coaching Relationship

---

#### Double-Loop Learning / Single-Loop Learning
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Governing belief revision vs. error correction, question-the-assumption learning
Argyris & Schön: single-loop learning detects and corrects errors within existing governing beliefs (doing the same thing better). Double-loop learning questions and revises the governing beliefs themselves (doing a different thing because the belief changed). Most management training produces single-loop learning at best. RPA coaching aims for double-loop: the leader's foundational belief about what the situation requires is examined and revised.
**See also:** Theory-in-Use · Defensive Routine · Immunity to Change · Competing Commitment

---

#### Theory-in-Use
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Actual operating belief, automatic behavior generator, what's really driving it
Argyris's distinction: espoused theory (what the leader says they believe and do) vs. theory-in-use (the actual beliefs generating behavior). Gaps between the two are often invisible to the person holding them. The narrative interview and coaching process surface theory-in-use — not by confronting the leader, but by examining specific past incidents in enough detail that the operating belief becomes visible.
**See also:** Double-Loop Learning · Defensive Routine · Competing Commitment · Narrative Interview

---

#### Defensive Routine
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Self-protective pattern, awareness-blocking behavior, rationalization sequence
Argyris: automatic patterns that protect people from experiencing the gap between their espoused beliefs and their operating beliefs. Self-sealing — they prevent the awareness that would allow correction. In coaching: a leader who consistently reframes disconfirming feedback (exception, condition, partial credit, reframe) is running a defensive routine. The coaching sequence is designed to gently interrupt these patterns over multiple sessions.
**See also:** Theory-in-Use · Double-Loop Learning · Big Assumption · Rationalization

---

#### Experiential Learning Cycle
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Full-cycle learning, learn by doing, Kolb's cycle
Kolb's framework: learning that transfers to behavior requires a complete cycle — concrete experience → reflective observation → abstract conceptualization → active experimentation. Training that skips experience and reflection delivers conceptualization and expects behavior to follow. It doesn't. RPA's facilitated observation session and coaching sessions are designed around the full cycle.
**See also:** Behavioral Experiment · Anticipatory Coaching · Retrospective Review · Kolb

## Section 5 — Organizational Culture and Identity

*How culture forms, how it transmits, and what actually changes it. Essential for the structural recommendations phase and for explaining why management-only approaches don't produce lasting culture change.*

---

### CLIENT-READY

---

#### Organizational Culture (Three-Level Model)
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** How things actually work here, the real operating system
Schein's model: culture operates at three levels — artifacts (visible behaviors and structures), espoused values (what the organization says it believes), and underlying assumptions (the actual, unconscious beliefs driving behavior). Most culture programs target levels 1–2 and produce surface change. Culture change that holds reaches level 3 — the assumption layer. This is where identity forms.
**Say it like this:** *"The culture you have isn't the one on your website. It's the one your managers carry in their nervous systems — what they assume about accountability, authority, and people. The visible behaviors are a symptom. The structural recommendations we deliver target the conditions that sustain the assumption layer."*
**See also:** Artifacts · Espoused Values · Underlying Assumptions · Identity Formation · Schein

---

#### Identity Formation / Organizational Identification
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Sense of belonging, feeling like they belong here, organizational membership meaning
The process by which employees come to see themselves as genuine members of the organization — not just workers there. Strong organizational identification predicts 40–60% lower turnover in hospitality (Kusluvan) and independently predicts guest satisfaction scores (Bowen & Schneider). Identity forms through the assumption layer, through veteran modeling, and through investiture (being welcomed as-is rather than having prior identity stripped). It does not form through orientation materials.
**Say it like this:** *"Your retention isn't primarily a compensation problem. It's an identity problem. People don't fight to stay somewhere they don't feel they belong. When the culture and the management make them feel genuinely valued and part of something, they fight to stay. The research is remarkably consistent on this."*
**See also:** Organizational Culture · Culture Carrier · Socialization Tactics · Retention · Ashforth & Mael

---

#### Socialization Tactics
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** How new employees are brought into the culture, onboarding culture transmission
Van Maanen & Schein's finding: how a new employee is brought into an organization determines how they relate to it. Formal, serial socialization (veteran modeling the culture for the newcomer) produces stronger identification than informal, individual approaches. Investiture tactics (affirming the newcomer's identity) produce higher commitment than divestiture (stripping prior identity). Translation: who your new hire interacts with in the first 30 days, and how those people treat them, matters more than the orientation content.
**Say it like this:** *"The onboarding question isn't what you're teaching them. It's who they're learning from — and whether that person makes them feel welcomed or evaluated. The research shows that's what determines whether they stay past 90 days."*
**See also:** Identity Formation · Culture Carrier · Retention · Onboarding Design

---

### PRACTITIONER ONLY

---

#### Underlying Assumptions (Cultural Level 3)
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Actual operating beliefs, what people really think is true, the assumption layer
Schein's deepest culture level: unconscious, taken-for-granted beliefs about how the organization really works — authority, accountability, human nature, risk. These are not articulated; they're demonstrated in behavior. Culture change that doesn't reach this layer produces compliance at levels 1–2 and leaves the actual operating system unchanged. The narrative interview accesses assumptions more directly than any survey.
**See also:** Organizational Culture · Espoused Values · Theory-in-Use · Narrative Coherence

---

#### Surface Acting / Deep Acting
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Performing emotions vs. feeling them, managed service vs. genuine service
Hochschild's distinction within emotional labor. Surface acting: suppress genuine feeling, display required feeling. Produces inauthenticity, faster burnout, and guest-detectable performativity. Deep acting: genuinely induce the required emotion through cognitive and physiological effort. Produces more authentic interaction but is still demanding. Genuine acting (not performance at all — feeling genuinely aligned with the interaction) is what identity formation enables.
**See also:** Emotional Labor · Identity Formation · Burnout · Hochschild

---

#### Secondary Traumatic Stress (STS) / Vicarious Traumatization
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Absorbing others' trauma, compassion fatigue, secondhand exposure harm
Exposure to others' distress or trauma produces its own stress response — particularly in helping-oriented roles. In hospitality, high-intensity guest interactions and witnessing colleagues' mistreatment produce cumulative secondary traumatic stress. This is distinct from burnout (need depletion) and contributes to the pattern-activation observed in long-tenured staff. Andrew notes this in assessment; scope boundary = refer to clinical professionals for treatment.
**See also:** Burnout · Emotional Labor · Trauma-Informed Practice · Scope Boundary

## Section 6 — Behavioral Patterns

*The four-dimension RPA framework — observable behavioral patterns under relational pressure. This is the core diagnostic language of the engagement.*

---

### CLIENT-READY

---

#### Withdrawal Pattern / Relational Withdrawal
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Going quiet, avoiding, disappearing under pressure, accountability avoidance
RPA Dimension 1: under relational pressure, this manager goes quiet. Avoids accountability conversations, delegates difficult feedback upward, exits emotionally from conflict situations. Below the Window of Tolerance (hypoarousal). Organizational cost: feedback vacuum, early departure of high performers who need honest input, and problems that escalate because they're never addressed directly. Most common pattern in hospitality management.
**Say it like this:** *"This manager knows what needs to be said. When the moment comes to say it — especially if there's conflict involved — they go somewhere else. The problem gets handed upstairs, or it just doesn't get addressed. The team notices, and it shapes how safe they feel to raise issues themselves."*
**See also:** Hypoarousal · Dysregulation · Accountability Delivery · Collaborative Capacity · Window of Tolerance

---

#### Escalation Pattern / Relational Escalation
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Turning up the intensity, pursuing, over-communicating under pressure
RPA Dimension 2: under relational pressure or anxiety, this manager becomes more intense — over-communicates, seeks reassurance, personalizes disagreement, escalates when not responded to immediately. Above the Window of Tolerance (hyperarousal). Organizational cost: team becomes guarded, stalls decisions to avoid triggering the manager, suppresses honest feedback to manage the manager's emotional state.
**Say it like this:** *"When this manager gets anxious, they don't go quiet — they pursue. More communication, more urgency, sometimes more pressure. The team learns to manage them rather than manage the problem. That's a significant drain on decision quality and honest feedback."*
**See also:** Hyperarousal · Dysregulation · Psychological Safety · Window of Tolerance · Collaborative Capacity

---

#### Disorganization Pattern / Authority Disorganization
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Inconsistency with authority, role confusion, unpredictable leadership, cycling behavior
RPA Dimension 3: inconsistent relationship with authority — cycles between compliance/submission and defiance/resistance, unclear role boundaries, performs accountability without internalizing it. Produces the most culture instability of the four dimensions because the team cannot predict the manager's response. In some cycles, standards are rigidly enforced; in others, they're abandoned. The team adapts by managing impressions rather than managing performance.
**Say it like this:** *"This is the hardest one for teams to navigate — because they can't predict it. Sometimes the manager holds people accountable, sometimes they don't, and the team can't read which mode they're in. So they play it safe. They manage the relationship instead of doing the work."*
**See also:** Window of Tolerance · Disorganization · Psychological Safety · Culture Stability · Escalation Pattern

---

#### Collaborative Capacity
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Staying regulated under pressure, relational effectiveness, the benchmark
RPA Dimension 4: the manager functions within the Window of Tolerance under activation — stays regulated, delivers feedback directly, repairs ruptures without drama, takes accountability without collapsing or escalating. This is not calmness or compliance — it's functional capacity under genuine relational pressure. It is the development target for all three prior dimensions.
**Say it like this:** *"What we're building toward is a management team that can stay in the conversation when things get hard. Not by suppressing their reaction — by having enough capacity that the conversation stays productive even when it's difficult. That's measurable and developable."*
**See also:** Window of Tolerance · Regulated Presence · Psychological Safety · Accountability Delivery

---

#### Activation Threshold
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** What triggers the pattern, stress level required to activate behavior, the tipping point
The level of relational pressure required to activate a leader's pattern — how much stress it takes before the withdrawal, escalation, or disorganization shows up. Low threshold: pattern activates at mild friction. High threshold: pattern activates only under significant pressure. Part of every individual profile; critical for understanding which operational conditions will predictably produce the behavior the owner is seeing.
**Say it like this:** *"The profile tells us not just what pattern a manager shows, but what it takes to trigger it. That matters for intervention — because a manager who only falls apart under severe pressure needs different work than one whose pattern shows up at the first sign of difficulty."*
**See also:** Behavioral Pattern · Window of Tolerance · Dysregulation · Pattern Profile

---

#### Regulatory Resources
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Built-in capacity, what the leader already does well, development foundation
The existing strengths and capacities a leader brings into the coaching — what they already do well under pressure that can be expanded and built upon. Every profile includes regulatory resources alongside pattern identification. The development approach leads with capacity rather than deficit — which directly serves buy-in and produces more durable change.
**Say it like this:** *"We don't hand someone a list of everything they're doing wrong. We identify what they're already doing that works — because that's the foundation the development is built on."*
**See also:** Behavioral Pattern Profile · Collaborative Capacity · Coaching Arc

---

### PRACTITIONER ONLY

---

#### Four Behavioral Dimensions / Four-Dimension Framework
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** RPA pattern classification, four-dimension assessment framework
The RPA-specific behavioral assessment structure: Withdrawal, Escalation, Disorganization, Collaborative Capacity. Mapped to Siegel's Window of Tolerance states (hypoarousal, hyperarousal, cycling, functional window). All assessment language is behavioral and observable; no clinical labels used with leaders. The framework is Andrew's internal diagnostic lens — presented to leaders and sponsors in plain behavioral language.
**See also:** Withdrawal Pattern · Escalation Pattern · Disorganization Pattern · Collaborative Capacity

---

#### Narrative Coherence
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Story holds together, account consistency, structural integrity of the telling
Narrative interview diagnostic marker: does the account hold together coherently, or does it collapse into vagueness, blame externalization, contradiction, or emotional flooding? Coherence reflects narrative organization and self-awareness. Incoherence signals that the relational event was activated, not processed — the person has not achieved narrative integration of the experience. Directly related to Mary Main's Adult Attachment Interview research.
**See also:** Narrative Interview · Affect Regulation (Assessment) · Self-Other Balance · Narrative Interview

---

#### Affect Regulation (in Assessment Context)
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Staying regulated while recounting, emotional stability during the interview
Narrative interview diagnostic marker: does the person remain regulated while recounting high-stakes relational events, or do they become flooded (hyperarousal) or flat (hypoarousal)? This is not measured by what they report about their emotions — it's observed in real time during the interview. A leader who becomes activated while describing a past event is showing you what happens when those conditions recur in the present.
**See also:** Narrative Interview · Narrative Coherence · Window of Tolerance · Self-Other Balance

---

#### Self-Other Balance
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Integration of self and others as agents, perspective balance, accountability proportion
Narrative interview diagnostic marker: does the account integrate both the leader's own role and others' roles as genuine causal agents? Or does the account collapse in one direction — all self (over-responsibility, catastrophizing) or all other (blame externalization, no accountability)? Self-other integration reflects perspective-taking capacity and genuine accountability; its absence predicts specific patterns in leadership behavior.
**See also:** Narrative Interview · Narrative Coherence · Accountability Delivery · Theory-in-Use

---

#### Rater Consensus / Self-Rater Divergence
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Agreement across raters, gap between self-report and others, blind spot signal
360 output: where rater consensus (supervisor + peers + direct reports) converges, the pattern is confirmed. Where self-rating diverges significantly from rater consensus (≥2.0 points), that gap is itself the most diagnostic data — it identifies a blind spot. A leader who rates themselves high on accountability delivery while direct reports rate them low has a pattern operating beneath conscious awareness. The divergence is the coaching entry point.
**See also:** Multi-Rater 360 · Behavioral Pattern Profile · Self-Awareness Gap · Coaching Arc

## Section 7 — Assessment Methods and Tools

*The three-source assessment architecture and the individual/aggregate deliverables it produces.*

---

### CLIENT-READY

---

#### Behavioral Pattern Profile
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Individual relational pattern assessment, four-dimension profile, individual report
The primary individual assessment deliverable: a 2–3 page document per leader, built from all three assessment sources. Sections: dimensional ratings with behavioral narrative, activation profile (threshold + SCARF domain profile + regulatory resources), and development implications (operational cost, development outcome, behavioral targets, coaching entry point). Delivered privately to the leader — not to the sponsor. The leader retains ownership.
**Say it like this:** *"Each leader gets their own profile — specific to how they function, what triggers their pattern, what it costs the team, and what development for them looks like. They receive it privately. You don't. What you receive is the aggregate picture — what your team looks like as a whole."*
**See also:** Team Aggregate Pattern Map · Coaching Arc · Four Behavioral Dimensions · Confidentiality Structure

---

#### Team Aggregate Pattern Map
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Team-level pattern analysis, what your management team shows collectively, sponsor deliverable
The assessment deliverable delivered to the sponsor (owner): pattern distribution across the assessed leaders, collective activation threshold and trigger conditions, structural observations, and development priorities — in operational language, without individual attribution. The owner sees the team picture; individual profiles remain confidential to each leader. This is the basis for structural recommendations.
**Say it like this:** *"What you get is the picture of your management team as a whole — what patterns are most prevalent, what situations trigger them, and what that means for how you structure the operation. Individual profiles stay between me and each leader. That's how the assessment stays honest."*
**See also:** Behavioral Pattern Profile · Structural Recommendations · Confidentiality Structure · Sponsor Debrief

---

#### Multi-Rater 360 / Behavioral 360
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** 360-degree feedback, multi-source feedback, behavioral feedback from multiple directions
Assessment method: structured survey completed by the leader's supervisor, peers, and direct reports (minimum 3 per group). Every item is a specific, observable behavior under pressure — not personality, not generic competency. Five behavioral clusters: accountability delivery, regulation under pressure, feedback receptivity, relational repair, authority clarity. Self-rating is collected separately; divergence from rater consensus is the primary diagnostic signal.
**Say it like this:** *"The feedback comes from people above, beside, and below each leader — on specific behavioral items, not opinions. The most useful data is the gap between how the leader sees themselves and how everyone else sees them. That gap is usually where the pattern lives."*
**See also:** Behavioral Pattern Profile · Rater Consensus · Self-Rater Divergence · Narrative Interview

---

### PRACTITIONER ONLY

---

#### Narrative Interview / Structured Narrative Interview
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Structured conversation, relational history interview, 45-minute diagnostic conversation
RPA Layer 1 assessment: 45–60 minute structured conversation about three significant relational incidents (accountability delivery, feedback that didn't land, difficult supervisor relationship). The diagnostic value is in *how* the person speaks — not the content of the events. Andrew tracks narrative coherence, affect regulation, and self-other balance in real time. Conducted before other methods to avoid priming.
**See also:** Narrative Coherence · Affect Regulation (Assessment) · Self-Other Balance · Narrative Interview Facilitation Guide

---

#### Facilitated Observation Session
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Designed meeting, controlled pressure observation, activation session
RPA Layer 1 and Layer 3 tool: a structured half-day session in which Andrew facilitates scenarios designed to create genuine relational pressure — peer challenge, authority challenge, repair moment. He observes what each leader actually does under pressure (not self-report, not rater report). Productive only after leaders have developed enough self-awareness (Stages 1–3 of coaching) to debrief against what happened. Also used as a Layer 3 intervention for team facilitation.
**See also:** Behavioral Pattern · Coaching Arc · Team Facilitation · Retrospective Review

---

#### Behavior Cluster
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Related behavior grouping, observable behavior category
RPA 360 structural element: observable behaviors grouped into five themed clusters for the instrument — accountability delivery, regulation under pressure, feedback receptivity, relational repair, authority clarity. Each cluster has 5 items; items are scored on a 5-point frequency scale by raters. Cluster means drive the profile pattern mapping.
**See also:** Multi-Rater 360 · Behavioral Pattern Profile · Four Behavioral Dimensions

## Section 8 — Coaching and Intervention Stages

*The arc from assessment to lasting change. Each stage is distinct; skipping a stage is a failure mode.*

---

### CLIENT-READY

---

#### Psychoeducation Stage
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Building language, introducing the framework, naming what's happening
RPA coaching Stage 1: introduce the behavioral framework in accessible, non-clinical language. Goal is vocabulary and naming capacity — not insight, not behavior change. When a leader can name their pattern, they can begin to observe it. Without this stage, coaching produces either defensiveness (the person has no framework to make sense of the feedback) or intellectualization (they understand the concept but don't apply it to themselves).
**Say it like this:** *"The first sessions aren't about changing anything. They're about giving the leader a language for what's happening — so when the pattern activates, they can name it rather than just live in it. That naming capacity is the foundation everything else builds on."*
**See also:** Retrospective Review · Competing Commitment · Stages of Change · Coaching Arc

---

#### Retrospective Review Stage
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Looking back at times that went wrong, mapping the pattern in their own story
RPA coaching Stage 2: work through specific past incidents with the leader — not to rehash or blame, but to map the pattern in their own story using the framework language built in Stage 1. This stage typically produces the richest diagnostic data because the leader brings their own incidents; Andrew's role is to observe what the retelling reveals. Yields activation triggers, competing commitments, and the first evidence of self-awareness depth.
**Say it like this:** *"Once a leader has language for the pattern, we start looking at their own history — specific times the pattern showed up, what triggered it, what happened. That's where they start to see themselves clearly."*
**See also:** Psychoeducation Stage · Anticipatory Coaching Stage · Competing Commitment · Narrative Interview

---

#### Anticipatory Coaching Stage
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** Preparing for a tough conversation, behavioral rehearsal, planning for situations
RPA coaching Stage 3: identify upcoming high-stakes situations and work through them before they occur. Convert self-awareness into behavioral experiment in advance. This stage is where coaching becomes operational — the leader is no longer analyzing past events but preparing to function differently in a future one. Produces the first behavioral experiments that generate real data about competing commitments and big assumptions.
**Say it like this:** *"We stop looking backward and start looking forward. There's a conversation coming — or a situation that always goes sideways. We work through it in advance: what'll get activated, what the competing commitment is, what you're going to try differently."*
**See also:** Retrospective Review Stage · Behavioral Experiment · Stages of Change · Competing Commitment

---

#### Structural Recommendations
*Client-Ready*
**Also called:** The systems adjustments that make it hold, operational changes that reinforce behavior change
The final deliverable of Phase 1: specific operational changes that reinforce what changes in the leaders. Covers: culture accountability structure for GMs, onboarding design (belonging infrastructure, not just job training), early warning systems for disengagement, supervisory accountability structures, and culture measurement producing leading rather than lagging indicators. Structural recommendations without behavior change produce compliance; behavior change without structural support reverts in 18 months.
**Say it like this:** *"The behavior changes we produce in the coaching need the environment to sustain them. The structural recommendations are what changes the environment — so the operation reinforces what the leaders are building, instead of pulling against it."*
**See also:** Culture Carrier · Socialization Tactics · Team Facilitation · Tracey & Hinkin

---

### PRACTITIONER ONLY

---

#### Coaching Arc (12-Session)
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** 12-session individual development arc, structured coaching engagement
RPA individual development design: 12 monthly sessions per leader, progressing through four stages (Psychoeducation → Retrospective Review → Anticipatory Coaching → Facilitated Observation). Stage sequencing is non-negotiable — observation is an intervention tool, not an assessment tool, and only becomes productive after Stages 1–3 have built language and self-awareness. Sessions are calibrated to each leader's pattern and Prochaska stage.
**See also:** Psychoeducation Stage · Retrospective Review Stage · Anticipatory Coaching Stage · Stages of Change

---

#### Team Facilitation Arc
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Three-session team development arc, group relational practice
Three structured facilitated sessions with the management team together: peer challenge session, authority challenge session, repair session. Designed to create conditions where individual coaching changes get tested against real team dynamics. Andrew facilitates and observes in real time; old patterns will re-emerge and are used as live coaching material. Runs parallel to individual coaching arc, typically after Sessions 4–6.
**See also:** Facilitated Observation Session · Psychological Safety · Coaching Arc · Team Aggregate Pattern Map

---

#### Scope Boundary / Non-Clinical Scope
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** What Andrew does vs. doesn't do, clinical referral threshold
RPA explicitly operates within a non-clinical scope: organizational consulting and coaching, not clinical assessment or therapy. Clinical referral threshold: active suicidal ideation, psychosis, substance dependency affecting functioning, or trauma presentation requiring clinical treatment. If a leader presents with clear clinical need, Andrew holds the coaching boundary and provides appropriate referral. This boundary is stated in the Individual Confidentiality Agreement and the Scope Agreement.
**See also:** Confidentiality Structure · Individual Confidentiality Agreement · Clinical vs. Non-Clinical Language

---

#### Confidentiality Structure / Three-Party Confidentiality
*Practitioner Only*
**Also called:** Who gets what, individual vs. aggregate data division
RPA confidentiality architecture: individual interview, 360, and coaching content are confidential between Andrew and the individual leader. The sponsor receives only aggregate data (Team Aggregate Pattern Map) and structural recommendations — never individual profiles or specific disclosures. This structure is what makes honest assessment possible; leaders will not be authentic if they believe the owner sees their individual data.
**See also:** Individual Confidentiality Agreement · Scope Agreement · Behavioral Pattern Profile · Team Aggregate Pattern Map
