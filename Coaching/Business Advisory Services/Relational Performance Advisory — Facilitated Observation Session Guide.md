# Relational Performance Advisory — Facilitated Observation Session Guide

**What this is:** Practitioner guide for designing and running the facilitated observation session — the third and final assessment method. Covers pre-session preparation, positioning, session design phase by phase, the three activation sequences, observation protocol during the session, and post-session documentation.

**The dual function:** Andrew is simultaneously facilitating a leadership session and conducting structured behavioral observation. These two roles require different skills and they create a genuine tension. This guide is designed around that tension — how to create the activation conditions that generate diagnostic data while maintaining the facilitator's credibility and the group's trust.

**Session duration:** Half-day — 3 to 3.5 hours including breaks.

**Position to leaders as:** *"A leadership team alignment workshop — working through a real challenge together and building some shared language for how this team operates."*

**Do not position it as:** An assessment, an evaluation, or part of the diagnostic process. The observation function is not hidden — it simply isn't the frame leaders hold while participating.

**Timing in the engagement:** Phase 2, weeks 9–10. All narrative interviews complete. 360 data collection underway (not yet closed). This is the last data-collection event before interpretation begins.

---

## Pre-Session Preparation

*Complete before the session. This preparation is what makes real-time observation possible — you cannot observe and retrieve at the same time.*

### Leader Preparation — One Review Per Leader (30–45 minutes total)

For each leader attending, pull their narrative interview checklist and 360 data and record these three things on a single reference sheet you keep on a small notepad during the session:

| Leader | Predicted primary pattern | Most sensitive SCARF domain | One specific observation question |
|---|---|---|---|
| [Name] | Withdrawal / Escalation / Disorganization | S / C / A / R / F | [e.g., "Does she initiate repair after the peer challenge or exit?"] |
| [Name] | | | |
| [Name] | | | |

The specific observation question focuses your attention during the session. You cannot track everything about everyone simultaneously — the pre-loaded questions direct observation to the highest-yield moments per leader.

**Flag any leader where narrative interview and 360 data diverge significantly.** The facilitated observation is particularly valuable for these cases — it provides a third data point for the discrepancy.

### Scenario Customization

The session requires a case scenario built around a real operational problem with no clean answer. Use the template scenario below (designed for hospitality) and customize it for this client's actual context before the session.

**Template scenario — "The Standard Response"**

> A property has been experiencing elevated guest complaint rates about room readiness — rooms not ready at checkout time, housekeeping quality variable. The GM implemented a new protocol: the housekeeping supervisor personally inspects and signs off on each room before checkout release.
>
> Two months in, room readiness complaints dropped significantly. But housekeeping staff turnover increased 30%, and last week the housekeeping supervisor — your best one — gave two weeks' notice. She said she felt micromanaged and that the protocol communicated she wasn't trusted to do her job.
>
> The GM followed the protocol. The problem got fixed. The supervisor is leaving.

**Customization:** Replace "housekeeping" with whatever operational area is most live for this client. Ask the sponsor before the session: *"What's the most active operational tension your management team is navigating right now?"* Use that. The scenario activates more reliably when it connects to real current stakes.

### Room Setup

- Round or square table — no head-of-table position. If one isn't available, Andrew sits at a corner, not a head position. Authority seating creates a different dynamic than the one needed for peer challenge.
- Private room. No glass walls, no foot traffic.
- Water, coffee, no formal presentation materials visible. This signals informal working session, not lecture.
- Andrew's observation notepad should be visible but unremarkable — same as in narrative interviews.

## Positioning Script

Deliver this at the opening — before any session content begins.

> "Thanks for making the time. What we're doing today is a working session — not a presentation or a workshop with a curriculum. I'm going to put a real problem in front of you and we're going to work through it together.
>
> The goal is to get a real picture of how this team thinks, what you agree on, where you see things differently, and what some common language might look like for how you work together. I'll be taking some notes — I want to make sure I capture what actually gets said, not my memory of it.
>
> There's no right answer to what I'm going to put in front of you. I'm more interested in how you think through it than in what conclusion you reach. Does that make sense?"

**What this framing does:**
- "Working session, not a workshop" — reduces performance mode
- "Real problem" — establishes that genuine engagement is expected
- "How you think through it" — signals that the process is what matters, which is true
- Note-taking disclosed — same principle as narrative interviews

## Session Design — Four Phases

### Phase 1 — Framing and Warm-Up (25–30 minutes)

**Purpose:** Establish enough safety that leaders engage authentically. Introduce a shared vocabulary frame that primes them for the case work.

**Opening question to the group:**
> "Before we get to the scenario — I want to start with a question. Think about the best working environment you've ever been part of. What made it work? What was different about how people operated?"

Allow 10–15 minutes of open discussion. Do not direct heavily. Observe:
- Who speaks first and how much (dominance signals)
- Who defers or waits (relational positioning)
- Who builds on others vs. redirects to their own content
- Initial regulation level across the group

**Introduce the four behavioral language terms as a shared frame:**
> "What I hear in what you're describing maps onto something I see consistently in high-performing teams. There tend to be four modes that show up under pressure — and most of us have a default. [Briefly describe withdrawal, escalation, disorganization, collaborative capacity in plain language — 2 minutes, not a lecture.]
>
> We're going to see all four of those show up in the scenario we're about to work through. I want you to notice them — in each other and in yourselves."

*This primes leaders to be self-observing, which serves the coaching arc and paradoxically generates better observational data — self-consciousness produces more visible behavior, not less.*

**→ Observation:** Note initial SCARF domain signals per leader. Note who the group naturally defers to before any authority structure has been formally established.

---

### Phase 2 — Case Scenario Work (55–65 minutes)

**Purpose:** Generate a sustained period of genuine engagement with an ambiguous problem. No clean answer. Multiple defensible positions. Real stakes.

**Deliver the scenario:** Read or display it. Give 3–5 minutes of silent reading time before discussion.

**Opening question:**
> "What happened here?"

Do not elaborate. Do not reframe. Let the group construct the meaning. Silence is acceptable — if it runs past 30 seconds, ask:
> "What's the first thing that strikes you about this?"

**The discussion will move through predictable stages:**

*Stage 1 — Fact-gathering (first 10–15 minutes):* Leaders ask clarifying questions, fill in what they don't know. Accept the ambiguity: "I don't have more information than what's on the page — that's intentional. What assumptions are you making to fill in the gaps?"

*Stage 2 — Position-forming (middle 20–25 minutes):* Leaders begin to take positions. This is the primary observation window. Watch for:
- Whose position the group gravitates toward (relational authority dynamics)
- Who asserts a position and holds it vs. shifts when challenged
- Who frames the problem in terms of the GM's decisions vs. the supervisor's response vs. the system
- Language about accountability — who carries it, who distributes it

*Stage 3 — Resolution pressure (last 15–20 minutes):* Push toward a recommendation:
> "If you were the owner, what would you tell the GM to do right now — specifically?"

This forces concreteness. Vague positions become visible here — leaders who were participating comfortably may disengage or become evasive when a specific, attributable decision is required.

**During Phase 2 — observation priority:**
Track which leaders are showing activation signals (increased pace, volume, personalization, or conversely — withdrawal, silence, over-qualification). Note specific language verbatim where possible. Use single words or short phrases on the notepad — full sentences during the session will cause you to lose the facilitation thread.

**→ Observation targets active in Phase 2:** Accountability language; self-other balance in how the GM's decision is analyzed; regulatory capacity under mild group pressure; who holds the room when challenged.

---

### Phase 3 — Activation Sequences (45–55 minutes)

*This phase contains the three deliberate activation conditions. They are sequenced: peer challenge first (lower threat), authority challenge second (higher threat), synthesis third (repair opportunity). Do not reorder.*

#### Activation 1 — Peer Challenge (15–20 minutes)

**Setup:** Identify two leaders who took different positions in Phase 2 — or who have different property contexts. Frame the challenge directly but without edge:

> "[Leader A], your read on this was [position]. [Leader B], yours was different — you seemed more focused on [different position]. I want you to take a minute and defend your view directly to each other. [Leader A] — what is [Leader B] not seeing?"

**Let them engage.** Do not mediate. Your role is to maintain the container, not resolve the tension. If the conversation devolves into agreement too quickly:
> "I don't think you actually agree on this. What are you not saying?"

If one leader capitulates without genuine engagement:
> "[Leader B], I don't think you've actually changed your mind. What would you need [Leader A] to account for before you'd move?"

**What you're observing per leader:**

*Withdrawal signals:* Sudden hedging, qualification of previously clear positions, deferring to the other's framing without genuine reconsideration, going quiet after direct challenge.

*Escalation signals:* Volume increase, personalization ("the problem is that people like you..."), pursuit of the argument beyond what the situation requires, inability to credit the other's position.

*Disorganization signals:* Position shifts without traceable reasoning, agreeing then subtly undermining, compliance with visible residual resistance, looking to Andrew for validation of which position is correct.

*Collaborative capacity:* Holds position under pressure while crediting the other's view; can say "I don't agree, and here's why" without losing regulation; moves toward resolution without collapsing position.

**→ Observation:** Note exact language used at peak challenge moment. This is often the highest-yield verbatim data of the entire session.

---

#### Activation 2 — Authority Challenge (15–20 minutes)

**Timing:** After the peer challenge has run its course — some resolution, natural lull. Do not rush to this.

**Setup:** Wait for group consensus to form around a position — or introduce one:
> "Let me test something. It sounds like the group is landing on [consensus position]. I want to challenge that."

**Deliver the challenge — stay curious, not confrontational:**
> "What you're describing makes sense within one frame. But I've worked with teams that thought exactly this and it made things worse. What are you assuming that might not hold?"

Or, if no consensus has formed:
> "I'm going to disagree with how this conversation has gone. We've been focused on [topic X] and I think we've been avoiding [topic Y]. Why aren't we talking about that?"

**What you're observing:**

*Submission:* Immediate accommodation of your position without engaging the challenge. "You're right, we probably should have..." Leader abandons prior position not because they were persuaded but because the authority figure redirected.

*Defiance:* Continues position without engaging the challenge. "I still think what we said was right." Can be healthy — watch for whether it's a regulated hold or a reactive one.

*Disorganization:* Was with the group, now uncertain, watches others to calibrate response, may produce both submission and subtle resistance in the same turn.

*Regulated disagreement:* Engages the challenge directly — asks what you mean, offers a counter, credits what's valid in your push while maintaining their position. This is the target behavior for development.

**Critical facilitation note:** This challenge must feel genuine, not manufactured. It requires that you actually find something in the consensus to genuinely push back on — which means you need to have engaged with the content, not just performed a challenge. Leaders will sense a hollow challenge and it will activate Status rather than produce behavioral data.

---

#### Activation 3 — Repair and Synthesis (15 minutes)

**Purpose:** Observe repair behavior — what leaders do after tension has peaked. Also provides the session's resolution arc, which protects trust and engagement.

**Transition:**
> "I pushed hard on some things in that last section — intentionally. What I want to do now is see what we actually agree on. What's the one thing this conversation has clarified for you?"

**What you're observing:**

Who moves toward the group after tension — makes a bridging comment, credits what someone else said, lightens the energy? This is repair behavior. Note it specifically.

Who remains guarded or withdrawn after the activation — still holds the elevated posture from the challenge sequence?

Who leads the synthesis — is this the same person who dominated Phase 2, or does a different leader emerge when the task shifts from debate to integration?

**Close the session:**
> "This was exactly what I hoped for — you didn't agree on everything, and you said so. That's what makes this useful. What I'll take from today feeds into the individual work and into a team picture I'll share with [owner name] in aggregate. Any questions about the process?"

## Observation Protocol During the Session

**The core constraint:** You cannot take detailed notes and maintain facilitation presence simultaneously. Resolution: note minimally during the session, reconstruct fully within 24 hours.

**What to note in real time (key words and exact phrases only):**
- Exact language at peak activation moments — specific phrases, not paraphrase
- Behavioral shifts — one word describing what changed (e.g., "went quiet," "volume," "deferred")
- Leader name + observation target + moment (e.g., "J — auth challenge — held position, voice steady")
- Anything surprising — a prediction from NI/360 that didn't match observed behavior

**What to reconstruct after (full observation notes):**
Everything else. The post-session window is where the real documentation happens.

**Dual attention technique:** Keep the per-leader observation questions loaded in working memory (from pre-session preparation). These act as filters — when a relevant moment occurs for a given leader, it registers. Without pre-loaded questions, everything looks equally significant and nothing gets captured.

## Integration into Behavioral Pattern Profile

Facilitated observation data feeds Section 1 and Section 4 of the Behavioral Pattern Profile:

**Section 1 — Behavioral Pattern Summary**
FO data confirms or complicates what NI and 360 showed. If all three methods converge: high-confidence finding. If FO diverges: note the divergence and report the more conservative of the two readings in the leader's copy.

**Section 4 — Source Notes (internal)**
Full observation notes per leader. Flag any prediction-observation divergences. Note verbatim language.

**Key integration question:** Did this leader behave the same way they described themselves in the narrative interview? A leader who described themselves as calm under pressure and showed significant activation in the facilitated session has a self-awareness gap — this becomes a coaching entry point.
