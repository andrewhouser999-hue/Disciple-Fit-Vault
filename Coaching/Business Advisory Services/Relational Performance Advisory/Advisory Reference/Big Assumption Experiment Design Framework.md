# Big Assumption Experiment Design Framework

**Output type:** Practitioner tool — session-ready. Research cited in single lines where it governs a design decision. Not a research brief.

**What this tool does:** Walks Andrew through designing, calibrating, observing, and interpreting Big Assumption behavioral experiments with a specific manager. Use it at the start of Stage 3 and return to it for each experiment in the arc.

**When Stage 3 is ready:**
- The manager has completed the ITC four-column map
- Column 4 (Big Assumption) has been named as a felt reality, not just an intellectual conclusion — the manager said something like "yes, that's actually what I believe will happen"
- The coaching relationship has sufficient trust for the manager to attempt genuine behavioral risk

**The primary change vehicle:** The implicit system updates through accumulated disconfirming experiential evidence — not through insight, knowledge, or intention. Three to five experiments producing disconfirming data the manager cannot fully rationalize are typically needed before Stage 4 indicators appear. (Nosek et al., 2011; Kegan & Lahey, 2009)

---

**Worked example used throughout this tool:**

> **Manager:** Chris, F&B Director, mid-size hotel with banquet operations, 20 direct reports
> **Column 4 Big Assumption:** "If I don't have the answer in front of my team, they will lose confidence in me and start going around me to the owner."
> **Competing commitment:** To always appear in command of operational details
> **Column 2 behavior:** Rapid redirection to his own answer when asked something unexpected; dismissal of supervisor suggestions in daily briefings; takes questions offline rather than opening them to the group

## Section 1: What the Experiment Is Doing

### The Mechanism in Plain Terms

The implicit belief system does not update through information or argument — that is the explicit system. The implicit system updates when it accumulates experience that contradicts what it predicted. The experiment is designed to produce that experience.

The manager's Big Assumption is running a prediction engine: every time a situation arises that could threaten authority, it predicts a specific negative consequence and activates the competing behavior to prevent it. That prediction has never been genuinely tested — or has been confirmed enough times to operate as simply true. The experiment tests it directly: the manager behaves contrary to the protection and observes what actually happens. When what happens contradicts the prediction, the implicit system receives a data point against its model. Accumulated across three to five experiments, the prediction model loses its hold. (Kegan & Lahey, 2009; Nosek et al., 2011)

### Disconfirmation — What It Is Precisely

**Disconfirming result:** The assumption predicted X. What happened was not-X.
> Chris predicted: "The team will lose confidence in me and start going around me." He said "I don't know — what does the team think?" Two supervisors responded immediately with useful input. No one signaled uncertainty about his leadership. That is disconfirmation.

**Neutral result:** The experiment was conducted but the outcome didn't clearly confirm or disconfirm. Common in Experiment 1. Redesign for observability before the next attempt.
> Chris said "I'm not sure" and the room went quiet. Was that loss of confidence or a slow morning? Ambiguous — not clean data.

**Confirming result:** The assumption's prediction appeared to come true. Informative, not a failure — tells Andrew whether the stakes were too high, the context is genuinely confirming, or the manager is reading confirmation into a neutral result. Redesign before proceeding.
> Chris admitted uncertainty and a supervisor immediately escalated to the owner. Data: either the assumption has genuine validity in this specific context, or stakes were too high.

Only disconfirming results produce belief revision. Neutral results prompt redesign. Confirming results are data about experiment quality and activation level.

### Why Genuine Behavioral Risk Is Required

An experiment the competing commitment doesn't resist isn't testing the Big Assumption. If the behavior is already comfortable, the assumption is not engaged and the result tells Andrew nothing.

But an experiment where stakes are too high produces the opposite failure: the assumption activates at full intensity, the competing behavior surges, and the manager either doesn't complete the experiment or can't observe what happened because they were inside the activation.

**The just-enough-risk principle:** enough discomfort that the assumption is genuinely engaged; not so much that the manager can't observe while it's happening. Target activation: 4–6/10.

## Section 2: Formulating the Big Assumption as a Testable Prediction

### The Formulation Protocol — Six Steps

Work through this in session. The manager does the work; Andrew asks the questions.

**Step 1 — Confirm the Big Assumption is stated as a prediction, not a value.**
Must take the form: "If [condition], then [negative consequence]."
> Chris: "If I don't have the answer in front of my team, they will lose confidence in me and start going around me."

**Step 2 — Identify the specific context where this assumption is most active.**
Where does the competing commitment show up most reliably? That context becomes the experiment.
> Chris: "In the 8am daily briefing when someone asks about banquet timing or events coordination I haven't confirmed."

**Step 3 — Identify the specific behavior the competing commitment is preventing.**
What would the manager do if the assumption weren't running?
> Chris: "Say 'I don't have that confirmed — does anyone know?' and wait for a response."

**Step 4 — State the testable prediction in if-then form.**
"If I do [specific behavior] in [specific context], then [what the assumption predicts] will occur."
> Chris: "If I say 'I don't have that confirmed — does anyone know?' in the morning briefing, my supervisors will look uncertain and someone will go to the owner directly."

**Step 5 — Check observability.**
Can Andrew or the manager see whether the prediction came true within the session itself? If not, revise for immediate observables.
> "Did someone go to the owner?" — takes too long; replace with: "Did anyone signal doubt or disengage within the meeting?"

**Step 6 — State the disconfirming outcome.**
"If [prediction does NOT occur], the assumption has been challenged."
> Chris: "If the team engages with the question, someone offers an answer, and no one signals uncertainty about my leadership, the prediction was wrong."

### Common Formulation Errors

**Error 1 — Over-broad prediction:** "People will think less of me."
Too vague — any interaction can be read as confirming or disconfirming.
*Fix:* Specify the observable. "My supervisors will disengage or defer to the owner rather than me within the same meeting."

**Error 2 — Under-specific context:** "In a meeting."
*Fix:* "In the 8am briefing with my seven supervisors, when I'm asked a banquet coordination question I haven't confirmed."

**Error 3 — Behavior stated as intention, not test:** "I will try being more open."
This says what the manager will do — not what will follow.
*Fix:* "If I say 'I don't know' out loud in the briefing, [here is what the assumption predicts will happen next]."

**Error 4 — Prediction not connected to the assumption:**
"If I give more positive feedback, the team will respond well." Tests something — but not Column 4.
*The assumption-reveal check:* "If you imagine doing this in that meeting, what do you notice?" No discomfort = not connected to the Big Assumption. The manager's hedges and qualifications reveal where the actual assumption is active — use them to find the right experiment.

## Section 3: The Five-Factor Design Checklist

Apply to every experiment before it is conducted. Any unanswered item means the experiment is not ready.

---
**Five-Factor Experiment Design Checklist**

☐ **Factor 1 — Stakes Calibration**
Manager's activation rating at the thought of this experiment: ___ / 10
Target range: 4–6. If 7+, reduce stakes before proceeding. If consistently 8+, return to Stage 2.

☐ **Factor 2 — Specificity of Conditions**
Which meeting/interaction (day, time, regular or ad hoc)? _______________
Who will be present? _______________
What trigger condition prompts the experiment? _______________
What exactly will the manager say or do? _______________
What will the manager observe immediately after? _______________

☐ **Factor 3 — Observability of Outcome**
Can the disconfirming outcome be observed within the session itself? _______________
Immediate observable indicators identified (not metrics; not what happens later): _______________

☐ **Factor 4 — Manageability of Activation**
Is the manager within the window of tolerance for this experiment? (Siegel, 1999)
If activation is 7+: use stakes-scaling tool or preparation practice below.
If activation is consistently 8+ regardless of stakes: return to Stage 2 or assess for Profile 3.

☐ **Factor 5 — Genuine Behavioral Risk**
Does imagining the experiment produce real discomfort for this manager?
If no discomfort: stakes are too low or behavior is not connected to the assumption.
Manager's qualifications and hedges (use these to locate where the real assumption lives): _______________

---

### Factor 1: Stakes-Scaling Tool

Move the same experiment across the activation scale. Use to find the right starting level and progress stakes as the assumption weakens.

| Level | Chris Example | Activation Est. |
|---|---|---|
| Lowest | "I'm not sure about that detail — let me confirm and come back to you" — one-on-one with most trusted supervisor | 2–3/10 |
| Low-moderate | "I don't have that confirmed — does anyone know?" — 3-person supervisor sub-team | 4–5/10 |
| Moderate | Same question, full 7-person daily briefing | 5–6/10 |
| Moderate-high | Publicly credits a supervisor: "That was [Name]'s catch — good pickup" — full briefing | 6–7/10 |
| High | "That's a good point I hadn't considered — what does the team think?" — owner informally present | 7–8/10 |

Do not advance the scale until the current level has produced disconfirming data.

### Factor 4: Preparation Practice (Within RPA Scope)

Use when activation is 6–7/10 before the experiment context. Takes 2 minutes.

1. Feet flat on the floor, comfortable posture
2. Three slow breaths — inhale 4 counts, exhale 6 counts
3. One orienting observation: name three things in the room you can see right now
4. One grounding statement: "I'm going to try something and observe what happens. The goal is data, not performance."

This is a regulation tool — not therapy. It reduces sympathetic activation enough to widen the window of tolerance for observation. (Siegel, 1999) Do not use if activation is 8+; reduce stakes instead.

## Section 4: Observation Structure — Before, During, and After

### Level A — Observer Use: What Andrew Watches in the Field

**Pre-observation session (with the manager):**
Establish: which experiment the manager plans to attempt, what they will do, what the prediction is. The manager knows Andrew is watching. Andrew notes baseline behavior in the first 15–20 minutes before the experiment moment — that is the comparison point.

**During the experiment moment — watch in sequence:**
1. **The trigger:** Does the anticipated situation arise? If not, note what prevented it.
2. **The behavior:** Does the manager execute as designed, or does the competing behavior override?
3. **Employee response (first 30 seconds):** Do employees engage? Go quiet? Signal uncertainty?
4. **The manager's reception:** Does the manager register what just happened? Signs: brief pause, micro-expression of surprise, slight shift in posture — processing something unexpected. (Ekman, 2003; Damasio, 1994)

**Signs the assumption is weakening in the room:**
- Manager pauses after the employee response before moving on
- Slight unmanaged facial expression — "huh" — not performed, brief
- Manager stays with the employee response rather than immediately redirecting to agenda

**Signs the experiment was not registered:**
- Manager pivots back to agenda within seconds of the experimental behavior — no uptake of the outcome
- Competing behavior reasserts within the same meeting (answers the question they just said they didn't know)

**If the experiment produced a confirming result:** Do not debrief in the session. Note the specific conditions. Collect and debrief in the next coaching session — do not react to it in the room.

---

### Level B — Manager Coaching Use: Designing and Debriefing

**Pre-experiment coaching session:**
Walk through Sections 2 and 3 with the manager. The manager states the experiment in their own words. Confirm activation rating. Calibrate stakes if needed. Hand the manager the observation record before they leave.

---
**Experiment Observation Record**

*Complete immediately after the experiment — within minutes, while the observation is fresh. Record facts first. Do not interpret yet.*

Date / Meeting / Context: _______________
What I planned to do: _______________
What I actually did: _______________
What I expected to happen (the prediction): _______________
What actually happened — factual, before interpretation: _______________
What I noticed in my body during the experiment: _______________
What I noticed in the room (faces, responses, energy): _______________
My first thought immediately after: _______________

---

**Debrief session sequence:**

1. **Manager reports:** "Walk me through what you wrote down."
2. **Andrew reflects without interpreting:** "So what I'm hearing is [repeats back the factual record only]." No conclusion. Silence is productive — the manager often continues.
3. **Andrew asks one question:** "What do you make of that?"
4. **The manager's interpretation is the data.** Listen for the four rationalization patterns (Section 5) or genuine disconfirmation reception.
5. **If genuine disconfirmation:** "What does this tell you about whether the prediction was accurate?" Do not rush to the conclusion. The manager's own recognition is more durable than Andrew's argument.
6. **If rationalization:** Use the responses in Section 5. Do not confront the rationalization directly.

---

### Level C — Sponsor Use: Translating Results into Operational Evidence

The sponsor receives operational data — not psychological or coaching language.

---
**Manager Progress Update — [Name], [Date]**

**What the manager did differently:**
In the [specific meeting], [Manager] [specific behavior in plain language].

**What happened:**
[Employee response in observable, operational terms — no jargon.]

**What this shows:**
[Manager]'s team responded with [observable behavior]. This is a measurable shift from the pattern documented in [prior observation date], where [prior pattern in plain terms].

**What to watch for next:**
[One specific behavior or interaction that will indicate the change is holding under operational pressure.]

---

**Worked example — Chris:**

> **Manager Progress Update — Chris, May 29**
>
> **What the manager did differently:** In Thursday's 8am pre-shift, Chris said "I don't have the banquet timeline confirmed — does anyone have the latest?" and waited for a response.
>
> **What happened:** Two supervisors responded immediately. The scheduling question was resolved in 90 seconds and Chris confirmed the information.
>
> **What this shows:** Chris's team has the information and the readiness to contribute when asked. This is a measurable shift from the April 15 observation where the same type of question produced a 10-minute loop with Chris providing incomplete information that required follow-up.
>
> **What to watch for next:** Whether supervisors begin volunteering operational updates before being asked — that is the signal the team dynamic is shifting beyond the experiment itself.

## Section 5: Interpretation Guide — Disconfirmation vs. Rationalization

### What Genuine Disconfirmation Looks Like

- Manager describes the result with mild surprise: "That wasn't what I expected."
- The Big Assumption is referred to with slight distance — past tense, or "I thought" rather than "I know": "I thought they'd disengage but they didn't."
- Manager shows curiosity about the result rather than urgency to resolve or explain it.
- Reduced activation rating before the next experiment compared to before this one.

### The Four Rationalization Patterns

**Pattern 1 — Exception attribution**
> "That worked because Maria is always supportive." / "That situation was different from the normal pressure."

Andrew responds: "That's possible. What would next week's experiment look like so that exception couldn't explain it away?"

---

**Pattern 2 — Condition limitation**
> "That kind of openness works in low-stakes situations. It would be different in a real crisis."

Andrew responds: "What would a version of this experiment look like in higher-stakes conditions? What's the nearest to crisis-level pressure where you'd be willing to test it?"

---

**Pattern 3 — Partial credit**
> "I didn't fully commit — I asked the question but then answered it myself right after."

Andrew responds: "Okay, so it was a partial experiment. What would the full version look like?"
*Note: Partial experiments are normal in Experiments 1–2. The fact that the manager answered their own question is itself behavioral data — the competing commitment still active. Note without over-interpreting.*

---

**Pattern 4 — Reframe to confirmation**
> "They responded, but I think they were just being polite."

Andrew responds: "What specifically made you read it as them managing you rather than genuinely engaging?"
Hold the manager in observation. "I don't know" is a valid answer and is itself data — it means the manager cannot yet read the result confidently, which is different from confirming the assumption.

---

### When to Redesign vs. When to Advance

**Redesign when:**
- The experiment produced a confirming result in two consecutive attempts in the same context
- The manager conducted the experiment but it did not engage the assumption — no discomfort before, no activation during; the result is positive but tells Andrew nothing about the assumption
- Activation before the experiment was 8+; the competing behavior overrode during the attempt

**Advance to the next experiment when:**
- Any disconfirming or partially disconfirming result was produced — even if rationalized
- The manager's rationalization is weakening across the debrief: "I keep trying to explain it away but I can't quite get there"
- The activation rating before the next experiment is lower than before the previous one

## Section 6: Activation Calibration — Three Profiles

### Profile 1 — Low Activation (Cognitive Encoding, Mild Somatic Response)

**How this manager presents:** Names the Big Assumption readily. Mild discomfort (2–4/10) at the thought of the experiment. No strong body response. Can discuss the assumption from some distance.

**Experiment design:** Standard ITC protocol. Moderate-stakes experiments appropriate from Experiment 1. Debrief is primarily cognitive.

**Ceiling effect:** The manager completes experiments fluently and acknowledges disconfirmation intellectually — but behavioral change in operations doesn't follow. Smooth verbal processing without transfer to undesigned contexts.
*Response:* Increase stakes or move to higher-activation operational contexts. Current experiments may not be engaging the assumption at sufficient depth.

---

### Profile 2 — Moderate Activation (Cognitive + Somatic, Manageable Range)

**How this manager presents:** Names the assumption but with visible discomfort (5–7/10). Somatic markers when discussing the experiment — tension in face or jaw, slightly faster speech, forward lean. The assumption feels current and live.

**Experiment design:** Begin at stakes 4–5/10. Use the preparation practice before the experiment session. Give the observation record to complete immediately after — captures data before somatic activation reframes the memory. Debrief at the *start of the next session*, not the same one.

**Ceiling effect:** Somatic activation dominates post-experiment interpretation. The manager felt the discomfort and reads it as evidence of danger rather than examining what happened in the room.
*Response:* Slow the debrief. Stay with the factual observation record before moving to interpretation. "Before we interpret — what did you write down that actually happened?"

---

### Profile 3 — High Activation (Strong Somatic Response, Window of Tolerance Risk)

**How this manager presents:** Activation 8–10/10 at the thought of the experiment. Physical signs: jaw tension, shortened breath, visible stilling or restlessness when discussing the experiment. May intellectually agree to the experiment but show competing commitment behavior within the same coaching session.

**Experiment design:** Do not proceed to Stage 3 until activation can be brought to 4–6/10. Use the stakes-scaling tool to find the lowest possible version. If no version of the experiment feels below 7/10, return to Stage 2 access work before attempting Stage 3.

**Ceiling effect:** Repeated attempts at this activation level produce consistent failure to observe — the competing behavior overrides and produces confirming data that reinforces rather than challenges the assumption.

**Referral indicator and Andrew's language:**
If Profile 3 is consistent across multiple sessions and activation does not reduce with stakes reduction, the implicit belief likely has somatic encoding the ITC cognitive-behavioral process alone will not reach.

> *"I've noticed that even when we reduce the stakes significantly, the discomfort around this experiment stays high. That's not unusual — it often means the belief is stored at a level that responds better to a different kind of approach than what I do. There are practitioners who work specifically at that level. I'd like to bring someone in alongside our work — not to replace it, but to add a layer that I think would make what we're doing together more effective."*

*Somatic trauma processing is outside RPA scope. This is the referral boundary.*

## Section 7: The Multi-Experiment Arc

### Five-Experiment Progression — Chris (Profile 2, Moderate Activation)

| # | Behavior | Context | Activation Est. | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "I'm not sure about that detail — let me confirm and come back to you" | One-on-one with most trusted supervisor | 3–4/10 | Establish that the experiment can be conducted; produce any disconfirming data, however partial |
| 2 | "I don't have that confirmed — does anyone know?" + waits for full response | 3-person supervisor sub-team, morning briefing | 5/10 | Test whether Experiment 1 disconfirmation holds in a slightly larger group |
| 3 | Credits a supervisor publicly: "That was [Name]'s catch — good pickup" | Full 7-person daily briefing | 6/10 | Test a different expression of the same competing commitment — credit-sharing vs. uncertainty-admitting |
| 4 | "I don't know — what do you all think?" + stays silent for 10 full seconds | Full daily briefing, unexpected operational question | 6–7/10 | Test in conditions closer to genuine operational pressure |
| 5 | "That's a good point I hadn't considered. Let's think about that together." | Any briefing when owner is informally present | 7–8/10 | Test in the highest-stakes condition identified |

### What Constitutes Accumulated Evidence

A completed experiment ≠ accumulated evidence. An experiment counts toward Stage 4 when:
1. The assumption was genuinely engaged — activation was 4+ before; the behavior was performed as designed
2. The outcome was disconfirming or partially disconfirming
3. The manager received the disconfirmation — at minimum, could not fully rationalize it away in the debrief session

**Three completed experiments all fully rationalized = zero accumulation.**
**Two experiments with genuine reception of disconfirmation = meaningful accumulation.**

Track explicitly. Andrew should be able to answer: "How many genuine disconfirmations has this manager accumulated so far?"

---

### Stage 4 Revision Indicators

**Behavioral (most reliable — look for these first):**
- The new behavior appears in a context Andrew and the manager did not design — transfer to unplanned situations is the clearest Stage 4 indicator
- The manager self-corrects mid-pattern without prompting — catches the competing behavior starting and adjusts before completing it
- The manager reports an instance of the new behavior before Andrew asks

**Internal (reported by the manager):**
- The Big Assumption referred to in the past tense or with distance: "I used to feel like if I didn't have the answer it meant I wasn't doing my job."
- Reduced activation: "I'm actually curious to see what happens rather than dreading it."

**Relational (observable in sessions):**
- The manager describes team members with more curiosity and less evaluation: "I wonder what's going on with her" rather than "she's resistant."
- The coaching conversation feels less effortful — less explanation and defense, more genuine examination.

**What is NOT a Stage 4 indicator:**
- Survey score improvement — may reflect early behavioral compliance
- Verbal endorsement of the new belief — intellectual conversion without behavioral transfer
- Absence of the competing behavior in one or two observations — too few data points

## Quick-Reference Card

*One page — for use during a session or observation.*

**Before the experiment:**
☐ Big Assumption stated as a testable if-then prediction
☐ Context, people, trigger, behavior, and observable outcome all specified
☐ Activation rating: 4–6/10 target
☐ Observation record given to manager

**During (observer use):**
Watch in sequence: trigger → behavior → employee response (first 30 sec) → manager's reception of outcome
Note: micro-expressions at the moment of reception; whether the competing behavior reasserted

**After (debrief sequence):**
Manager reports → Andrew reflects factually → silence → "What do you make of that?"
Listen for: genuine disconfirmation reception OR the four rationalization patterns

**Advancing the arc:**
Redesign if: confirming result twice; assumption not engaged; activation overrode the experiment
Advance if: any disconfirming data produced; rationalization weakening; activation rating dropping

**Stage 4 signs:**
New behavior in undesigned contexts · self-correction without prompting · past-tense reference to the assumption
