# CAPS — Clinical Supervision Outreach & Discussion Guide

**What this is:** Complete outreach and meeting preparation package for contacting the Center for Applied Psychology Services (CAPS) at the University of Nebraska Omaha to establish a clinical supervision relationship for the RPA practice.

**Why CAPS:** UNO has a strong I-O psychology program. CAPS sits at the clinical-organizational intersection — the specific combination required for this supervision relationship. Faculty or advanced doctoral students working at the psychology-organizational consulting intersection represent the ideal supervision profile for RPA.

**Companion document:** [[Relational Performance Advisory — Practitioner Competency Build — Full Evaluation]] — full rationale for why supervision is required and what it specifically provides.

---

Dr. Roni Reiter-Palmon,

I am an organizational consultant developing a leadership advisory practice that operates at the intersection of behavioral assessment, executive coaching, and organizational systems consulting — specifically targeting management teams in hospitality and related industries.

The service I have built — the Relational Performance Advisory — draws on established frameworks from interpersonal neurobiology, attachment-informed leadership research, motivational interviewing, and developmental psychology (Siegel, Perry, Rock, Kegan & Lahey, Miller & Rollnick, Prochaska) to assess and develop management teams. The diagnostic methodology includes structured narrative interviews, multi-rater 360 assessment, facilitated behavioral observation, individual pattern profiling, and a sustained coaching arc.

I am explicitly non-clinical. My scope is behavioral observation and organizational coaching — not therapy, not diagnosis, not clinical intervention. However, the level at which this work operates — regulatory state identification, attachment-informed pattern recognition, stress-response mapping in a management context — sits close enough to clinical territory that I have concluded I need clinical supervision to practice responsibly.

I am contacting CAPS because the supervision I need is specific: I am looking for a licensed psychologist or organizational psychologist with experience in both clinical methodology and organizational consulting contexts who can provide:

- Review of my behavioral pattern assessments for accuracy, scope appropriateness, and clinical adequacy
- Consultation on scope boundary calls — when what I am observing in a leader warrants referral rather than continued coaching
- Calibration of my diagnostic reads across active cases
- Ongoing professional accountability for the implicit-level dimensions of my practice

This is not a request for training in clinical practice. It is a request for supervised accountability from someone qualified to hold the clinical line I am operating adjacent to.

I believe this may represent a genuine opportunity for CAPS — both as a consultation relationship and potentially as a real-world practice context for faculty or advanced doctoral students working at the psychology-organizational consulting intersection.

I would welcome a conversation to explore whether this is a fit. I am available at your convenience and happy to share the full practice documentation in advance of any meeting.

## Meeting Discussion Framework

*Use this to structure the conversation once they agree to meet. Bring the RPA framework documents listed at the bottom. Know these talking points cold — do not read from them.*

---

### Opening — Frame What You Are and Are Not

Lead with this. Don't let them misclassify you as a therapist seeking clinical licensure supervision, or a coach seeking general mentorship.

> "I want to be precise about what I am and what I'm not, because it determines what I actually need from you.
>
> I am a non-clinical organizational consultant. My scope is behavioral — I observe, name, and coach to observable patterns in how managers function under pressure. I do not diagnose. I do not provide therapy. I do not work at the intrapsychic level. I have explicit scope limits and referral protocols built into my practice.
>
> What I do work with is the mechanism level beneath behavior — regulatory states, stress-response patterns, attachment-informed relational patterns as they show up in a management context. The frameworks I use are drawn from clinical research. That proximity to clinical territory is exactly why I'm here."

---

### Describe the Diagnostic Methodology — Specifically

Don't generalize. They will want to know exactly what you're doing.

> "The assessment phase includes three components: a structured narrative interview adapted from narrative coherence methodology, a multi-rater 360 instrument built around five behavioral clusters, and a facilitated observation session. From these three data sources I build an individual behavioral pattern profile for each manager — identifying their primary regulatory pattern, activation threshold, most sensitive SCARF domains, and coaching entry point.
>
> The narrative interview is where I get closest to clinical territory. I'm tracking narrative coherence as a diagnostic signal — adapted from the Adult Attachment Interview methodology, applied in a non-clinical context. I'm observing Window of Tolerance position in real time. I'm reading self-other balance as a developmental stage indicator.
>
> That's the work. And that's why I need someone who can look at my reads and tell me when I'm in my lane and when I've drifted out of it."

---

### Name What You Need — Four Specific Things

Be concrete. They need to know exactly what the supervision relationship looks like.

> "Here's what I'm asking for specifically — four things:
>
> **One — Pattern identification review.** I build behavioral pattern profiles from multi-source data. I need a supervisor who can review my interpretations and tell me where my reads are accurate, where they're thin, and where I'm misreading the data.
>
> **Two — Scope boundary consultation.** When something surfaces in a narrative interview or a coaching session that may cross from organizational coaching into clinical territory — active depression, disclosed trauma, a severity of dysregulation that exceeds what my context can hold — I need someone to consult with before I decide how to proceed. Real-time availability between sessions is part of what I'm asking for.
>
> **Three — Contamination calibration.** My own patterns will influence how I read my clients' patterns. I need an external observer who can identify where my own relational history is distorting my diagnostic reads across cases.
>
> **Four — Professional accountability.** I want to operate with the backing of a clinical professional who has reviewed my work and can confirm that I am practicing within appropriate scope. This matters for my professional integrity and for the protection of my clients."

---

### Describe the Format You're Proposing

> "What I'm envisioning: monthly supervision sessions, 60-90 minutes. I bring two to three active cases — my assessment data, my profile interpretations, anything that surfaced in recent sessions that gave me pause. You review, respond, and give me the calibration I need.
>
> In between, I'd want to know I can reach you if something surfaces that needs an immediate call. That's rare — but the cases where it matters, it matters quickly.
>
> I would expect to pay a professional consultation fee appropriate to your time. I'm not approaching this as a student seeking free mentorship. This is a professional accountability relationship and I intend to treat it that way."

---

### Frame the Opportunity for Them

> "I want to be honest about what I think the opportunity is for you — and I'll let you tell me if I'm reading it right.
>
> What I'm building is an early example of something that doesn't yet have a defined professional category: organizational consulting that operates at the implicit behavioral level, with explicit scope limits, non-clinical language, and a clinical supervision infrastructure. There isn't an established guild for this. The frameworks exist in clinical research but the organizational application is being developed in practice.
>
> For a faculty member or an advanced doctoral student working at the psychology-organizational consulting intersection, this is a live practice to supervise — not a simulated case study. Real assessments, real leaders, real organizations, real scope boundary questions.
>
> There may also be research interest here. The question of whether implicit-level diagnostic methodology adapted from clinical research produces measurable behavioral change in organizational management populations is not answered in the literature. I'm building a practice that will generate case data. Whether there's a research partnership to be explored is something I'd want to discuss with you."

---

### Anticipate Their Concerns — Address Directly

**They may ask: "Are you qualified to be doing this work at this level?"**

> "That's the right question and it's exactly why I'm here. My qualifications are: CPCC from The Coaches Training Institute, four years of active coaching practice, and significant self-directed study in the frameworks I'm using. I am not a licensed psychologist. I am not a clinician. I am a practitioner who has built a rigorous methodology and who is honest enough to know that rigor requires external accountability. If my qualification level is insufficient for you to take on the supervision relationship, I want to know that — because it would tell me something important about whether I should be operating at this level yet."

**They may ask: "What happens if something clinical surfaces and we're not available?"**

> "I have a referral protocol built into the practice — documented scope flags, specific referral language, and the discipline to exit a session and follow up rather than work beyond my scope. What I don't have is someone who has validated that protocol and confirmed it's sufficient. That's part of what I'm asking you to do."

**They may ask: "Why not just get more clinical training yourself?"**

> "That's a legitimate answer and I haven't ruled it out long-term. In the near term, I have an active client engagement being prepared and a practice that needs to operate responsibly now. Supervision is the fastest path to responsible practice. Additional training is the long-term path to expanded scope. I need both and I'm starting with supervision."

---

### How to Close the Meeting

> "What I'm looking for at the end of this conversation is one of three things: a yes, a no, or a 'here's what would need to be true for this to work.' Any of those is useful. What I'm not looking for is a vague referral to someone else. If CAPS isn't the right fit, I'd rather know that directly — and I'd value your honest read on where I should be looking."

Let them ask for more.

## Ideal Supervisor Profile

Not any clinical supervisor. The context requires someone who holds both clinical training and organizational practice understanding. A therapist who has never worked with a business owner will misunderstand the context. An organizational consultant without clinical training can't make scope boundary calls.

**Target profile:**
- Organizational psychologist in private practice, or
- Licensed counselor/clinical social worker who works specifically with leaders and organizations, or
- ICF MCC-level coach with clinical psychology background, or
- Hogan-certified assessor with supervision experience (specifically for the assessment component)

**Format:** Monthly minimum. Bi-weekly preferred while building the practice.
**Cost expectation:** $150-300 per session. High-leverage investment relative to practice protection.

## If CAPS Is Not the Right Fit

Ask them directly: "Where should I be looking?" Specific alternatives to pursue if CAPS declines or isn't the right match:

- UNO psychology faculty who consult independently outside of CAPS
- Nebraska Psychological Association — referral to organizational psychologists in private practice
- ICF Nebraska chapter — MCC-level coaches with clinical training
- Hogan Assessment Systems — certified coach network, some with supervision capacity
- Society for Industrial-Organizational Psychology (SIOP) — member directory for I-O practitioners who do executive assessment supervision
