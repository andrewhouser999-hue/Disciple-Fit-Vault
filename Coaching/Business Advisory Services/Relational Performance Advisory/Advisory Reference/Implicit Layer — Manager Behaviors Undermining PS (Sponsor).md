# Implicit Layer — Manager Behaviors Undermining Psychological Safety (Sponsor/Owner Communication Use)

**Use case:** Sponsor/owner communication — equipping Andrew to explain to a client owner or sponsor what is actually happening in their organization, why prior training efforts have not held, and why the RPA approach is structurally different.
**Depth:** Level 1 (mechanism) is primary — the argument for why policy and training alone cannot resolve this. Level 2 is light — enough to make the behavior concrete and recognizable to someone who manages the operation. Level 3 is the RPA value proposition — flagged as the argument for why what Andrew does is different from what the sponsor has tried.

**Language calibration:** The sponsor thinks in operational and business terms. Research is present throughout but translated. Non-clinical, non-academic framing throughout. Every significant claim is connected to a business outcome — turnover, performance, guest experience, error reporting, or cost. The research is used to answer the sponsor's questions, not to demonstrate the field's sophistication.

**The sponsor's implicit questions this brief answers:**
1. What is actually going on with my managers — and why?
2. Why hasn't training fixed it?
3. How much is this costing me?
4. What makes your approach different?
5. What should I expect to see change, and when?

## Part 1: What Is Actually Going On — The Implicit Layer in Sponsor Language

### The Gap Between Intent and Impact

Most managers in most organizations genuinely want their teams to perform well. They are not, in the majority, trying to undermine the people who work for them. The owner who hires good managers and trains them on communication and leadership is doing the right thing. The problem is that the training addresses a different layer of the problem than the layer where the problem actually lives.

The research on this is consistent across organizational psychology, neuroscience, and management studies over the past four decades. Here is what it shows:

Every person operates on two levels simultaneously. The first is the level of **stated values and intentions** — what they believe about themselves, what they say they're committed to, how they'd describe their own management style. The second is the level of **operating beliefs** — the automatic assumptions that actually drive behavior in real time, particularly under pressure, time constraint, or challenge. Argyris & Schön (1974, *Theory in Practice*, Jossey-Bass), who spent decades researching organizational effectiveness, called these "espoused theory" and "theory-in-use." The gap between them is not dishonesty. It is the normal architecture of human belief — and it is present in virtually every manager, at every level, in every organization they studied.

The manager who says "I have an open door, I want people to bring me problems" — and means it — may simultaneously hold the operating belief that being challenged in front of their team is a threat to their authority. That second belief is not conscious. They would not list it if asked to describe their management values. But it governs their behavior in the moments that matter — when someone raises a concern, when an error is surfaced, when a team member pushes back on a decision.

The operating belief layer is what produces the behavior that employees read in the room. And what employees read in the room is what determines whether they bring problems forward, admit mistakes, raise concerns, or stay quiet and manage appearances. The technical term for what they're reading is **psychological safety** — the team's shared sense of whether speaking up is safe or dangerous. The manager's operating beliefs are the primary input into that calculation.

### Why This Is Not About Bad Managers

Before describing what this looks like, the most important framing for the sponsor conversation: **this is not a bad-manager problem.** It is a human nervous system problem — and that distinction matters for everything that follows, including whether the intervention is training-and-replace or actual change.

Bargh and Chartrand (1999, *American Psychologist*) reviewed decades of experimental research and concluded that most social behavior is driven by automatic processes — activated before conscious deliberation occurs. The manager who sends a dismissive signal in the moment of receiving unwanted input did not decide to send it. The signal was produced by an automatic nervous system response — the operating belief activating faster than a choice was possible.

Keltner, Gruenfeld and Anderson (2003, *Psychological Review*) documented that holding organizational authority produces structural changes in how managers process others automatically. As people gain positional power, their nervous systems become less attuned to others' emotional signals — not through choice or character failure but through a documented neurological shift. The manager who "doesn't seem to hear" what employees are telling them is, in many cases, neurologically less equipped to hear it than they were before they held the position.

The practical implication for the sponsor: replacing managers who are producing these patterns will likely replace them with managers who produce the same patterns — because the patterns emerge from universal human mechanisms operating in the authority role, not from the specific character flaws of specific individuals. The leverage point is not personnel; it is the operating belief layer.

### The Neurobiological Mechanism in Plain Terms

Damasio's somatic marker research (1994, *Descartes' Error*, Putnam) established that the body responds to familiar situations before the mind has consciously evaluated them. The manager who has spent years in authority positions has accumulated bodily responses to specific situations — being challenged, having their judgment questioned, encountering someone who doesn't follow direction — that activate automatically. The response shows up as a change in tone, a slight shift in body orientation, a facial expression, a shortened acknowledgment, a response that communicates "this isn't worth my full attention" — before the manager has consciously decided how to respond.

Porges (2011, *The Polyvagal Theory*, Norton) documented that human nervous systems are designed to regulate one another through proximity. A manager's internal state — their level of calm, engagement, or subtle threat response — transmits to the people around them through vocal tone, pacing, facial expression, and body posture. Employees' nervous systems pick up these signals automatically and continuously, and adjust their own state accordingly. This is not metaphorical; it is a documented mechanism of how the social nervous system functions.

The practical result: what the manager is experiencing internally in a meeting is being transmitted to the team without the manager choosing to transmit it. If the manager's operating beliefs produce a subtle threat response when challenged — tightening, dismissal, the slight signal that says "this is a problem, not a contribution" — that response transmits through the room. Every person present registers it. And the team's assessment of whether it is safe to speak up in the next meeting is updated accordingly.

## Part 2: Why Training Hasn't Fixed It

### What Training Actually Changes

Management training — communication skills, active listening, leadership presence, psychological safety workshops — operates primarily at the level of **behavior**. It teaches managers what to do differently: ask more questions, paraphrase before responding, acknowledge contributions before redirecting. These are legitimate skills and they produce real, measurable improvement — in the short term, in low-stakes situations, and when the manager is actively thinking about the training.

The problem is documented extensively in the organizational effectiveness literature: **behavioral training without belief-level change produces compliance that erodes under pressure** (Ely, Ibarra & Kolb, 2011, *Harvard Business Review*; Argyris, 1990, *Overcoming Organizational Defenses*). The mechanism is straightforward:

The trained behavior requires deliberate effort to produce. The untrained operating belief runs automatically. When the situation is low-stakes and the manager has bandwidth to be deliberate, the trained behavior appears. When the situation is high-stakes — a tight deadline, a difficult customer situation, a direct challenge to the manager's judgment — the operating belief takes over because deliberate effort is not available. The automatic behavior reasserts. The team observes the reversion and updates their psychological safety assessment downward — not upward.

This is why organizations consistently report that training shows initial improvement on survey measures that fades within three to six months. The improvement reflects behavioral compliance in contexts where the training is salient. The fade reflects the operating belief resuming control when conditions are normal. The survey scores did not lie — the behavior changed temporarily. The belief did not change, and behavior follows belief.

### The Specific Failure Mode in Hospitality

Hospitality operations are characterized by sustained time pressure, high guest-contact intensity, and a service culture that places significant weight on manager authority for operational execution. These conditions create exactly the environment where operating beliefs dominate over trained behavior:

- **Time pressure** eliminates the deliberate bandwidth required to apply trained behaviors. The manager under pressure defaults to automatic patterns.
- **Service urgency** frames manager authority as operationally necessary — the belief that "I need to move fast and decisively" directly competes with the belief that "I should slow down and genuinely hear what this person is raising."
- **Hierarchy in guest service contexts** carries a specific operating belief pattern: guests are served by following clear direction from management; employees who raise concerns about direction are slowing the service apparatus. This belief produces dismissal of employee input framed internally as operational efficiency — and it is invisible to the manager as dismissal because it feels like leadership.

The result: hospitality managers often show the largest gap between training performance and operational performance of any industry, because the operational context is most consistently activating the operating belief layer. The training works when things are calm. The operating beliefs run the show when things aren't — which in hospitality is most of the time.

### The Self-Monitoring Problem

Atwater & Yammarino (1992, *Personnel Psychology*, 45(1)) documented across multiple studies that managers consistently rate their own behavior as more inclusive, open, and collaborative than their direct reports rate it — and that this gap increases with management level. Keltner's research adds the neurological mechanism: authority positions structurally reduce the accuracy of self-assessment.

The manager who completed training and believes they are doing it well is often not wrong from inside their experience. They are applying the trained behaviors deliberately in contexts where they remember to. They do not register the automatic patterns that operate in the remaining contexts — because those patterns are automatic. They are not experienced as choices and therefore are not available for self-critique. The employee, who is attending to the manager with high vigilance, registers both the trained behavior and the automatic pattern. They assign more weight to the automatic pattern because they understand, implicitly, that it is more reliable as a signal — it cannot be strategically managed.

This means the sponsor cannot rely on the manager's self-assessment as evidence that training is holding. The evidence is in employee behavior: whether they raise concerns, whether error reporting rates are accurate, whether the manager consistently hears about problems early or late.

## Part 3: What It Looks Like in the Room

### Observable Patterns — Making the Implicit Layer Concrete

These patterns are drawn from the research of Detert & Edmondson (2011, *Academy of Management Journal*), Brown (2018, *Dare to Lead*), Ekman (2003, *Emotions Revealed*), Gottman (1999, *The Seven Principles*), Porath (2016, *Mastering Civility*), and Keltner (2016, *The Power Paradox*). They are translated here into operational language.

**Pattern 1: The ranked response**
The manager responds differently to the same kind of input depending on who raised it. Input from senior staff or peers gets follow-up questions, visible engagement, extended eye contact. Input from lower-tenure or lower-status employees gets a briefer acknowledgment and faster movement to the next item. The differential is not a policy — it is automatic. Employees at every level observe it and draw the same conclusion: what you say matters less than who you are. This conclusion is registered as an assessment of how safe it is to speak up — and the answer is that it is not equally safe for everyone.

**Pattern 2: The certainty performance**
The manager never says "I don't know" or "I'm not sure about that." When faced with a question they cannot fully answer, they answer confidently anyway, reframe the question, or redirect to something they can answer. This is not deliberate deception. It is the automatic expression of an operating belief: uncertainty should not be visible to the team. The downstream effect: employees learn that uncertainty is not acceptable in this environment. They stop surfacing it. Problems that the manager doesn't know about — because employees have learned not to bring them — continue developing until they become crises.

**Pattern 3: The quick close**
The manager acknowledges contributions with brief, efficient responses and moves to the next agenda item. This is often experienced internally as good meeting management — keeping things on track. Employees experience the brevity as a signal that the contribution didn't warrant real engagement. The pattern produces teams that stop contributing beyond what is directly requested — because the brief acknowledgment communicates that extended contribution isn't welcome. In operational terms: the team's problem-solving capacity is constrained to what the manager already knows to ask about.

**Pattern 4: The correction loop**
When an employee's suggestion differs from the manager's position, the manager's response — however diplomatically worded — consistently returns to the manager's original position. The employee's input may be acknowledged verbally, but it does not change the outcome. Over time, employees learn that input is received but not integrated. They stop raising suggestions. The manager is then genuinely puzzled that employees "don't show initiative" — not registering that they have trained the initiative out of the team through the correction loop.

**Pattern 5: The reversion under pressure**
All of the trained behaviors — acknowledging contributions, asking questions, pausing before responding — disappear when the manager is under operational pressure. Employees can predict exactly when the manager will behave differently based on the current operational load. This predictability means they know precisely when speaking up is least safe — which corresponds to the moments when speaking up would matter most.

### What Employees Are Doing With These Signals

Frazier et al. (2017, *Personnel Psychology*, 70(1), 113–165) — a meta-analysis of 136 studies involving more than 22,000 workers — found that psychological safety predicts team learning behavior and performance above all other team-level factors. Specifically, when psychological safety is low, employees engage in a specific set of protective behaviors:

- **Impression management over honest reporting:** employees present themselves and their work in the most favorable light rather than giving accurate accounts of what is happening
- **Error concealment:** mistakes are managed quietly rather than surfaced early, increasing the ultimate cost of the error
- **Selective voicing:** employees speak about safe topics and withhold information about unsafe ones — the manager receives a carefully filtered version of operational reality
- **Initiative suppression:** employees do what is asked and no more; they do not bring forward problems they weren't asked about or solutions to problems the manager hasn't noticed

In a hospitality operation, these patterns manifest as: managers who are consistently surprised by problems that had been visible to staff for weeks; turnover that is attributed to external factors but is driven by employees' experienced sense of diminishment; guest experience inconsistency that correlates with manager-team relational patterns rather than training compliance; and the absence of the kind of team-generated operational improvement that produces competitive differentiation.

## Part 4: What This Is Costing

### The Documented Business Costs

**Turnover:** The research consistently shows that the primary driver of voluntary turnover is the direct manager relationship, not compensation, not benefits, not organizational factors (Gallup, 2015, *State of the American Manager*; Harter et al., 2002, *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 87(2), 268–279). Specifically, the quality of the manager-employee relationship — characterized significantly by whether employees feel heard, whether their input matters, whether they can bring real problems — predicts turnover more reliably than satisfaction with pay. In hospitality, where turnover costs are documented at 30–50% of annual salary per position (National Restaurant Association; SHRM industry data), the implicit layer costs are direct and calculable.

**Cognitive performance suppression:** Porath & Erez (2007, *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 92(5), 1307–1316) found experimentally that a single episode of mild incivility — including non-overt forms like dismissive tone, brief non-acknowledgment, or the kind of micro-dismissal documented in Pattern 1 above — reduced cognitive task performance in targets by approximately 30% and creative output by approximately 45% in subsequent tasks. The mechanism: the implicit layer signal activates a threat response that consumes working memory. The employee who just experienced a micro-dismissal from their manager is operating at significantly reduced cognitive capacity for the next task — which in hospitality is typically a guest interaction.

**The bystander effect:** Porath & Pearce (2009) found that witnesses to incivility show nearly identical cognitive performance deficits as targets. An implicit-layer incident involving one employee and the manager — even brief, even mild — degrades the performance of every person present. In a pre-shift briefing, a service recovery conversation in front of colleagues, or a table-side correction — the performance cost extends across the team.

**Error reporting lag:** Edmondson's healthcare research (1996, 1999) — which has been replicated in manufacturing, service, and technology contexts — found that teams with low psychological safety report errors significantly later than teams with high psychological safety, and that the lag is positively correlated with the severity of outcomes. In the operations context: problems surface to management after they are already expensive, rather than while they are cheap to correct. The implicit layer is directly implicated — managers whose operating beliefs produce dismissal of unwanted information systematically receive that information later and in worse condition.

**Guest experience:** The co-regulation mechanism (Porges, 2011) does not stop at the manager-employee boundary. An employee whose nervous system is in a threat state — because of how they experienced their last interaction with management — carries that state into guest interactions. The employee who is experiencing chronic low-grade threat in the manager relationship cannot fully access the social engagement behaviors that exceptional hospitality requires: warmth, attunement, genuine interest in the guest's experience. This is not attitude or effort — it is nervous system state. The manager's implicit layer reaches the guest through the employee.

## Part 5: What Makes the RPA Approach Different — The Level 3 Argument

### The Training Layer vs. The Belief Layer

Standard management training addresses **Column 2**: what the manager does. It teaches different behaviors, provides models for different responses, practices new patterns. This is legitimate work and produces real change — at the behavioral level, in contexts where the training is salient and the manager has deliberate bandwidth.

The RPA approach addresses **Column 4**: the operating belief that is generating the Column 2 behaviors. This distinction is drawn from Kegan & Lahey's Immunity to Change research (2009, Harvard Business Review Press), which documented that the gap between genuine intention to change and the behaviors that undermine that intention is maintained by an unconscious competing commitment — a goal the operating belief is protecting. Until the operating belief itself is addressed, Column 2 training produces compliance that the Column 4 belief will systematically erode.

**In concrete terms:**

| Training addresses | RPA addresses |
|---|---|
| "Ask more questions in meetings" | "What belief makes receiving input feel like a threat?" |
| "Acknowledge contributions before redirecting" | "What operating belief determines whose input is worth acknowledging?" |
| "Create space for dissent" | "What does dissent mean to this manager — and what does that meaning produce automatically?" |

Training can change what a manager does when they remember to apply what they learned. The operating belief changes what a manager does automatically — including in the moments when they don't remember, when pressure is highest, and when the behavior matters most.

### What the Sponsor Should Expect to See

**Timeline calibration:** Operating belief change is slower than behavioral training. Behavioral training produces visible change in weeks. Operating belief work typically produces observable behavioral shift — the kind that holds under pressure — in three to six months of consistent engagement. The sponsor who expects the same pace as training will misread the process.

**Leading indicators of belief-level change:**
- Managers begin to surface problems to the sponsor that they previously managed quietly — belief change on the manager side produces the same voice behavior it produces on the team side
- Managers describe team members' concerns and input differently — with less evaluation and more curiosity
- Error reporting rates and early-warning problem surfacing increase — not because of a reporting policy but because the relational environment is shifting
- Managers ask questions in situations where they previously provided answers

**What is not an indicator of belief-level change:**
- Survey scores improving shortly after engagement begins — this reflects training-style compliance and will be tested by time
- Manager self-report of improved behavior — the self-monitoring gap means self-report is unreliable until well into the process
- Single-session behavioral improvement — the operating belief reasserts under pressure; consistency across pressure conditions is the test

### Why This Is the RPA Value Proposition

The sponsor who has invested in management training and seen it fade is not wrong to be skeptical of more training. The investment was sound; the level was wrong. The implicit layer is where the behavior is being generated — and that is where the leverage is. What makes RPA different is not better training content; it is working at the right level.

## Part 6: The "Not Bad People" Frame — Essential for Sponsor Buy-In

### Why This Frame Determines What the Sponsor Does Next

If the sponsor concludes from this conversation that their managers are bad people who are deliberately undermining the organization, there are two available responses: fire them or tolerate them. Neither produces the outcome the sponsor actually wants — which is a management team whose behavior creates the conditions for exceptional operational performance. The "not bad people" frame is not therapeutic softening; it is the argument that makes intervention the logical response instead of personnel action.

The research supports this frame on three grounds:

**Ground 1 — The behavior is automatic:** Bargh's automaticity research (1994) establishes that the behaviors producing implicit-layer undermining are not choices — they are automatic outputs of operating beliefs that activate faster than deliberation is possible. The manager is not choosing to dismiss; their nervous system is dismissing before choice is available. This is the mechanism that makes replacement ineffective: the next manager will have the same nervous system operating in the same authority role, and will produce the same automatic patterns unless the operating belief layer is specifically addressed.

**Ground 2 — Authority changes the brain:** Keltner's power research (2016, *The Power Paradox*; Keltner, Gruenfeld & Anderson, 2003) documents that holding positional authority produces neurological changes — reduced empathy processing, reduced self-monitoring accuracy, increased automatic dominant behavior — that are not character features but consequences of the role. The manager who was attentive and collaborative as a peer may genuinely be less attentive and less collaborative as a manager — not because they changed who they are but because the role changed how their nervous system processes others. This is documented, replicable, and not a moral failing.

**Ground 3 — The gap is universal:** Argyris & Schön (1974) found the theory-in-use / espoused theory gap in virtually every organizational leader they studied — including leaders who were performing exceptionally on every available metric. The gap is not evidence of managerial inadequacy. It is evidence of the normal structure of human belief and the conditions under which automatic behavior takes over from deliberate behavior. The exceptional manager who emerges from operating belief work is not a different person; they are a more self-aware version of the same person, with the automatic layer aligned more closely to their actual intentions.

**For the sponsor conversation:** "Your managers are not the problem. The operating belief layer is the problem. And operating belief patterns can be worked with — but only if we're working at that level rather than at the behavioral level where training operates."
