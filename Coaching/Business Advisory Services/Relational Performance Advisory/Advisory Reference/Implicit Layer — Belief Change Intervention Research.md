# Implicit Layer — Belief Change Intervention Research (Level 3)

**Purpose:** Establish what the evidence says actually changes implicit beliefs — specifically the operating beliefs that generate automatic manager behaviors undermining psychological safety. This is the methodological foundation of the RPA engagement: the argument that working at the belief level is both necessary and sufficient, and the research basis for what that work involves.

**The central question:** Can behavioral training change implicit beliefs — or must the belief be addressed directly? If directly, through what mechanisms and methods?

## Part 1: The Core Question — Can Behavioral Training Change Implicit Beliefs?

### The Answer the Research Supports

The honest answer from the implicit cognition and organizational change literature: **behavioral training alone does not reliably change implicit beliefs.** It changes deliberate behavior in contexts where the training is salient and the person has sufficient bandwidth to apply it. It does not change the automatic processes that generate behavior when deliberate bandwidth is unavailable.

The evidence base for this conclusion:

**Greenwald & Banaji (1995, *Psychological Review*, 102(1), 4–27)** established that implicit attitudes are functionally separate from explicit attitudes — they are stored differently, activated differently, and changed through different mechanisms. A person can simultaneously hold an explicit attitude ("I value input from all team members equally") and an implicit attitude ("this person's input is not worth taking seriously") that are in direct conflict. Behavioral training modifies the explicit attitude and the deliberate behavior it generates. The implicit attitude, operating in a separate system, continues to produce automatic behavior in the windows between deliberate responses.

**Nosek, Hawkins & Frazier (2011, *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 15(4), 152–159)** — a comprehensive review of implicit attitude malleability research — found that implicit attitudes can change, but they respond to different inputs than explicit attitudes:
- Explicit attitude change: information, argument, persuasion — deliberate cognitive processes
- Implicit attitude change: repeated experience with disconfirming exemplars, associative relearning, affective conditioning, and experiential evidence that the belief's predictions are wrong

Training that provides information and argument about what attitudes and behaviors should look like operates in the explicit system. The implicit system does not update through information and argument; it updates through experience.

**Ely, Ibarra & Kolb (2011, *Harvard Business Review*)** documented the organizational consequence of this distinction: leadership development programs that focus on behavioral skill acquisition without addressing the identity-level beliefs that produce behavior show short-term change that does not sustain under pressure. The mechanism is exactly what the implicit cognition research predicts — the explicit system carries the training in low-pressure contexts; the implicit system resumes control when pressure is applied.

### What Behavioral Training Does Accomplish — The Caveats

The conclusion is not that behavioral training is worthless. It accomplishes two things that are genuinely useful:

**1. It demonstrates the gap between stated intention and actual practice.** When a manager participates in active listening training and then watches video of themselves doing the opposite, the cognitive dissonance this produces is a necessary precondition for the belief-level work. Training can function as the diagnostic tool that surfaces the gap, even if it cannot close it.

**2. Implementation intentions (Gollwitzer, 1999, *American Psychologist*, 54(7), 493–503) — a specific behavioral strategy that bridges the gap during belief revision:**
"If-then" planning ("If an employee raises a concern in a meeting, then I will pause and ask one follow-up question before responding") creates an explicit behavioral link that can intercept automatic processes under specific, predictable conditions. The evidence shows significant behavior change from implementation intentions across domains (Gollwitzer, 1999; meta-analysis by Gollwitzer & Sheeran, 2006, *Advances in Experimental Social Psychology*, 38, 69–119).

The mechanism: the if-then plan becomes encoded as its own automatic response, competing with the original automatic behavior at the moment of the specified cue. Unlike behavioral training, it is designed to operate without deliberate bandwidth — once formed, the implementation intention is itself automatic.

The limitation: it works when the cue matches the planned condition and the manager's implicit system has not been activated strongly enough to override it. Under high pressure or strong emotional activation, the original automatic response typically wins. Implementation intentions are a bridging strategy — useful during the period when belief revision is underway but the automatic behavior is still active. They are not a substitute for belief-level work.

## Part 2: What Actually Changes Implicit Beliefs

### Double-Loop Learning — Argyris & Schön

Argyris & Schön (1978, *Organizational Learning*, Addison-Wesley; Argyris, 1991, "Teaching Smart People How to Learn," *Harvard Business Review*, 69(3), 99–109) drew the foundational distinction between two kinds of learning:

**Single-loop learning:** Detecting and correcting errors within the existing framework of governing beliefs and values. The thermostat example: the thermostat detects that temperature is below the set point and activates the heat — it does not question whether the set point is correct. In organizational terms: the manager learns to ask more questions in meetings while the belief that their role is to have the answers remains unchanged. Single-loop learning is what behavioral training produces.

**Double-loop learning:** Questioning and revising the governing belief itself — the set point, not just the error-correction mechanism. The manager who examines and revises the belief that their authority depends on not being challenged is engaging in double-loop learning. The behavior change that follows is structurally different from single-loop compliance: it is generated by a different belief, not maintained by effort against the original one.

**What produces double-loop learning:**

Argyris identified two necessary conditions:
1. Encountering evidence that the current governing belief is not achieving the intended result — evidence that cannot be dismissed or explained away
2. A context in which the defensive routines that would normally seal off awareness of this evidence are not triggered

The second condition is the harder one. Defensive routines — the automatic patterns that protect people from experiencing the gap between their espoused and operating beliefs — are themselves self-sealing. They prevent the awareness that would enable double-loop learning. The manager who is not achieving psychological safety in their team can maintain the defensive routine of "my team doesn't bring problems to me because they're not engaged" indefinitely, because the routine explains away the evidence rather than engaging with it.

Argyris's intervention approach: structured reflection processes (the left-hand column technique, case studies of real incidents analyzed to surface the theory-in-use) conducted in groups or pairs, where the facilitator maintains the conditions for genuine inquiry rather than defensive explanation. The group context is important: hearing that other capable managers have the same theory-in-use gap reduces the shame-activation that would otherwise trigger defensive routines.

**What Argyris's research cannot do:** Argyris documented the process of double-loop learning and the conditions that enable it, but did not produce experimental evidence of its long-term durability. The evidence base is primarily case study and theoretical — compelling but not at the level of experimental research on belief change mechanisms.

### Kegan & Lahey — The Immunity to Change Process

The ITC process (Kegan & Lahey, 2009, *Immunity to Change*, Harvard Business Review Press) is the most fully developed organizational methodology for implicit belief change. It is specifically designed for the structure of the problem: a genuine, sincere commitment to change is simultaneously undermined by an automatic behavior that is serving a hidden competing commitment, which is maintained by a Big Assumption that has never been examined.

**The change mechanism in ITC:**

The Big Assumption is not changed by being identified. Identification is necessary — you cannot test something you haven't named — but insight alone does not produce belief revision. Kegan & Lahey's evidence across organizational applications is clear: leaders who understand their Big Assumption intellectually continue to produce the behaviors that Big Assumption drives until they generate experiential evidence that the assumption is wrong.

The change mechanism is **assumption testing through designed behavioral experiments:**

1. The leader identifies a safe, low-stakes context in which to test whether the Big Assumption is actually true
2. They design a small behavioral experiment that would disconfirm the assumption if the assumption is false — "If I say 'I don't know' in this meeting, what actually happens?"
3. They conduct the experiment and observe what actually happens — not what the assumption predicts
4. If the actual outcome disconfirms the assumption ("I said I didn't know and no one lost confidence in me — they engaged more actively"), this creates experiential evidence that the assumption's prediction is wrong
5. Over multiple experiments, the accumulated disconfirming evidence weakens the assumption's hold — the "as if it were simply true" quality of the assumption diminishes

**Why experience, not argument, produces the change:** The Big Assumption is stored in the implicit system and activated automatically. The implicit system does not update through verbal argument or logical persuasion — those operate in the explicit system. The implicit system updates through repeated experience with outcomes that contradict its predictions (Nosek et al., 2011). The ITC behavioral experiment is designed to generate exactly this type of updating experience.

**Evidence base:** Kegan & Lahey (2009) report program evaluation data across multiple cohorts in organizations including healthcare, education, manufacturing, and financial services. The outcomes reported include sustained behavioral change assessed at 12+ months. The evidence level is program evaluation rather than RCT — no control group, no blinding, no random assignment. This is an honest limitation. The theoretical mechanism is well-grounded in the implicit cognition research; the organizational application evidence is strong but not at the highest methodological standard.

**What the ITC process cannot address:** Implicit beliefs with strong somatic activation — beliefs that are not only cognitively held but physiologically encoded as threat responses. For these, the cognitive-behavioral experiments of ITC may not produce sufficient disconfirmation because the threat response activates before the cognitive belief can be examined. This is the territory where somatic approaches are indicated — addressed in Part 3.

### Reflective Function and Mindsight — Daniel Siegel

Siegel (2010, *Mindsight*, Bantam Books; 1999, *The Developing Mind*, Guilford Press) introduced the concept of **mindsight** — the capacity to observe one's own mental states (thoughts, feelings, intentions, memories) from a slight distance, without being driven by them. Mindsight is the first-person equivalent of what Fonagy et al. called **reflective function** (Fonagy, Gergely, Jurist & Target, 2002, *Affect Regulation, Mentalization, and the Development of the Self*, Other Press): the capacity to understand one's own and others' behavior in terms of underlying mental states.

**Why mindsight is the neurological mechanism for implicit belief change:**

An implicit belief that operates automatically — below conscious awareness — cannot be modified unless it can first be observed. Observation requires the capacity to step back from an internal state and notice it rather than be driven by it. This is exactly what mindsight enables. The manager who can notice "I'm experiencing a threat response right now — something in me evaluated that input as dangerous" is in a position to examine and potentially revise the belief that generated the threat response. The manager who cannot make this observation is simply driven by the threat response, and the automatic behavior follows without any window for examination.

**How mindsight develops:**

Siegel's interpersonal neurobiology research establishes that mindsight is not a fixed capacity — it develops and deepens through specific experiences:

1. **Secure attachment relationships:** Siegel draws on attachment research (Ainsworth, Main, Fonagy) showing that secure attachment — characterized by a caregiver who accurately reflects the child's mental states back to them — develops reflective function. Adults who did not develop this in childhood can develop it through corrective relational experiences in adulthood.

2. **Mindfulness practice:** Sustained mindfulness practice produces measurable changes in prefrontal cortex function — specifically the areas associated with observing one's own mental states, regulating emotional responses, and maintaining attention in the face of activation (Davidson et al., 2003, *Psychosomatic Medicine*, 65(4), 564–570). The mechanism: mindfulness trains exactly the capacity for observing internal states that mindsight requires.

3. **Being mentalized:** Fonagy's research on mentalization establishes that being in a relationship with someone who accurately and compassionately understands and reflects back one's mental states develops one's own capacity to do this — for oneself and for others. The coaching relationship that mentalizes the manager develops the manager's mindsight. This is one mechanism by which the coaching relationship itself produces change.

**The window of tolerance requirement (Siegel, 1999):**
Mindsight work — accessing and staying with implicit material — requires that the person be within the window of tolerance: neither hyper-aroused (flooded, reactive, fight/flight) nor hypo-aroused (shut down, dissociated, collapsed). Outside the window, the capacity for reflective observation is compromised. The coach's primary regulation function is maintaining conditions that keep the manager within the window — which is the same function described in the coaching use brief as the precondition for accessing implicit material.

**Evidence base:** The neuroscience of reflective function development is well-established — the developmental attachment research is among the most robust in psychology. The direct application to organizational coaching is theoretically grounded but less experimentally developed. Davidson's mindfulness-brain research (2003) is replicated; Lueke & Gibson (2015) showed brief mindfulness reduced implicit bias; the specific application to organizational leader implicit belief change awaits more direct experimental evidence.

### Mindfulness as an Access Enhancer

Beyond its role in developing mindsight over time, mindfulness practice produces a specific effect relevant to implicit belief change: it increases the gap between stimulus and automatic response. Kabat-Zinn (1994, *Wherever You Go, There You Are*, Hyperion) describes this as "the space between stimulus and response" — the brief window in which a choice is possible before the automatic behavior completes.

Lueke & Gibson (2015, *Social Psychological and Personality Science*, 6(5), 581–589): even brief mindfulness meditation (8–10 minutes) significantly reduced implicit racial and age bias on IAT measures compared to controls — effect sizes were modest but consistent. The mechanism is consistent with Siegel's mindsight framework: mindfulness increases awareness of the automatic process, creating a window for examination and modification.

Hülsheger et al. (2013, *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 98(2), 310–325): mindfulness-based intervention in the workplace (RCT, N=64): reduced emotional exhaustion, improved job satisfaction, and reduced surface acting in customer interactions. The mechanism operates through increased awareness of internal states — the same mechanism that creates access to the implicit belief layer.

The RPA application: mindfulness is not a standalone intervention for implicit belief change — the evidence doesn't support that claim. It is an access enhancer: it increases the manager's ability to notice their implicit states, which is the precondition for the ITC and Argyris double-loop processes to work. Brief mindfulness practices integrated into the coaching process — not as the primary methodology — are evidence-supported as an adjunct.

## Part 3: The Somatic Question — Does Implicit Belief Change Require Body Access?

### The Damasio Argument for Somatic Necessity

The most significant open question in implicit belief change methodology: if implicit beliefs are stored as somatic markers (Damasio, 1994, *Descartes' Error*) — as bodily states associated with specific stimuli — then accessing and revising them may require more than cognitive intervention. The somatic marker is not only expressed as behavior; it is stored as a body-level state. Cognitive-behavioral approaches (ITC, Argyris) address the cognitive representation of the belief. Whether they reach the somatic storage is the question.

Damasio's hypothesis implies that for beliefs with strong somatic encoding — beliefs that are accompanied by significant physiological activation when the relevant stimulus is encountered — the cognitive revision process may be incomplete without corresponding somatic processing. The cognitive belief may change while the body continues to produce the original somatic marker, driving the original automatic behavior.

### The Somatic Therapy Evidence

**Levine — Somatic Experiencing:**
Levine (1997, *Waking the Tiger*, North Atlantic Books; 2010, *In an Unshakeable Present*, Sounds True) developed Somatic Experiencing (SE) as an intervention for trauma stored in the nervous system. The core claim: experiences that produce intense threat activation — particularly when the threat response is incomplete (the defensive action was prevented or overwhelmed) — become stored as physiological patterns in the nervous system. These patterns continue to activate in response to cues that resemble the original experience, regardless of cognitive understanding that the original threat is no longer present.

SE works by returning the body to the point of activation and completing the interrupted defensive response — through titrated, body-oriented processing — allowing the nervous system to discharge the stored activation and update the threat response.

**The organizational application:** Most implicit beliefs in organizational leaders are not traumatically stored in the clinical sense. But beliefs formed through repeated, emotionally significant experiences of what is safe and unsafe in authority contexts may have somatic components that cognitive-behavioral approaches don't fully reach. The manager whose authority-protection belief activates with significant physiological urgency — physical tightening, elevated heart rate, the felt sense of threat when challenged — may need body-level access to fully revise the belief.

**Ogden — Sensorimotor Psychotherapy:**
Ogden, Minton & Pain (2006, *Trauma and the Body*, Norton) developed Sensorimotor Psychotherapy, which works with the body's habitual response patterns — posture, movement, gesture — as entry points to implicit material. The body's habitual responses are the behavioral expression of implicit beliefs; working directly with these responses can produce access to the belief level that top-down (cognitive) approaches cannot always reach.

The principle: some implicit material is more accessible from the body upward than from the mind downward. Noticing and staying with the body's response to a specific memory or cue — rather than immediately narrating the experience — can produce access to the implicit evaluation that the body is expressing.

**Van der Kolk (2014, *The Body Keeps the Score*, Viking):**
Documents that body-oriented approaches (EMDR, yoga, SE, somatic therapies) produce outcomes for traumatically stored implicit material that talk therapy and behavioral approaches do not reliably produce. The mechanism: the implicit material is stored at a level of the nervous system that is not effectively reached through language-based processing.

### Determining When Somatic Access Is Indicated

The honest synthesis from the literature:

**Cognitive-behavioral approaches (ITC, Argyris double-loop, reflective function development) are likely sufficient when:**
- The implicit belief is accompanied by mild to moderate physiological activation when triggered
- The belief was formed primarily through social learning and repeated professional experience rather than through specific high-threat events
- The manager can access the felt sense of the belief and stay with it within the window of tolerance without flooding or shutdown
- The ITC experiments produce genuine disconfirmation that the manager can register and integrate

**Somatic access is likely indicated when:**
- The implicit belief activates with strong, rapid physiological response — significant tightening, urgency, or activation that makes the window of tolerance difficult to maintain
- The manager has done the cognitive work (named the Big Assumption, understands the competing commitment) but the behavior doesn't change — the body is still running the original response
- The manager reports knowing what they should do differently and genuinely intending to do it, but finding that under pressure the original response takes over — even with implementation intentions in place
- There are indicators that the belief was formed in the context of significant threat experiences (not necessarily clinical trauma; experiences of public failure, significant authority challenges, professional humiliation)

**The RPA boundary:** Andrew is a non-clinical organizational consultant. Full somatic trauma work is outside his scope. The indicators above are diagnostic — they identify when a manager may need a referral to a practitioner with somatic therapy training, or when the engagement should include a somatic-oriented coach as part of the team. Within the RPA scope, body-awareness practices (attending to the felt sense in coaching sessions, mindfulness-based access) are appropriate; clinical somatic trauma processing is not.

## Part 4: The Coaching Relationship as a Change Mechanism

### The Relationship Is Not the Delivery Vehicle — It Is the Intervention

The implicit cognition and interpersonal neurobiology research converge on a conclusion that the coaching field has been slower to formalize: **the coaching relationship itself — independent of the specific techniques used within it — is a primary change mechanism for implicit belief revision.** Not a warm container for technique delivery, but an active ingredient.

**The common factors research:**
In psychotherapy outcome research, the quality of the therapeutic alliance consistently accounts for more variance in outcome than the specific therapeutic model — across hundreds of comparative studies (Wampold, 2001, *The Great Psychotherapy Debate*, Erlbaum; Horvath & Bedi, 2002, in *Psychotherapy Relationships That Work*, Oxford). The "common factors" — therapeutic alliance, empathy, genuineness, positive regard — are more predictive of outcome than the technique. Stober & Grant (2006, *Evidence-Based Coaching Handbook*, Wiley) applied this finding to coaching: relationship quality is the primary outcome predictor in coaching as well.

The mechanism, from Siegel's interpersonal neurobiology (1999; 2010): **a securely regulating relationship changes the nervous system.** The experience of being accurately understood, genuinely regarded, and not shamed when vulnerable activates the social engagement system (Porges, 2011) and creates the neuroceptive conditions for the kind of reflective processing that implicit belief revision requires. The manager who experiences the coaching relationship as safe — over time, through repeated evidence — develops the window of tolerance and the mindsight capacity that the belief change work requires.

### Earned Secure Attachment — Siegel and Fonagy

Siegel draws on Main's (1991) concept of **earned secure attachment** — the finding that adults who did not develop secure attachment in childhood can develop secure attachment functioning through corrective relational experiences in adulthood. The attachment system — which governs the capacity for the kind of safe reflective relationship that implicit belief change requires — is not fixed after childhood. It continues to develop through relational experience.

Fonagy et al. (2002, *Affect Regulation, Mentalization, and the Development of the Self*) add the mechanism: being in a relationship with someone who **mentalizes** accurately — who perceives and reflects back one's mental states with curiosity and care, without evaluation or dismissal — develops one's own capacity for mentalization. The manager who is mentalized by the coach over time develops greater mindsight — the capacity to observe their own implicit states — which is the mechanism by which the belief revision work becomes possible.

**The practical implication:** The coaching relationship should be established and maintained deliberately — not as a warm-up to the real work but as one of the primary interventions. The conditions that characterize a mentalizing relationship:

- **Curiosity without evaluation:** The coach is genuinely interested in the manager's experience without having a predetermined conclusion about what it means
- **Accurate reflection:** The coach reflects back what they are observing and hearing in a way that the manager recognizes — "you're hearing me" is the felt experience of being accurately mentalized
- **Non-shame activation:** The coach maintains a tone that communicates "this is interesting and worth examining" rather than "this is wrong and needs to be corrected"
- **Sustained attention to mental states:** The coach asks about internal experience — what the manager was noticing, feeling, intending — not just external behavior

### Co-Regulation as a Therapeutic Factor

Porges' co-regulation mechanism (2011) applies to the coaching relationship. The coach's regulated state — calm, engaged, curious, not activated by what the manager reveals — transmits to the manager's nervous system through the co-regulation pathway. This is the mechanism by which the coach's internal state matters more than their technique in moments of high activation.

A coach who is subtly frustrated that the manager is not progressing, or anxious about whether the approach is working, or evaluative about what the manager reveals — will transmit this through co-regulation signals. The manager's nervous system will register the threat and the window of tolerance will narrow. The coach who maintains genuine regulated engagement — which requires the coach's own implicit belief work to be ongoing — creates the neurophysiological conditions for the manager's access to their own implicit layer.

## Part 5: What Does Not Change Implicit Beliefs — The Failure Modes

### Knowledge and Insight Alone

Knowing that implicit beliefs exist and produce automatic behavior does not change the beliefs. This is one of the most replicated findings in implicit cognition research (Wilson, Lindsey & Schooler, 2000, *Psychological Review*, 107(1), 101–126): awareness of an implicit attitude does not produce spontaneous revision of that attitude. The explicit system can acquire the knowledge; the implicit system continues operating from its existing associations.

In coaching terms: the manager who can explain the ITC framework fluently, who can describe their competing commitment and Big Assumption accurately, and who genuinely intends to change is not yet in the change process. Intellectual comprehension is a precondition, not the mechanism.

**The double-bind of insight in coaching:** The manager who develops insight quickly and then presents that insight articulately may be using insight as a form of armor — staying at the level of ideas prevents the need to access the felt sense of the belief, which is the more threatening engagement. The coach who mistakes fluent insight for genuine access will miscalibrate the depth of work remaining.

### Single-Session Feedback

A single feedback session — even powerful, even well-delivered, even received without defensiveness — does not produce lasting implicit belief change. The implicit system updates through repeated experiential evidence, not through a single informational input. The feedback may produce a period of increased deliberate effort to behave differently; the underlying belief continues operating and will reassert when deliberate effort is not available.

This is why 360-degree feedback programs consistently show limited long-term behavioral change without sustained coaching engagement. The feedback surfaces the gap; it does not close it.

### Effort and Intention Without Access

The manager who is trying harder — consciously monitoring their responses, deliberating before speaking, working to apply the trained behaviors — is applying deliberate effort against an automatic process. This strategy has two failure modes:

1. **Pressure depletion:** Deliberate effort consumes cognitive resources. Under sufficient pressure, time constraint, or emotional activation, the resources are not available and the automatic process runs unimpeded. This is why the reversion under pressure is so consistent — it is not weakness of character but depletion of the resource the deliberate strategy depends on.

2. **Behavioral compliance without internal change:** The manager succeeds at producing the desired behavior through effort — employees receive it; surveys may show improvement — but the internal experience is one of performing rather than being. This performance is detectable through the implicit layer: the tone, the timing, and the felt quality of the interaction communicate effort rather than genuine engagement. Employees register the difference, and their psychological safety assessment reflects it.

### Behavioral Training Without the Belief Layer

As established across multiple briefs: behavioral training addresses Column 2 without Column 4. The belief layer continues generating the Column 2 behaviors. Under low-pressure, high-salience-of-training conditions, the trained behavior may override; under high-pressure, low-salience conditions, the belief layer wins. This is the mechanism behind every training program that showed strong initial results and poor six-month outcomes.

## Part 6: The Change Sequence — What the Process Actually Looks Like

### The Four-Stage Sequence

The research across Argyris, Kegan & Lahey, Siegel, and the implicit cognition literature converges on a four-stage sequence for genuine implicit belief revision:

**Stage 1 — Awareness: The manager becomes aware that a gap exists**
- Source: external data, not self-report — observation notes, 360 feedback, specific incident analysis, the left-hand column exercise
- What produces readiness for Stage 2: the manager can hold the evidence without dismissing it and without being flooded by it — they are within the window of tolerance for this information
- Indicator that Stage 1 is complete: the manager is curious about the gap rather than defensive about it or devastated by it
- Ceiling effect: the manager who is in persistent shame about the gap cannot progress — shame produces defensiveness or self-condemnation, neither of which enables examination

**Stage 2 — Access: The implicit belief is surfaced**
- Source: the ITC four-column process; the left-hand column exercise; the coaching question "what were you noticing internally in that moment?"; the felt sense approach
- What it looks like when access is genuine: the manager makes contact with the competing commitment or Big Assumption not just as an idea but as a felt reality — "yes, that is what is happening in me"
- The difference between intellectual access and genuine access: genuine access has a quality of recognition — the manager encounters their own implicit belief and recognizes it rather than concluding about it
- Ceiling effect: the manager who stays at the level of ideas about their implicit beliefs has not accessed them; they have described them from outside. The coaching task is to stay in the access question — "tell me more about what that is like for you" — rather than moving to solution

**Stage 3 — Experiment: The Big Assumption is tested in low-risk conditions**
- Source: Kegan & Lahey's designed behavioral experiment methodology
- The experiment design principles: the test must be specific enough to produce clear data; the stakes must be low enough that the manager can conduct the experiment without the Big Assumption activating at full intensity; the outcome must be something the manager can observe
- What disconfirming evidence looks like: the manager says "I don't know" in a meeting and the team engages more actively rather than losing confidence in them. The manager receives a challenge without immediately defending and the conversation goes better. The manager surfaces uncertainty and their authority is not undermined.
- Why multiple experiments are needed: one disconfirming data point can be rationalized as an exception. The implicit system updates through accumulated evidence, not through a single event. Three to five disconfirming experiments across varied conditions produce the experiential weight that weakens the Big Assumption's hold.
- Ceiling effect: if the manager's threat response is too strong to conduct the experiment — if the thought of saying "I don't know" in a meeting produces such strong activation that they cannot attempt it even in a low-stakes context — this indicates somatic access is needed before the experiment can be conducted

**Stage 4 — Revision: The assumption is revised; the automatic behavior loses its driver**
- What revision looks like: the Big Assumption's quality of "simply true" diminishes — the manager begins to hold it as "an assumption I used to have, and I'm not sure it's accurate" rather than as fact. This is a shift in epistemological relationship to the belief, not necessarily elimination of the belief.
- Behavioral indicator: the new behavior begins to appear in conditions where the manager is not deliberately monitoring — consistency without effort is the marker. The manager who behaves well only when they're thinking about it has not yet revised; the manager who behaves consistently across conditions — including under pressure — has revised at the belief level.
- Timeline: the full sequence, conducted with sustained engagement, typically produces observable Stage 4 indicators in three to six months. Deeper beliefs with stronger somatic encoding take longer. Beliefs with moderate cognitive encoding and moderate somatic activation are most responsive to the ITC process within this timeline.

### Where Ceiling Effects Appear

**Between Stage 1 and Stage 2:** Shame activation prevents access. The manager who is flooded by self-condemnation when the gap is named cannot move to genuine access — the shame response is itself a defensive closure. The coaching response: slow down; work on the shame-belief relationship (Brown's work on shame and worthiness) before approaching the implicit belief directly.

**Between Stage 2 and Stage 3:** Somatic activation prevents the experiment. The manager can name their Big Assumption but cannot conduct the behavioral test because the activation is too strong. The coaching response: somatic access work, referral to somatic practitioner, or significant reduction of experiment stakes before Stage 3 is attempted.

**Between Stage 3 and Stage 4:** Rationalization prevents revision. The manager explains each disconfirming data point as an exception rather than updating the assumption. The coaching response: return to the accumulation of evidence without pushing for the conclusion — "I notice we now have five situations where the prediction didn't hold. What do you make of that pattern?" The manager's own recognition of the pattern is more durable than the coach's argument.

## Part 7: RPA Methodology Implications

### What the Evidence Base Says the RPA Engagement Should Include

**1. Observation data as the entry point, not self-report**
The self-monitoring gap (Keltner; Atwater & Yammarino) makes self-report unreliable as diagnostic input. The engagement should begin with Andrew's observation of the manager in their actual operational environment — capturing the implicit layer signals in their natural context — before the coaching conversation addresses the belief level. This provides the external data that Stage 1 (awareness) requires and avoids the rationalization that self-report enables.

**2. A coaching modality that accesses felt sense, not just cognitive description**
The coaching questions that access the implicit layer are experiential, not descriptive: "What were you noticing in yourself in that moment?" rather than "Why do you think you responded that way?" The latter invites espoused theory; the former invites the implicit layer. The RPA coaching methodology should train this distinction as a core practice — the shift from "why" to "what were you noticing" is the shift from the explicit to the implicit system.

**3. The ITC four-column process as the primary intervention tool**
The ITC process is the most developed, most evidence-supported methodology for the exact structure of this problem: genuine intention + automatic sabotaging behavior + hidden competing commitment + Big Assumption. The four-column map should be a standard deliverable in the manager coaching track — not a one-time exercise but a working document refined over multiple sessions.

**4. Designed behavioral experiments as the primary change vehicle**
Big Assumption testing through low-risk behavioral experiments is the mechanism the evidence supports for implicit belief revision. The experiment design is a coaching skill — the stakes must be calibrated, the conditions must be specific, the observation must be structured so the manager can notice what actually happened rather than what the Big Assumption predicted. Andrew's role includes experiment design, not just process facilitation.

**5. Sustained engagement over time — not a workshop**
The sequence requires multiple coaching sessions over months — not a training event. The implicit system updates through accumulated experiential evidence, not through a single insight or workshop. The RPA engagement structure should build in sufficient sessions for the full sequence: typically eight to twelve focused coaching conversations over three to six months for managers with moderate activation; longer for managers with strong somatic encoding.

**6. Mindfulness as an adjunct access enhancer**
Brief mindfulness practice integrated into coaching sessions — as a way of increasing the manager's access to the felt sense of the implicit layer — is evidence-supported as an adjunct. Not the primary methodology; an access tool. A brief body-scan or focusing exercise at the opening of a coaching session can increase the manager's capacity for the implicit layer work that follows.

**7. Somatic assessment and referral protocol**
Some managers will present with implicit beliefs that have strong somatic encoding — beliefs that produce significant physiological activation and are not reached by the ITC cognitive-behavioral process alone. The RPA engagement should include a protocol for recognizing these presentations and either including a somatic-oriented coaching practitioner or making an appropriate referral. The indicators are in Part 3.

**8. The coaching relationship as a primary intervention — not background**
The mentalizing quality of the coaching relationship — curiosity, accurate reflection, non-shame activation, sustained attention to mental states — is itself a change mechanism. This is not soft context; it is an active ingredient with a documented neurobiological mechanism (Siegel; Fonagy; Porges). The RPA practitioner's own capacity for this quality of engagement — which requires the practitioner's own ongoing self-awareness work — is a core competency, not a personality trait.

### How to Present the RPA Approach to a Sponsor

The Level 3 research gives Andrew the argument that the sponsor brief's value proposition requires:

**"Standard management training addresses Column 2 — what managers do. It teaches different behaviors and produces real change in contexts where the training is salient. What it doesn't reach is Column 4 — the operating belief that is generating the Column 2 behaviors. The operating belief continues running automatically, especially under pressure. The RPA engagement is designed to reach Column 4. The process is different from training: it involves observation, structured coaching, and designed experiments — and it takes months, not days. But the change it produces is different in kind: behavior that holds under pressure because the belief generating it has changed, not behavior that holds only when the manager is thinking about it."**

The sponsor should understand: they are not buying better training content. They are buying a different level of intervention.
