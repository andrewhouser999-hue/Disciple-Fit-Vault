# Relational Performance Advisory — Competitive Landscape Reference

**Practitioner use note:** Internalize before any conversation where a prospect is evaluating options or has named a program they've already tried. The goal is not to disparage competitors — it is to locate the gap those programs leave and explain what RPA addresses instead.

**The one-sentence differentiation:** Every program below teaches managers what to do; RPA addresses why they don't do it even after training.

---

## The Core Argument

Standard management training operates on a competency-deficit assumption: managers underperform because they lack skills or awareness. Teach the skill, close the gap.

That assumption is wrong in the cases that matter most. The managers who most need to change have often already been through training. The problem is not information — it is that relational and regulatory patterns operate below the level that curriculum reaches. A manager can articulate the principles of a crucial conversation and still avoid the conversation. A leader can score well on a 360 and still create an environment where no one tells them the truth.

RPA targets the mechanism those programs don't reach: the relational dynamics that determine whether managers can actually execute what they know.

---

## Competitive Landscape

### By Category

**Habit & Curriculum Programs** — Sell systematic skill repetition. Assume the problem is missing knowledge or underdeveloped habits.

**Communication Technique Programs** — Sell specific conversation frameworks. Assume the problem is a skills gap in a particular domain.

**Assessment & Awareness Programs** — Sell self-knowledge and style awareness. Assume the problem is insufficient insight.

**Developmental / Assessment Platforms** — Sell growth frameworks tied to 360 data. The most sophisticated category; some explicitly use adult development theory.

**Facilitation & Process Programs** — Sell meeting effectiveness and collaborative process skills. Assume the problem is poor group process.

## What RPA Has That None of Them Have

**1. Behavioral observation in the room**
Every program above is assessment-dependent or curriculum-delivered. Andrew watches what actually happens — not what leaders self-report, not what raters perceive, but what the leader does when challenged, when authority is questioned, when repair is required. That's a different class of data.

**2. Relational safety as a performance prerequisite**
None of these programs treat relational safety as a precondition for what they're teaching. They assume a functional relational environment and layer skills on top. RPA addresses the environment itself.

**3. Developmental stage as a performance variable**
The Leadership Circle comes closest, but their mechanism is survey data. The question of whether a manager is operating from an approval-seeking framework (Socialized Mind) vs. a results-generating one (Self-Authoring) determines whether any other intervention lands. Most training programs deliver content to managers who aren't yet capable of receiving it the way it was designed.

**4. Change readiness before intervention**
Prochaska/Miller — if a leader isn't ready to change, training is waste. RPA assesses readiness before determining what to offer. No program on this list does that.

**5. Organization-level architecture with individual-level precision**
Every program is either individual (coaching, 360, profile) or group (cohort, facilitation). RPA operates at both simultaneously — individual assessment + team facilitation + structural recommendations — within a single engagement.

## Positioning Decision: Two Different Markets

**Market A — Alternative to training:** The prospect has not yet purchased training and is evaluating options. Position RPA as a different category entirely — not a training program, an advisory engagement. The value proposition is precision over curriculum: "We find out what's actually happening before we recommend what to do."

**Market B — Post-training failure:** The prospect has tried training (any of the above) and still has the same management problems. This is RPA's strongest entry point. The prospect already knows training didn't hold. The question is why, and what addresses the underlying issue.

**For AOI:** Determine in the first conversation whether the owner has tried any programs above. If yes — that's Market B positioning. If no — that's Market A. The core argument is the same; the entry sequence is different.

## Conversation Notes by Scenario

**"We've already done DiSC / CliftonStrengths"**
Acknowledge the investment. Validate the insight value. Then: "What changed in terms of how your managers actually handle difficult moments?" — That question exposes the transfer gap without requiring you to criticize the program.

**"We were looking at Crucial Conversations"**
"That's a well-researched program for the specific skill of high-stakes dialogue. Before you make that investment — what do you know about why those conversations aren't happening now? If the answer is 'managers don't know how,' that program addresses it. If the answer is something else, we should talk about what."

**"I've heard of The Leadership Circle — is that what you do?"**
"It's in the same territory — it actually uses some of the same research base. The difference is the diagnostic mechanism. The Leadership Circle tells leaders what their 360 raters perceive. I observe what the leader actually does in the room. Both are useful; they answer different questions. I can also build a development arc from the findings, not just deliver a report."

**"We had a management trainer come in last year"**
"How long did the behavior change last?" — Let them answer. Most will name a number of weeks. Then: "That's the transfer gap. Training delivers well in the room. What it doesn't reach is the pattern that comes back under pressure. That's the mechanism I work on."
