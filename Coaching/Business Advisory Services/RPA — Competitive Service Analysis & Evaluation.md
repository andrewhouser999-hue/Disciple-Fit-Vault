# RPA — Competitive Service Analysis & Evaluation

**What this is:** Strategic reference identifying which firms have attempted services comparable to the full RPA arc — structured assessment, individual coaching, team facilitation, and structural recommendations — along with an honest evaluation of where each stops short and why. Distinct from [[Relational Performance Advisory — Competitive Landscape Reference]], which catalogs specific training programs. This document addresses the full-stack service question.

---

## Who Has Attempted the Full Stack

### Closest Full-Stack Matches

**Korn Ferry**
- What they do: Structured interviews, behavioral observation, KF360 multi-rater, individual leader profiles (pattern-specific through their competency/trait model), coaching engagements, team effectiveness work, organizational design recommendations at close.
- Outcome: Successfully delivered at enterprise scale.
- Gap: Not SMB. Not hospitality specifically. Minimum engagement cost prices out the owner-operator. Requires HR infrastructure SMB doesn't have.
- Implicit level: Operates at explicit behavioral/competency level. No theory of regulatory state or attachment-informed pattern driving the behavior.

**DDI (Development Dimensions International)**
- What they do: Assessment centers with behavioral observation, structured interviews (they developed behavioral interviewing methodology), 360s, individual profiles, coaching arcs, team facilitation. Full stack.
- Outcome: Successfully delivered at enterprise and mid-market. Moderately successful.
- Gap: Enterprise-focused delivery model. Same infrastructure problem as Korn Ferry at SMB level.
- Implicit level: Explicit behavioral level only.

**Center for Creative Leadership (CCL)**
- What they do: 360 assessments, individual profiles, coaching arcs, team facilitation. Strong track record. Academic credibility.
- Gap: Not diagnosis-forward. Does not frame as "why accountability breaks down" — frames as leadership development. Not industry-specific.
- Implicit level: Explicit behavioral/developmental level. Some frameworks touch implicit territory but it is not the diagnostic target.

**The Table Group (Lencioni)**
- What they do: Team assessment, individual leader work, team facilitation, structural recommendations at close. Built around dysfunction diagnosis — accountability is explicitly one of the five dysfunctions.
- Gap: Operates at the team health level, not the individual implicit level. Does not work where the behavioral problems actually exist — the regulatory state, attachment-informed pattern, stress-response mechanism driving the behavior.
- Implicit level: **Does NOT work at the implicit level.** Model names dysfunctions and builds explicit team commitments and agreements. Has no theory of WHY a specific person cannot tolerate conflict or hold accountability at the mechanism level. Interventions produce change that holds in low-pressure conditions and fails when Window of Tolerance is exceeded.
- Mid-market friendly. Has been delivered in hospitality chains.

**Hogan Assessments Ecosystem**
- What they do: Hogan personality profiles are explicitly pattern-specific and non-generic, built around derailment — why leaders fail, including accountability failure. Certified Hogan coaches bundle this with 360 + coaching arcs. Closest to "your pattern, not a generic report."
- Gap: The ecosystem is assessment-focused. Structural recommendations at close are not a standard deliverable. Hospitality SMB is not the target market.
- Implicit level: Hogan profiles touch implicit-level personality structure (derailment under pressure). Closer to implicit level than most competitors but does not use regulatory state or attachment frameworks explicitly.

**Right Management / ManpowerGroup**
- What they do: Workforce assessment, 360s, coaching, outplacement, organizational consulting.
- Gap: Generalist. No hospitality industry specificity. Assessment-heavy, structural recommendations less consistent.

## Why Competitors Stop Short — What They Would Say

### The Business Model Argument (Enterprise Firms)
Enterprise firms cannot profitably serve SMB. They need HR infrastructure that SMB doesn't have. The gap isn't competence — it's business model. They built for Fortune 1000 procurement cycles, not a hotel chain owner making a handshake decision.

### The Skill Integration Gap (Individual Coaches)
The full arc requires simultaneous competence across four distinct professional domains: behavioral assessment/I-O psychology, executive coaching, group facilitation/OD, and management consulting/org design. Most practitioners are strong in one or two. The combination — calibrated specifically for hospitality SMB — is genuinely uncommon. No one has assembled it for this buyer segment at this scale.

### Why They Don't Work at the Implicit Level — The Wisdom of Established Providers

These are not naive organizations. Their choice to stay at the explicit level carries legitimate professional reasoning.

**Scope and liability.** Working at the implicit level is clinically adjacent. The closer you get to regulatory state, attachment pattern, and stress-response physiology, the closer you get to therapeutic territory. Staying at the explicit behavioral level is scope integrity, not intellectual laziness. Consultants who overextend into clinical-adjacent territory without clinical training cause real harm.

**Scalability.** Implicit-level work is deeply individualized, slow, and relationship-intensive. Enterprise firms need methodologies that can be deployed by teams across hundreds of clients simultaneously. Implicit-level work cannot be standardized or templated.

**Buyer readiness.** Most organizational buyers — HR directors, CHROs, CEOs — are not purchasing psychological depth. They are buying behavioral outcomes and culture metrics. The market has not asked for implicit-level work at scale.

**Evidence standard for organizational ROI.** Behavior change at the explicit level can be measured: 360 score movement, behavioral frequency, retention metrics. Implicit-level change is harder to tie to organizational metrics in a way that satisfies CFO scrutiny.

**The harm risk — the most serious argument.** Surfacing implicit-level content — regulatory failure, attachment wounds, shame-based reactivity — without the clinical container to hold it can destabilize a functioning leader. A destabilized leader manages a team. The organizational consequences are real and the consultant bears responsibility. Most organizational consultants are not equipped for that responsibility and don't know it.

**The change mechanism question.** There is a legitimate scientific debate about whether implicit-level change in adults is achievable in organizational timeframes. Attachment patterns form early and reinforce over decades. Staying at the explicit behavioral level is epistemically humble — explicit behavior change is achievable and evidenced; implicit pattern change in six-month coaching arcs is a larger claim with less organizational scale evidence.

**The organizational context doesn't provide clinical containment.** Therapy works in part because of the container — weekly sessions, a bounded relationship, a practitioner with clinical supervision, a protected space outside the performance context. Organizational coaching operates inside the performance context. The manager being coached is simultaneously being evaluated by the owner who hired the consultant. That context imposes real limits on psychological depth.

## Where RPA's Differentiation Lives

Not in the methodology — structured interviews, 360s, behavioral observation, individual profiles, coaching arcs, team facilitation, structural recommendations are all standard OD/leadership development tools.

**Target.** The buyer is an owner-operator with equity on the line, not a CHRO buying a development benefit. Speaking to that buyer requires business-outcome language, not HR language.

**Framing.** "Why accountability breaks down" is a causation claim. Most providers say: here are your managers' development areas. RPA says: here is the mechanism by which this organization fails to hold accountability — and I can show you specifically.

**The close.** Structural recommendations as a deliverable, not a development plan. Org design work that makes behavior changes hold.

**The implicit level, diagnostically.** RPA targets the mechanism level beneath behavior — regulatory states, stress-response patterns, attachment-informed relational patterns — for more accurate diagnosis and more targeted coaching. The claim is not that RPA does therapy. The claim is that RPA sees more accurately why the behavior is automatic.

---

## Honest Constraints on the Implicit Level Claim

The RPA model is built with explicit scope limits — non-clinical language, behavioral observation only, documented referral protocol. The implicit level is targeted diagnostically, not therapeutically.

However: the line between observing the implicit level diagnostically and intervening at it therapeutically is thinner in practice than in a document. A leader who receives an accurate pattern profile and begins connecting it to their history will not stay in the behavioral lane without a practitioner equipped to hold that boundary — and who has enough clinical literacy to recognize when it's been crossed.

Clinical supervision addresses this constraint. See [[CAPS — Clinical Supervision Outreach & Discussion Guide]].
