# Keeping Eyes on Jesus — Teacher's Manual

*Coaching Curriculum | Disciple Fit Covenant Identity Coaching*

## How to Use This Manual

This manual provides the full biblical and theological framework behind the *Keeping Eyes on Jesus* client study. It is coach-facing only.

**When to deploy:** After a breakthrough moment in session — when a client has been pulled up from under a wave and is in a posture of receptivity. This is the moment to hand them the Client Study for independent reading between sessions.

**How the documents connect:**
- **This manual** — Full framework, author reference, coaching tools
- **Client Study** — 2-3 page distillation of the mechanics section for client independent reading
- **Coach Diagnostic** — Identifies which mechanical gap the client is experiencing before or during session
- **Practice Reference Card** — Client-facing keeper listing all formation practices with explanations

**Session flow:**
1. Use the Coach Diagnostic before or early in session to identify the client's primary gap(s)
2. Pull the client up from the wave in session using coaching prompts in Parts 2 and 3 below
3. Provide the Client Study as a take-home after the breakthrough moment
4. Verbally recommend 1-2 practices from the Reference Card based on their identified gap
5. Next session: debrief the study; assign practices more formally if appropriate

**For scripted session language, deployment timing, and return session protocol:** See the companion document — *Keeping Eyes on Jesus — Session Facilitation Guide* (Biblical Session Tools folder). It covers: how to read the moment before handing the study, exact words to use at handoff, how to frame the Greek in session, what to listen for when the client returns, and how to assign the first practice.

### The Biblical/Theological Foundation

Matthew 14 is precise. Peter's failure isn't *noticing* the waves — it's **transferring full attention to them**. Two things are clinically important:

- The Greek word for "doubt" is *distazō* — to stand in two ways, to waver between two objects of attention. Not unbelief. **Divided attention.**
- Hebrews 12:2 uses *aphorao* — "looking away from everything else to fix on." It implies an active, deliberate redirect, not a passive feeling.

Peter didn't fail because the storm was overwhelming. He failed because his attentional faculty hadn't been trained to hold steady under load. That's the real diagnostic.

**"Keeping your eyes on Jesus" is not:**
- Denial of circumstances
- Emotional positivity
- Willpower to "not worry"

It is a **trained attentional faculty** — the soul's capacity to return its gaze to Christ when reality pulls it away. The mechanism is stated in 2 Cor 3:18: beholding → transformation. You become like what you behold. This means the practice isn't just about surviving storms; it's about *who you are becoming* through sustained attentiveness.

### The Non-Obvious Insight

The storm didn't cause Peter's problem — it *revealed* his formation gap. This means the practice of keeping eyes on Jesus is **not primarily a crisis skill**. It's a daily formation discipline that you only discover matters when waves hit.

The person who keeps their eyes on Jesus under pressure is the person who has been keeping their eyes on Jesus when it costs nothing.

### Client Translation Notes

*For use when the client hasn't read the study, has low theological literacy, or when you need to describe a concept verbally without Greek. One plain-language version and one in-session phrasing per concept.*

---

**Divided attention** *(distazō)*
Peter didn't stop believing in Jesus when he sank — he split his attention. He was tracking two things at once, and the storm got more weight.
*In session:* "It's not a faith problem. It's an attention split. Something else got more weight than Jesus in that moment. What's getting the weight right now?"

---

**The gaze has two directions** *(aphorao)*
Keeping your eyes on Jesus has two moves, not one. First you turn away from what's been holding your attention. Then you turn toward Him. You can't add Jesus on top of an already full field — you have to clear space first.
*In session:* "Think of turning to look at something behind you — you can't actually face it while still facing forward. There's a turn-away that has to happen first. What would you need to turn away from to make room for this?"

---

**The looking produces the change** *(katoptrizomenoi)*
As you keep looking at Jesus — sustained, ongoing — you start to change. Not because you're trying hard to change. The change happens *through* the looking. It's the byproduct of sustained attention.
*In session:* "Change isn't the goal you're aiming at. It's what happens when you do the looking. The looking is the practice. The change comes with it."

---

**Your role is to show up; the Spirit's role is everything else** *(metamorphoumetha)*
You're not responsible for producing the transformation. You're responsible for positioning yourself for it. Your part is showing up and looking. His part is the actual change.
*In session:* "You're not trying to force change to happen. You're putting yourself where He can do what only He can do. That's it."

---

**The wave-narrative**
When life goes sideways, it's not just a problem — it's a claim. Circumstances say something about what's real, what's possible, what to be afraid of. That claim competes with what Jesus says about your situation. The question is which account gets to govern your next step.
*In session:* "The wave isn't just scary — it's making an argument. Jesus is making a different argument. Which one has more weight in your thinking right now?"

---

**The covenant gaze**
This isn't one-directional — you trying to look at Jesus. He is already looking at you. The reason it's so hard to sustain looking at Him is that most people haven't actually received the reality that His eyes are already on them. You're returning a gaze He started.
*In session:* "Before this becomes about you sustaining your attention on Him — have you actually received the fact that He's already attending to you right now? Not in theory. Actually received it?"

---

**Training vs. trying**
Trying is what you do in the storm — you muster everything to hold on. Training is what you do in the calm — you build the capacity before you need it. A musician doesn't learn the piece during the concert. The concert reveals what the practice built.
*In session:* "You can't train for a storm during the storm. The question is: what are you building now, in the calm, that'll be there when the waves hit?"

### The Anatomy of the Moment

Matthew 14 is precise. Peter's failure isn't *noticing* the waves — it's **transferring full attention to them**. Two things are clinically important:

- The Greek word for "doubt" is *distazō* — to stand in two ways, to waver between two objects of attention. Not unbelief. **Divided attention.**
- Hebrews 12:2 uses *aphorao* — "looking away from everything else to fix on." It implies an active, deliberate redirect, not a passive feeling.

Peter didn't fail because the storm was overwhelming. He failed because his attentional faculty hadn't been trained to hold steady under load. That's the real diagnostic.

> **Coaching Prompts**
> 1. "When you picture this moment with Peter — where do you most identify with him? At the moment of stepping out, or the moment of noticing the wind?"
> 2. "What does 'the waves' represent in your life right now — what is pulling your attention away from Jesus?"
> 3. "If attention is a resource like time or money — where would you say most of yours actually goes right now?"
> 4. "Have you ever been walking toward Jesus and something shifted your gaze? What happened, and how quickly did you notice?"
>
> **Watch for This**
> - Clients who identify with Peter stepping out may be in a courage-building season. Don't rush to the wave conversation — affirm the step first.
> - Clients carrying shame about past "sinking" moments need to hear that Jesus reached out *immediately* before any analysis of why they sank.
> - Some clients will intellectualize fluently (identify the waves easily) but avoid feeling the weight of where their attention actually lives. Fluency without conviction is a sign the content hasn't landed yet.

### 1. The Greek Word Architecture

Four words form the mechanical skeleton. Everything else hangs on these.

**distazō** (Matt 14:31 — "you of little faith, why did you *doubt*?")
Root: *dis* (twice) + a standing position. Means to stand in two places simultaneously. Not skepticism. Not unbelief. **Divided attentional weight.** Peter didn't stop believing in Jesus when he sank — he split his attention between two competing authorities and the weight shifted. This is precise. The problem was not what he saw. It was where his weight landed.

**aphorao** (Heb 12:2 — "*fixing our eyes* on Jesus")
Compound: *apo* (away from) + *horao* (to see). The *apo* is critical and usually missed. This is not just looking at Jesus — it's **looking away from** something to look at him. The act has two movements, not one. Whoever translated it as "fixing" captured only half. The word implies you are turning your gaze from a prior object. What you turn *away from* is as much the practice as what you turn *toward*.

**katoptrizomenoi** (2 Cor 3:18 — "*beholding* the glory of the Lord")
From *katoptron* (mirror). Present active participle — ongoing, not completed. "As we are continuously beholding, we are continuously being transformed." The transformation is not a separate event. It is the **byproduct of sustained beholding**. You don't behold and then get transformed. The beholding *is* the mechanism. This is the most important mechanical word in the New Testament for this topic.

**metamorphoumetha** (2 Cor 3:18 — "we are *being transformed*")
Same root as Matthew 17:2 — the Transfiguration. Present passive. It is being done *to* you while you behold. The agent named at the end of 3:18 is "the Spirit of the Lord." So the mechanics are: **you behold → the Spirit transforms.** Your role is attentional consent. The Spirit's role is metamorphosis. Conflating those two roles is what makes the practice either passive (waiting to feel something) or exhausting (trying to produce change by force).

> **Coaching Prompts**
> 1. "*Distazō* — standing in two places at once. What are the two places you're standing right now? What is competing for the attention that belongs to Jesus?"
> 2. "*Aphorao* has two movements — turning away from AND turning toward. Which movement is more familiar to you? Which one feels harder?"
> 3. "*Katoptrizomenoi* tells us that beholding and transformation happen simultaneously — not sequentially. How does that shift how you've been thinking about change in your own life?"
> 4. "If your role is attentional consent and the Spirit's role is metamorphosis — which role have you been trying to do? Which one have you been neglecting?"
>
> **Watch for This**
> - The *distazō* insight can produce shame: "I've been divided all along." Redirect quickly — divided attention is the diagnostic, not the verdict. Jesus still reached out.
> - Some clients will fixate on *metamorphoumetha* (transformation as passive) and use it to justify disengagement: "so I just wait?" Clarify: consent is active. Attentional orientation is genuine effort.
> - Clients with charismatic backgrounds may resist the "consent" frame and want the Spirit to "take over" without their active attention. Hold the tension: both are true.
> - The Greek can feel academic if moved through too quickly. Slow down. Let each word land before moving to the next. Watch for the moment a word actually connects — that's where to linger.

### 2. The Image-Bearing Architecture

This is the theological foundation underneath the mechanics.

Humans are *imago Dei* — but the image is not static. It is **directional and dynamic**. You bear the image of what you behold. This is not metaphor. It's embedded in Scripture as a structural reality:

- Psalm 115:8 — "Those who make idols become like them; so does everyone who trusts in them."
- Hosea 9:10 — Israel "consecrated themselves to Baal and became as detestable as the thing they loved."
- 2 Cor 3:18 — beholding the glory of the Lord → being transformed into the same image.

The mechanism is identical in all three texts. The direction changes. Idolatry is not just worship misdirection — it is a **re-imaging process**. You become what you attend to most. This means attention is not a neutral faculty. It is the *shaping mechanism* of the soul. Every sustained object of attention leaves its image on you.

This is why Peter's problem is serious beyond the immediate crisis. Habitually looking at waves doesn't just make you fearful in storms. It *forms* you into someone whose default read of reality is threat-first.

> **Coaching Prompts**
> 1. "If you become like what you attend to most — what image is currently being formed in you? What has had the most sustained claim on your attention this past month?"
> 2. "Hosea 9:10: they 'became as detestable as the thing they loved.' Is there anything in your life that has been re-imaging you — not through overt worship, but through sustained attention?"
> 3. "What would it look like if your default lens on reality shifted from threat-first to Jesus-first? What would be different about how you see your current situation?"
> 4. "Attention is the shaping mechanism of the soul. Does that change how you think about what you consume — media, conversations, what you rehearse mentally?"
>
> **Watch for This**
> - "You become what you behold" can land as condemnation for a client in a long season of wave-fixation. Frame it as explanation, not accusation: "This is why it's been hard — not why you've failed."
> - Clients will often immediately list overt sins as the "idols." Widen the diagnostic — the re-imaging process is often subtler: anxiety rehearsal, social comparison, relational preoccupation, media consumption.
> - Perfectionistic clients may use this framework to create a new performance metric: "I need to make sure I'm always beholding God." Clarify: the goal is not perfect attention, it's practiced return.

### 3. The Trinitarian Mechanics

The practice is not psychological. It is participatory in Trinitarian life.

The structure of 2 Cor 3:18 is: the believer beholds → "from glory to glory" → "by the Spirit of the Lord." Three actors. The human provides attentional orientation. The Spirit provides transformation. The Father's glory is the object. Remove any leg and the structure collapses.

This has a critical implication: **the practice is consent, not performance.** You are not trying to generate transformation through sustained attention. You are making yourself available for the Spirit's work by positioning your gaze. Dallas Willard named this precisely: "Grace is not opposed to effort. It is opposed to earning." The effort is to look. The transformation is grace.

This is also why the practice cannot be mechanized into technique. Brother Lawrence and Frank Laubach both discovered this — you cannot manufacture the encounter by perfecting the method. The method is simply returning. The encounter is the Spirit's initiative.

> **Coaching Prompts**
> 1. "How have you typically understood your role in your own spiritual growth — what have you thought you were responsible to produce?"
> 2. "Willard: 'Grace is not opposed to effort — it is opposed to earning.' How does that land for you? Where does it feel true, and where does it feel uncomfortable?"
> 3. "If the Spirit transforms through beholding and your role is consent — what does that free you from? What does it still require of you?"
> 4. "Have you been trying to generate transformation, or positioning yourself for transformation? What does the difference look like in practice for you this week?"
>
> **Watch for This**
> - This is where passivity errors are most likely. Some clients hear "the Spirit transforms" and disengage from effort entirely. Be explicit: consent is active. You position, you show up, you return. The Spirit does the transforming.
> - Clients from works-based backgrounds may find the "consent" frame liberating but destabilizing — it removes familiar performance structures. Watch for anxiety beneath the relief.
> - "You cannot manufacture the encounter" can be over-applied: some clients use it to avoid committing to specific practices. "I'll just be open to the Spirit" without assigned practices is not the application.

### 4. The "Glory to Glory" Progression

Jonathan Edwards contributed something most pastoral treatments miss.

The phrase "from glory to glory" (2 Cor 3:18) is not decoration — it describes **progressive transformation through sustained beholding**. Edwards argued in *Religious Affections* that God's beauty and holiness are not merely moral categories — they are *aesthetic* categories. The primary catalyst of transformation is not obligation or fear. It is **awakened desire**.

The implications:
- You cannot sustain looking at Jesus by sheer willpower. Willpower depletes.
- You can sustain it if beholding awakens genuine desire for what you see — because desire self-perpetuates.
- The discipline of the practice is creating conditions for desire to be awakened and fed, not white-knuckling the gaze.

This is why worship, Scripture saturation, and community around the beauty of God are not supplements to the practice — they are the conditions that make sustained attention sustainable. Edwards: "The devil and the damned see God's holiness clearly. They do not see its beauty." Spiritual perception of *beauty* is what generates the affective pull that sustains attention.

> **Coaching Prompts**
> 1. "Edwards says the primary catalyst of transformation is awakened desire, not obligation or fear. What does your desire for God actually look like right now — is it present, dormant, or absent?"
> 2. "What has most awakened your desire for God in the past? What were the conditions around that?"
> 3. "If you've been trying to sustain attention through willpower — how has that been working? How long does it hold before it depletes?"
> 4. "What would it look like to arrange your life around conditions that awaken desire rather than conditions that demand compliance?"
>
> **Watch for This**
> - "Desire" language can be misread as purely emotional — "I need to feel something to connect with God." Clarify: desire is directional orientation of the will, not necessarily a feeling. The practices create conditions for desire, not conditions that require it first.
> - Some clients use "I don't feel desire for God" as a reason not to practice. Flip the sequence: the practice creates conditions for desire to be awakened. You don't wait for desire to start — you start to awaken desire.
> - Clients in prolonged spiritual drought may have deep resistance here. Don't push. Acknowledge that "from glory to glory" assumes a starting point, and sometimes the starting point is absence. That's where you begin.

### 5. The Covenant Gaze — The Mutuality Most Teachers Miss

This is the piece that most treatments of "keep your eyes on Jesus" get wrong by omission.

The practice is presented as unidirectional: *you* look at *him*. But the covenant frame of Scripture is mutual orientation.

- Numbers 6:24-26 — the Aaronic blessing is God **turning his face toward** Israel. The covenant formula is God's face-turning, not Israel's.
- Psalm 27:8 — "My heart says of you, 'Seek his face!' Your face, LORD, I will seek." The seeking is initiated by hearing His invitation. You seek because He first turned toward you.
- The Hebrew *panim* (face/presence) implies **mutual orientation**. Face-to-face requires two parties facing each other.

Brennan Manning identified the pastoral problem here: the reason people cannot sustain the gaze toward Jesus is not attentional weakness — it is failure to believe He is **already looking at them**. The gaze is first received, then returned. If you believe you are performing for an evaluating audience, you will look for signs of approval in the waves (circumstantial evidence that God is or isn't with you). If you believe you are being *held in a gaze of love* already, the return becomes natural rather than effortful.

The practice of keeping eyes on Jesus is downstream of receiving the reality that His eyes are already on you.

> **Coaching Prompts**
> 1. "Manning's distinction: Do you believe God loves you, or do you believe the love of God? What's the difference in how those two things feel?"
> 2. "When you pray, do you begin with the awareness of being looked at, or with the effort of looking? Which is more natural?"
> 3. "What do you imagine God's face looks like toward you right now — in this moment, not in theory?"
> 4. "If God's face is already turned toward you — what does that change about what you've been trying to do in your devotional life?"
> 5. "What might be blocking you from receiving the gaze before returning it?"
>
> **Watch for This**
> - This is the most emotionally activating section for clients with father wounds or conditional-love history. Move slowly. The theological statement is important; the experienced reality may require significant time to settle.
> - "God is already looking at you" can be received as surveillance (threat) rather than love, depending on the client's internal God-image. Diagnose this before proceeding: "When you picture God looking at you, what does His expression look like toward you in this moment?"
> - Clients who've been in performance-orientation Christianity for years may verbally receive this and functionally disbelieve it. Watch for the gap between verbal assent and actual posture change. Receiving Prayer is the bridge — don't skip it.

### 6. What the Waves Are — Theologically

This is the part the metaphor flattens if you're not careful.

The waves are not just external problems. They are a **counter-narrative about reality.** When Peter looks at the waves, he is implicitly reading them as more authoritative than the word Jesus spoke: "Come." The waves don't just threaten his body — they claim interpretive authority over what is real, what is possible, what governs outcomes.

This is why the crisis is theological, not just psychological. Peter has two competing accounts of the situation:
- **Jesus's word:** "Come" (water can hold you; the supernatural is the operating context)
- **The waves' word:** "This is impossible; you will die" (physical laws govern; circumstances are authoritative)

*Distazō* is what happens when you give both narratives weight simultaneously and let the waves' narrative gain ground.

Keeping eyes on Jesus is, at the cognitive level, **maintaining the interpretive authority of Jesus's word over the interpretive authority of circumstances.** It is a persistent choice about which account of reality you will let govern your next step.

This is why Scripture saturation is not optional for the practice. You cannot hold Jesus's interpretive authority in the storm if you haven't been regularly living inside His account of reality in the calm. The account needs to be more familiar, more automatic, more deeply grooved than the wave-narrative — and that only happens through formation *before* the storm.

> **Coaching Prompts**
> 1. "What is the wave-narrative in your life right now — not just the circumstance, but what does it actually claim? What does it say is true about your situation?"
> 2. "And what is Jesus's word over that same situation? What does He say?"
> 3. "Which account of reality has more weight in your actual thinking right now — His word or the wave's word? Be honest."
> 4. "What would it look like to make your next decision based on Jesus's account rather than the wave's account? What would that require?"
>
> **Watch for This**
> - The "interpretive authority" frame can be misapplied to produce denial: "I just need to ignore reality and believe harder." Be precise: acknowledge the wave, don't give it interpretive authority. Peter's wave was real — Jesus's word was still more authoritative.
> - Clients in genuinely severe circumstances (medical crisis, financial emergency, relational breakdown) may experience this framework as minimizing. Name it directly: "The wave is real. The question isn't whether the storm is real — it's which account governs your next step."
> - Some clients will make wave-narrative analysis a cognitive exercise rather than a faith act. The question "what is Jesus's word over this?" should be answered from Scripture, not from their own optimistic reasoning.

### 7. The Shema as Foundation

Deuteronomy 6:4-5 — "The LORD our God, the LORD is one. Love the LORD your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your strength."

This is Jesus's greatest commandment (Matt 22:37). It is also the **attentional claim** underlying the whole biblical vision.

"All your heart, soul, and strength" — with no reserve — is the precise opposite of *distazō*. The Shema is the theological declaration that there is one object worthy of total, undivided attention. Keeping eyes on Jesus is not a New Testament innovation — it is the fulfillment of the Shema in a Person. The ancient command to undivided attention finds its object in Christ.

This means Peter's failure on the water is not just a lapse of nerve. It is, structurally, a Shema failure — a moment when the waves claimed a portion of the loyalty that belongs to God alone.

> **Coaching Prompts**
> 1. "All your heart, soul, and strength — with no reserve. What part of your heart is reserved right now? What is it reserved for?"
> 2. "Peter's failure was, structurally, a Shema failure. Where in your own life are you giving something else the loyalty that belongs to God alone?"
> 3. "The Shema is a declaration that there is one object worthy of total attention. What would it mean for you to live the Shema this week — not as a performance, but as a daily reorientation?"
>
> **Watch for This**
> - The Shema should not become a new guilt mechanism: "I'm failing the Shema." Frame it as aspiration and direction, not a current performance standard.
> - "One object worthy of total attention" can produce anxiety in clients whose attention is extremely fragmented. The Shema describes the design of the human attentional system and the goal — not a standard they're expected to immediately meet.

## Part 3: Practical Daily Formation

---

### The Core Principle: Training vs. Trying

Dallas Willard drew the line that everything else depends on.

**Trying** is what Peter did on the water — mustering effort in the moment of crisis to hold attention on Jesus. It fails because the attentional faculty under threat-load defaults to whatever it has been grooved to do by prior repetition — not to what you *want* it to do in the moment.

**Training** is what formation is. You build attentional grooves in the calm so that under load, the groove is already there. A musician doesn't learn their instrument during the performance. The performance reveals the formation.

This reframes the entire project. The question is not "how do I keep my eyes on Jesus when the waves come?" The question is "what am I doing *now* that is building the attentional faculty I'll need *then*?" Every practice below is an answer to the second question.

> **Coaching Prompts**
> 1. "When have you tried — in the storm — to do something you hadn't trained for in the calm? What happened?"
> 2. "What are you asking God for in prayer that you haven't been building toward in practice?"
> 3. "If the storm is the performance — what is the formation program that should precede it?"
>
> **Watch for This**
> - This distinction can produce shame: "I haven't been training." Reframe immediately as orienting information, not condemnation. The coaching relationship exists precisely to start building now.
> - Some clients resist the "training" frame because it sounds too functional for a relationship with God. Affirm the relationship first, then show that relationships that go deep are intentionally cultivated, not just organically grown.
> - Watch for clients who grasp the concept but don't change their practice. Ask specifically: "What changes in what you do this week?"

---

### The Fundamental Unit: The Return

The fundamental unit of attentional formation is not the gaze itself — it is **the return**. Brother Lawrence failed constantly to maintain conscious awareness of God. He returned constantly. His formation was built not by unbroken attention but by 10,000 gentle returns over decades. Frank Laubach's journals document the same thing — he loses God-awareness, notices, returns. Loses, notices, returns.

This matters because people abandon formation practices when they fail to maintain attention, concluding the practice isn't working. The failure to maintain attention is not a sign the practice isn't working. **The return after failure is the practice.** It is the exact attentional motion you need in a storm — noticing you've drifted to the waves, and redirecting to Jesus. Every return in the calm is a repetition of that motion under low stakes.

The formation goal is: make the return so practiced, so grooved, so automatic that it becomes the soul's reflex.

> **Coaching Prompts**
> 1. "Brother Lawrence failed constantly and returned constantly. Which part of that is harder for you — the failing or the returning?"
> 2. "What happens for you after you've drifted from God-awareness? Do you return quickly, or does something get in the way?"
> 3. "If the return is the practice — not the sustained attention — does that change anything about how you've been measuring your devotional life?"
>
> **Watch for This**
> - Perfectionist clients will hear "the return is the practice" with relief, then immediately apply it as a new performance metric — counting returns, measuring frequency. Watch for re-perfectionizing the return itself.
> - Some clients return slowly after drifting because shame delays them: "I've been away too long to just come back." Address the shame directly: the return has no minimum gap requirement. Return immediately.
> - "10,000 gentle returns" should not imply the process is always easy. Gentleness refers to self-regard during the return, not the ease of returning.

### The Daily Formation Stack

*Organized by which mechanical gap each practice closes.*

---

**Gap 1: The "turn away from" movement is missing**
*(aphorao — the gaze has two directions)*

Most people practice the "turn toward" half of Hebrews 12:2 and skip the "turn away from" half. The result is trying to add God-attention on top of an already saturated attentional field — and God loses, because He doesn't compete for attention.

**Silence and Solitude** — Henri Nouwen's sequence begins here for a reason. Solitude is not absence of people. It is *removal of competing inputs* so the attentional field clears enough to orient toward God. Start with 10-15 minutes of actual silence before any other practice. No phone, no music, no podcast. Let the noise settle. This is the *aphorao* movement before the gaze can happen.

**The Daily Examen** (Ignatius of Loyola) — At end of day, review where your attention *actually* lived. Not where you intended it. Where it actually went. Consolation (moments drawn toward God) vs. desolation (moments pulled away). This builds *meta-awareness* — the capacity to notice where your attention is, which is prerequisite for returning it.

**Fasting** — Most underestimated formation practice for attention. When you fast from food (or anything that routinely commands attention — phone, media, comfort behaviors), you expose exactly how much attentional weight you've been giving competing authorities. The hunger or discomfort becomes a trigger to return to God rather than to the usual comfort object. Fasting doesn't build willpower. It reveals what has been governing your attention and interrupts it.

> **Coaching Prompts**
> 1. "What would you have to give up to protect 15 minutes of silence at the start of your day? What currently occupies that space?"
> 2. "When you think about sitting in silence with no input — what comes up? Discomfort? Relief? Resistance? What does the resistance tell you?"
> 3. "If you fasted from [specific competing input] for a week — what do you think you'd find? What are you afraid it would reveal?"
> 4. "In the Daily Examen — what do you predict your desolation moments would cluster around this week?"
>
> **Watch for This**
> - Silence is activating for clients with unprocessed trauma or anxiety. Do not assign extended silence to clients in acute distress without checking first. Start with 5 minutes, not 15.
> - Fasting can trigger control dynamics or disordered eating. Know the client's history before assigning food fasting. Screen gently: "Is fasting something you have a complicated relationship with?"
> - The Examen should not become a performance review. Clients with self-critical patterns will turn it into an inventory of failures. Frame it specifically as noticing God's presence and absence — not evaluating personal performance.

---

**Gap 2: Beholding hasn't been sustained long enough to produce transformation**
*(katoptrizomenoi — ongoing, not instantaneous)*

The transformation mechanism in 2 Cor 3:18 is *continuous* beholding. The image being formed requires dwell time. Most devotional practices are too brief and too cognitively active to allow actual beholding — they produce information about God rather than encounter with Him.

**Lectio Divina** — The practice of slow, ruminative Scripture reading. Four movements:
1. *Lectio* — Read slowly until a word or phrase stops you.
2. *Meditatio* — Repeat it. Let it work. No rush toward interpretation.
3. *Oratio* — Respond to God from what surfaced.
4. *Contemplatio* — Rest in the presence without words.

The critical discipline is staying in each movement longer than feels productive. Speed is the enemy of beholding. The formation happens in the dwell, not in the accumulation of content.

**Extended Worship** — Not performance or emotional stimulation. Sustained engagement with God's character and beauty through music/song long enough for the affective response to shift. Jonathan Edwards: transformation requires awakened desire, and desire is awakened by beauty encountered, not beauty catalogued. Ten minutes is orientation. Thirty minutes is sometimes where beholding begins.

**Icons and Sacred Objects** — Physical focal points that train the eyes to rest on a representation of Christ. For most Protestant clients: a meaningful object, open Bible, candle — anything that gives physical gaze a place to rest during prayer.

> **Coaching Prompts**
> 1. "Walk me through what your current Scripture engagement looks like. How long do you typically stay with one passage?"
> 2. "If the formation happens in the dwell and not the accumulation — what would it mean to stay with less material for longer?"
> 3. "When was the last time something in worship actually moved you? What were the conditions around that?"
>
> **Watch for This**
> - Lectio Divina can be cognitively hijacked by strong analytical thinkers. They'll stay in *Lectio* and *Meditatio* at the intellectual level indefinitely. Check: "When you say you're meditating on it, what's actually happening — are you analyzing or dwelling?"
> - Extended worship can become emotional stimulation if left unmonitored. Calibrate expectations: "Not every session will feel the same. The practice is sustained engagement, not the feeling it produces."
> - Some clients have negative associations with worship (manipulative or overly emotional church experiences). Allow them to start with instrumental music or silence rather than forcing the worship frame immediately.

---

**Gap 3: The soul receives the gaze before it can return it**
*(the covenant mutuality — Manning's insight)*

**Receiving Prayer** — Begin prayer by sitting, not speaking. Remain in the awareness that you are being looked at, not looking. This directly addresses the performance-oriented stance that makes sustained attention exhausting. Brennan Manning's practice: simply sit with the phrase "Abba, I belong to you" and let it land. Not as a technique — as a reality being received. This is the recovery of the covenant frame: the gaze is first His toward you.

**Confession and Absolution** — Unconfessed sin creates a functional conviction that God's face is turned away. Whether or not that's theologically accurate in the moment, it functions psychologically as a barrier to receiving the gaze. Regular, honest confession clears the obstruction and restores the experienced reality of being seen and loved. This is attentional hygiene, not just moral hygiene.

> **Coaching Prompts**
> 1. "Try this right now: sit with 'Abba, I belong to you.' Not as a request — as a reality being received. What happens?"
> 2. "Is there anything unconfessed that you're aware of that might be creating functional distance between you and God's felt presence?"
> 3. "If prayer began with receiving rather than requesting — what would your prayer life look like differently?"
>
> **Watch for This**
> - Receiving Prayer can be deeply disorienting for clients who have never experienced unconditional positive regard from any human being. Their functional God-image may be conditional. Take it slowly. The theological statement may need to be planted long before the experiential practice can take root.
> - Confession can be weaponized by clients with scrupulosity or OCD-adjacent patterns — they'll confess repetitively without receiving absolution. Watch for confession without closure. Always close with a specific absolution statement: "Therefore now no condemnation" (Romans 8:1).
> - Some clients will experience Receiving Prayer as empty or flat initially. Manage expectations: "This may feel like nothing at first. That's normal. Stay anyway."

---

**Gap 4: The competing narrative (waves) hasn't been countered with a practiced counter-narrative**

**Scripture Memorization** — Not for information retention. For narrative saturation. The reason waves win the interpretive battle in crisis is that the physical evidence is immediate, sensory, and overwhelming — and Scripture's counter-account is abstract and distant. Memorized Scripture is *not* distant. It is in the same real-time cognitive space as the wave-narrative. Psalm 23, Isaiah 43, Romans 8 — these become available as immediate counter-accounts when the sensory world is screaming a different story.

**Preaching to Yourself** — Martyn Lloyd-Jones named this in *Spiritual Depression*: "The main art in the matter of spiritual living is to know how to handle yourself. You have to take yourself in hand, address yourself, preach to yourself, question yourself." The alternative is listening to yourself — which, under stress, means listening to the wave-narrative. This is a daily practice: intentionally speaking the true account of reality to your own soul rather than passively receiving what anxiety broadcasts. Psalms of lament model this exactly — the psalmist argues against his own despair.

**The Daily Office / Fixed-Hour Prayer** — Not as legalism. As a structural counter-narrative interruption. Every 3-4 hours, stop. Reorient to God. The purpose is to prevent any stretch of waking life from becoming fully governed by the wave-narrative without interruption. It fragments the wave's authority before it accumulates. Northumbria Community's *Celtic Daily Prayer* and the *Book of Common Prayer* both provide usable forms.

> **Coaching Prompts**
> 1. "What is the wave-narrative that most frequently runs in your mind under stress? If you had a Scripture that directly countered that specific narrative — what would it be?"
> 2. "Have you ever caught yourself listening to yourself rather than speaking to yourself? What does the difference feel like?"
> 3. "What's the earliest point in a difficult situation that you notice the wave-narrative beginning to build? What would it take to interrupt it there?"
>
> **Watch for This**
> - Scripture memorization fails when the passage isn't matched to the specific lie or wave. Generic memorization is far less effective than targeted memorization. Be specific with the client about which passage and exactly why it addresses their particular wave.
> - Preaching to yourself is often confused with positive self-talk. Clients familiar with secular self-help will dismiss it as the same thing. Clarify: you're not generating an optimistic alternative from your own reasoning — you're aligning your cognitive weight with divine authority.
> - The Daily Office can feel legalistic to clients from non-liturgical backgrounds. Frame it functionally: "This is an alarm system that prevents the wave-narrative from running uninterrupted for 8 hours straight."

---

**Gap 5: The attentional faculty hasn't been trained to return automatically**
*(the fundamental unit — practiced until reflexive)*

**The Jesus Prayer** — "Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner." From the hesychast tradition. Practiced continuously — breath in, "Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God"; breath out, "have mercy on me, a sinner." The goal over time: the prayer becomes semi-automatic, running beneath conscious activity. Not replacing engagement with the world — providing an undercurrent of God-orientation beneath it. The "anchor line" to Jesus while moving through ordinary tasks.

**Frank Laubach's Game with Minutes** — Set a timer or use hourly phone prompts. Each hour: did you return to conscious God-awareness at least once? Not sustained — just returned. Track it without guilt. Laubach ran this experiment for months and documented both the failures and the gradual deepening. The structure prevents the day from fully disappearing into unconscious wave-focus.

**Breath Prayer** — A short personalized prayer (typically 6-8 syllables) practiced on the breath. Biblical examples: "Be still and know" / "that you are God." Your own: "You are with me" / "I am not alone." Practiced during transitions, commutes, waiting, stress. This is the most portable formation practice — it requires no time block, no environment, no material. It is available exactly when the waves appear.

**Centering Prayer** (20 min, twice daily ideally) — Choose a sacred word as symbol of your consent to God's presence and action. When you notice your attention has wandered to any thought, gently return to the sacred word. That's it. The practice is entirely the return. Thomas Keating's contribution is recognizing that the frequency of distraction and return in a session is not a sign of failure — it is the exercise. Each return is one repetition. This is the most direct training of the exact attentional motion needed on the water.

> **Coaching Prompts**
> 1. "Right now, in this moment — what would your breath prayer be? Let's put one together. Try it once."
> 2. "If you set hourly alarms for a week and tracked God-awareness — what percentage of hours do you think you'd return a 'yes'? What does that tell you?"
> 3. "What's the first thing your mind goes to in transitions — getting in the car, walking into a meeting, waiting in line? What occupies that space right now?"
>
> **Watch for This**
> - The Jesus Prayer can feel formulaic initially. Clients may report "it doesn't feel like prayer." Acknowledge: familiarity through practice is the goal. The feeling comes later, if at all. The practice is the point.
> - Centering Prayer can raise concerns about Eastern meditation for some clients. Clarify: the sacred word is not a mantra to achieve emptiness — it is a symbol of consent to a personal God's specific presence and action. The orientation is relational, not technique-based.
> - The "Game with Minutes" tracking can become performance-oriented. Watch for clients counting returns as an achievement. The tracking is noticing, not scoring.

---

**Gap 6: Attentional restoration is absent**
*(Weekly cadence — see Formation Map)*

**Sabbath** — The discipline that most directly addresses attentional overload. One day structurally removed from the systems that fragment attention (work demands, screens, productivity). Not because rest is nice. Because sustained attention on God requires a cleared field, and the week's accumulation of wave-narrative needs a full-day interrupt. Walter Brueggemann: Sabbath is resistance. It declares that the wave-narrative does not govern time.

**Extended Prayer / Retreat Block** — Even 2-3 hours weekly of unhurried, unscheduled prayer and Scripture. Not study. Not journaling necessarily. Extended dwell time. The formation that happens in long slow sessions cannot be replicated by accumulated short sessions. Once a week, let the beholding go long enough to get past the restlessness.

> **Coaching Prompts**
> 1. "What would it reveal about what's actually governing your life if you stopped everything for one full day?"
> 2. "What are you afraid would happen if you stopped?"
> 3. "What is the specific, named obstacle to protecting two hours a week for extended prayer? Let's name it."
>
> **Watch for This**
> - Sabbath is the most resisted practice in the entire stack for productivity-oriented clients. The resistance is rarely logistical — it's theological: they don't functionally believe the world will hold without their management of it. Name this directly rather than solving the scheduling problem.
> - Extended prayer blocks feel "unproductive" to output-oriented clients. Reframe: this is maintenance, not luxury. It's the depth session that makes all other practices sustainable.

---

**Gap 7: No accountability structure**
*(Weekly cadence — see Formation Map)*

**Formation Community** — People who know you well enough to say "you're looking at the waves right now." Most people have no one in their life with this role and permission. The formation community is not optional for sustained attentional life — you cannot maintain the gaze in isolation long-term because you lose the ability to see when you've shifted. You need people who can see it.

> **Coaching Prompts**
> 1. "Who in your life currently knows where you actually are spiritually — not the version you present, the real thing?"
> 2. "What would it cost you to give someone that kind of permission? What are you afraid they would see?"
> 3. "Is there one person you can think of who could hold this role? What's in the way of having that conversation?"
>
> **Watch for This**
> - Some clients will identify a spouse as their formation community. This is often insufficient — a spouse has too much stake in the client's wellbeing to provide impartial observation. Gently distinguish: a spouse can be supportive; formation accountability is a specific role that may need someone outside the marriage.
> - Clients burned by community will have real resistance here. Acknowledge the risk — this role requires trust, and trust can be violated. The response isn't "trust anyway" — it's "build trust carefully before extending full permission."

### Crisis Application: What It Looks Like When Waves Hit

This is the test. What does formed attention look like in the actual storm?

**It does not look like calm.** The waves are real. The fear response is physiological. Formation doesn't eliminate the threat-detection response — it determines what happens in the 2-3 second window after the amygdala fires.

**What it looks like practically:**

1. **Notice the wave.** Don't deny it. Peter saw the wind — that was appropriate. The problem was dwelling there. Name the wave without letting it make a claim: "This is real and this is not the whole story."

2. **Speak before you think.** The wave-narrative runs faster than deliberate cognition under stress. You need something already in your mouth — a memorized verse, the Jesus Prayer, a breath prayer — that occupies the space before the wave-narrative fills it. This is why memorization is not academic; it is tactical.

3. **Return, not recover.** The goal in the storm is not to regain full peace or unshaken confidence. It is the single act of return — re-acknowledging Jesus's presence and authority over the situation. One return is enough. Then another. The storm may not stop. The returns keep you afloat.

4. **Recognize the interpretive battle.** In the moment: "The waves are claiming authority that belongs to Jesus. His word over this situation is ____." This is not denial. It is choosing which account of reality you will build your next move on.

5. **Use the body.** The attentional state is physiological under stress. Use breath — slow, deliberate breath activates the parasympathetic system and interrupts the threat-response cascade enough to make return possible.

> **Coaching Prompts**
> 1. "In your last significant storm — what was your actual first response? Not what you wish it had been. What it actually was."
> 2. "What do you have in your mouth right now that could occupy the space before the wave-narrative fills it? Is it there?"
> 3. "What would 'return, not recover' look like in your current situation? What's the one return you could make right now?"
>
> **Watch for This**
> - The crisis framework should not be introduced mid-crisis for the first time. Introduce it in a relatively stable moment so it's available as a reference when the storm hits. Crisis is not the time to teach — it's the time to deploy what was already taught.
> - "Use the body" (breath) can feel dismissive if the client's wave is severe. Acknowledge the wave fully first. The breath tool creates a moment of access — it is not a cure and should not be presented as one.
> - Some clients in crisis need to process — to talk through the storm. That's legitimate and often necessary. The return framework is not a substitute for pastoral presence in the session; it's the tool the client takes with them after the session ends.

## Part 4: Coaching Framework Application

### Overview: How the Frameworks Relate

The mechanics of attentional formation are not a standalone module in the Disciple Fit coaching framework — they are the *underlying mechanism* that explains why each of the framework's other tools either takes hold or fails to take hold in a client's life.

Every coaching tool in the framework operates on the same basic dynamic: something true and transformative is offered, and the question is whether it lands in soil capable of receiving and sustaining it. The attentional formation framework explains *why* soil is in the condition it's in, and *how* to change it. The other tools — lie-nuking, identity formation, soils diagnosis — operate on the surface of the attentional architecture. This framework goes underneath.

The integration has five contact points:

1. **The Soils Diagnostic** — soil types map directly to formation gaps
2. **Lie Nukes** — the wave-narrative mechanic explains lie nuke vulnerability and counter-measures
3. **Identity Formation** — the 7-part format maps to the Greek mechanics of beholding
4. **The Three-Part Session Prep Model** — each part connects to specific formation gaps
5. **The CBC Framework** — CBT/coaching/biblical dimensions each have a formation corollary

---

### Contact Point 1: The Soils Diagnostic and Attentional Formation

*See [[A-Parable of the Soils — Kingdom Coaching Deep Framework]] for full diagnostic criteria, soil-type descriptions, and in-session sequence.*

The Parable of the Soils (Matthew 13) describes the condition of internal terrain that determines whether the Word of God takes root, grows, and bears fruit. The soil types are not character judgments — they are diagnostic descriptions of attentional terrain. Each soil type maps to a specific cluster of formation gaps.

**The Hardened Path**
The Word is immediately snatched away — it never penetrates. The soil has been compacted by repeated traffic (cognitive patterns, cultural conditioning, wound history) until nothing gets through.

*Formation gap mapping:* Gap 2 (beholding depth) — the Word never stays long enough to germinate; the client reads or hears truth but moves through it too quickly for any encounter to occur. Gap 4 (counter-narrative) — the wave-narrative has hardened the interpretive framework so firmly that the Word is automatically filtered through it and neutralized before it can take hold.

*Coaching implication:* Lectio Divina and extended dwell time are the primary soil-breaking practices. The hardened path client needs slowed-down exposure to the Word before any other tool will penetrate.

---

**The Rocky Soil**
The Word is received with initial enthusiasm but has no depth of root — it withers under tribulation. This is the Matthew 14 client in pure form: spiritually alive in the calm, collapsed in the storm.

*Formation gap mapping:* Gap 5 (return reflex) — no practiced return means no reflex under load. Gap 6 (attentional restoration) — no Sabbath or extended prayer means no depth session. The rocky soil client has never let the root go deep because they've never stayed long enough. This is precisely the training vs. trying distinction — they try in the storm what they never trained in the calm.

*Coaching implication:* Extended practices (Lectio Divina, extended worship, extended prayer blocks, Sabbath) are the root-building tools. The weekly rhythm builds the depth that daily practices alone cannot reach.

---

**The Thorny Soil**
The Word is received and takes root but is choked out by "the cares of this world and the deceitfulness of riches." The thorns don't replace the Word — they choke it by competing for the same attentional space.

*Formation gap mapping:* Gap 1 (turn away from) — the client has never executed the *aphorao* movement; competing attentional objects have never been intentionally cleared. Gap 4 (counter-narrative) — the "cares of this world" function as a sustained wave-narrative: financial anxiety, relational worry, status pursuit — all making persistent interpretive claims that crowd out the Word's authority.

*Coaching implication:* Fasting and silence/solitude are the thorn-clearing tools. These expose the extent to which competing attentional objects have been governing the field. The Daily Office provides structural interruption that prevents the thorns from accumulating uninterrupted throughout the day.

---

**The Good Soil**
The Word is received, held, and produces fruit. This is not a personality type — it is a *formed* condition. Good soil is the result of: cleared field (Gap 1 addressed), depth cultivation (Gap 2 addressed), received gaze (Gap 3 addressed), narrative saturation (Gap 4 addressed), practiced return (Gap 5 addressed), regular restoration (Gap 6 addressed), and community accountability (Gap 7 addressed).

*Coaching implication:* The formation framework IS the soil cultivation program. Each gap corresponds to a specific tilling, watering, or clearing operation. The coach's long game is moving the client through the gap hierarchy until the soil supports sustained fruit.

---

**The Transitional Instability Zone**

As a client moves from one soil condition toward better soil, there is a period of particular vulnerability — the old pattern is disrupted but the new formation hasn't stabilized. This is when wave-fixation is most dangerous and Lie Nuke detonation is most likely.

*Coaching response:* During the transitional instability zone, activate Gap 5 (return reflex) and Gap 7 (accountability) first. These are the stabilizing structures. The client needs a practiced return mechanism and at least one person who can see what they cannot. Do not introduce complex new practices during this period — deepen the simplest ones already in place.

### Contact Point 2: Lie Nukes and the Wave-Narrative

*See [[Lie Eliminator]] for the 7-part format and individual lie entries.*

The first move of the session prep model is to locate lies that are primed for circumstantial detonation — lies layered on existing wounds, woven into empty cavities, or already partially active through agreements the client has made.

The wave-narrative mechanic reveals *why* these lies detonate: waves (circumstances) function as interpretive triggers that activate the lie's claim to authority. The lie isn't just a wrong belief sitting inert — it is a competing account of reality that, when triggered by matching circumstances, claims interpretive authority over the situation.

**The mechanics of detonation:**

1. A wave appears (triggering circumstance)
2. The wave carries a narrative claim (implicit interpretation of reality)
3. The lie primed in the client's interior matches that claim
4. *Distazō* — attention splits between Jesus's word and the lie's word
5. If no counter-narrative is available and no return reflex fires, the lie gains weight
6. The client sinks

**Attentional formation as counter-measure — three layers:**

*Layer 1 — Pre-detonation:* Scripture memorization (Gap 4) fills the specific cavity the lie occupies with a corresponding truth. The lie cannot expand into a space already occupied. The coach's task is matching the memorized passage to the specific lie being targeted — not generic Scripture, but the passage that directly addresses this client's particular wound and corresponding lie.

*Layer 2 — At detonation:* Preaching to yourself (Gap 4) and Breath Prayer (Gap 5) are the in-crisis deployment tools. When the wave hits and the lie activates, the client needs something in their mouth before the lie-narrative fills the cognitive space. This is why these practices must be trained in the calm — they need to be accessible under cognitive load.

*Layer 3 — Post-detonation:* Confession and Receiving Prayer (Gap 3) restore the covenant gaze after the lie has done damage. Detonation always carries a shame element: "I should be past this by now." Return to the received gaze is what prevents shame from compounding the original lie.

**Sequencing lie-nuking work with attentional formation:**

Lie-nuking is most effective when:
- Gap 3 (covenant gaze) is minimally stable — the client can receive truth as gift rather than obligation
- Gap 4 (counter-narrative) has at least one memorized passage ready for immediate deployment
- Gap 5 (return reflex) has a practicing mechanism in place

Attempting lie-nuking before these three gaps are minimally addressed typically results in the client intellectually affirming the counter-declaration but being unable to access it under stress. The theology lands in the head; the lie still governs in the storm.

### Contact Point 3: Identity Formation and the Beholding Mechanics

*See [[Identity Installer]] for the full 22-category format and [[Lie Eliminator]] for the lie-level version of the same attentional structure.*

The 7-part Identity Installer format maps directly onto the Greek mechanics of beholding:

| Identity Format Step | Beholding Mechanic | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Identity Statement | *katoptrizomenoi* — the image being formed | Names the object of the beholding gaze |
| Anchor Verse | Counter-narrative (Gap 4) | The Word that carries the true account of the identity |
| My Evidence | Received gaze (Gap 3) | Grounds the identity in experienced reality, not just received theology |
| What I'm Putting Off | *aphorao* — turn away from | First movement of the gaze: naming what the old image required and what releasing it costs |
| What I'm Putting On | *aphorao* — turn toward | Second movement: what the new image looks like in concrete behavior |
| The Felt Sense | Somatic formation | Embodied beholding — the identity landing in the body, not just the mind |
| Today I Will | Return reflex (Gap 5) | Daily return practice that re-anchors the identity through concrete action |

**When a client can't hold the Identity Installer:**

- "What I'm Putting Off" doesn't feel possible → Gap 1 (competing attentional object has never been named and addressed; fasting may expose it)
- "Anchor Verse" doesn't fire under stress → Gap 4 (not memorized deeply enough; extended memorization work needed)
- Identity statement doesn't feel true despite intellectual acceptance → Gap 3 (performance orientation is filtering truth before it can land; received gaze work first)
- "Today I Will" never becomes automatic → Gap 5 (no return reflex built; daily action needs to be linked to a breath prayer or other continuous practice)
- Identity keeps being overwritten by the wave-narrative → Gap 4 (counter-narrative not grooved deeply enough; more Lectio Divina and preaching-to-self repetitions needed)

**The Lie Eliminator and the same mechanics:**

The Lie Eliminator's 7-part format (Who God Is → Who I Am → His Promise → The Lie → Declaration → Therefore I Will → Scripture Arsenal) runs the same attentional structure at the level of specific lies:

- "Who God Is" + "His Promise" = the object of the beholding gaze (covenant gaze received)
- "The Lie" = the competing narrative named and exposed
- "Declaration" = preaching to yourself (counter-narrative spoken)
- "Therefore I Will" = the return reflex embedded in daily action
- "Scripture Arsenal" = the memorized counter-narrative available for immediate deployment at detonation

The Lie Eliminator is, mechanically, an attentional formation tool — it trains the client to recognize the lie's narrative claim and respond with a practiced counter-declaration before the lie's weight can shift.

### Contact Point 4: The Three-Part Session Prep Model

**Move 1: Locate Lie Nukes**

*Attentional formation connection:* The wave-narrative mechanic is the diagnostic lens. Before session, identify what waves (circumstances) the client is currently facing and which of their known lie-cavities each wave directly triggers. The Coach Diagnostic's Gap 4 indicators identify clients most vulnerable to detonation. Lie Nuke location work is mechanically a counter-narrative gap analysis — finding where the client has no practiced counter-account available.

*Formation implication for session:* When Lie Nuke territory is active, identify which practice will fill that specific cavity most directly (usually Scripture memorization matched to the specific lie) before closing the session.

---

**Move 2: Address Eternal Foundation**

*Attentional formation connection:* The eternal foundation — salvation, adoption, covenant love — is the *content* of the received gaze. It is what the client is receiving when they sit in Gap 3 (Receiving Prayer: "Abba, I belong to you"). If the eternal foundation is shaky or intellectually held but experientially absent, Gap 3 cannot function. The client is trying to receive a gaze from someone they don't fully believe is looking at them with the covenant disposition Scripture describes.

*Formation implication for session:* Eternal foundation work IS Gap 3 work. Confirmation of salvation, deepening of adoption identity, and receiving of covenant love are the theological content that makes receiving prayer possible as more than technique. Do not assign Receiving Prayer if the eternal foundation is significantly unstable — address the foundation content in session first. The practice then becomes the daily anchoring of what was established theologically.

**Move 3: Build Wound Awareness**

*Attentional formation connection:* Wounds are the historical explanation for why the soil is in the condition it's in. Wounds train the threat-detection system to monitor for specific triggers — which becomes wave-fixation when those trigger-patterns are activated. Understanding the wound explains:

- Why this client's soil is compacted in this particular way (hardened path)
- Why depth doesn't form in this specific area (rocky soil — the wound prevented the vulnerability required for deep root)
- Which thorns are wound-compensating behaviors that grew in response to unmet needs (thorny soil)

*Formation implication for session:* Wound awareness work refines the diagnostic. It helps the coach distinguish between gaps that are wound-sourced (require more patience; may involve emotional activation during practice) and gaps that are simply untrained (no wound history, just no formation). The coach assigns practices accordingly: wound-adjacent gaps get gentler entry points. Breath Prayer before Centering Prayer, for instance, if extended silence activates the wound.

### Contact Point 5: The CBC Framework

**CBT Dimension**

Cognitive distortions are wave-narratives. The cognitive restructuring move in CBT — identify the distorted thought, evaluate evidence, generate a more accurate alternative — is structurally identical to the counter-narrative mechanic. The difference is the source and authority of the alternative account:

- CBT replaces irrational thought with rational thought
- Disciple Fit CBC replaces the wave-narrative with the Word of God

Both target the same attentional mechanism (*distazō* — divided weight between two competing accounts). The CBC version has greater durability because the counter-narrative is not just more rational — it carries divine authority. The client is not arguing themselves out of a cognitive distortion; they are aligning their attentional weight with the word of the One who governs the situation.

*Integration point:* CBT homework can be reframed as attentional formation. Thought stopping = the return reflex. Cognitive restructuring = preaching to yourself with Scripture rather than rational self-talk. Behavioral activation = the "Today I Will" step of identity formation.

**Coaching Dimension**

The GROW model maps cleanly:

| GROW | Attentional Formation Function |
|---|---|
| Goal | What does formed attention look like for this client? Which gaps are being targeted? |
| Reality | Coach Diagnostic — which gaps are active, at what severity? |
| Options | Which formation practices fit this client's life, temperament, and theological literacy? |
| Will | Which one practice will they commit to starting this week? |

The coaching model's emphasis on client agency maps to the attentional formation principle: transformation is consent, not performance. The coach does not produce formation — the coach creates conditions and assigns practices. The Spirit produces transformation through the client's consent.

**Biblical Dimension**

The biblical framework provides two things the CBT and coaching dimensions cannot:

1. *Theological authority for the counter-narrative.* The client is not just working to feel better — they are aligning with the account of reality given by the One who governs reality. The counter-narrative has weight because it comes from outside the client's own cognitive system.

2. *Trinitarian agency.* The coach is not the change agent. The Spirit is the agent of metamorphosis through beholding (*metamorphoumetha* — present passive). This relieves both coach and client of the burden of producing transformation, while preserving the genuine requirement of attentional effort and consent.

### Session Deployment Sequence

**Intake and Assessment Phase:**
1. Diagnostic Intake Form → presenting terrain and primary stressors
2. Soils Diagnostic → soil condition
3. Coach Diagnostic → which formation gaps are active
4. Together: a layered picture of the client's formation state from multiple diagnostic angles

**Early Sessions — Foundation:**
- Priority: Gap 3 (receiving the covenant gaze)
- Work: Eternal foundation confirmation; adoption identity; covenant love as experiential reality
- Practice assigned: Receiving Prayer
- Rationale: Gap 3 is the foundation that makes other gaps addressable. Performance orientation filters every other intervention. The client must have at least minimal access to the received gaze before lie-nuking or identity formation can land beyond the intellectual level.

**Middle Sessions — Soil Work:**
- Priority: Primary soil type identified; corresponding gaps addressed
- Work: Lie-nuking matched to specific wave-triggers; Identity Installer for key categories; formation practices targeted at active gaps
- Practices assigned: 1-2 at a time maximum
- Rationale: Formation is built incrementally. Assigning more than 2 new practices at once fragments the client's attention across the practices themselves, defeating the purpose.

**Ongoing Sessions — Depth and Accountability:**
- Priority: Gap 5 (return reflex) embedded and automated; Gaps 6 and 7 sustained
- Work: Debrief formation practices; adjust; advance to less-addressed gaps; Soils check-in
- Practices: Refine and deepen rather than add new
- Rationale: The return reflex is the long-term deliverable of the whole framework. When it becomes automatic — when the client's default response to waves is the practiced return — the formation objective is substantially complete.

**Crisis Points / Transitional Instability Zone:**
- Activate: Breath Prayer (Gap 5) and Formation Community (Gap 7) immediately
- Simplify: Return to the most basic, most practiced intervention — not the newest
- Frame: The crisis is not a setback; it is the performance of the formation. "The return keeps you afloat."
- Do not introduce new material during acute crisis — deepen what is already practiced
