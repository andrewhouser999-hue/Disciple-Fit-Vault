# Pedagogy of Spiritual Formation — What Expert Thought Confirms

*Research synthesis: What pedagogical science says about the structural weaknesses in how Western Christians learn biblical truth. Covers foundational sequencing, schema theory, God-representation, whole-person learning, and what is typically missing.*

---

## What Pedagogy Confirms — and Where It Goes Further

### 1. Foundational Knowledge Must Precede Complex Content

**David Ausubel's Advance Organizer Theory** (1960s) is the most direct support: *"The most important single factor influencing learning is what the learner already knows."* Without correct anchoring concepts, new material gets assimilated into whatever schema exists — often distorting it. His prescription: teach *advance organizers* (broad, abstract, foundational frameworks) before specific content. Starting with "Who is God" is textbook Ausubel.

**Robert Gagné's Learning Hierarchies** agree: prerequisite knowledge must be secured before superordinate learning is possible. Teaching propositional biblical truth without doctrinal scaffolding produces what Gagné would call failed transfer — content is remembered but cannot be properly applied or integrated.

**Bruner's Spiral Curriculum** adds nuance: you don't teach foundational things once and move on — you return to them with increasing depth as the learner's capacity grows.

---

### 2. Prior Schemas Actively Distort Incoming Information — This Is the Central Problem

**Piaget's Assimilation vs. Accommodation** is the theoretical spine. When new information cannot fit existing schemas, learners either:
- **Assimilate** — force the new content into the old schema (distortion), or
- **Accommodate** — restructure the schema to fit the new content (actual learning)

Most people assimilate. Accommodation is effortful, often uncomfortable, and doesn't happen without specific pedagogical conditions.

**Conceptual Change Theory** (Posner, Strike, Hewson & Gertzog, 1982) — the most directly applicable framework — argues that prior misconceptions are highly resistant, and four conditions must exist for genuine conceptual change:
1. **Dissatisfaction** with the current conception
2. The new conception must be **intelligible**
3. It must be **plausible**
4. It must be **fruitful** (opens up new understanding)

Passive sermon exposure almost never produces schema restructuring. Dissatisfaction must be surfaced, the old conception made visible, the new one introduced with clarity and plausibility.

**Frederick Bartlett** (1932) demonstrated that memory itself is *reconstructive*, not reproductive — people rebuild what they've heard through the lens of existing schemas, cultural scripts, and expectations. This is not a failure of effort — it's how cognition works.

### 3. God-Representation and Attachment Are Interpretive Filters — Pedagogy Doesn't Usually Account for This

**Ana-María Rizzuto's** *The Birth of the Living God* (1979) is the foundational clinical text. She demonstrated through object relations research that people construct a "God-representation" out of parental object relations, early emotional experiences, and cultural images — and this representation operates largely unconsciously as a filter on all theological content. A person can affirm propositionally that "God is Father" while internally processing it through the template of an abusive or absent father. The cognitive belief and the functional God-representation can be radically dissociated.

**Lee Kirkpatrick's** attachment research (*Attachment, Evolution, and the Psychology of Religion*) extends this: insecure attachment styles (anxious, avoidant) produce characteristic God-images (punitive, distant, hypervigilant) that are stable and highly resistant to propositional correction alone.

**What this means pedagogically:** You cannot teach people out of their God-representation with better theology. Rizzuto's clinical data suggests the representation changes through *relational experience and corrective emotional/relational encounters* — not information. This is a hard limit on purely cognitive theological instruction.

---

### 4. Whole-Person Learning — The Pedagogy Here Is More Radical Than You May Have Framed It

**Jack Mezirow's Transformative Learning Theory** is the most relevant adult education framework. Mezirow argues that adult learning worth the name is *frame of reference transformation*, not content accumulation. Transformation requires:
- A **disorienting dilemma** (an experience that doesn't fit the existing frame)
- **Critical self-reflection** on underlying assumptions (not just content)
- **Rational discourse** with others who are also examining their frames
- **Action** that enacts the new perspective

Gradual exposure without disorienting dilemmas and critical reflection produces *informational learning* — additive, surface — not transformational learning.

**James K.A. Smith** (*Desiring the Kingdom*, 2009) presses harder: humans are primarily *desiring, embodied, habitual creatures* — not primarily rational processors of propositions. Formation happens through **liturgy, practice, and repetition** that shape loves, not primarily through correct information. You can restructure a schema cognitively and still have deeply ingrained embodied habits pulling in the opposite direction. This is a significant gap in most theological education.

---

### 5. What Pedagogy Says Is Often Missing

**Communities of Practice (Lave & Wenger)** — Legitimate peripheral participation in a community of practice is itself a learning mechanism. Sermons and Bible studies, while unstructured, do have situated learning value that pure systematic instruction lacks. The issue isn't that they're wrong — it's that they operate without the schema-restructuring work that transformation requires.

**The Body** — If Smith and embodied cognition researchers (Lakoff, Johnson — *Philosophy in the Flesh*) are right, schema work done purely cognitively-verbally may not transfer to lived behavior. Somatic and practice-based elements need to be integrated for full-person change.

**Metacognition** — Research consistently shows that making learners aware of *how they are thinking* (not just what they think) is among the highest-leverage interventions for schema change. Teaching people to notice their interpretive moves in real time — not just correcting the output — produces more durable change.
