# CBC Session Skills

`[CURRICULUM]` `[SESSION-TOOL]`

Cognitive Behavioral Coaching (CBC) is a set of coach-safe CBT-informed tools deployed within the biblical framework — not a primary methodology. In the Covenant Identity model, the biblical framework governs, coaching is the delivery vehicle, and CBC tools are reached for selectively when cognitive distortions are blocking a client's movement or reception of covenant truth. This file covers the session-level skills: how to deploy Socratic questioning, guided discovery, the ABC model, behavioral experiments, and cost-benefit analysis in a coaching context. For the theoretical foundation, see [[CBT & Cognitive Reframing - Evidence-Based Foundation for Performance Coaching]].

---

## The Honest Assessment

CBC tools carry the strongest evidence base of any coaching methodology, and they converge naturally with biblical framework. CBT's core claim — that beliefs drive emotions and behavior — is structurally identical to the biblical claim that transformation begins in the mind (Rom 12:2; Prov 23:7). This is not a forced parallel. It is genuine convergence.

Within the Covenant Identity model, CBC tools are not the methodology — they are the tool set reached for when a client cannot receive covenant truth because a cognitive distortion is maintaining the false identity. The coach enters through the biblical framework, coaches from within it, and deploys CBC tools when that level of psychological integration is needed. The trigger is not a scheduled session phase; it is a live clinical judgment about what the client needs to move.

The key distinction from CBT in therapy: coaching works with normal-range cognitive patterns that limit functioning and identity integration — not clinical disorders. A coach using CBC tools is not treating depression; they are working with distorted thinking that is blocking the client's reception of covenant truth. Staying in scope means working with what is impairing effective functioning, not what is causing clinical-level suffering. When in doubt, refer.

Evidence level: **Established** for CBT principles; **Supported** for coaching application (Neenan & Dryden, 2014; Grant, 2003; Palmer, 2007). CBC has the strongest evidence base of any coaching methodology.

## The Foundational Framework — ABC Model

All CBC work is built on the ABC model:

| Component | What It Is |
|---|---|
| **A — Activating Event** | The situation, trigger, or circumstance that precedes the emotional/behavioral response |
| **B — Belief** | The interpretation the client applies to the event — the meaning-making |
| **C — Consequence** | The emotional and behavioral outcome that follows from the belief |

**The coaching target is B — not A or C.**

Most clients present at C (*"I feel anxious / stuck / worthless"*) and assume A caused it. The CBC coach helps locate B — the belief connecting A to C. B is where change happens.

**In session — surfacing B:**
- *"When that happened [A], what went through your mind?"*
- *"What did you tell yourself about that?"*
- *"What does that say about you, or about what it means?"*

Once B is surfaced: *"Is that thought accurate? What's the evidence?"*

## Core Session Skills

### 1. Socratic Questioning

**What it is:** Drawing out the client's own reasoning rather than providing answers. Named for Socrates' method of guided inquiry — the teacher asks until the student discovers the truth themselves.

**Why it matters:** Insights reached through the client's own reasoning hold far longer than insights delivered by the coach. The CBT research base consistently shows that guided discovery outperforms direct instruction for belief change.

**The non-negotiable discipline:** Never answer your own question. Ask one question and wait. The silence is the work.

**Question types:**

| Type | Purpose | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Clarifying** | Define terms; avoid assumed meaning | *"What do you mean when you say 'failure'?"* |
| **Probing assumptions** | Surface what the client is taking for granted | *"What are you assuming here?"* |
| **Evidence probing** | Test the factual basis of a belief | *"What makes you believe that is true?"* |
| **Perspective shifting** | Introduce an alternative frame | *"How would someone who loves you describe this situation?"* |
| **Implication** | Follow the belief to its logical conclusion | *"If that is true, what follows from it?"* |
| **Meta** | Create distance from the thought | *"Why do you think you keep returning to this?"* |

---

### 2. Guided Discovery

**What it is:** Walking alongside the client as they investigate their own thinking — not directing them toward a predetermined conclusion but accompanying them through genuine inquiry.

**Distinction from Socratic Questioning:** Socratic Questioning is a specific technique; Guided Discovery is the broader posture of collaborative inquiry in which it is embedded. Guided Discovery is a way of being in the session; Socratic Questioning is a tool within it.

**The session posture:** *"I don't know what's true for you — let's find out together."* The coach is genuinely curious, not strategically guiding toward a conclusion already formed.

**When it stalls:** When the coach has a strong hypothesis about the client's belief and starts leading questions toward it. This produces insight that looks like discovery but is coach-imposed — and it is brittle. The client hasn't actually moved; they have agreed.

---

### 3. Thought Challenging

**What it is:** Examining the evidence for and against a distorted belief to determine whether it is accurate, and co-constructing a more balanced alternative.

**The five-step sequence:**
1. *"What is the exact thought?"* — get the belief stated precisely
2. *"What evidence supports this thought?"* — steelman the belief first
3. *"What evidence contradicts this thought?"* — challenge the belief
4. *"What would be a more accurate and useful way to see this?"* — build the alternative
5. *"How does your emotional response shift when you hold the balanced thought?"* — anchor the change

**Common traps:**
- Moving to step 3 before step 2 is complete — makes the challenge feel dismissive
- Accepting vague alternative thoughts (*"I'm not that bad"*) — push for specificity grounded in actual evidence
- Rushing to the alternative before the client has genuinely felt the weight of the original belief

**Biblical integration:** This is the mechanism of Romans 12:2 — the renewing of the mind operates through truthful examination of what we believe. The alternative thought is not positive thinking; it is accurate thinking anchored in what is actually true. The identity declaration format takes it further: accurate thought confirmed by what God says is true.

### 4. Behavioral Experiment Design

**What it is:** Creating small, low-risk tests that generate real-world evidence about a belief.

**Why it is more powerful than thought challenging alone:** Thought challenging works cognitively. Behavioral experiments work cognitively AND experientially. When a client believes *"If I speak up, people will see me as incompetent,"* no amount of reasoning fully dislodges that belief. A behavioral experiment — speaking up once in a low-stakes context and observing the actual result — produces evidence the brain cannot argue with.

**Design criteria for a sound experiment:**
- **Specific** — clear action, clear timeframe, clear observation to make
- **Safe-to-fail** — stakes are low enough that failure is survivable and informative
- **Hypothesis-linked** — directly tests the specific belief under examination
- **Debrief-planned** — review conversation is agreed before the experiment runs

**In session:**
1. *"You believe that if you ask for help, people will see you as weak."*
2. *"What would be a small, low-stakes situation this week where you could ask for help and observe what actually happens?"*
3. *"What will you be watching for? What would confirm the belief? What would disconfirm it?"*
4. *"Let's plan to start next session with what you observed."*

**Biblical integration:** This is the "put on" side of Ephesians 4:24 — acting toward truth before feeling it is both CBT mechanism and covenant obedience. The experiment does not wait for the emotion to change; it acts in the direction of what is true and lets the emotion follow.

### 5. Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA)

**What it is:** A structured examination of the short-term versus long-term payoffs of holding a belief or maintaining a behavior pattern.

**When to use:** When a client acknowledges that a belief is distorted but continues to act from it. The reason is almost always that the belief or behavior has short-term benefits the client has not named explicitly. CBA surfaces those benefits.

**The CBA structure:**

| | Short-Term | Long-Term |
|---|---|---|
| **Benefits of keeping the belief/behavior** | | |
| **Costs of keeping the belief/behavior** | | |

Complete all four cells before analysis.

**The key question:** *"If this belief is costing you so much, why do you still hold it? What does it protect you from?"* The answer is the real coaching territory — the secondary gain beneath the stated problem.

**In session:**
- *"What does this belief give you in the short-term?"*
- *"What does it cost you in the long-term?"*
- *"Is that trade worth it to you?"* — the client's decision, not the coach's

**Biblical integration:** Secondary gain is the hidden payoff Paul describes in Romans 7 — *"I do not do the good I want to do, but the evil I do not want to do — this I keep on doing."* The competing commitment (the benefit column) is the hidden reason. Naming it is the first move toward freedom. See also [[Immunity to Change — Kegan & Lahey Framework]].

### 6. Psychoeducation

**What it is:** Briefly explaining a psychological concept to help the client understand their own experience.

**When it is appropriate:** When a client is confused or shamed by their own pattern (*"Why can't I just stop thinking this way?"*). Explaining the mechanism reduces shame and increases cooperation with the work.

**The scope boundary:** One to three sentences maximum. This is not a lecture — it is a brief orienting frame. If explaining exceeds 90 seconds, the coach has crossed from coaching into consulting.

**Examples:**
- *"What you're describing is called catastrophizing — your mind is jumping to the worst-case scenario. It's a very common pattern. Let's look at whether the evidence actually supports it."*
- *"The brain's negativity bias means it files difficult experiences more deeply than positive ones. That's why one criticism can feel more real than five compliments. It's not a character flaw — it's how the brain is wired."*

## Palmer's PRACTICE Model

A structured CBC session flow for problem-focused coaching (Palmer, 2007):

| Stage | Description |
|---|---|
| **P** — Problem identification | What is the specific issue? Get precise. |
| **R** — Realistic and relevant goals | What does the client want? Is it achievable and owned? |
| **A** — Alternative solutions generated | What are the options? Quantity before quality. |
| **C** — Consideration of consequences | What happens if each option is pursued? |
| **T** — Target the most feasible solution | Which option will the client commit to? |
| **I** — Implementation | Specific action with accountability |
| **C** — Celebration and evaluation | What worked? What was learned? |

PRACTICE maps closely to GROW but makes consequences analysis explicit (second C) and builds celebration into the structure (final C). Useful when the presenting issue is clearly defined and the client is ready for structured problem-solving.

## Scope Boundaries

CBC skills are coaching-appropriate for normal-range cognitive patterns. The following are referral indicators:

- Suicidal ideation, active self-harm, or intent to harm others
- Trauma responses flooding sessions without external clinical support
- Depression or anxiety severe enough to impair basic daily functioning
- Presentations requiring diagnosis or medication assessment
- Persistent patterns that are not moving despite sustained coaching — may indicate clinical-level disorder

When in doubt, consult with a licensed mental health professional. Coaching scope is broad; it is not unlimited.

## Bottom Line

CBC is the most evidence-grounded coaching methodology and the most naturally integrated with biblical framework. The ABC model is the engine. Socratic Questioning is the primary tool. Behavioral Experiments are the most powerful single intervention because they produce real-world evidence that cognitive work alone cannot generate. Stay in scope, go deep on the skills, and the framework will serve clients well.
