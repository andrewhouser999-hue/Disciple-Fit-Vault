# ICF Core Competencies

`[CURRICULUM]`

The ICF (International Coaching Federation) 2019 Core Competencies define the professional standard for all coaching practice. This file is a practitioner reference — what each competency requires, why it matters, and how it applies in CBC sessions. These competencies are the floor that every other coaching method in this library builds on.

---

## The Honest Assessment

The ICF competencies are the clearest professional standard the coaching field has produced. They do not prescribe a methodology — they define the foundational skills embedded in all sound coaching. If practice consistently violates these competencies, something other than coaching is happening: advising, mentoring, consulting, or something else.

The 2019 revision is a significant improvement over the 2008 model. It added a critical competency (Embodies a Coaching Mindset), clarified the distinction between listening and evoking awareness, and organized all eight competencies into four logical clusters. Worth studying as a whole, not just referenced piecemeal.

Evidence level: **Established** as the professional standard; individual underlying skills — active listening, powerful questioning, goal-setting, accountability — have independent robust research support across psychology and coaching literature.

---

## What Makes Coaching Coaching

Before the competencies, the foundational distinction:

| Discipline | Primary Orientation | Practitioner's Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Coaching** | Present functioning → desired future | Partner; draws out the client's own wisdom |
| **Therapy** | Past wounds → present healing | Clinician; addresses disorder and dysfunction |
| **Mentoring** | Experience transfer | Advisor; shares own knowledge and path |
| **Consulting** | Problem diagnosis + solution delivery | Expert; provides recommendations |
| **Spiritual Direction** | God's movement in the person | Companion; listens with and for God |

The CBC model explicitly integrates coaching with spiritual direction posture and CBT-informed skills — all within coaching scope. The distinctions matter most at the boundary: when a client needs clinical intervention, the coach refers. This is not a limitation — it is ethical practice.

## The 8 Competencies — 4 Clusters

---

### Cluster A: Foundation

#### 1. Demonstrates Ethical Practice

**What it requires:** Understanding and consistent application of coaching ethics, standards, and guidelines. Maintaining confidentiality. Distinguishing coaching from other disciplines and referring out when appropriate.

**The boundary in practice:** Coaching works with present functioning and forward movement. When a client's presenting issues require clinical intervention — active trauma, suicidality, personality disorder, active addiction, severe depression — the coach refers. Having a referral network is not optional. It is part of ethical practice.

**Red flags for referral:**
- Suicidal ideation or intent to harm
- Active trauma flooding without external clinical support
- Symptoms consistent with clinical-level depression or anxiety
- Presentations requiring diagnosis or medication assessment

When in doubt, consult with a licensed professional rather than extend coaching scope.

---

#### 2. Embodies a Coaching Mindset

**What it requires:** Developing and maintaining a mindset that is genuinely open, curious, flexible, and client-centered. Ongoing self-reflection and professional development. Awareness that the coach's own psychology affects the work.

**The practical content:**
- Holding genuine curiosity about the client's world — not assumption or premature hypothesis
- Tolerating not-knowing; sitting with ambiguity without rushing to resolution
- Ongoing self-supervision: *What am I bringing into this session that is not the client's?*
- Commitment to development over time — this is not a static credential

**What violates this competency:**
- Giving advice disguised as questions
- Having an agenda for where the client should go
- Rescuing clients from necessary discomfort
- Getting activated by the client's material and losing objectivity

**Biblical integration:** The coaching mindset is not passive neutrality — it is the Proverbs 20:5 posture: *"The purposes of a person's heart are deep waters, but one who has insight draws them out."* Curiosity as intentional, skilled attention.

---

### Cluster B: Co-Creating the Relationship

#### 3. Establishes and Maintains Agreements

**What it requires:** Partnering with the client to establish clear agreements about the coaching relationship, the process, and each individual session.

**Two levels of agreement — both required:**

*Relationship-level (coaching contract):* What is coaching? What will the engagement look like? What is expected of both parties? How will progress be measured?

*Session-level agreement:* What do we want from this specific session? This is the most commonly skipped agreement in practice.

**How to establish a session agreement:**
- *"What would you like to get from our time today?"*
- *"What would make this session useful to you?"*
- *"How will you know we worked on the right thing?"*

Without a session agreement, the coach is implicitly directing. With one, the client owns the hour.

---

#### 4. Cultivates Trust and Safety

**What it requires:** Creating an environment in which the client feels safe to take risks, be honest, and move toward the edges of their experience.

**What trust requires from the coach:**
- Consistent presence — not distracted, not performing
- Demonstrated confidentiality — what happens in session stays there
- Genuine respect for the client's pace
- Non-judgment of the client's choices, values, or struggles
- Willingness to be questioned or challenged by the client

**The speed of trust:** Trust is not established in one session. Clients with significant relational wounds may need multiple sessions before genuine depth is available. Rushing depth before trust is earned produces surface work and damaged rapport.

**Biblical integration:** Safety is a prerequisite for honesty. The prophetic tradition consistently operates from established covenant relationship before confrontation. Challenge without trust is pressure, not care.

---

#### 5. Maintains Presence

**What it requires:** Being fully conscious and present with the client in the moment. Accessing intuition. Managing one's own emotional state during challenging sessions.

**The components of coaching presence:**
- **Physical** — body is calm, grounded, regulated
- **Emotional** — not activated by the client's material
- **Cognitive** — attention is on the client, not on the next question
- **Intuitive** — noticing what is present beneath the surface of the conversation

**What destroys presence:**
- Preparing the next question while the client is still speaking
- Getting triggered by the client's content
- Carrying pre-session stress into the room
- Having an agenda for where the session should land

**Developing presence:** This is a practice, not a skill. Session transition rituals. Breath before appointments. Personal supervision or therapy for coaches. Spiritual disciplines that cultivate stillness.

**Biblical integration:** *"Be still and know that I am God"* (Ps 46:10) — stillness as a precondition for knowing. Simone Weil: *"Attention is the rarest and purest form of generosity."* Coaching presence is an act of love.

---

### Cluster C: Communicating Effectively

#### 6. Listens Actively

**What it requires:** Attending to the client at multiple levels — words, tone, emotion, body language — and to what is absent as well as present. Reflecting and summarizing in service of the client's own understanding, not the coach's.

**Three levels of listening (see also [[Co-Active Coaching — Skills Reference]]):**
- **Level 1 — Internal:** Coach attending to own reactions. Minimize this.
- **Level 2 — Focused:** Full attention on client's words and expression. The coaching baseline.
- **Level 3 — Global:** Attending to the field — what is present AND absent; what is charged or unspoken. Where the deepest coaching happens.

**What Level 3 listening accesses:**
- The feeling underneath the words
- The topic the client keeps circling without landing on
- The word or phrase repeated (often a diagnostic clue)
- The subject the client carefully avoids
- The emotion present in the body that contradicts the spoken words

**Reflecting and summarizing:** Reflect back what you heard without interpretation. *"What I hear you saying is..."* — not *"What that means is..."* The client confirms, corrects, or deepens. Either response moves the coaching forward.

---

#### 7. Evokes Awareness

**What it requires:** Facilitating the client's insight and learning through questioning, silence, metaphor, reframing, direct communication, and intuition-sharing.

This is the art of coaching. Any practitioner can ask questions. The ability to evoke genuine awareness — to help a client see something they genuinely could not see before — separates adequate coaching from excellent coaching.

**Powerful Questions:**
Questions that are open-ended, thought-provoking, and serve the client's agenda. One question at a time. Tolerate the silence after asking — it is where the real answer forms.

*Examples:*
- *"What would be possible if you stopped believing that?"*
- *"What are you most afraid would happen if you actually succeeded?"*
- *"What is this situation asking of you?"*
- *"Who would you be without this problem?"*

**Direct Communication:**
Naming what the coach observes without interpretation or judgment. *"I notice you light up when you talk about X and go flat when you talk about Y."* The client either confirms or corrects — both responses are useful.

Always request permission before sharing intuition: *"May I share an observation?"*

**Reframing:**
Offering an alternative perspective without insisting on it. *"What if the thing you're calling failure is actually information about what you're not designed for?"*

**Silence:**
The most underdeveloped skill. After a powerful question, most coaches wait 3–5 seconds before speaking. The deepest answers require 15–30 seconds minimum. Silence is not empty — it is where the client's real response forms.

---

### Cluster D: Cultivating Learning and Growth

#### 8. Facilitates Client Growth

**What it requires:** Partnering with the client to transform insight into action, accountability, and sustained learning over time.

**The components:**
- Designing actions together — not prescribing them
- Planning and goal-setting with specificity
- Managing progress and accountability across the engagement
- Celebrating growth explicitly — clients routinely run past their own progress

**Designing actions together:** *"What action comes to mind for you?"* before *"Would you be willing to try X?"* Actions the client generates are more owned than actions assigned by the coach.

**Accountability without policing:** The coach's job is to hold the client to what the client said mattered to them — not to enforce compliance. *"You said this was important to you. What happened?"* is accountability. *"Why didn't you do what you said?"* is prosecution.

**Celebrating growth:** Name progress explicitly. *"Three months ago you couldn't have done what you just described. Do you see that?"* Many clients need the coach to slow them down long enough to register what has actually changed.

---

## Bottom Line

The ICF competencies are not a ceiling — they are the floor every other coaching skill rests on. If these eight are not consistently operational, no advanced methodology will compensate. If they are, every other skill in the library becomes more effective.
