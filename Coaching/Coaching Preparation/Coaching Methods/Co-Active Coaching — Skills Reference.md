# Co-Active Coaching — Skills Reference

`[CURRICULUM]` `[SESSION-TOOL]`

The Co-Active model is the most comprehensive life coaching methodology available, developed by CTI (Co-Active Training Institute). It provides both a philosophy of the client (4 Cornerstones) and a full vocabulary of coach skills deployable in live sessions. This file is the practitioner reference for those skills within the CBC coaching context.

---

## The Honest Assessment

Co-Active gives you the richest named skill vocabulary in life coaching. No other methodology produces as many discrete, deployable skills for responding to what is alive in a session. The 4 Cornerstones provide a coherent philosophical foundation; the skill set is practically distinct — you can learn a specific skill, know when to deploy it, and track whether you used it.

The primary limitation requires theological adaptation: CRW (clients are naturally creative, resourceful, and whole) assumes a level of human agency that secular coaching can hold but biblical anthropology cannot. The CBC adaptation is not rejection of CRW — it is a grounded version. Clients bear the imago Dei and therefore possess genuine agency, creativity, and worth. But not self-sufficiency. The coach works from their inherent dignity, not their assumed wholeness.

Evidence level: **Supported** — most widely adopted life coaching framework; ICF-aligned; 25+ years of practitioner consensus; limited RCT evidence for the integrated model; individual components (empathic listening, powerful questioning, accountability) have independent strong support.

---

## The 4 Cornerstones

These are philosophical commitments, not techniques. They shape every skill application.

**1. People are naturally creative, resourceful, and whole (CRW)**
The client already has what they need. The coach's job is evocation, not installation. This posture prevents advice-giving and positions the client as the primary expert on their own life.

*CBC adaptation:* Replace "naturally whole" with "bears the image of God and therefore has genuine agency, creativity, and worth." Coaching confidence rests in what God has already placed in the client — not in the client's autonomous self-sufficiency.

**2. Dance in the moment**
Follow what is most alive in the conversation, not the agenda. The best coaching session often goes nowhere near where it was planned to go. Rigidity kills presence.

*Practical implication:* Hold session agendas lightly. If the client arrives emotionally flooded, that is the session.

**3. Focus on the whole person**
The client is not a goal-delivery system. Feelings, body, relationships, and spiritual state that enter the room are part of the coaching work — not distractions from it.

**4. Evoke transformation**
The deepest aim of coaching is not action or insight — it is a shift in who the client is being. Actions change behavior; transformation changes the person.

## Three Levels of Listening

Listening level determines what the coach can perceive and access.

| Level | Description | What It Accesses |
|---|---|---|
| **Level 1 — Internal** | Coach attending to own reactions and next question | Least effective; coach is largely absent |
| **Level 2 — Focused** | Full attention on client's words, tone, emotion, energy | The coaching baseline — what the client is expressing directly |
| **Level 3 — Global** | Attending to the field — what is present AND absent; what is charged, unspoken, underneath | Intuition, body cues, the thing the client can't quite say — where deepest coaching happens |

Move from Level 2 toward Level 3 as trust builds. Early sessions rarely have the safety for Level 3 work.

## The 9 Core Skills

---

### 1. Championing

**What it is:** Naming and holding the client's best self when they cannot see it. Not cheerleading — a specific identification of a capacity or quality the coach genuinely observes.

**When to use:** Client minimizes themselves, doubts their ability, or says "I can't" about something the coach has evidence they can do.

**How to deploy:**
- Be specific: *"I have watched you navigate X. What you're calling impossible is something you already know how to do."*
- Hold it with conviction: *"I believe in this more than you do right now. That's part of my job."*
- Do not soften into compliment: *"You've got this"* is not championing. *"I've watched you move through harder than this without crumbling"* is.

**Biblical resonance:** Prophetic seeing — naming what God sees rather than what circumstances suggest. Jesus names who Simon will become (John 1:42), not who he currently is.

---

### 2. Acknowledging

**What it is:** Naming WHO the client is being — their character, their way of showing up — not what they accomplished.

**Distinction from compliment:** A compliment addresses what someone did. An acknowledgment addresses who someone is.

**When to use:** Client does something hard, shows up differently than their pattern, or demonstrates a quality that should be named and landed.

**How to deploy:**
- *"What I see is a person with real courage. You came into this conversation ready to look at something most people avoid."*
- *"What strikes me is your honesty. You didn't deflect — you went straight at it."*

**Why it matters:** Clients build identity from what they are seen as. Consistent acknowledgment of the right things shapes who the client comes to believe they are.

---

### 3. Clearing

**What it is:** Brief, contained emotional processing that restores cognitive access. When a client is flooded, anxious, or carrying something heavy, the rest of the session cannot work until that is released.

**Important boundary:** Clearing is not therapy. It is a brief container — 5–10 minutes maximum — not an invitation into extended emotional processing or trauma work. The coach holds the container and brings the client back to forward-focused work.

**When to use:** Client arrives visibly carrying something. Client is stuck on an emotion and cannot move past it. Client is too activated for cognitive work.

**How to deploy:**
1. Name what you observe: *"You came in with something heavy today."*
2. Invite expression: *"Before we do anything else — what do you need to say?"*
3. Listen without rescuing. Do not minimize, fix, or move on too fast.
4. Transition when ready: *"Thank you for bringing that. Where do you want to go from here?"*

---

### 4. Bottom-Lining

**What it is:** Cutting through elaboration to name the core issue. When a client is circling — talking around something without landing — the coach bottom-lines.

**When to use:** Client has been talking for several minutes but no clear issue has surfaced. Lots of detail, little clarity. Something is being avoided.

**How to deploy:**
- *"I want to interrupt for a second. Underneath everything you're describing — what is the real issue?"*
- *"If you had to name the one thing at stake here, what would it be?"*
- *"We've been orbiting something for a while. What are we not saying?"*

**The discipline:** Do not bottom-line too early. Clients need space to arrive at what is true. But at some point the coach's job is to cut through the noise and surface the signal.

---

### 5. Requesting

**What it is:** Asking the client to commit to a specific action by a specific time. A request has three acceptable responses: yes, no, or counteroffer.

**Distinction from a question:** *"What might you do this week?"* is a question. *"I want to request that you have that conversation with your boss by Thursday. Are you willing?"* is a request.

**When to use:** Client has insight but no traction. Session has generated energy but no committed action. Client's pattern is knowing without doing.

**How to deploy:**
- Name the action specifically — not *"work on your resume"* but *"send two applications by Friday noon."*
- Include the timeframe.
- Accept the full response. If the client says no, explore the resistance. If they counteroffer, respect it.

**Not accountability policing:** The request invites the client to make a real commitment to themselves, witnessed by the coach.

---

### 6. The Challenge

**What it is:** Inviting the client to try something they believe they cannot do. A direct, respectful stretch past the client's stated limit.

**Distinction from Requesting:** A request asks the client to do something they have agreed is possible. A challenge invites them to stretch past what they believe is possible.

**When to use:** Client has drawn a hard line about their own capacity (*"I could never do that"*) and the coach sees evidence they are wrong.

**How to deploy:**
- *"I want to challenge you. You said you can't do X. I think you can. I want to invite you to try it once this week."*
- Name it as a challenge: *"I'm going to push on something here."*
- The client can decline. The conversation about the decline is often the coaching.

---

### 7. Visioning

**What it is:** Guiding the client to inhabit and describe an ideal future in sensory, present-tense detail.

**Distinction from goal-setting:** Goal-setting is analytical (what do I want to achieve?). Visioning is experiential (what does it feel like to be living the life I want?).

**When to use:** Client lacks direction. Client is operating purely from avoidance. Client needs motivation that analytical goals cannot generate.

**How to deploy:**
1. Ground the client briefly (breath or body awareness).
2. Invite: *"Imagine it's five years from now and your life is exactly as you want it. What are you doing? Who is around you? What do you feel when you wake up?"*
3. Ask for sensory detail: *"What do you see? What does your body feel like?"*
4. Follow with: *"What needs to be true for that to be your life?"*

**Biblical integration:** Visioning is not secular projection — it is discernment of design and calling. The image that arises may be closer to what God has placed in the client than their analytical goals are.

---

### 8. Forwarding the Action

**What it is:** Keeping the session moving toward tangible, owned next steps. The coach's responsibility to ensure sessions produce traction, not just insight.

**When to use:** Throughout the session, and especially in the final 10–15 minutes.

**How to deploy:**
- *"What are you taking from this session into the week?"*
- *"What specifically will you do, and by when?"*
- *"What do you need from me to stay accountable to this?"*

**The failure mode to prevent:** Sessions that produce great insight but no action. Insight without traction is pleasant but not coaching.

---

### 9. Deepening the Learning

**What it is:** Pausing to name and consolidate what the client is discovering about themselves in the moment.

**When to use:** After a significant moment of insight, emotion, or realization. The client has just seen something true — this skill helps them absorb it rather than pass it.

**How to deploy:**
- *"What are you noticing about yourself right now?"*
- *"What does this tell you about how you've been operating?"*
- *"What shifts when you sit with that?"*

**Why it matters:** Coaching creates moments of insight. Without consolidation, those moments dissolve within hours. Deepening the Learning is the integration move.

## The Three Life Dimensions

Co-Active coaching works across three dimensions simultaneously, not sequentially.

| Dimension | What It Addresses |
|---|---|
| **Fulfillment** | Living a life of meaning, purpose, and full expression — not just success |
| **Balance** | The client's relationship to competing forces; making conscious choices rather than reactive ones |
| **Process** | The quality of the journey, not just the destination; how the client is being, not just what they are doing |

A coaching relationship that addresses only goals (Fulfillment) without attending to Balance and Process will eventually stall.

---

## Session Posture Summary

- Be for the client more than the client is for themselves — hold their best self as more real than their current self-assessment
- Be curious, not diagnostic — the coach's agenda is zero; the client's agenda is everything
- Trust the client's wisdom — they already know more than they are saying; the coach's job is to draw it out
- Hold the tension without filling it — silence, discomfort, and not-knowing are not problems to solve

---

## Bottom Line

Co-Active gives the most deployable skill vocabulary in life coaching. The 9 skills are named, distinct, and tied to specific session situations. CRW requires theological adaptation for faith-based work — imago Dei is the biblical ground for the same coaching posture. Master these skills and a significant range of response is available in any session moment.
