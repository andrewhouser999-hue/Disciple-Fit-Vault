# Solution-Focused Coaching

`[CURRICULUM]`

## The Honest Assessment

Solution-Focused Brief Therapy (SFBT) transfers to coaching better than almost any clinical framework because it was never primarily about pathology. Developed by Steve de Shazer and Insoo Kim Berg through direct observation of what actually moved clients forward — not theory first, observation first — it bypasses problem analysis entirely and builds toward a preferred future by amplifying what is already working. The RCT base is solid. The translation to coaching is natural.

The risk is shallow use. The miracle question and scaling questions look deceptively simple. Coaches who deploy them without understanding the underlying logic — that change is built on existing competence, not installed from outside — turn precision instruments into clichés. The miracle question done poorly produces vague fantasy. Done well, it builds a concrete, behavioral, client-owned picture of a different life that the client already partially knows how to live.

One honest limitation: SF coaching is not the right entry point when a client is still fully embedded in the problem and needs to be heard there first. Jumping to preferred-future work before the client feels understood produces a pleasant conversation that changes nothing. Sequence matters.

Evidence quality: **Established** for SFBT in clinical contexts across a wide range of presentations. **Supported** for coaching applications — the direct coaching transfer has fewer RCTs but is consistent with the clinical evidence and well-supported by practitioner research.

## Elaborate Overview

### The SFBT Spirit — What Makes It Distinct

SFBT operates from a fundamentally different assumption than problem-focused approaches. Where CBT asks *what thought is distorted and how do we fix it*, SFBT asks *when is the problem not happening, and what is the client already doing then?* The client is not a broken system to be repaired — they are a competent person who has temporarily lost sight of their own competence.

Three foundational SFBT principles:
1. **If it isn't broken, don't fix it** — don't introduce problems that the client didn't present
2. **If something works, do more of it** — find existing solutions and amplify them
3. **If it's not working, do something different** — the definition of insanity applied directly

This produces a coaching posture that is genuinely counter-cultural: resist the pull to analyze the problem. The problem is not where the solution lives.

---

### The Miracle Question — Mechanism and Proper Use

The miracle question is SF's most misunderstood tool. It is not a visualization exercise or a positive thinking prompt. It is a precision instrument for constructing a *preferred future in behavioral, observable detail* — the kind of detail that generates specific, actionable next steps.

**The standard form** (de Shazer's original):
*"Suppose that tonight, while you are sleeping, a miracle happens. The miracle is that the problem that brought you here is solved. But because you are sleeping, you don't know the miracle has happened. When you wake up tomorrow, what would be the first small thing you would notice that would tell you the miracle had happened?"*

**Why "first small thing" matters:** It grounds the miracle in observable behavior, not abstract feeling states. *"I'd feel happy"* is not the answer the question is asking for. *"I'd get up at 6am instead of lying there"* is. The behavioral specificity is what makes the miracle question actionable.

**The follow-up sequence:**
- *"What else would you notice?"*
- *"What would your partner / colleague / child notice that was different about you?"*
- *"What would you be doing that you're not doing now?"*

The goal is to construct a rich, multi-sensory, behaviorally specific picture of the preferred future that the client already knows how to partially describe — because they have partially lived it already.

---

### Exception Questions — The Most Empirically Active Element

Research consistently identifies exception questions as the most clinically active element of SFBT. An exception is any time the problem is not happening, or happening less.

**The diagnostic move:** Every problem has exceptions. The dominant story — *"I always do this / it always fails / nothing ever works"* — systematically excludes them. The coach's job is to find the exceptions the client has been explaining away as flukes.

**The exception question sequence:**
- *"When is this problem not happening? Even a little?"*
- *"When was the last time it was even slightly better? What was different?"*
- *"What are you doing then that you're not doing now?"*
- *"What would it take to have more of those times?"*

**The amplification move:** Once an exception is found, do not move past it. Spend time there.
*"What does it say about you that you were able to do that? What did you draw on?"*

The exception is not a fluke — it is evidence of existing competence that the client's dominant story has been discounting.

---

### Scaling Questions — Creating Movement Without a Leap

Scaling questions do two things simultaneously: they establish a concrete baseline and they make movement feel possible without requiring transformation.

**Standard form:** *"On a scale of 0–10, where 10 is the problem is completely solved and 0 is as bad as it's ever been — where are you today?"*

**The critical follow-up:** *"What makes it a [stated number] rather than a 0?"* — This surfaces existing resources and partial solutions the client hasn't named. A client who says *"I'm at a 3"* has never explicitly articulated why it is not a 0. The question makes that visible.

**The movement question:** *"What would one point higher look like? Not a 10 — just one point. What would be different?"* — Small movement is more achievable and more motivating than large transformation.

**For commitment:** *"On a scale of 0–10, how confident are you that you could take that one step? What would make it a point higher?"*

---

### Coping Questions — When Life Is Hard

Coping questions are SF's most underused tool and most appropriate for clients in genuine difficulty who need recognition before preferred-future work.

**Form:** *"Given how hard this has been — how have you kept going? What has kept you functioning?"*

This is not consolation. It is genuine inquiry into existing resilience mechanisms the client has deployed without crediting. The answer builds self-efficacy: *"I hadn't thought of it that way — I have been managing somehow."*

Coping questions establish a resource base before any preferred-future work begins. They honor the reality without getting stuck in it.

---

### The Preferred Future — More Than the Miracle Question

The preferred future is built over multiple questions and exchanges — the miracle question opens it, exception questions supply evidence for it, scaling questions measure proximity to it. Together they construct a *lived image* of a different life rather than an abstract aspiration.

The preferred future works because it is:
- Behaviorally specific (not *"I'd be happier"* but *"I'd start my day this way and respond to conflict that way"*)
- Client-authored (not the coach's vision imposed)
- Evidence-connected (grounded in exceptions that have actually happened)
- Achievable in pieces (scaling allows movement without requiring the full picture at once)

## Tier 1: Empirical Foundation

| Finding | Evidence Level | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| SFBT produces significant positive outcomes across diverse populations | **Established** | Kim (2008) meta-analysis — 22 studies, effect sizes moderate to strong; Stams et al. (2006) meta-analysis confirms |
| Exception questions most clinically active element | **Supported** | Process research; consistent across multiple practitioners but limited direct comparative RCTs |
| SFBT equivalent or superior to other approaches for behavioral concerns | **Established** | Gingerich & Eisengart (2000) — 15 controlled studies; positive outcomes in 5, mixed in 7, no difference in 2 |
| Scaling questions produce measurable movement in single sessions | **Supported** | Practitioner research; consistent with hope theory (Snyder, 1994) on goal-directed thinking |

## What to Ignore

- Reductive SF: *"Just ask what's working."* The framework has internal logic and sequencing. Deployed without that logic, it produces pleasant conversations without traction.
- Using SF to avoid problems that need to be heard. If a client is in genuine pain, coping questions come before preferred-future work. Jumping to the miracle question before the client feels understood is therapeutic bypassing.
- Conflating SF with positive thinking. SF is strategic attention to existing competence — not manufactured optimism. The exception question is finding what is real, not pretending the problem isn't.
- The watered-down coaching version that asks *"What's your ideal outcome?"* and calls it the miracle question. Ideal outcome is a goal question. The miracle question is a behavioral future-construction instrument. They operate differently.

## Coach-Safe Scope

**What a life coach CAN do with this modality:**
- Use all core SFBT tools: miracle question, exception questions, scaling questions, coping questions
- Build preferred futures with clients through behavioral specificity
- Use exception-finding to surface existing client competence in any presenting issue
- Deploy scaling questions for both assessment and movement-building
- Use the SF framework as the primary session structure for clients who are ready to move

**What requires clinical training:**
- Using SF with clients whose "preferred future" reveals psychotic thinking or significantly disconnected reality — the miracle question can surface content requiring clinical assessment
- Deploying SF with trauma presentations where exception-finding may inadvertently minimize the reality of the trauma

**Referral indicator:**
A preferred future that is internally incoherent, grandiose, or completely detached from the client's stated life — or a client who cannot identify any exceptions whatsoever (no moment when things were even slightly better) — warrants clinical assessment rather than continued SF coaching.

### Without Biblical Framework — Christian Client

**Verdict: Fixable.** The exception-finding method and preferred-future construction both need biblical interrogation.

The exception question ("when is the problem not happening?") surfaces what is working, but cannot detect that the exception may be when the client was living in dependence on God vs. self-effort — the spiritual posture beneath the behavior is invisible without biblical framing. A client may be "succeeding" functionally through control, avoidance, or self-protection — solutions that are spiritually evasive and will not produce the exception the biblical framework is looking for.

The preferred future is entirely client-defined. Biblical framework asks: preferred by whom? A client's preferred future may exclude suffering, sacrifice, and calling — exactly where God may be leading. Without this lens, SF coaching orients the Christian toward comfort and self-authored flourishing rather than faithfulness.

**Fix:** When building the preferred future, add a discernment layer — *"What does faithfulness look like here?"* When finding exceptions, probe spiritual posture alongside behavior: *"What were you trusting in during those moments?"*

## Self-Directed Therapy/Coaching Questions

### Preferred Future
- "If the problem that has been weighing on you most were resolved — what would your daily life actually look like? Not how you'd feel, but what you'd be doing."
- "What is the first small thing you would notice in the morning that told you something was different?"
- "What would the people closest to you notice about you that was different?"

### Exception Finding
- "When is this problem not happening, or happening less? Even once, even slightly?"
- "When was the last time things were even a little better? What was different about that time?"
- "What were you doing in those moments that you're not doing now?"

### Scaling
- "On a scale of 0–10, where are you with this right now?"
- "What makes it that number and not lower? What resources or strengths are keeping you there?"
- "What would one point higher look like? What is the smallest step toward it?"

### Coping
- "Given how hard this has been — what has kept you going?"
- "What have you been doing, even imperfectly, to hold things together?"
- "What does your capacity to still be here and still be working on this say about you?"

## Coaching Application — In Session

**Sequencing principle:** Engage first, then focus, then build preferred future. Do not open with the miracle question. Open with a genuine hearing of where the client is.

**The entry:**
*"Before we build forward — tell me what's been going on. I want to understand where you are."*

Listen for existing competence and exceptions even while hearing the problem. They are present in every client narrative — mark them for later.

**Moving to preferred future:**
*"Given everything you've said — I'd like to try something. Can I ask you a slightly unusual question?"* [Introduce the miracle question with the standard form.]

After the initial response, use follow-up questions relentlessly. The first answer is rarely specific enough. *"What else would you notice? What would you be doing differently? What would your [person] notice?"*

**Connecting preferred future to exception:**
*"You said things were a little better on [occasion]. When I listen to the way you described that, it sounds a lot like what you described in the miracle scenario. Is that right? What were you doing that made it like that?"*

This moment — connecting the preferred future to an existing exception — is often the most powerful moment in an SF session. The client realizes the miracle is not entirely foreign; they have partially lived it.

**Closing commitment:**
Scale the client's current position, ask what one point higher looks like, and anchor a specific step. *"What is the one thing you could do this week that would be a small move in that direction?"*

## Bottom Line

SF coaching's discipline is attention — specifically, attention to what is already working rather than what is broken. This is not a technique set; it is a fundamental reorientation of where the coach looks and what they amplify. The client already contains partial solutions, existing competence, and regular exceptions to their dominant problem story. The SF coach's job is to find them, name them, and build forward from them. In a coaching culture dominated by deficit-focused diagnostics, this is genuinely countercultural — and clinically supported.
