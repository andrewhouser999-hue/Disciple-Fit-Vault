# Motivational Interviewing — CBC Application

## The Honest Assessment

Motivational Interviewing (MI) is the most evidence-based conversation method for resolving ambivalence and eliciting change. It was developed in clinical addiction work and has since been validated across health, mental health, criminal justice, education, and coaching contexts. It is not a personality style or a vibe — it is a rigorously defined method with specific skills, measurable fidelity, and a clear theoretical model.

Most coaches are doing a degraded version of MI without knowing the model. Active listening, reflection, avoiding advice-giving — these are MI-adjacent habits, not MI. The actual framework has a specific spirit, four core processes, and eight core skills that operate in a defined sequence. If you're using "empathy" and "reflection" without the underlying architecture, you're building on sand.

Advisor note: MI is particularly non-negotiable for Kingdom coaching because your clients are almost universally ambivalent — not about whether they believe, but about whether they want to change. They want the life; they don't always want to pay the price for it. MI is the method for that precise territory.

## Elaborate Overview

### The MI Spirit — Four Core Dimensions

The spirit of MI is not just warmth and acceptance. It is a deliberate stance toward the client that defines every technique beneath it. Without the spirit, the techniques are manipulation.

**1. Partnership (Collaboration)**
MI is done *with* the client, not *to* or *for* them. The coach is not the expert on the client's life. The coach is the facilitator of the client's own discovery. This is not false modesty — it is an accurate epistemological claim: only the client has access to their own internal motivational system.

**2. Acceptance**
Acceptance in MI has four components:
- *Absolute worth* — the client's unconditional value as a person, independent of change
- *Accurate empathy* — the disciplined effort to understand their perspective as they experience it
- *Autonomy support* — respecting the client's right to choose, including the right to not change
- *Affirmation* — actively acknowledging strengths and efforts

The counterintuitive finding: when clients feel accepted as they are, they are more willing to consider change. When they feel pressured, they defend the status quo.

**3. Compassion**
Prioritizing the client's welfare and needs, not the coach's agenda for them. MI explicitly warns against "righting reflex" — the coach's instinctive desire to fix, advise, or correct. The righting reflex is the enemy of MI.

**4. Evocation**
Change motivation is not installed by the coach — it is *drawn out* from the client. The client already contains the reasons for change. The MI practitioner's job is to create the conditions in which those reasons surface and strengthen.

---

### The Four Processes of MI

MI is not a toolbox — it is a sequential process. Practitioners who jump to planning without establishing engagement produce poor outcomes.

**Process 1: Engaging**
Establishing a working relationship built on trust, safety, and accurate empathy. Without this, nothing else works. The coach who skips engagement to get to the "real work" destroys the very foundation the real work requires.

Skill focus: Open questions, reflective listening, affirming, summarizing.

**Process 2: Focusing**
Developing a clear, agreed-upon direction for the conversation. What are we working on? Which aspect of change is most relevant? Without focus, the conversation is warm but aimless.

Skill focus: Agenda mapping, exploring client's priorities, establishing a shared focus.

**Process 3: Evoking**
The heart of MI. Drawing out the client's own motivations, values, and reasons for change. This is where change talk is elicited and strengthened, and where sustain talk is handled without amplifying it.

**Process 4: Planning**
When the client has moved toward readiness, collaboratively building a specific, feasible plan. Planning too early (before sufficient motivation) produces the "yes but" pattern — committed plans that quietly collapse. Planning at the right moment builds momentum.

---

### Change Talk — The Core Target

**Change talk** is any client statement that moves in the direction of change. Research by Moyers and Miller shows that the more change talk a client produces in a session, the more likely they are to change. The coach's job is to *elicit and reinforce change talk.*

Change talk falls into two categories:

**Preparatory Change Talk — DARN:**
- **D — Desire:** *"I want to..."* / *"I wish things were different..."*
- **A — Ability:** *"I could..."* / *"I think I might be able to..."*
- **R — Reasons:** *"Because..."* / *"It would mean..."*
- **N — Need:** *"I have to..."* / *"It's important that..."*

**Mobilizing Change Talk — CAT:**
- **C — Commitment:** *"I will..."* / *"I'm going to..."*
- **A — Activation:** *"I'm ready to..."* / *"I'm willing to..."*
- **T — Taking steps:** *"I already started..."* / *"Last week I actually..."*

**Sustain talk** is the opposite — statements favoring no change: *"But it's too hard,"* *"I've tried before,"* *"I'm not sure it's worth it."*

The key insight: **reflecting sustain talk amplifies it; reflecting change talk amplifies it.** The coach selectively reinforces what is already moving.

---

### The Eight Core Skills — OARS + EARS

**Core MI Skills (OARS):**
- **O — Open questions:** Invite elaboration; cannot be answered yes/no. *"What's been weighing on you about this?"*
- **A — Affirming:** Genuine recognition of strengths, efforts, values. Not praise — acknowledgment of what is real. *"You've kept showing up even when it hasn't been working. That says something about you."*
- **R — Reflecting:** The most critical skill. Not parroting — strategic reflection that emphasizes the change-talk content and deepens exploration.
  - *Simple reflection:* Mirror back what was said
  - *Complex reflection:* Add meaning, emotion, or implication not explicitly stated
  - *Amplified reflection:* Slightly overstate to invite correction (useful with ambivalence)
  - *Double-sided reflection:* Reflect both sides of ambivalence — *"Part of you wants this, and part of you is protecting what you'd have to give up."*
- **S — Summarizing:** Collecting change talk and offering it back, reinforcing momentum. *"So let me make sure I've got this — you've said you're tired of how things are, you believe things could be different, and you know what the first step would be. Is that right?"*

**Evoking Skills (EARS) — specifically for eliciting change talk:**
- **E — Elaborating:** *"Tell me more about that."* / *"What does that look like?"*
- **A — Affirming:** Reinforcing change-relevant strengths
- **R — Reflecting change talk back:** Strategic selection of what to mirror
- **S — Summarizing change talk:** Bundling and returning the client's own reasons

### The Ambivalence Map

The core problem MI addresses is *ambivalence* — the simultaneous pull toward and away from change. Ambivalence is not pathology. It is the normal human response to change that involves real costs.

```
For Change                     Against Change
-----------                    --------------
"I want it to be different"    "Change is risky / painful"
"I know what it's costing me"  "What I have now is familiar"
"I believe I could do it"      "I've failed before"
"It matters to me"             "What if I try and still fail?"
```

The MI practitioner does not argue for the "For Change" side. Arguing for change triggers the client's counterargument (the righting reflex in the coach produces reactance in the client). Instead, the coach creates the conditions for the client to argue both sides — and then leans toward the change-relevant material.

**The paradox:** The more the coach pushes for change, the more the client defends against it. The less the coach pushes, the safer it is for the client to move toward it.

## Tier 1: Empirical Foundation

| Finding | Evidence |
|---|---|
| MI significantly outperforms direct advice for behavior change | Meta-analysis: Lundahl et al. (2010), 119 studies — MI superior to no treatment and equivalent to other active treatments across domains |
| Change talk predicts outcome | Moyers et al. (2007) — change talk in session is a significant predictor of behavior change post-session |
| Reflective listening is the active mechanism | Millar & Rose (2009) — the quality of reflective listening (not the total number of reflections) drives outcomes |
| Righting reflex backfires | Research consistently shows advice-giving from a position of authority increases sustain talk and resistance |

## What to Ignore

- MI described as "being nice to clients" or "empathetic coaching" — this is the spirit without the structure
- Teaching MI by reading about it — MI is a behavioral skill set. You need practice with feedback. The MITI (Motivational Interviewing Treatment Integrity) coding system exists specifically because MI is hard to self-assess
- "Decisional balance" exercises without understanding where they fit in the process — used at the wrong stage (before engaging, or too late in planning) they amplify ambivalence rather than resolving it
- Confusing MI with non-directive therapy — MI has a directional agenda (change) while remaining non-coercive. It is not client-centered therapy; it is strategically directional within a client-centered stance

## Self-Directed Therapy/Coaching Questions

### Engaging with Your Own Ambivalence
- "What is something I genuinely want to be different in my life right now — something I've wanted for more than six months?"
- "Have I been taking action on it? What have I been doing instead?"
- "What is the part of me that wants to change? What does it say when I listen to it?"
- "What is the part of me that resists? What is it protecting? What would I lose if I changed?"
- "On a scale of 0–10, how important is this change to me? What makes it that number and not lower?"
- "On a scale of 0–10, how confident am I that I could change if I decided to? What makes it that number?"

### Eliciting Your Own Change Talk
- "What are my best reasons for making this change?"
- "What would be different about my life one year from now if I made this change?"
- "What does staying the same cost me — in relationships, in my sense of self, in my relationship with God?"
- "What do I already know about how to do this? What have I done before that worked?"
- "What is one small step I could take that would signal to myself that I'm moving?"

### Identifying the Righting Reflex (as coach or person)
- "When I see someone doing something that I think is wrong or harmful — what is my first instinct? Correct them, advise them, or understand them?"
- "When someone shares ambivalence with me, do I find myself pushing toward the change I think is right? How does that affect them?"
- "What would it feel like to hold back my advice and just reflect what I hear?"

## Coaching Application — In Session

**MI for the coaching entry point:**
When a client presents a problem but has not asked for change — or when they've named a change goal but aren't acting on it — MI is the entry, not GROW. Using GROW on an unresolved ambivalence produces a plan that doesn't hold.

**Practical MI sequence for a Kingdom coaching session:**

1. **Engage first:** *"Before we get into what you want to work on — how are you, actually?"* Create safety before direction.
2. **Explore the ambivalence:** *"You've said you want [X]. What makes that important to you? And what has made it hard to move on it?"*
3. **Listen for change talk. Reflect it back.** Every time the client articulates desire, ability, reason, or need — reflect it. *"So part of you really does believe this is possible."*
4. **Summarize change talk before planning:** *"Let me reflect back what I've heard — you've said it matters deeply, you've done hard things before, and you know what the first step would be. Does that feel accurate?"*
5. **Invite committed action from the client's own words:** *"Based on everything you've just said — what do you want to do?"*

**Detecting sustain talk — don't argue, don't ignore:**
- Client: *"But it's probably too late."* → Wrong response: *"It's not too late! Here's why..."*
- Right response: *"Part of you worries that the window might have passed. Say more about that."* (then listen for the change-adjacent material that usually follows)

## Bottom Line

MI is the method for the gap between knowing and doing — the ambivalence every client carries but most coaching models skip past. You have been working around this gap with forward-focused questions. Learn the framework underneath those questions and you will stop accidentally amplifying the resistance you're trying to resolve.
