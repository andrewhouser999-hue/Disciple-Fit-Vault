# Narrative Therapy & Coaching — Re-Authoring Identity

## The Honest Assessment

Narrative Therapy (NT) was developed by Michael White and David Epston in Australia and New Zealand in the 1980s. It has a different epistemological base than CBT — it is postmodern and constructivist, meaning it does not assume there is one objective truth about a person that therapy uncovers. Instead, it holds that people *construct* their identity through the stories they tell about themselves, and that those stories are always partial, always emphasize certain events over others, and can always be re-told.

This epistemological difference makes some empirical researchers uncomfortable. The RCT base for Narrative Therapy is thinner than CBT, ACT, or MBCT. Know that going in. What NT offers is not primarily a clinical intervention with an RCT package — it is a radically client-honoring methodology for working with identity, meaning, and the stories people live from. For Kingdom coaching, where identity reconstruction is central work, this framework is arguably more useful per session hour than most of the evidence-based frameworks that study anxiety symptoms but don't touch who a person believes they are.

The gap to close: use NT's insights with the intellectual humility to know where the evidence base is thin, and integrate it with frameworks that have stronger empirical grounding (identity-based habits, self-efficacy, ACT values work).

## Elaborate Overview

### The Foundational Premise

People do not simply *have* experiences — they *narrate* them. The story a person tells about why something happened, what it means about them, and what it predicts for the future is not a neutral report. It is a construction. And constructions can be reconstructed.

Dan McAdams' (Northwestern University) narrative identity theory — grounded in developmental psychology with a substantial empirical base — operationalizes this: *personal identity is a psychobiographical story that a person constructs about their own life, giving it coherence, continuity, and purpose.* The story is not the sum of what happened. It is the interpretation of what happened — with heroes and villains, themes and turning points, and a theory about what kind of person the narrator is and what they can expect from the world.

The implications:
- The client is not an object to be assessed — they are an author who has been writing a story, often without awareness that they were making choices
- Problems are not internal defects — they are chapters in a story, and stories can be continued differently
- The coach's role is not to diagnose or fix — it is to help the client discover the story they're living from, evaluate it critically, and author a different one

### White & Epston's Core Techniques

#### 1. Externalizing the Problem

**The central move of Narrative Therapy.** The problem is separated linguistically from the person's identity. Instead of *"I am anxious"* (identity statement), the client is helped toward *"Anxiety has been telling me..."* (externalized problem with its own character and voice).

This is not semantic wordplay. It changes the relationship between the person and the problem. When the problem is *me*, I cannot step outside it, evaluate it, or choose to disagree with it. When the problem is *it* — something that has been operating in my life — I can examine what it tells me, whether those things are true, how long it has been around, what it takes from me, and what it would mean to refuse it.

**In practice:**
> *"Anxiety — let's give it a name if you'd like — has been giving you a very specific message before you speak in groups. What exactly does it say to you? How long has it been saying that? Has it been right? What has following its instructions cost you?"*

**Kingdom coaching application:** Sin, lies, and spiritual oppression can be externalized without diminishing personal responsibility. *"This shame narrative — this voice that says you're disqualified — where does it come from? When did you first hear it? Has it been telling you the truth?"* This is standard Kingdom coaching territory, now with a structural method.

---

#### 2. The Dominant Story and the Unique Outcome

**The dominant story** is the saturated narrative — the thick, problem-saturated story the person has come to identify with. *"I am someone who fails. I have always been someone who fails. I will probably continue to fail."*

**Unique outcomes** (White's term) are events that contradict the dominant story — moments when the problem *didn't* dominate, when the person acted against the story's prediction, when something different happened.

**The diagnostic question:** *"Has there ever been a time when [the problem] was less powerful? A moment when you acted differently than the story would predict?"*

Every person has these. The dominant story systematically excludes them — they are treated as flukes, exceptions, or "that doesn't count." The Narrative Therapy work is to find unique outcomes and build an *alternative story* that takes them seriously as evidence of a different kind of person.

**This is exactly what the Appreciate Inquiry's Discover phase does** — it is looking for the same thing (the best of what already exists) from a narrative framework.

---

#### 3. Re-Authoring

Once unique outcomes are identified, the work is *thickening* the alternative story — building it out with more examples, more detail, more history — until it becomes a viable narrative the client can live from. This is not positive thinking. It is the deliberate construction of a more complete story that includes what the dominant story left out.

**Questions for thickening:**
- *"What does it say about you that you did that? What does that suggest you value?"*
- *"Who else has seen this quality in you? How long have they known it was there?"*
- *"If you wrote the next chapter of your life from this quality rather than from the old story — what would it look like?"*

**The connection to Identity Installer:** The Identity Installer document is an applied re-authoring tool — replacing dominant lie-stories with identity statements built from alternative, biblical-sourced evidence. Narrative Therapy is the framework underneath it.

---

#### 4. The Absent but Implicit

One of White's more sophisticated contributions: when a person expresses a problem or pain, there is always something *implicit in the complaint* — a value, a preference, an expectation of what ought to be — that is *absent* from the current experience.

*"I feel like I don't matter to anyone"* implies *mattering to others matters to me* — which is a value. Exploring the absent implicit (what you're implicitly asserting by your complaint) surfaces the person's actual commitments and cares. These are the values ACT is trying to clarify through a different entry point.

**In practice:**
> *"When you say you feel like you don't matter — that feeling only makes sense if mattering to others is important to you. Is it? What does that tell you about who you are beneath the feeling?"*

### Dan McAdams — Narrative Identity Theory

McAdams' research (empirically grounded, not just theoretical) identifies several narrative identity components predictive of wellbeing:

**The contamination sequence:** A life narrative in which good things turn bad — early hope followed by defeat, promise followed by disappointment. Associated with lower wellbeing, depression, lower generativity.

**The redemption sequence:** A narrative in which bad things lead to good — suffering that produces growth, failure that opens doors, loss that generates meaning. Associated with higher wellbeing, resilience, and generativity.

**Critical finding:** These narrative patterns predict wellbeing *beyond personality traits.* How a person constructs the story of their life matters independently of what actually happened to them.

**Coaching implication:** The client who has experienced genuine suffering can tell it as a contamination sequence (*all that good led to nothing*) or as a redemption sequence (*that pain became the foundation for this*). Reality does not determine which story is told. Story-work matters.

**Kingdom connection:** This is the gospel narrative structure: death followed by resurrection, suffering followed by glory, loss followed by abundance. The resurrection is not just a theological event — it is the definitive pattern for how suffering transforms into redemption. Helping clients find the redemption sequence in their own story is Kingdom coaching at its most direct.

### Identity Foreclosure and Narrative Flexibility

Psychologist James Marcia (building on Erikson) identified *identity foreclosure* — a developmental state in which a person has adopted an identity without ever exploring alternatives. Many faith-background clients have foreclosed on a religious identity without ever critically examining it — which explains both rigidity and collapse when the identity is challenged.

Narrative Therapy opens narrative flexibility: the capacity to hold multiple possible stories about oneself without existential threat. A person with narrative flexibility can say *"I am this — and also that — and there are parts of me I haven't discovered yet"* without crisis.

|---|---|

| Redemption sequences predict wellbeing and generativity | McAdams et al. (2001); McAdams & Guo (2015) |
| Externalizing reduces problem-saturation in clients | White & Epston (1990) — practitioner literature; RCT evidence thin; process research supports |
| Contamination vs. redemption narrative profiles | McAdams, Diamond, de St. Aubin & Mansfield (1997) *Journal of Personality* |

*Note: NT's empirical base is thinner than CBT, ACT, or MBCT. The strongest research comes from McAdams' narrative identity program. Use NT techniques with intellectual honesty about the evidence level.*

## What to Ignore

- NT used to deny real events or suffering — externalizing is not a denial of responsibility or reality; it is a change in the relationship between the person and their experience
- Pure NT without empirical grounding — the postmodern epistemology can slide into "all stories are equal" relativism, which is incoherent for Kingdom coaching. Stories are evaluated against truth (Scripture, evidence, the Holy Spirit) — not just against what the client prefers
- NT as the primary clinical framework for trauma — narrative work on unprocessed trauma requires clinical training. Coaching-adapted narrative work is appropriate for identity and meaning, not for processing traumatic memory
- Confusing NT's "dominant story" with the psychoanalytic unconscious — they are different theoretical constructs operating differently

## Self-Directed Therapy/Coaching Questions

### Dominant Story Identification
- "What is the story you tell yourself most often about why things don't work out for you? What is its plot — what kind of person are you in it, and what keeps happening?"
- "When did that story start? What event first made you believe it might be true?"
- "What events or evidence does the story emphasize? What does it systematically leave out or explain away?"
- "If that story were a movie, what would the title be? What genre — tragedy, thriller, drama?"

### Unique Outcomes
- "When was a time that the story's prediction was wrong — you succeeded where the story said you would fail, or you were received where the story said you would be rejected?"
- "Are there people in your life who seem to hold a different story about you than the one you carry? What do they see? Why haven't you believed them?"
- "What qualities or capacities do you have that the dominant story has no room for? What happens when you try to include them?"

### Re-Authoring
- "If you wrote the next chapter of your life starting from the unique outcome rather than from the dominant story — what would it look like? Who would be the main character?"
- "What would you name this alternative story? What is its theme — what kind of person does it describe?"
- "Who in your life could witness and affirm this alternative story? What would it mean to tell it to them?"

### Redemption Sequence
- "Looking back at the hardest seasons of your life — has anything good come from them? Not that the suffering was good, but that something good grew in or through it?"
- "What has suffering taught you that comfort never could? What is different about who you are because of what you've been through?"
- "If the painful chapters of your story are not the end but the middle — what does the arc suggest about where things are going?"

## Coaching Application — In Session

**The narrative diagnostic at intake:**
> *"Before we talk about what you want to work on — tell me the story of how you got here. Not the logical summary — the story. What happened, what it meant, what you've concluded about yourself."*

Listen for: dominant themes, contamination sequences, who holds the narrative authority (is the client the protagonist or a passive subject?), and what evidence has been excluded.

**Externalizing in session — example:**
Client: *"I'm just someone who self-sabotages. I always do this."*

> *"Let's separate you from the self-sabotage for a moment. If self-sabotage were a voice — what does it tell you right before you do it? How long has it been part of your story? When did you first meet it? Has it been telling you the truth?"*

**Thickening the alternative story:**
Once a unique outcome is identified, spend time there. Don't move past it quickly:
> *"What does it tell you about yourself that you did that? Not luck, not accident — what did it take from you? Who were you in that moment?"*

Anchor the alternative story with specificity. Vague alternative stories don't hold. *"I'm actually capable"* doesn't survive the first setback. *"I am someone who stayed present under pressure on [specific occasion] and that is true of me"* is more durable.

**Narrative letters:** Adapted from Epston — at the end of a significant session, write the client a brief letter (or have them write one to themselves) naming the alternative story that emerged. This externalizes and formalizes what happened in the session and gives the client something tangible to carry.

## Bottom Line

Every client walks in carrying a story about who they are and why their life has gone the way it has. That story was written collaboratively — by experience, by what they were told, by what they chose to include and exclude. It is not fixed. Narrative Therapy gives you the methodology to work directly with that story — to find what it excludes, build what it's missing, and help the client become the author rather than the subject of their own life. For Kingdom coaching, where identity reconstruction is the primary work, this is not supplementary — it is the work.
