# Appreciative Inquiry Coaching

`[CURRICULUM]` `[SESSION-TOOL]`

Appreciative Inquiry (AI) is a strengths-based coaching methodology that begins from what is already working rather than what is broken. Originally developed for organizational change by Cooperrider and Whitney, it translates powerfully into individual coaching — particularly for shame-heavy, deficit-focused, or direction-seeking clients. This file covers its application as a coaching method.

---

## The Honest Assessment

AI is the most important corrective to the default deficit-bias in both therapy-informed coaching and much Christian discipleship work. Most helping frameworks begin with what is wrong and work toward fixing it. AI begins with what is genuinely present and builds forward. That is not avoidance — it is a different and often more effective entry point, especially for clients who have internalized shame so deeply that deficit-focused work activates defensiveness before it can reach anything real.

The limitation is real and worth naming: AI is not the right entry point for every client, and naive AI use can collude with avoidance. A client who is in pain needs to be heard in that pain before moving to strength. A client using strengths-focus to bypass examination of a damaging pattern is being enabled, not coached. AI creates the foundation that makes harder work possible — it is not a substitute for that harder work.

Evidence level: **Established** for organizational AI (Cooperrider & Whitney; meta-analytic support across organizational change contexts); **Supported** for individual coaching application (Orem, Binkert & Clancy, 2007; consistent with Broaden-and-Build theory — Fredrickson, 2001; positive psychology research base).

---

## Core Premise — The Positive Core

Every person has a positive core: genuine strengths, peak experiences, life-giving forces, and moments of real agency that are already present and repeatable. The coaching task is to discover and build from this core — not manufacture it.

**The biblical ground:** The positive core is not secular optimism and should not be presented as such. It is imago Dei made practical. God's image in the client is real and functional, even in broken people. There is something in every client that already reflects what God made them to be. AI gives the coach a systematic method for finding it.

This also maps to Philippians 4:8 — *"whatever is true, whatever is noble, whatever is right... think about such things."* Disciplined attention to what is genuinely good in a person's experience is not avoidance of hard truth; it is a spiritual practice and a coaching skill.

**The necessary caution:** AI without CBC and biblical discernment can collude with the client's natural tendency toward self-justification. The goal is clear-eyed appreciation — seeing what is genuinely there — not flattery designed to produce comfort. The positive core is real; it is not comprehensive.

## The 5-D Cycle

AI coaching is organized around a five-stage inquiry. Not all stages are used in every session — the cycle is a framework, not a script.

---

### 1. Define

**What it does:** Establishes the focus of inquiry. Not problem identification — purpose clarification. The orienting question is *"What do you want more of?"* rather than *"What do you want to fix?"*

**In session:**
- *"What aspect of your life do you most want to amplify or develop?"*
- *"If this coaching engagement were completely successful, what would you have more of?"*

---

### 2. Discover

**What it does:** Excavates the client's positive core — specific stories of peak experience, genuine agency, and existing strength. This is the heart of the AI method.

**The discipline:** Push for narrative specificity. *"Tell me about a specific time"* is essential. Vague positive statements (*"I've always been good with people"*) are not useful. Specific memories with observable detail are.

**Key questions:**
- *"Tell me about a time when you felt most alive, most effective, most yourself in this area. Take me there."*
- *"What was happening? What were you doing? Who was around you?"*
- *"What was it about that situation that allowed you to show up that way?"*
- *"What strengths were you drawing on — maybe without even realizing it?"*
- *"When is this problem NOT happening? What are you doing then?"*

The last question is the bridge between AI and Solution-Focused Coaching — finding the exception is the same move from different theoretical directions.

---

### 3. Dream

**What it does:** Invites the client to imagine a life where the conditions discovered in Discover are the norm rather than the exception.

This is generative and expansive — not goal-setting (that is GROW territory) but a rich, present-tense picture of a flourishing life built on actual strengths.

**Key questions:**
- *"If those peak moments were your everyday reality — not the exception but the norm — what would be different?"*
- *"What would you be doing? Who would you be with?"*
- *"Describe that life in present tense, as if you're already living it."*

---

### 4. Design

**What it does:** Identifies the specific conditions, structures, practices, and relationships that would make the Dream more consistently possible.

This is where AI becomes action-oriented. The Dream is the destination; Design is the architecture.

**Key questions:**
- *"What made your peak moments possible? What conditions, habits, or relationships enabled them?"*
- *"What would need to change in your current life to create more of those conditions?"*
- *"What structures would support this vision?"*

---

### 5. Destiny / Deploy

**What it does:** Converts the Design into a committed next step.

Without Destiny, AI is generative but not traction-producing. The commitment move is essential.

**Key questions:**
- *"What is one thing you will do this week to move toward what you've described?"*
- *"What is the smallest possible step that would still be real movement?"*

---

## Key Question Types

| Type | Purpose | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Generative** | Excavate peak experience | *"Tell me about a time you felt most fully yourself..."* |
| **Positive exception** | Find when the problem is absent | *"When is this NOT happening? What is different then?"* |
| **Strengths amplification** | Name what the client is already doing right | *"What strengths were you drawing on in that moment?"* |
| **Dream invitation** | Expand into desired future | *"If this were normal rather than exceptional, what changes?"* |
| **Condition analysis** | Find what made the peak replicable | *"What made that moment possible?"* |

## When to Use AI

**Strong use cases:**
- Client is stuck in deficit thinking and cannot access forward movement
- Shame-heavy client who shuts down under problem-focus or direct confrontation
- Early in an engagement — building hope and agency before wound work begins
- Client knows they are dissatisfied but cannot name what they want (direction-seeking)
- Vocation and calling discernment — discovering what God has already built into the client
- Following a difficult season — helping the client locate what survived and what they learned

**Use with caution or sequence carefully:**
- Client who needs to be heard in pain first — hear before appreciating
- Client using strengths-focus to avoid examining a damaging pattern — name the avoidance
- Client in active crisis — stabilize before using AI

**Not a substitute for:**
- Direct confrontation of patterns causing genuine damage
- MI work when ambivalence is the real presenting issue
- Grief and lament work when loss needs to be honored before movement

## Distinction from Solution-Focused Coaching

Both AI and SF coaching are strengths-based and forward-oriented. The practical distinction:

| | Appreciative Inquiry | Solution-Focused |
|---|---|---|
| **Entry point** | Client's positive core — who they are at their best | Preferred future — what they want |
| **Primary question** | *"When were you most fully yourself?"* | *"What would be different if the problem were solved?"* |
| **Builds from** | Existing identity and strength | Imagined preferred future |
| **Best for** | Identity work, shame-heavy clients, calling discernment | Clients with a clearer sense of desired outcome |

Both are useful. AI is the deeper identity-formation tool. SF is often faster for goal-focused work. They are complementary, not competing.

## Biblical Integration

**Imago Dei as the ground:** The positive core is not self-esteem scaffolding. It is the recognition that God's image in a person is real, present, and discoverable. The coach operating from AI posture is cooperating with what God has already placed in the client — not manufacturing something new.

**Creation goodness:** God declared creation *"good"* before the fall (Gen 1). That goodness — distorted but not destroyed — is still present in human beings. AI coaching is, in part, the practice of seeing clearly what is genuinely there.

**Appreciation as covenant practice:** The biblical tradition holds honest assessment of brokenness (Lament, Prophets) and genuine celebration of what God has done (Psalms, Philippians) together. AI is not the whole of formation — but it is a legitimate and often neglected half of the picture.

**The prophetic dimension:** When the coach names a client's positive core back to them — the strength they couldn't see, the gift they dismissed — this is a prophetic act. It names what God has placed there, even when the client has explained it away as luck or irrelevance.

---

### Without Biblical Framework — Christian Client

**Verdict: Fixable.** The source and orientation of strengths are the two specific gaps.

Without biblical framing, the source of the client's positive core is left ambiguous — is it personal virtue, or is it grace? For a Christian, this distinction matters for humility, gratitude, and proper credit. AI coaching that celebrates strengths without naming them as gift can subtly reinforce the self as the primary resource.

More seriously: AI can make a client more confidently self-directed by reinforcing what they're already good at, without asking whether those strengths are being oriented toward God's purposes. A charismatic natural leader built up through AI coaching with no biblical framework may become more skillfully self-centered rather than more submitted to God's calling.

**Fix:** Frame strengths as gift and stewardship, not personal asset. The Dream and Design stages need a discernment layer: *"Is this vision oriented toward God's purposes, or toward the client's preferred version of themselves?"*

---

## Bottom Line

AI is a necessary corrective to deficit-first coaching in a faith-based context. Shame-heavy clients and direction-seeking clients often need to discover what is genuinely present before they can receive confrontation of what is not. The Discover stage is the heart of the method — the quality of the question determines the quality of what surfaces. Use AI to build the foundation that makes deeper work possible.
