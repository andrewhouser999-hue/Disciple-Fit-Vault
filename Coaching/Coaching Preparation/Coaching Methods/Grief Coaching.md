# Grief Coaching

`[CURRICULUM]`

## The Honest Assessment

Grief coaching is a legitimate, distinct practice — and the clinical line matters. Most grieving people do not need therapy. George Bonanno's longitudinal research established that roughly 65% of bereaved individuals follow a resilience trajectory without clinical intervention. The coaching population is the majority, not the exception. What they need is not pathology treatment — they need a knowledgeable, steady presence that can hold loss without rushing toward resolution, and a framework for meaning-making that doesn't require them to "get over it."

The clinical line is clear: Prolonged Grief Disorder (persistent intense grief causing significant functional impairment beyond 12 months, added to DSM-5-TR in 2022), grief entangled with trauma, and grief presenting with suicidal ideation all require licensed treatment. Everything else — including the vast majority of loss experience — is appropriately served by informed coaching.

The most common coaching failure in grief work is not going too deep. It is rushing too quickly toward resolution, reframe, and meaning-making before the loss has been adequately honored. The coach who cannot sit quietly in the weight of someone's loss will consistently underserve grieving clients.

Evidence quality: **Established** for grief resilience trajectories and meaning-making as the primary long-term integration mechanism. **Supported** for grief coaching specifically — direct coaching RCTs are limited, but the task-based and companioning frameworks used in coaching are empirically grounded.

## Elaborate Overview

### What Grief Actually Is — Definitional Breadth

The cultural default is to limit grief to death. This is far too narrow. Grief is the natural human response to any significant loss — and a coaching practice that only recognizes bereavement will miss the majority of grief presenting in sessions.

**Types of loss that generate genuine grief:**
- *Death* — the most socially recognized form; primary grief
- *Relationship loss* — divorce, estrangement, friendship rupture
- *Role loss* — job loss, retirement, end of a parenting season, loss of health
- *Identity loss* — the loss of a self-conception no longer sustainable (who I was, what I was building toward)
- *Dream loss* — infertility, career ambitions that didn't materialize, a vision that didn't come to pass
- *Ambiguous loss* — losses without resolution or social acknowledgment (estrangement, dementia of loved one, miscarriage, a relationship that faded without ending)
- *Disenfranchised grief* — loss that is not socially validated (loss of a pet, miscarriage, loss of a pregnancy, grief over a relationship that wasn't "official")

Naming the loss accurately — including its type and the client's right to grieve it — is often the first substantive coaching work.

---

### The Stage Model — What to Keep and What to Correct

Kübler-Ross's five stages (denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance) are among the most widely cited and most widely misunderstood findings in psychology. Kübler-Ross herself clarified they are not a linear sequence, not a prescription, and were originally observed in terminal patients — not in bereaved survivors.

**What the stages do well:** They normalize the range of emotional experiences in grief. They give grievers language for what they are experiencing. They make grief visible as having dimensions.

**What the stages fail to do:** They imply a sequence and a destination. The implicit message — that acceptance is the goal and grief moves progressively toward it — is not empirically supported and creates harm. Clients who "jump" stages feel defective; clients who return to earlier emotional states feel they are failing.

Bonanno's research (2004, 2009) is the corrective: grief trajectories are highly variable. Most people are resilient. Some experience delayed grief. Some experience chronic grief (the clinical population). The stage model describes *some* experiences some of the time — it does not describe a universal sequence.

The "grief work" hypothesis — that one must actively process loss or suffer consequences — is also not empirically supported as universal. Some people integrate loss without intensive processing. Prescribing grief work to naturally resilient grievers can actually slow their recovery.

---

### Worden's Four Tasks Model — The Coach's Framework

William Worden's task-based model is the most useful for coaching because it implies agency rather than passive progression through states. Tasks can be supported, measured, and returned to — which maps well onto coaching sessions.

**Task 1 — Accept the reality of the loss**
Both cognitive and emotional acceptance. The cognitive acceptance often comes first; emotional acceptance follows, sometimes much later. A client may know intellectually that a marriage is over while still not having accepted it emotionally. Both dimensions need attention.

**Task 2 — Work through the pain of grief**
Sitting in the pain rather than bypassing it through activity, numbing, or forced positivity. The coach's job here is to create a container wide enough to hold the pain without fixing it. The temptation to reframe, silver-lining, or problem-solve is strong and almost always premature.

**Task 3 — Adjust to a world in which the lost person or thing is absent**
This involves multiple simultaneous adjustments: external (what actually changes in the client's life), internal (who am I now without this?), and existential (what does this loss say about life, meaning, and God?). This is often where identity work becomes central.

**Task 4 — Find an enduring connection with what was lost while embarking on a new life**
This is Worden's most significant revision from older grief models. The goal is not *detachment* from the lost person or thing — it is *integration*. The relationship continues in transformed form: the person is remembered, honored, and carried forward even as new life is built. This is the continuing bonds model, and it is the empirically supported alternative to the older "letting go" prescription.

### Meaning-Making — The Most Empirically Supported Mechanism

Crystal Park's (2010) meaning-making model is the strongest empirically grounded framework for long-term grief integration. Two types of meaning are at work:

**Global meaning:** The client's existing beliefs, goals, and life narrative — including faith, worldview, and sense of identity. This is the structure within which all experience is interpreted.

**Situational meaning:** What *this specific loss* means — how it fits (or doesn't fit) into global meaning.

When the loss violates global meaning — a death that contradicts a faith conviction, a suffering that breaks a previous worldview — the grief becomes existential as well as emotional. The meaning-making work is more significant, slower, and cannot be artificially accelerated.

Kessler's sixth stage (*meaning*) extends Kübler-Ross's model in this direction: not that meaning is found in loss as a given, but that meaning can be actively built *from* loss — as legacy, as growth, as reorientation. The coach's role is not to manufacture meaning on the client's behalf. It is to create the conditions in which the client can discover or construct it.

---

### The Companioning Model — Grief Coaching's Posture

Alan Wolfelt's companioning model establishes the posture distinction between grief therapy and grief coaching more clearly than any other framework. The companioning approach:
- Follows the griever's lead rather than directing the process
- Prioritizes presence over technique
- Treats the mourner as the expert on their own grief
- Defines the coach's role as *witness* and *companion*, not *fixer*

This is not a technique set — it is a relational posture. A coach who holds the technique of meaning-making but has a rushed internal relationship to grief will consistently underserve clients. The Wolfelt model is a formation framework as much as a skill framework.

---

### Secondary and Ambiguous Loss

**Secondary loss:** Every primary loss carries secondary losses — losses generated by the primary loss. A divorce is also the loss of the anticipated future, the shared social network, the home, the daily routines, the identity as a spouse. A job loss is also the loss of purpose, structure, professional identity, and financial security. Coaching that only attends to the primary loss misses the majority of what the client is actually grieving.

**Ambiguous loss** (Pauline Boss, 1999): losses that lack resolution or social recognition. Estrangement from a living family member, caring for someone with dementia, infertility, pregnancy loss, a relationship that ends without a clear ending, the loss of a dream without a defined moment of failure. These losses are frequently disenfranchised — society doesn't provide rituals or language for them, so grievers often feel they have no right to grieve. Naming the legitimacy of the loss is often the primary coaching intervention.

## What to Ignore

- **The five stages as a prescription.** They describe experiences; they do not prescribe a sequence or a destination. A coach who tracks clients against the stages will consistently pathologize natural resilience and pressure unnecessary processing.
- **Pressure toward resolution, acceptance, or "moving on."** These are almost always the coach's discomfort with sustained loss projected onto the client as an objective. The client's pace is the client's pace.
- **The grief work assumption applied universally.** Not all grievers need intensive processing. Bonanno's research is clear: naturally resilient grievers do not benefit from prescriptive grief work and may be slowed by it. Read the client, not the protocol.
- **Attempting to manufacture meaning prematurely.** Kessler's sixth stage is something that *emerges* with time — it is not a reframe the coach applies to reduce discomfort with the client's pain. Premature meaning-making is an invalidation.
- **Coaching grief entangled with clinical conditions.** Prolonged Grief Disorder, grief complicated by trauma, grief with suicidal ideation — all require clinical treatment. Recognize the boundary and refer without delay.

## Coach-Safe Scope

**What a life coach CAN do with this modality:**
- Support non-clinical grief across all loss types: death, relationship, role, identity, dream, ambiguous, disenfranchised
- Create a container where loss is named and honored without pressure toward resolution
- Use Worden's Four Tasks as a coaching framework for grief navigation
- Support meaning-making work — creating conditions for meaning to emerge, not manufacturing it
- Use identity questions to help the client navigate who they are now that this is gone
- Work with secondary loss — naming what else has been lost alongside the primary loss
- Name ambiguous and disenfranchised grief as legitimate and worthy of attention
- Use the biblical lament framework as a structure for grief before God (for faith clients)
- Recommend client-facing grief reading as between-session resources

**What requires clinical training:**
- Treatment of Prolonged Grief Disorder (DSM-5-TR diagnosis; clinical intervention required)
- Grief entangled with complex trauma or PTSD
- Grief presenting with clinical depression requiring psychiatric or therapeutic treatment
- Any presenting suicidal ideation — immediate safety protocol required

**Referral indicator:**
Grief that significantly impairs functioning beyond 12 months warrants clinical assessment for Prolonged Grief Disorder. Grief entangled with depression, trauma, or substance use requires referral to a licensed mental health professional. Any expression of suicidal ideation — however passive — requires an immediate referral conversation and safety assessment before coaching continues.

## Self-Directed Therapy/Coaching Questions

### Naming the Loss
- "What, exactly, have you lost? Include everything — not just the primary loss, but every secondary loss it has brought."
- "Does this loss feel like it has the right to be grieved? Have you given yourself permission to grieve it fully?"
- "How long have you been carrying this? When did you first let yourself feel the full weight of it?"

### Working Through Pain (Task 2)
- "What does this grief feel like when you let yourself actually feel it? What do you usually do to avoid that?"
- "What would it mean to sit in this without trying to fix it, reframe it, or find the silver lining?"
- "Is there something you've been afraid to say out loud about this loss?"

### Adjusting and Identity (Task 3)
- "Who are you now that this is gone? How has this loss changed your sense of yourself?"
- "What has this loss revealed about what you most valued? What does that tell you about what matters going forward?"
- "What still makes sense in your life? What no longer fits?"

### Meaning and Continuing Bonds (Task 4 / Kessler)
- "What do you want this loss to mean — not what it has to mean, but what you want to be true about what you do with it?"
- "What did this person/relationship/dream bring into your life that you want to carry forward, even now that it's gone?"
- "If this grief has taught you anything — about yourself, about what matters, about God — what is it?"

## Coaching Application — In Session

**Sequencing principle:** Do not move toward Tasks 3 or 4 until Tasks 1 and 2 have been adequately served. Naming the loss and sitting in the pain are not preliminary — they are often the primary work of grief coaching sessions.

**The entry:**
*"I want to understand what you've lost — all of it. Not just [primary loss], but everything else this has taken. Tell me what's gone."*

Let the client name the full scope. Do not editorialize, reframe, or silver-lining during this phase. The task is to witness.

**Normalizing and honoring:**
*"That's a genuine loss — and you have every right to grieve it. I'm not going to rush you past this."*

For ambiguous or disenfranchised grief:
*"Sometimes the losses that don't get socially recognized are the hardest to carry — because you feel like you're supposed to be fine. You're not required to be fine about this."*

**When to introduce meaning:**
Only after the loss has been adequately named and the pain has been honored. Kessler's timing is important: meaning is a sixth stage because it comes after the others have been lived through, not instead of them.
*"You've been in this for [time]. I'm not asking you to be over it. But I'm curious — is there anything in you that's beginning to notice what this is doing, forming, or revealing? Not silver lining — just what you're learning about yourself through it?"*

**Biblical lament for faith clients:**
*"The psalms of lament model something powerful — they don't explain the suffering or rush past it. They just bring it directly to God: 'Where are you? This is what it costs. I'm still here, but this is the truth of it.' Is there something you need to say to God about this that you've been holding back?"*

**Tracking movement:**
*"Grief doesn't move in a straight line. Is it moving — even slowly? Or does it feel stuck? There's no right answer."*

If stuck: probe for what is anchoring it — unresolved secondary loss, meaning violation, lack of permission, or clinical complexity that warrants referral.

## Bottom Line

Grief coaching earns its place because most grieving people are not clinical — they are human. They need a knowledgeable, steady presence that can hold loss without rushing toward resolution and a framework for meaning-making that honors the real weight of what has been lost. The coach who has internalized the lament tradition and Worden's task model — and who can sit in someone's grief without needing to fix it — offers something genuinely rare in a culture that is systematically uncomfortable with loss.
