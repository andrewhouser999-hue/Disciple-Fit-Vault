# Keeping Eyes on Jesus — Practice Reference Card

*You do not need to practice all of these. Your coach will identify 1-2 starting points based on your primary gap. This card is a keeper — add to it as your practice grows.*

---
**The beholding practice as session tool:**
Guided attention to specific aspects of Christ's character — not just as aspiration but as identity origin point — is *underused in coaching*. The question isn't "How can I be more patient?" but "What would it look like to behold Christ's patience long enough for it to begin forming in me?"

## Understanding the Map

Each practice addresses a specific gap in attentional formation. The gap names come from Scripture:

| Gap | Scripture Root | What It Means |
|---|---|---|
| Turn away from | *aphorao* — Heb 12:2 | Clearing the field before orienting toward God |
| Beholding depth | *katoptrizomenoi* — 2 Cor 3:18 | Sustained gaze long enough for transformation to occur |
| Receiving the gaze | *panim* — Num 6, Ps 27 | The gaze is first received, then returned |
| Counter-narrative | *distazō* — Matt 14:31 | Countering the waves' interpretive authority with Scripture |
| Return reflex | The fundamental unit | Practicing the return until it becomes the soul's reflex |
| Attentional restoration | Sabbath rhythm | Clearing accumulated wave-narrative weekly |
| Accountability | Formation community | Others who can see what you cannot |

## The Practices

---

### Silence and Solitude
**Gap addressed:** Turn away from
**What it is:** Removing competing inputs so the attentional field clears enough to orient toward God. Solitude is not absence of people — it is removal of noise. 10-15 minutes before any other morning practice with no phone, music, or podcast. Let the noise settle. This is the *aphorao* movement — the "away from" — before the gaze can happen. You cannot add God to a saturated field; you have to clear it first.
**How to start:** Set your alarm 15 minutes earlier. No input of any kind for those 15 minutes. Sit. Be still. Nothing else required yet.

---

### Fasting
**Gap addressed:** Turn away from
**What it is:** Removing something that routinely commands your attention to expose how much weight you've been giving it — and using the resulting hunger or discomfort as a trigger to return to God rather than to the usual comfort object. Fasting from food is the classic form, but media, screens, and comfort behaviors can be fasted as well. Fasting doesn't build willpower. It reveals what has been governing your attention and interrupts it.
**How to start:** One meal fast, once a week. When hunger comes, instead of eating or distracting, pause and return briefly to God. Notice what it reveals about where your attention has been living.

---

### Daily Examen
**Gap addressed:** Turn away from
**What it is:** An end-of-day review of where your attention *actually* lived — not where you intended it. Two questions: When today were you drawn toward God (consolation)? When were you pulled away (desolation)? This builds meta-awareness — the capacity to notice where your attention is — which is prerequisite for returning it deliberately.
**How to start:** Five minutes before bed. No journal required to start. Just honest review: one consolation and one desolation from today.

---

### Lectio Divina
**Gap addressed:** Beholding depth
**What it is:** Slow, ruminative Scripture reading with four movements: (1) *Lectio* — read slowly until a word or phrase stops you; (2) *Meditatio* — repeat it, let it work, no rush toward interpretation; (3) *Oratio* — respond to God from what surfaced; (4) *Contemplatio* — rest in the presence without words. Speed is the enemy of beholding. The formation happens in the dwell, not in the accumulation of content.
**How to start:** Pick 4-6 verses. Read them slowly three times. Stay in *Meditatio* longer than feels productive — this is where most people give up too early. 20 minutes minimum.

---

### Extended Worship
**Gap addressed:** Beholding depth
**What it is:** Sustained engagement with God's character and beauty through music or song long enough for the affective response to shift. Not performance or emotional stimulation — creating conditions for desire to be awakened. Jonathan Edwards: transformation requires awakened desire, and desire is awakened by beauty *encountered*, not beauty catalogued. Ten minutes is orientation. Thirty minutes is sometimes where beholding begins.
**How to start:** One session per week, 30 minutes minimum, alone. Choose music that is theologically substantive. Let it go long enough to get past restlessness — the restlessness is normal, not a sign to stop.

---

### Receiving Prayer
**Gap addressed:** Receiving the covenant gaze
**What it is:** Beginning prayer by sitting, not speaking. Remaining in the awareness that you are being looked at, not looking. This directly addresses the performance-oriented stance that makes sustained attention exhausting. The covenant reality is that the gaze is first His toward you. You return what He has already given. Brennan Manning: the problem is not inability to look at Jesus — it is failure to believe He is already looking at you.
**How to start:** Sit for 5-10 minutes with one phrase: "Abba, I belong to you." Not as petition. As reality being received. Let it land without immediately responding with requests or analysis.

---

### Confession
**Gap addressed:** Receiving the covenant gaze
**What it is:** Regular, honest acknowledgment of sin — not as self-punishment but as attentional hygiene. Unconfessed sin creates a functional conviction that God's face is turned away, which blocks the received gaze regardless of theological accuracy in the moment. Confession clears the obstruction and restores the experienced reality of being seen and loved. It is attentional hygiene, not just moral hygiene.
**How to start:** At the end of the Daily Examen, add one honest moment of confession. Then receive absolution — *"There is therefore now no condemnation"* (Romans 8:1) — not as a formula but as a fact being accepted.

---

### Scripture Memorization
**Gap addressed:** Counter-narrative
**What it is:** Committing Scripture to memory not for information retention but for narrative saturation. Memorized Scripture is in the same real-time cognitive space as the wave-narrative. When circumstances are screaming a counter-account, a memorized passage is immediately available — not abstract and distant, but present and accessible. This is tactical, not academic. The reason waves win the interpretive battle in crisis is that the physical evidence is immediate and sensory. Memorized Scripture meets it in the same space.
**How to start:** One passage fully committed before adding another. Suggested starting points: Psalm 23, Isaiah 43:1-3, Romans 8:38-39. Write it on a card. Review morning and night. Use it intentionally when waves appear.

---

### Preaching to Yourself
**Gap addressed:** Counter-narrative
**What it is:** Intentionally speaking the true account of reality to your own soul rather than passively receiving what anxiety broadcasts. Martyn Lloyd-Jones: *"The main art in spiritual living is to know how to handle yourself. You have to take yourself in hand, address yourself, preach to yourself, question yourself."* The Psalms of lament model this — the psalmist argues deliberately against his own despair. The alternative is listening to yourself — which under stress means listening to the wave-narrative.
**How to start:** When you notice the wave-narrative running, stop and ask: "What is actually true here?" Then speak it aloud if possible. Use your memorized Scripture. This takes 60 seconds. Do it deliberately every time you catch yourself listening to the waves.

---

### The Daily Office / Fixed-Hour Prayer
**Gap addressed:** Counter-narrative
**What it is:** Structured prayer at fixed points throughout the day — typically 3-4 times. Not as legalism but as a structural counter-narrative interruption. Every few hours, stop and reorient to God. The purpose: prevent any extended stretch of waking life from being fully governed by the wave-narrative without interruption. It fragments the wave's authority before it accumulates. Resources: Northumbria Community's *Celtic Daily Prayer* or the *Book of Common Prayer*.
**How to start:** Set two phone alarms — midmorning and midafternoon. At each alarm, stop for 2-3 minutes. Use a fixed prayer or simply re-acknowledge God's presence and authority over your day.

---

### The Jesus Prayer
**Gap addressed:** Return reflex
**What it is:** *"Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner."* From the Eastern Christian hesychast tradition. Practiced on the breath — inhale: "Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God"; exhale: "have mercy on me, a sinner." The goal over time is for the prayer to become semi-automatic, running beneath conscious activity as an undercurrent of God-orientation while you move through ordinary tasks. Not replacing engagement with the world — providing an anchor line to Jesus within it.
**How to start:** Practice it during one specific daily activity — commuting, washing dishes, walking. Say it on the breath. Don't force it. Let it become natural in that one context first before expanding.

---

### Breath Prayer
**Gap addressed:** Return reflex
**What it is:** A short, personalized prayer of 6-8 syllables practiced on the breath — inhale phrase, exhale phrase. Examples: "Be still and know / that you are God." "You are with me / I am not alone." "Jesus, I trust / you with this." Practiced during transitions, stress, waiting — the moments when the waves most commonly appear. The most portable formation practice — no time block, no environment, no material required. It is available exactly when the waves hit.
**How to start:** Choose or write your breath prayer. Practice it for one week during one specific transition (e.g., every time you get in the car or walk into a meeting). Then expand.

---

### Centering Prayer
**Gap addressed:** Return reflex
**What it is:** Choose a sacred word as the symbol of your consent to God's presence and action (e.g., "Jesus," "Abba," "Peace," "Come"). Sit in silence. When you notice your attention has wandered to any thought, gently return to the sacred word. That is the entire practice. Thomas Keating: the frequency of distraction and return is not a sign of failure — it *is* the exercise. Each return is one repetition of the exact attentional motion you need when waves come. 20 minutes, ideally twice daily.
**How to start:** 10-15 minutes, once daily. Set a timer. Choose your word. Return whenever you drift. Do not evaluate the session by how often you drifted — evaluate it by whether you returned.

---

### Frank Laubach's Game with Minutes
**Gap addressed:** Return reflex
**What it is:** A structured experiment in maintaining God-awareness across the day. Set hourly phone prompts. At each prompt, ask: Did I return to conscious God-awareness at least once this past hour? Not sustained — just once. Track it without guilt. Frank Laubach ran this experiment for months and documented both the failures and the gradual deepening. The structure prevents the day from fully disappearing into unconscious wave-focus. The tracking is not performance measurement — it is noticing.
**How to start:** Set 4-6 hourly alarms. At each one, simply notice: Was God in my awareness in the past hour? Yes or no. No guilt attached. Just noticing. The noticing is the practice.

---

### Sabbath
**Gap addressed:** Attentional restoration
**What it is:** One day each week structurally removed from the systems that fragment attention — work demands, productivity, screens. Not a productive rest day. A stop. The week's accumulated wave-narrative needs a full-day interrupt. Walter Brueggemann: Sabbath is resistance. It declares that the wave-narrative does not govern time. The cleared field of Sabbath is also the space where extended beholding becomes possible in a way it rarely does during the week.
**How to start:** Identify one day or half-day and protect it. Define what "stop" means for you specifically — what comes off, what comes on. Keep it simple. Begin the practice before perfecting the theology of it.

---

### Extended Prayer Block
**Gap addressed:** Attentional restoration
**What it is:** 2-3 hours weekly of unhurried, unscheduled time alone with God. Not Bible study. Not journaling necessarily. Extended dwell time — the beholding that goes long enough to get past the restlessness and into actual encounter. The formation that happens in long, slow sessions cannot be replicated by accumulated short sessions. This is the weekly depth session that sustains the daily practices.
**How to start:** Protect one morning per week. No agenda. Bring your Bible. Sit. Let it be unproductive if it needs to be at first. Stay for the full time regardless.

---

### Formation Community
**Gap addressed:** Accountability
**What it is:** At least one relationship in which you have given another person explicit permission to name what they observe in your spiritual life — including when you are looking at the waves. This is not a prayer partner or a friend who listens supportively. It is someone who knows your actual state and has been given both the role and the explicit permission to speak into it. You cannot maintain the gaze in isolation long-term because you lose the ability to see when you've shifted. You need someone who can see it.
**How to start:** Identify one person. Have a direct conversation: "I want to be honest about where I actually am spiritually, and I want to give you permission to tell me what you see." That conversation is the beginning.

---

## The Fundamental Motion

All of these practices share one thing: they build the capacity for **the return.**

You will drift. The waves will appear. The wave-narrative will make its claim.

The practice is not sustained, unbroken attention. It is the return — noticing you've drifted, and coming back. Again and again. Without guilt. Without delay.

Every return in the calm is a repetition of the motion you will need in the storm. Build the groove now.

*"Immediately Jesus reached out his hand and caught him." — Matthew 14:31*
