# CBT-Based Tools

---

## 1. Thought Record

**Category:** CBT
**Evidence Base:** Fact — core CBT tool, decades of RCT validation
**When to Use:** Sessions 1–3 when a presenting problem surfaces; also as between-session homework throughout engagement

**How to Run:**
1. Identify a recent situation where the client felt stuck, reactive, or defeated
2. Walk through 5 columns on paper:
   - **Situation** — what happened, facts only
   - **Automatic Thought** — what did your mind say instantly?
   - **Emotion** — what did you feel? Rate intensity 0–100
   - **Evidence For / Against** — what is actually true?
   - **Balanced Thought** — what is a more accurate statement?
3. After completing it, ask: *"What shifts when you read the balanced thought?"*

**Biblical Integration:**
Column 4 (Evidence Against) IS the renewing of the mind (Rom 12:2). The automatic thought is the lie candidate. The balanced thought becomes the declaration anchor. Feed directly into the Identity Installer — the Balanced Thought in column 5 becomes the raw material for the Declaration field.

## 2. Cognitive Restructuring

**Category:** CBT
**Evidence Base:** Fact — core CBT, RCT-supported
**When to Use:** Immediately following Thought Record work; also when a client keeps circling back to the same narrative

**How to Run:**
1. Take the automatic thought identified in the Thought Record
2. Identify the distortion category (catastrophizing, all-or-nothing, mind reading, personalization, etc.)
3. Ask: *"If a trusted friend believed this about themselves, what would you tell them?"*
4. Build the replacement thought together — specific, believable, grounded in evidence
5. Have client say it aloud three times; note their body response

**Biblical Integration:**
Pair the restructured thought with a specific Scripture. The replacement thought is not just positive psychology — it is what God says is true. This is where the Lie Eliminator format (Lie → Declaration → Scripture Arsenal) sits precisely. Cognitive restructuring provides the clinical mechanism; Scripture provides the eternal anchor.

## 3. Behavioral Activation

**Category:** CBT
**Evidence Base:** Fact — strong evidence, particularly for depression and behavior change
**When to Use:** Mid-coaching arc, after identity work has begun; when a client knows what they should do but is not doing it

**How to Run:**
1. Identify what the client is avoiding (often tied to fear, shame, or hopelessness)
2. Ask: *"If you were living as the person God made you to be, what would you be doing that you are not doing now?"*
3. Schedule one concrete, small, specific action this week — name the day, time, and duration
4. Remove barriers in advance: *"What would stop you? Let's solve that now."*
5. Review at next session — not whether they felt like doing it, but whether they did it

**Biblical Integration:**
This is the "Put On" side of Eph 4:24. Identity is confirmed through embodied action, not belief alone. Frame it: *"We are not trying to feel differently first — we are acting our way into the person God already says you are."* Action precedes motivation; this is both CBT science and Kingdom obedience.

## 4. Socratic Questioning

**Category:** CBT
**Evidence Base:** Fact — core CBT technique, well-validated across therapy and coaching
**When to Use:** Every session — this is not a tool deployed situationally; it is the baseline mode of coaching conversation

**How to Run:**

Core question types:
- **Clarifying:** *"What do you mean when you say...?"*
- **Probing assumptions:** *"What are you assuming here?"*
- **Evidence probing:** *"What makes you believe that is true?"*
- **Perspective shifting:** *"How would someone who loves you describe this situation?"*
- **Implication questions:** *"If that is true, what follows?"*
- **Meta questions:** *"Why do you think you keep coming back to this?"*

The rule: never answer your own question. Sit in silence. The discomfort is the work.

**Biblical Integration:**
Jesus used Socratic questioning consistently (*"Who do you say I am?" / "Which of the two did the father's will?"*). The coach's posture is to ask what the Spirit is already surfacing — not to install new information but to draw out what God has already placed in the client.

## 5. Core Beliefs Inventory (Schema Work)

**Category:** CBT — Schema Therapy
**Evidence Base:** Fact — validated in CBT and schema therapy context (Young's Schema Questionnaire)
**When to Use:** Sessions 3–5, after rapport is established; when surface-level goals keep getting undermined by the same pattern

**How to Run:**
1. Use a published core beliefs checklist (Young's Schema Questionnaire is the gold standard; simplified versions are available)
2. Client rates how much each belief applies to them (e.g., *"I am fundamentally defective," "I am unlovable," "The world is dangerous"*)
3. In session, identify clusters — which schemas repeat?
4. Ask: *"When is the first time you remember believing that about yourself?"*
5. Trace the chain: origin event → belief formed → current behavior it produces

**Biblical Integration:**
Core beliefs are the deepest soil layer. They predate conversion and often persist post-conversion because they were formed experientially, not propositionally. This is the Lie Nuke diagnostic — a core belief is a lie so embedded it feels like identity itself. The 7-part Identity format (Identity Statement → Anchor Verse → My Evidence → Putting Off → Putting On → Felt Sense → Today I Will) is the restructuring tool for this level of work.

## 6. Values Clarification (ACT)

**Category:** CBT / Acceptance and Commitment Therapy
**Evidence Base:** Fact — ACT has a strong evidence base; values work is central to the ACT model
**When to Use:** Sessions 2–3; excellent diagnostic for establishing coaching direction

**How to Run:**
1. Give client a values list (50–100 words: courage, connection, faith, integrity, freedom, etc.)
2. Have them select top 10, then narrow to top 5
3. For each top value, ask: *"On a scale of 1–10, how consistently does your current life reflect this value?"*
4. Surface the gap: *"You rated 'faith' a 10 in importance but a 3 in consistency. What is in the way?"*
5. That gap is the coaching work

**Biblical Integration:**
Values are conscience-level convictions. When lived values contradict stated values, that is the double-minded man (James 1:8). The ACT framework calls the barrier "experiential avoidance" — in the Disciple Fit framework that is secondary gain or agreement with a lie. Same territory, different language. Use ACT to surface it; use Biblical framework to name and address it.

## 7. Wheel of Life — Biblical Version

**Category:** Diagnostic — Whole-Person Assessment
**Evidence Base:** Strong inference — widely used, limited direct empirical validation; sport psychology literature supports self-assessment tools for awareness and motivation
**When to Use:** Sessions 1–2 as baseline intake; revisit at midpoint and close of engagement

**How to Run:**
1. Draw or print a circle divided into 8 spokes with these labels:
   - Spirit / Communion with God
   - Soul — Mind (thoughts, beliefs)
   - Soul — Emotions (processing, health)
   - Body (physical health, rest, energy)
   - Relationships (family, marriage, community)
   - Vocation / Calling
   - Resources (finances, time, stewardship)
   - Church / Body of Christ
2. Client rates each area 1–10 from center outward
3. Connect the dots — the resulting shape shows the "ride" of their life
4. Ask: *"What do you notice?"* and *"Which area, if improved, would most impact the others?"*
5. That answer sets the coaching focus

**Biblical Integration:**
Shalom is whole-person flourishing. An uneven wheel is a shalom diagnostic — it reveals where the enemy has leveraged a foothold, where wounding is active, and where spiritual formation is stalled. The 8-spoke Biblical version maps directly onto the spirit/soul/body framework (1 Thess 5:23) and expands it into relational and vocational domains.

## 8. Genogram

**Category:** Diagnostic — Family Systems
**Evidence Base:** Fact — Murray Bowen's family systems theory; strong evidence base in family therapy
**When to Use:** Sessions 3–5, after trust is established; use when presenting issues seem to have roots the client cannot explain

**How to Run:**
1. Draw client, parents, and grandparents using standard symbols (squares = male, circles = female, horizontal lines = marriage, vertical lines = children)
2. Add: marriages, divorces, deaths, estrangements, addictions, mental illness, dominant emotional tones per person
3. Ask pattern questions:
   - *"Who in this family believed the same thing about themselves that you believe about yourself?"*
   - *"What was never talked about?"*
   - *"Who did you learn to be in this family to stay safe?"*
4. Look for: triangulation, enmeshment, cutoff, legacy expectations, unresolved grief
5. Name what the client inherited versus what belongs to them alone

**Biblical Integration:**
Iniquity patterns run generationally (Ex 20:5). A genogram is not a blame exercise — it is an identification exercise: what was handed down so the client can consciously break agreement with it. Many lies in the Lie Eliminator are inherited, not individually created. The genogram surfaces the transmission pathway.

## 9. Immunity to Change

**Category:** Diagnostic — Change Framework
**Evidence Base:** Strong inference — Harvard-developed (Kegan & Lahey); constructive developmental psychology research base; widely validated in executive coaching
**When to Use:** Sessions 3–6 mid-engagement; when a client is stuck in a pattern despite clear goals and stated motivation

**How to Run — The 4-Column Map:**

| Column 1 | Column 2 | Column 3 | Column 4 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Commitment** — what I say I want | **Doing/Not Doing** — behaviors that undermine it | **Hidden Competing Commitment** — what I am actually protecting | **Big Assumption** — the belief that makes the competing commitment feel necessary |

Session process:
1. **Column 1:** *"What do you genuinely want to change or achieve?"*
2. **Column 2:** *"What do you do — or fail to do — that works against that commitment?"* (Behaviors only, not feelings)
3. **Column 3:** Flip the Column 2 behaviors — *"If you imagine doing the opposite of these behaviors, what are you worried would happen?"* The answer reveals the hidden commitment (e.g., *"I am committed to not being seen as incompetent"*)
4. **Column 4:** *"What belief makes that worry feel true?"* (e.g., *"If I fail publicly, I will be rejected"*) — this is the Big Assumption
5. Design one small safe-to-fail experiment that tests whether the Big Assumption is actually true

**Biblical Integration:**
Column 3 is secondary gain. Column 4 is the lie at the root. The experiment phase is behavioral activation toward truth-testing. The hidden competing commitment explains why thorny soil resists good-soil interventions — the client is not being disobedient; they are protecting a wound with a system. Identify the system before addressing the behavior.

## 10. Spiritual Disciplines Inventory

**Category:** Diagnostic — Spiritual Formation
**Evidence Base:** Strong inference — Willard's framework; contemplative neuroscience supports the practices themselves; the inventory format is a coaching adaptation
**When to Use:** Sessions 2–4; after Wheel of Life surfaces a spiritual deficit; pairs with eternal foundation work

**How to Run:**

Willard's two categories:
- **Disciplines of Abstinence:** Solitude, Silence, Fasting, Frugality, Chastity, Secrecy, Sacrifice
- **Disciplines of Engagement:** Study, Worship, Celebration, Service, Prayer, Fellowship, Confession, Submission

1. Give client the full list — have them mark each: *Practiced regularly / Occasionally / Rarely / Never*
2. For each "Never" or "Rarely," ask: *"What do you imagine would happen if you practiced this?"*
3. Look for avoidance patterns — the disciplines most avoided are often the most needed
4. Ask: *"Which discipline, if practiced consistently, would most change how you experience God?"*
5. Set one discipline as a 2-week experiment with specific parameters (time, frequency, method)

**Biblical Integration:**
This is the scaffolding work from the three-part session prep model (Build daily scaffolding). Disciplines are not performance — they are the training ground where God reshapes the will. Willard: *"A discipline is anything we can do that enables us to do what we cannot yet do by direct effort."* The inventory makes the abstract concrete and creates a measurable starting point.

## 11. TRAC360°

**Category:** Diagnostic — Christian Personal Development Assessment (Proprietary)
**Evidence Base:** Opinion/estimate — proprietary tool; validation status not publicly documented; widely used in Christian leadership coaching
**When to Use:** Pre-engagement or session 1 as intake diagnostic; revisit at midpoint

**How to Run:**
1. Client completes the online TRAC360° assessment at taketrac.com before the session
2. They receive a personalized report with interpretive guide
3. In session, review the four domain scores together
4. Ask: *"Where does this feel most accurate? Where does it surprise you?"*
5. Use the generated coaching plan as a working document — conversation starter, not prescription
6. Cross-reference with Comprehensive Discipleship Markers for gap analysis

**Biblical Integration:**
The TRAC framework specifically frames development in terms of where God is at work — not just where the client wants to grow. That diagnostic posture aligns with the Disciple Fit model: *"What is God already doing that we should cooperate with?"* Use the report to surface Spirit-led awareness, not to create a performance checklist.

## 12. Life Harmony Values Cards

**Category:** Values Assessment — Christian
**Evidence Base:** Opinion/estimate — popular Christian coaching tool; no published psychometric validation; clinically, ACT Values Clarification is the stronger validated alternative
**When to Use:** Sessions 1–2; use either this or ACT Values Clarification, not both

**How to Run:**
1. Spread the 52 cards face-up (or use the written worksheet version)
2. Client sorts into three piles: *Resonates strongly / Somewhat / Not at this time*
3. From the strong pile, narrow to 7, then to 3
4. For each top value:
   - *"Tell me about a time you lived this value well. What made it possible?"*
   - *"Tell me about a time you violated this value. What was at stake?"*
5. The gap between the two answers is the coaching territory

**Biblical Integration:**
Each card includes a Scripture — the bridge is built into the tool. Values are not preferences; they are conscience-level convictions reflecting how God wired this person. Use the Scripture on each card as an anchor: *"This is why this value matters — it reflects something God has written into you."* The card sort externalizes values in a way that feels exploratory rather than evaluative, lowering defensiveness.

## 13. DISC

**Category:** Behavioral / Relational Assessment
**Evidence Base:** Strong inference — median reliability coefficient .87; convergent validity established; predictive validity is debated; adequate for coaching use, not clinical diagnosis
**When to Use:** Relational and leadership coaching contexts; mid-engagement after rapport is built; do not lead with it

**How to Run:**
1. Client completes assessment (Everything DiSC is the most validated version)
2. Review primary and secondary style profile
3. Focus on three areas in session:
   - **Strengths:** What comes naturally in this style?
   - **Stress behavior:** What does this profile look like under pressure? (Often surprises clients)
   - **Relational friction:** Which other styles create tension and why?
4. Ask: *"Where do you see this profile show up in your current challenges?"*
5. Use the stress behavior profile to map to presenting patterns — stress behavior often reveals the activated core belief

**Biblical Integration:**
DISC describes the natural man's operating system — not destiny, but a lens on how the flesh tends to respond. The coaching bridge: *"When you are in your stress behavior, which of your core beliefs is getting activated?"* DISC → Core Belief → Lie. That is the chain. DISC names the behavior; the Biblical framework names the root.

## 14. CliftonStrengths (StrengthsFinder)

**Category:** Strengths Assessment / Vocation
**Evidence Base:** Strong inference — Gallup research base; convergent validity established with Big Five personality factors
**When to Use:** Vocation and calling sessions; when a client is lost, underperforming, or in transition

**How to Run:**
1. Client completes assessment at gallup.com; share results before the session
2. Review top 5 themes together — read the personalized descriptions aloud
3. For each theme ask: *"When have you used this strength without even trying? What were you doing?"*
4. Then: *"Where is this strength currently underused in your life?"*
5. Identify one domain (vocation, relationships, spiritual life) where intentionally deploying this strength would create the most movement

**Biblical Integration:**
Strengths are stewardship data, not ego inventory. They are what God built into this person to fulfill specific Kingdom function. Use it to move toward the vocational question: *"The way you are wired is a clue to what you were wired for."* Pairs directly with Kingdom IKIGAI for a full calling-discernment session.

## 15. Somatic Awareness & Nervous System Regulation

**Category:** Somatic
**Evidence Base:** Fact — somatic therapies (Somatic Experiencing, sensorimotor psychotherapy) have growing evidence base; body-mind connection is well-established in neuroscience
**When to Use:** Any session where emotional content is present; also as a session-opening ritual to shift from reactive to receptive mode

**How to Run:**

**Grounding — 5-4-3-2-1:**
- Name 5 things you see, 4 you can touch, 3 you hear, 2 you smell, 1 you taste
- Use when client is dysregulated, dissociating, or emotionally flooded

**Box Breathing:**
- Inhale 4 counts, hold 4, exhale 4, hold 4
- Activates parasympathetic nervous system within 60–90 seconds
- Use at session start or when emotional content spikes

**Body Scan:**
- *"Bring your attention to your chest. What do you notice? Where in your body do you feel this belief?"*
- Emotions are stored somatically; naming the body location interrupts the cognitive loop

**Titration (for trauma-adjacent content):**
- Do not push into full emotional flooding
- Work at the edge — approach the sensation, then back off, then approach again
- The goal is regulated processing, not catharsis

**Biblical Integration:**
The body is not separate from spiritual formation. Willard explicitly argues the body must be trained for godliness (1 Tim 4:8). Chronic nervous system dysregulation is a terrain problem — the soil is physiologically inhospitable to peace and spiritual receptivity. Somatic regulation is not secular technique; it is literal soil preparation. A dysregulated nervous system cannot receive what God is speaking.

## 16. Sabbath & Rhythm Audit

**Category:** Somatic / Biblical Formation
**Evidence Base:** Strong inference — rest and rhythm research is robust in occupational psychology and sleep science; Sabbath as a formation practice has theological validation across traditions
**When to Use:** When the Body spoke is low on the Wheel of Life; when a client is chronically overwhelmed and cannot engage deeper work

**How to Run:**
1. Have client map a typical week in time blocks (simple 7-day grid, hour by hour or half-day blocks)
2. Color-code: work / sleep / relational / solitary rest / spiritual practice / physical movement / margin (unscheduled)
3. Ask: *"What do you notice when you look at this?"*
4. Identify: Where is margin? Where is rest? Where is play? Where is God-time?
5. Locate the deficit — most clients have margin crushed by work and screens
6. Design one rhythm adjustment: one protected rest block, one tech-off window, one Sabbath practice — with specific parameters

**Biblical Integration:**
Sabbath is not a preference — it is a creation ordinance and covenant sign (Ex 31:13). Chronic rest deficit is not only burnout risk; it is covenant non-compliance and a spiritual formation blockage. Frame it plainly: *"You cannot hear God at 90mph."* The audit makes the invisible visible — most clients do not know what their week actually looks like until they draw it.

## 17. Appreciative Inquiry

**Category:** Strengths-Based Coaching Methodology
**Evidence Base:** Strong inference — Cooperrider's organizational research base; positive psychology literature supports strengths-first approaches
**When to Use:** When a client is stuck in deficit thinking, shame spirals, or chronic self-criticism; also useful in early sessions to build hope and agency before going into wound work

**How to Run — Four-Stage Model (Discover → Dream → Design → Destiny):**
1. **Discover:** *"Tell me about a time in your faith / relationships / work when you felt most alive and effective. What was happening?"*
2. **Dream:** *"If that experience were the norm rather than the exception, what would your life look like?"*
3. **Design:** *"What specific conditions made that peak experience possible? How do we build those conditions intentionally?"*
4. **Destiny:** *"What is one commitment you will make this week to move toward that vision?"*

**Biblical Integration:**
AI works from imago Dei forward — God's image is already present and functional in the client. The coaching task is to discover and amplify it, not install it. This is a useful counterweight when Lie and wound work dominates a session. When the client needs to reconnect with what God has already built in them before they can receive deeper confrontation, AI creates that bridge.

## 18. Lectio Divina

**Category:** Faith Practice — Contemplative Scripture Engagement
**Evidence Base:** Strong inference — long-established contemplative tradition; neuroscience of slow reading and contemplative practice is growing; not RCT-validated as a coaching tool
**When to Use:** As between-session homework, especially after identity or lie work; assign the passage intentionally to address the specific territory just opened in session

**How to Run:**
1. Select a specific short passage (4–6 verses) tied directly to the session's core theme or lie surfaced
2. Instruct the client:
   - **Read 1 (Lectio):** Read slowly. What word or phrase stands out?
   - **Read 2 (Meditatio):** Read again. What does this stir in you?
   - **Read 3 (Oratio):** Read again. What is God's invitation to you in this?
3. Client journals their response after each reading
4. Bring the journal entry to the next session as an opening

**Biblical Integration:**
This is direct Holy Spirit agency in the coaching arc. Between-session Lectio is not just homework — it is a structured encounter with God. It bridges session content to the client's personal God-relationship and prevents formation from becoming coach-dependent. The passage selected should not be random; it should be the Scripture that corresponds to the declaration work done in session.

## 19. Listening Prayer

**Category:** Faith Practice — Contemplative Prayer
**Evidence Base:** Opinion/estimate — spiritually validated by historic Christian tradition; not empirically validated as a clinical tool; requires established trust and theological grounding in the client
**When to Use:** Mid-to-late engagement after trust is well established; most powerful when intellectual work is cycling without breakthrough; not a session 1 tool

**How to Run:**
1. After identifying a core question or tension in session, pause
2. Lead a brief grounding or breath exercise (60 seconds)
3. Invite: *"Let's bring this to God directly. I want you to ask Him one question — keep it simple. Then notice what comes: an image, a word, a Scripture, a memory, a sense of peace."*
4. 2–3 minutes of silence
5. Ask: *"What came? Do not edit it — just share it."*
6. Process what surfaced without rushing to interpretation; ask: *"What do you make of that?"*

**Biblical Integration:**
John 10:27 — *"My sheep hear My voice."* This practice assumes God is already speaking. The coach's role is to create the conditions for the client to hear what they have been too noisy or afraid to receive. This is the Holy Spirit agency layer of the Soils framework made operational in session. What surfaces in listening prayer often bypasses the client's intellectual defenses entirely.

## 20. Kingdom IKIGAI

**Category:** Vocational / Calling Discernment
**Evidence Base:** Opinion/estimate — IKIGAI is a conceptual framework, not a validated instrument; adapted here for Kingdom calling discernment; useful as a structured conversation tool
**When to Use:** Vocation and calling sessions; clients in career transition, young adults without direction, or high-achievers who are successful but empty

**How to Run — Four-Circle Overlap Model:**

The four questions (each answered in writing first):
- **Gifted:** What am I naturally gifted to do? *(inform with CliftonStrengths / spiritual gifts)*
- **Called:** What does the world around me need that I uniquely can provide?
- **Viable:** What can I be compensated for — what is stewardship-sustainable?
- **Alive:** What do I love doing — what creates energy rather than draining it?

Session process:
1. Have client write answers to all four questions before session
2. In session, map the overlaps: where do two circles intersect? Three? All four?
3. Where there is no intersection, ask: *"What is blocking the overlap here?"*
4. Use Genogram data if available — inherited family expectations often crowd out the Kingdom voice, especially in the "Viable" circle
5. Name the center: *"This is the territory of your calling. What would it take to move toward it?"*

**Biblical Integration:**
Kingdom IKIGAI is not career coaching — it is stewardship theology made practical. The four circles map onto Paul's language: gifts given by the Spirit (1 Cor 12), calling confirmed by the body (Eph 4), provision as Kingdom evidence (Phil 4:19), and the joy of the Lord as strength (Neh 8:10). Where all four overlap is where obedience and flourishing converge.
