# Values Clarification
*Coach use: ACT values work | Client label: TBD*

## Purpose
Surface the client's deepest commitments to anchor decision-making, resolve ambivalence, and expose the gap between stated values and lived behavior.

## Modality
[[ACT — Acceptance & Commitment Coaching]]

## When to Use
Values-action misalignment, decision paralysis, lack of direction, motivation gaps, competing commitments, boundary work.

## Coach's In-Session Guide
1. Present 6–8 life domains: faith, relationships, work/calling, health, community, finances, personal growth, family
2. For each domain: "What kind of person do you most want to be here? What does living fully in this area look like?"
3. Have the client rank domains by current *importance* (not current performance)
4. Compare stated values to actual time/energy/money allocation — "Does your calendar reflect this ranking?"
5. Identify the highest-leverage gap
6. Anchor a commitment: "What one action this week would move you toward the person you want to be in [domain]?"

## Key Questions / Prompts
- "What matters most to you in [domain]?"
- "When you imagine yourself living fully in this area, what does it look like?"
- "What would people who know you best say you actually value — based on your behavior?"
- "Does that match what you believe you value?"
- "Which value, if you honored it more fully, would have the biggest ripple effect?"
- "What are you tolerating that violates what you say matters most?"

## Without Biblical Framework — Christian Client

**Verdict: Fatal as a standalone. Secular values clarification treats the client as the sovereign author of their values. For a Christian, values are discovered and submitted to — God is the authority, not the client's preference.**

A Christian will frequently name sub-Christian values — comfort, approval, security, control — and secular values clarification honors these as authentic. Biblical framework names them as disordered loves or idols. Coaching a client toward their idols with high skill is malpractice in a Christian context. There is also no mechanism for addressing the disorder of the will: fallen humans don't naturally value what is true, good, and beautiful. Stated values may be sincere and still be spiritually corrupted.

This tool's domain-based structure also covers the Wheel of Life function. Applied secularly, domains are evaluated by client satisfaction; biblically they should be evaluated by faithfulness and fruitfulness — which may require imbalance in some seasons and will not default to comfort as the telos.

**Fix:** Evaluate client-named values against biblical categories (calling, stewardship, covenant, worship) before anchoring commitments. See [[09 — Functional Savior Audit]] and [[14 — Calling Discernment Framework]].
