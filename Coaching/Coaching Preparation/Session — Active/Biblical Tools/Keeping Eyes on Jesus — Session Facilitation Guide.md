# Keeping Eyes on Jesus — Session Facilitation Guide

*Scripted coaching language for deploying the Client Study in session*

---

## The Session Arc

This study enters the coaching engagement at a specific moment — not at intake, not as general curriculum, but after a breakthrough. The arc:

1. A wave surfaces in session — a fear, a lie activated, a collapse of perspective
2. Coach pulls the client up from under it — the session does its work
3. A window opens — the client is in a posture of receptivity, not stability
4. **The handoff happens here**
5. Client reads the study independently before the next session
6. **The return session** — debrief, diagnose, assign first practice

The study is most powerful when it gives language to something the client just experienced. Hand it too early and it's information. Hand it at the window and it's revelation.

---

## Phase 1: The Handoff

**Reading the moment before you hand it:**

- Still in the emotional wash of the session? Wait. Let them stabilize. Don't add content to an unsettled moment.
- Intellectually engaged, emotionally landed? This is the window.
- Hungry to understand what just happened — asking "why do I do this?" or "what is this?" Hand it now.

**What to say:**

*Give them the physical document or the file.*

"There's a study I want you to read before we meet again. It's called *Keeping Eyes on Jesus* — and it will give you language for what you just experienced in this session.

Peter walking on water isn't just an encouraging story. What happened to him is exactly what happens to us — and the Greek gives us a precise diagnosis. Not condemnation. A diagnosis. This will help you understand what was happening in your own moment today, and what the path forward actually looks like.

It's about five or six pages. Find a quiet twenty or thirty minutes — not scrolling on your phone, not in the middle of something else. Sit with it and let it land."

---

**If they ask "what's it about?" before reading:**

"It's about attention — specifically where we put the weight of our attention when circumstances are screaming at us. It uses Matthew 14 as the diagnostic. Just read it. We'll unpack it when we meet."

---

**If the session was particularly raw and they're not in a reading posture:**

Don't hand it today. Close the session well and say:

"I have something I want to give you — a study that will help make sense of what we've been working through. You're not quite ready for it today, and that's fine. I'll bring it next time."

---

## Phase 2: Framing the Greek in Session

*Use this language if you introduce any of the Greek words verbally in session — before or after the client reads the study. The goal: plant the word as a tool, not a fact. Each framing ends with a question, not a lecture.*

---

**distazō — "to stand in two places at once"**

"The Greek word translated 'doubt' in Matthew 14 doesn't mean skepticism or unbelief. It literally means to stand in two places simultaneously — to have your weight divided between two competing things. Peter didn't stop believing in Jesus when he sank. He split his attention between two claims about reality and the weight shifted toward the waves.

That's not primarily a faith problem. It's an attention problem.

Where are you standing in two places right now?"

---

**aphorao — "look away from in order to look at"**

"Hebrews 12 says 'fixing our eyes on Jesus' — but the Greek word is actually a compound: 'away from' plus 'to see.' It means to look away from something in order to look at Him. The turn toward Jesus isn't the whole move. There's a turn away from first.

Most people skip that first movement. They try to add Jesus on top of an already saturated field of attention — and He doesn't compete for space.

What would you need to turn away from to make room for the gaze?"

---

**katoptrizomenoi — "ongoing beholding that produces ongoing transformation"**

"There's a word in 2 Corinthians 3:18 that changes how you think about transformation. It says 'beholding the glory of the Lord, we are being transformed.' The word for 'beholding' is a present participle — ongoing, continuous, not completed. And the transformation isn't a separate event that follows the beholding. It happens as the beholding happens. Simultaneously.

This means transformation isn't something you wait for after you've done everything right. It's the byproduct of sustained attention. As you behold, you're changing.

What would change if you actually believed that?"

---

**metamorphoumetha — "being transformed — present tense, passive voice"**

"The word for 'being transformed' in that same verse is the same root as the Transfiguration — Matthew 17. And it's present passive. Being done to you, right now, ongoing. And then it names the agent: 'by the Spirit of the Lord.'

Your role is attentional. The Spirit's role is the metamorphosis. You're not responsible for producing the change — only for positioning yourself for it.

Most people get this wrong in one of two directions: they try to produce the change themselves, which is exhausting and fails. Or they wait passively for the Spirit to do everything without their participation, which produces nothing. The actual mechanic is you behold, He transforms. That's the contract.

Which side of that have you been getting wrong?"

---

## Phase 3: The Return Session

*The client comes back having read the study. How they received it is highly diagnostic — it often reveals the primary gap more clearly than any direct question.*

---

**Opening the return:**

Don't start with "what did you think?" That invites a performance response — they'll tell you what they think you want to hear.

Start here instead:

**"Before we go anywhere — what landed? Not what you understood. What *landed*."**

Then wait. The silence is diagnostic.

---

**Reading what comes back:**

| What the client says or does | What it likely signals | How to respond |
|---|---|---|
| Summarizes content accurately but abstractly | Intellectualized — processed it, didn't receive it | "That's what it says. What did it say to *you* specifically?" |
| One word or phrase stopped them | Something genuinely connected — follow that thread | "Tell me more about why that one stopped you." |
| "The covenant gaze section hit me" | Gap 3 active — performance orientation toward God | Move toward Receiving Prayer; don't stay in theology |
| "The wave-narrative part" | Gap 4 active — circumstances have interpretive authority | "What is the wave's claim in your situation? And what is Jesus's word over it?" |
| "Distazō — standing in two places" | They recognized themselves precisely | "What are the two places? Name them." |
| Didn't read it | Don't shame. "What got in the way?" Re-hand it — don't advance without it |
| Read it, felt nothing | Don't push. "Which part felt most distant? That's usually the most important one." |
| It broke something open — emotional | Don't explain. Sit in it. Ask: "What is that?" Let them locate it before you name it |

---

**Follow-up by what landed:**

*If they connected with Peter stepping out of the boat:*
"You're in a stepping-out season — you've already gotten out of the boat. Where are you right now in relation to the waves — just noticing them, or starting to feel the weight shift?"

*If they connected with Peter sinking:*
"You identified with the sinking. And Jesus reached out immediately — not after Peter figured out what went wrong, not after he composed himself. What does it mean for you that the catch happened before the analysis?"

*If they connected with training vs. trying:*
"You saw the gap between training and trying. So — what have you been trying that you haven't been training for? And what's the first practice that would start closing that gap this week?"

*If they connected with the covenant gaze:*
"That's the piece most people miss. Before you can return the gaze, you have to receive it. What's making it hard to receive it right now — what's in the way?"

*If they connected with the image-bearing section:*
"You become like what you attend to most. So — what have you been attending to? And what image is being formed in you right now as a result?"

---

**If the study didn't land — or they intellectualized it:**

Don't re-explain the study. Ask one question:

"Which section felt the most distant — like it was talking about someone else's experience, not yours?"

That distance is usually the location of the primary gap. The concept that feels most foreign is often the one most needed. Name it:

"It's interesting that that section felt distant. That usually means it's pointing at something real. What would it mean for you if it were actually true — that [specific concept]?"

---

## Phase 4: Assigning the First Practice

After the return debrief, assign one practice from the Reference Card. Not two. One.

The diagnostic question to guide your choice: *Based on what landed — what gap is most clearly active right now?*

Then frame the assignment this way:

"I want you to try [practice name] this week — not because you have to, and not to measure how well you do it. I want you to try it because [specific reason tied to exactly what they said in session].

The goal isn't to do it perfectly. The goal is to show up to it and return when you drift. That's the whole thing.

Can you commit to [specific frequency — e.g., 'ten minutes every morning before you pick up your phone']?"

---

**If they push back on the practice:**

Don't argue. Explore.

"What's the resistance? Tell me what happens when you imagine actually doing it."

The resistance is almost always more diagnostic than the practice itself. It often locates the gap more precisely than the Diagnostic tool does.
