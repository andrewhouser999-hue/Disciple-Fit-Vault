# Suffering & Cruciformity — Formation Through Pain

The wounds file treats suffering as something to heal *from*. That is necessary but incomplete. Scripture also treats suffering as something God forms you *through*. These are not the same. A wound is an injury requiring healing. Cruciformity is a path requiring traversal. Both are real. Confusing them produces either avoidance of necessary pain or passive resignation to injury that should be treated.

> "It was good for me to be afflicted so that I might learn your decrees." — Ps 119:71

---

## The Core Distinction

| Wound | Cruciform Suffering |
|---|---|
| An injury caused by sin, evil, or brokenness | A trial, discipline, or loss permitted by God for formation |
| Requires healing | Requires endurance and faith |
| God heals it | God works *through* it |
| The goal is restoration | The goal is maturity and conformity to Christ |
| Primary response: lament, treatment | Primary response: trust, perseverance |

In practice these overlap and are not always cleanly separable — but the distinction matters for how a client relates to their suffering.

## Biblical Support

### 1. Suffering Has a Production Chain

| Verse | Content |
|---|---|
| **Rom 5:3–5** | "We also glory in our sufferings, because we know that suffering produces perseverance; perseverance, character; and character, hope. And hope does not put us to shame, because God's love has been poured out into our hearts through the Holy Spirit" — suffering has a named production chain that ends in identity and hope |
| **James 1:2–4** | "Consider it pure joy whenever you face trials of many kinds, because you know that the testing of your faith produces perseverance. Let perseverance finish its work so that you may be mature and complete, not lacking anything" — trials produce maturity when perseverance is allowed to complete its work |
| **1 Pet 1:6–7** | "These have come so that the proven genuineness of your faith — of greater worth than gold, which perishes even though refined by fire — may result in praise, glory and honor when Jesus Christ is revealed" — faith is proved and refined through suffering, not despite it |
| **Heb 12:10–11** | "God disciplines us for our good, in order that we may share in his holiness... No discipline seems pleasant at the time, but painful. Later on, however, it produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it" — suffering as divine training; the fruit is real but delayed |

### 2. Christ Himself Was Formed Through Suffering

This is the most theologically significant claim: **suffering was not incidental to Christ's formation — it was the mechanism of it.**

| Verse | Content |
|---|---|
| **Heb 2:10** | "In bringing many sons and daughters to glory, it was fitting that God... should make the pioneer of their salvation perfect through what he suffered" — Jesus was *made perfect* through suffering. Not sinless (he already was), but complete, fully equipped for his role |
| **Heb 5:8–9** | "Although he was a son, he learned obedience from what he suffered and, once made perfect, he became the source of eternal salvation for all who obey him" — Jesus *learned* obedience through suffering. This is the pattern for all his followers |
| **Isa 53:3–5** | "He was despised and rejected by mankind, a man of suffering, and familiar with pain... by his wounds we are healed" — his wounds become our healing. The cruciform pattern is not merely exemplary — it is redemptive |
| **Phil 2:8** | "He humbled himself by becoming obedient to death — even death on a cross" — the cross is the ultimate act of cruciform obedience. The shape of his life is the shape of ours |

### 3. Participation in Christ's Sufferings

| Verse | Content |
|---|---|
| **Phil 3:10** | "I want to know Christ — yes, to know the power of his resurrection and *participation in his sufferings*, becoming like him in his death" — Paul explicitly pursues suffering as a formation path, not a detour around formation |
| **Rom 8:17** | "If we are children, then we are heirs — heirs of God and co-heirs with Christ, if indeed we share in his sufferings in order that we may also share in his glory" — sharing in suffering and sharing in glory are linked |
| **Col 1:24** | "Now I rejoice in what I am suffering for you, and I fill up in my flesh what is still lacking in regard to Christ's afflictions, for the sake of his body, which is the church" — Paul's suffering has participatory and redemptive meaning |
| **2 Cor 4:10–11** | "We always carry around in our body the death of Jesus, so that the life of Jesus may also be revealed in our body" — the death/resurrection pattern is embodied in ongoing suffering |

### 4. Suffering Produces Specific Capacities

Suffering does not merely make you endure more — it produces capacities that nothing else can.

| Verse | Capacity Produced |
|---|---|
| **2 Cor 1:3–4** | "The God of all comfort, who comforts us in all our troubles, so that we can comfort those in any trouble with the comfort we ourselves receive from God" — the capacity to comfort others is produced by having been comforted in suffering |
| **Ps 119:71** | "It was good for me to be afflicted so that I might learn your decrees" — suffering produces knowledge of God unavailable by other means |
| **Heb 5:8** | Jesus "learned obedience from what he suffered" — obedience of a particular depth is learned only through suffering |
| **Rom 5:4** | Suffering → perseverance → character — character of a proven kind (dokimē — tested, genuine) requires the test |
| **2 Cor 12:9–10** | "My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness... when I am weak, then I am strong" — the experience of God's sufficiency requires the experience of one's own insufficiency |

### 5. Pruning — Suffering Applied to the Fruitful

| Verse | Content |
|---|---|
| **John 15:2** | "Every branch that does bear fruit he prunes so that it will be even more fruitful" — pruning is not applied to the barren branch. It is applied to the fruitful one. Suffering does not indicate God's absence; it may indicate his attentiveness |
| **Heb 12:6–8** | "The Lord disciplines the one he loves, and he chastens everyone he accepts as his son... If you are not disciplined... then you are not legitimate, not true sons and daughters at all" — the absence of suffering may be the more alarming condition |

### 6. The Refining Fire

| Verse | Content |
|---|---|
| **Isa 48:10** | "I have refined you, though not as silver; I have tested you in the furnace of affliction" — God is the refiner; affliction is the furnace |
| **Zech 13:9** | "I will refine them like silver and test them like gold" |

### 7. Lament as the Legitimate Response to Suffering

Cruciformity does not require silent acceptance. The biblical response to suffering includes full, honest lament — which is itself a formation practice.

| Verse | Content |
|---|---|
| **Ps 88** | The darkest psalm — no resolution, no turn at the end. God includes unresolved lament in Scripture as a legitimate posture |
| **Lam 3:1–20** | Raw grief before any turn: "He has driven me away and made me walk in darkness rather than light" |
| **Job 3, 7, 10, 16** | Job protests his suffering directly to God throughout — and God does not rebuke him for it. He rebukes the friends who offered tidy theological answers |
| **Job 42:7–8** | "You have not spoken the truth about me, as my servant Job has" — God validates Job's honest wrestling over the friends' theological correctness |
| **Ps 22:1–2** | "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me? Why are you so far from saving me?" — honest lament is not faithlessness; it is the language of someone still in relationship with God |

### 8. The Cruciform Pattern — Death Then Resurrection

| Verse | Content |
|---|---|
| **John 12:24** | "Unless a kernel of wheat falls to the ground and dies, it remains only a single seed. But if it dies, it produces many seeds" — the death/life pattern is the basic formation structure of the kingdom |
| **2 Cor 4:17** | "For our light and momentary troubles are achieving for us an eternal glory that far outweighs them all" — suffering is not without purpose or proportion |
| **Rom 6:3–5** | Baptism into death and resurrection — the cruciform pattern is embedded in the initiation rite of Christian identity |
| **Gal 2:20** | "I have been crucified with Christ and I no longer live, but Christ lives in me" — the death of the old self is required for the new self |

## Theological Support

### Dan Allender — *The Wounded Heart*, *To Be Told*
Suffering is not a detour around formation — it is frequently the primary road. Allender distinguishes between wounds (injuries that require healing) and cruciform suffering (trials that produce maturity). Both require engagement. Neither can be bypassed without paying a formation cost. "The wounds of God are not the same as the wounds of sin" — but both must be faced honestly.

---

### Larry Crabb — *Shattered Dreams*
When our dreams are shattered — when life does not deliver what we expected from God — we face a choice: bitter disappointment or deeper trust. Crabb argues that shattered dreams are God's primary tool for dismantling our false gods (comfort, control, approval) and driving us toward the one thing that can truly satisfy. Formation through loss is not God's Plan B; it is frequently his Plan A.

---

### C.S. Lewis — *The Problem of Pain*
"God whispers to us in our pleasures, speaks in our conscience, but shouts in our pains: it is His megaphone to rouse a deaf world." Pain is not evidence of God's absence but sometimes the instrument of his most urgent communication. Lewis also — importantly — does not sentimentalize this. His *A Grief Observed* written after his wife's death is the honest correction to the tidy theology of *The Problem of Pain*.

---

### Martin Luther — Theology of the Cross
Luther distinguished the *theologian of glory* (who looks for God in success, power, and triumph) from the *theologian of the cross* (who finds God hidden in suffering, weakness, and failure). The cross is not a problem to explain away — it is the revelation of who God is. Formation in the Lutheran tradition is fundamentally cruciform: you do not grow by ascending to God's strength but by descending into your own weakness, where Christ meets you.

---

### Hans Urs von Balthasar
The cross is not an interruption of God's love — it is the fullest expression of it. To be conformed to Christ is to be conformed to the one who suffered and died. Cruciformity is not incidental to salvation; it is its shape.

---

### N.T. Wright — *Paul and the Faithfulness of God*
Paul's theology is cruciform throughout. The apostle does not suffer despite his calling — he suffers *as* his calling. The shape of Christian vocation in the present age is cross-shaped, awaiting the resurrection that vindicates it. Formation cannot skip the cross and arrive at resurrection.

## Key Synthesis

### The Formation Question in Suffering

The question is not "why is this happening to me?" but "what is being formed in me?" — and more specifically, "what capacity is God producing through this that cannot be produced any other way?"

This is not a dismissal of lament. Honest lament comes first. But lament that stays lament without eventually asking the formation question will produce bitterness rather than maturity.

### The Two Errors to Avoid

| Error | Description | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **Avoid all suffering** | Treat every pain as a wound to be healed | Miss formation that only comes through the fire |
| **Baptize all suffering** | Treat every pain as God's discipline | Miss wounds that need healing, not endurance |

The diagnostic question: *Is this a wound (injury caused by sin/evil that needs healing) or a trial (permitted by God for formation that needs endurance)?* Often it is both simultaneously.

### Coaching Application

- Clients will arrive in suffering. The first response is always to be present with them in it — not to quickly reframe it as "God's formation plan"
- Lament must be honored before any formation framing is offered
- The formation question comes later, when the client is ready: "What might God be producing in you through this?"
- The capacity a client develops through suffering often becomes their primary ministry resource (2 Cor 1:3–4)
- Suffering that is resisted or bypassed tends to produce bitterness; suffering that is engaged tends to produce maturity — the coach's job is to help the client engage rather than escape

## Cross-links
- Scripture & the Subconscious — suffering surfaces and reveals what is stored below conscious awareness; it exposes what the deep heart actually trusts
- Scripture & Emotional Wounds — distinguish wound (requires healing) from cruciform suffering (requires endurance); often both are present simultaneously
- We Become What We Believe — what you believe about suffering determines whether it forms or destroys you; the cruciform narrative is a belief framework
- We Become What We Do — suffering is a practice context; how you respond to pain becomes habitual and eventually character
- We Become Who We Are Loved By — suffering endured in community is more formative than suffering endured alone; isolation in suffering produces despair (1 Kings 19)
- The Holy Spirit as Primary Agent — the Spirit produces fruit through suffering; without him suffering produces bitterness rather than maturity (Rom 5:3–5)
- Narrative & Story — suffering requires a narrative large enough to hold it; without the right story, suffering collapses into meaninglessness (Rom 8:28)
- The Will — suffering tests and reveals what the will is actually committed to beneath declared intentions; it surfaces secondary gain and false comfort
- Shame — suffering can be interpreted as evidence of worthlessness, producing shame; the cruciform narrative reframes suffering as refining, not condemning
- Emotions as Formation Agents — suffering produces specific emotions (grief, fear, anger) that must be honestly engaged rather than suppressed to produce formation
- Soils Framework — rocky soil cannot sustain through suffering; deep soil endures and bears fruit through it
