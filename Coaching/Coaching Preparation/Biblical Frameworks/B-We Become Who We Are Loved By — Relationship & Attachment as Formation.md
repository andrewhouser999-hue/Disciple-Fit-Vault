# We Become Who We Are Loved By — Relationship & Attachment as Formation

All four companion files in this series (subconscious, wounds, belief, practice) describe individual formation mechanisms. A Christian psychologist and theologian would name this as the most significant gap: **formation does not happen in isolation — it happens in relationship.** This is not a supplement to the other four files. It is the context in which all four operate.

> "We are the most relationally dependent creatures on the planet." — Curt Thompson

---

## The Core Claim

We do not become who we are *alone*. We become who we are *in relationship* — for good and for ill. The deepest beliefs were installed by relationships. The deepest wounds are relational wounds. The deepest healing is relational healing. And the primary formation community God designed is not the individual quiet time — it is the church.

---

## Biblical Support

### 1. The Trinity — Relational Being as the Foundation of Human Nature

Before any human relationship is discussed, Scripture establishes that God himself is irreducibly relational.

| Verse | Content |
|---|---|
| **Gen 1:26** | "Let *us* make mankind in our image" — God speaks in plurality before creating humanity. Relational being precedes human existence |
| **John 17:20–23** | "May they be one, Father, just as you are in me and I am in you... that they may be one as we are one" — Trinitarian unity is the *model* for human relational unity |
| **1 John 4:8** | "God is love" — love requires an other. God's nature is not solitary; it is inherently relational. We are made in the image of a relational God |
| **John 15:9** | "As the Father has loved me, so have I loved you. Abide in my love" — the relational dynamic of the Trinity overflows into human formation |

**Implication:** We are structurally relational because we bear the image of a relational God. Isolation is not neutral — it contradicts our nature at the deepest level.

## Psychological and Theological Support

### John Bowlby — Attachment Theory
The primary human psychological need is a secure attachment figure — a person who is available, responsive, and able to be a safe base from which to explore the world. Early attachment patterns (secure, anxious, avoidant, disorganized) form the template for all subsequent relationships — including relationship with God. These patterns form before language, before conscious belief, before intentional practice. They operate below the subconscious level mapped in the first file of this series.

---

### Curt Thompson — *Anatomy of the Soul*, *The Soul of Shame*
The brain is a social organ — literally shaped by relationships through the mechanism of neuroplasticity. "The mind is the embodied and relational process that regulates the flow of energy and information" (Dan Siegel). We are *known into being* — identity is not self-generated; it emerges in the experience of being known by another.

Key insight: shame is healed specifically through being **known and not rejected** — a relational experience, not a cognitive or behavioral one. No amount of correct belief or disciplined practice heals shame. Only being seen at depth and received rather than rejected does.

---

### Larry Crabb — *Connecting*, *Inside Out*
"The connecting of two people's souls may be the most powerful therapeutic force available." We were designed for deep connection — with God and with other people. The church is intended to be a healing community, not merely a teaching institution. Crabb argues that most Christian counseling and discipleship fails because it addresses symptoms (behavior, belief) without the relational context in which transformation actually occurs.

---

### Dan Allender — *The Wounded Heart*, *Bold Love*, *To Be Told*
Most wounds are relational wounds — betrayal, abandonment, shame, abuse. Most healing is relational healing — being truly known by another person who does not reject. The counseling or coaching relationship is not merely a delivery mechanism for content; it is itself a formation context. The quality of the relational presence *is* the intervention.

---

### Henri Nouwen — *The Wounded Healer*, *Life of the Beloved*
We are formed as beloved sons and daughters first through relationship. Nouwen's formation arc: **Beloved → Broken → Given**. Before you can give yourself in ministry (given), you must know yourself as loved (beloved) — and that knowing comes through relational experience, not only intellectual assent to theological propositions.

---

### Todd Hall — Attachment to God Research
Empirical research confirms: people's attachment patterns with primary caregivers are transferred to their relationship with God. An anxiously attached person tends to experience God as demanding and unpredictable — regardless of what they theologically believe about him. An avoidantly attached person tends to experience God as distant and disengaged. Secure attachment to human caregivers correlates with secure attachment to God.

**Implication for coaching:** A client can have entirely orthodox beliefs about God's love and still experience God as unsafe, distant, or demanding — because the relational template formed before the theology. Addressing only the belief content misses the relational formation layer underneath.

---

### Eastern Orthodox Theology — *Perichoresis* and Theosis
The Cappadocian Fathers developed *perichoresis* — the mutual indwelling of the Trinity. The Father, Son, and Spirit exist in a relational dance of complete self-giving and receiving. Humans are made to participate in this — *theosis* (deification) is not a solitary spiritual achievement; it is relational participation in the life of God. Formation is entry into a relationship, not mastery of a technique.

---

### Martin Buber — *I and Thou*
Genuine human existence is I-Thou relationship — encountering another as a full subject, not as an object or means to an end. God is the eternal Thou. Formation happens in genuine encounter. Treating people as problems to solve (I-It) stunts formation. Being truly present with another person (I-Thou) opens the space where transformation is possible.

## Key Synthesis

### The Relational Formation Stack

| Layer | What Forms It | Key Support |
|---|---|---|
| **Deepest** — attachment templates | Primary caregiver relationships in infancy/childhood | Bowlby, Hall, Thompson |
| **Deep** — identity and shame | Being known or rejected by significant others | Thompson, Allender, 1 John 4:19 |
| **Middle** — beliefs about God and self | Relational experiences interpreted through story | Hall, Calvin, Luke 15 |
| **Upper** — practices and habits | Community structures and one-another commands | Smith, Aquinas, Heb 10:24 |
| **Surface** — behavior and output | Visible actions shaped by all layers below | James 2, Gal 6:7 |

Coaching that addresses only the upper layers without the relational foundation will produce change that does not hold.

### The Coaching Relationship Is a Formation Context

The coaching relationship itself is not neutral. It is a relational experience that either:
- Reproduces the client's existing attachment patterns (confirms their template)
- Or provides a corrective relational experience (begins to reshape the template)

A coaching relationship characterized by consistent presence, non-shaming honesty, curiosity rather than judgment, and genuine care is not a nice add-on. It is doing formation work at the deepest layer — below the level that belief content or assigned practices can reach.

### What This Means in Practice

- A client's attachment pattern will appear in the coaching relationship — watch for it
- Beliefs installed by relationships cannot be fully dislodged by better arguments alone — they need relational correction
- The between-session question is not only "what did you practice?" but "who were you in relationship with, and what happened?"
- Healing homework done *in community* has far more formation power than homework done alone
- Assessing the client's relational ecology (marriage, friendship, church, family of origin) is not background information — it is primary diagnostic data
