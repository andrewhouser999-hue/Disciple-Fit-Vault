# Greatest Commandment — Soil-Specific Practice Differentiation
*Coach reference — domain practices differentiated by soil type*

*Prerequisite: [[Greatest Commandment — Diagnostic Overlay]] — identify the domain and soil type before using this document*

---

## The Fundamental Rule

The diagnostic overlay identifies where the blockage is. This document tells you how to work it by soil type.

The same practice deployed across different soil types produces different results — including harmful ones. Gratitude journaling is a well-evidenced condition-creation practice for the affections domain. It is also the practice most likely to produce performance religion in Path soil clients who will complete it dutifully without any of it landing. Lament prayer that opens Path soil to genuine affect will amplify distress in a Thorny soil client who has no margin to process what surfaces. Understanding the soil isn't background context. It determines the intervention.

Every cell in this document has three components: what the blockage looks like in that combination, the primary entry point, and the key contraindication. Use them together. The entry point without the contraindication is half the information.

## Domain 1: Mind
*Beliefs about God — the functional picture, not the theological statement*

The mind domain is not primarily about what a client believes theologically. It is about the internal working model — who God actually is to this client under pressure, loneliness, and failure. That model was formed by experience, and it runs underneath theology without the client's awareness. Each soil type has a characteristic mind-domain obstruction, and the same tools that help one soil can reinforce another's defenses.

---

### Path Soil — Mind Domain

**What the blockage looks like:**
Path soil clients often appear theologically well-informed. The armor in the mind domain is intellectualization — they can engage doctrinal concepts about God's character while none of it touches the functional picture that actually governs their interior life. The gap between stated theology ("God is good") and operating assumption ("God wasn't there when I needed Him") is wide and defended. The armor keeps the content out, and theological engagement can paradoxically deepen it by making the client feel they've done the mind-work when they've only performed it.

**Entry point:**
Before any belief restructuring, surface the functional model. The diagnostic question from the overlay — *"When things are hard and you haven't heard from God — what do you actually assume?"* — is the entry, not the Lie Eliminator. The gap between the theological answer and the honest answer is the work. Stay there before moving to correction. A Path soil client who acknowledges their functional God-picture differs from their theology has done something significant. Do not rush past it.

Validation precedes excavation: name the wound that produced the functional model before naming the distortion. "That makes complete sense given what you experienced" disarms the armor enough for the actual work to begin. Skip validation and the armor re-engages.

**Key contraindication:**
Do not deploy the Lie Eliminator or Identity Installer in the mind domain until the client has acknowledged the gap between their functional and theological God-picture and has some degree of curiosity about it. Information-heavy sessions feed the intellectualization pattern. The Path soil client will engage the content analytically and report that the session was helpful. The soil will be unchanged.

---

### Rocky Soil — Mind Domain

**What the blockage looks like:**
Rocky soil clients have an emotionally reactive God-model — it shifts with their experiential state. After a worship service or a breakthrough in prayer, God feels real, present, and good. After a hard week, unanswered prayer, or a relational wound, God feels uncertain or absent. The mind-domain problem is not a wound-based distortion (as in Path soil) but experiential instability: the God-picture has no cognitive anchoring independent of emotional weather. When the feeling is gone, the belief goes with it.

**Entry point:**
Object constancy work — building the cognitive architecture to hold God's character as stable across varied experience. Two specific practices:

*Evidence File:* A running record of specific, historical, datable instances of God's faithfulness in this client's life — not theological statements but personal evidence. The function is identical to what a secure adult does implicitly when trusting someone who is temporarily absent: they access memory of consistent past behavior. Rocky soil clients have the experiences; they've never catalogued them as evidence. Building the file creates what didn't exist: a cognitive resource accessible when affect is low.

*Passages of God's consistency* read slowly, non-analytically, in the Lectio Divina mode: Lamentations 3:22-23, Hebrews 13:8, Malachi 3:6. The goal is not information intake but slow, repeated encounter with the constancy claim. The repetition, over weeks, builds cognitive anchoring.

**Key contraindication:**
Do not do emotional processing of God-experience with Rocky soil clients in the mind domain. Questions like "Tell me about a time you felt close to God" or spiritual history exploration that generates affect amplifies the experience-dependence. The goal is cognitive anchoring, not emotional activation. The tools are stabilizing, not intensifying.

---

### Thorny Soil — Mind Domain

**What the blockage looks like:**
The Thorny soil client's God-picture is typically intellectually intact. They believe correctly about God — and God is functionally absent from their daily life anyway. The mind-domain obstruction is not distortion but displacement: their cognitive resources are fully allocated to the anxieties, competing demands, and relentless mental activity that constitute the thorns. Belief restructuring work requires sustained attention. Thorny soil clients cannot bring sustained attention to anything that doesn't have immediate urgent relevance. They agree to everything you say and implement nothing.

**Entry point:**
Before any belief work, name the attention capture. The question that opens this: *"When did you last spend 10 minutes in actual silence — not thinking about God, thinking about nothing? What happened?"* The answer (usually: they haven't, or they tried and it was agitated and they stopped) surfaces the structural issue. Attention is the prerequisite for all mind-domain work. The thorns are not just time — they are mental occupation. A client whose background mental process is running at full load cannot receive or integrate belief work.

The first mind-domain intervention for Thorny soil is often architectural: identify the one or two primary cognitive occupants (the anxiety that runs, the to-do list that never stops, the relational concern that sits in background) and address the capture before introducing any practices that require sustained attention.

**Key contraindication:**
Do not do complex belief archaeology or extensive Lie Eliminator work with Thorny soil clients in the mind domain before the attention competition has been named and reduced. They will engage it intellectually in session and have no cognitive bandwidth to carry it into the week. The sessions will feel productive. The transformation rate will be low. Thorns first, beliefs second.

---

### Good Soil — Mind Domain

**What the blockage looks like:**
Good soil in the mind domain presents as theological stability, a reasonably accurate functional God-picture, and some capacity to hold the faith cognitively across difficult experiences. The risk is not distortion — it is calcification. A settled mind-domain God-picture can become a ceiling rather than a foundation: the client has organized God into a framework they manage comfortably, and the management has replaced the relationship. The Pharisee pattern operates here: high theological competence, low transformation rate.

**Entry point:**
The work in Good Soil's mind domain is deepening and challenging, not rebuilding. Two moves:

First, press past settled answers: *"You said God is trustworthy even when He doesn't make sense. When was the last time you encountered Him in a way that genuinely unsettled you — not a crisis, but something that didn't fit the framework you already had?"* Good soil clients are ready for theological disruption they can absorb. Shallow soil clients are not. Use this.

Second, address the specific attack on Good Soil in the mind domain: the pride that comes from fruitfulness. The enemy's lie to the stable, mature believer is "you've arrived; you can manage this from here." The mind-domain work for Good Soil includes explicit attention to whether God is still the one governing the theological framework, or whether the client has become the primary curator.

**Key contraindication:**
Do not let the settled theology become the ceiling. A mind-domain that has stopped being challenged is not mature — it has become self-managed. Good soil clients need the same challenge that made them Good Soil in the first place: honest encounter with what God is doing that doesn't fit the framework yet.

## Domain 2: Affections
*Capacity to receive love — the emotional experience of being loved vs. intellectual assent*

The affections domain is where the Rocky Soil warning is most critical. Carry it into every session on this domain regardless of soil type: the goal is never to feel more love for God. The goal is to identify what blocks love from being received, and to create conditions where the Spirit produces love as fruit. The moment a client-facing practice implies "you should feel more" you have produced a target that generates experiential-dependence behavior. Frame consistently: *obstacle removal and condition creation*, not emotional attainment.

---

### Path Soil — Affections Domain

**What the blockage looks like:**
Path soil clients typically have very limited access to affective experience of any kind — including in the affections domain. The armor that compacts the soil does not discriminate: it blocks the reception of love for the same reason it blocks the reception of truth. Opening to feel loved is experienced as vulnerability that has historically produced wound. For many Path soil clients, the inability to receive God's love is not primarily a theological problem or a belief problem — it is a self-protection problem. The body has learned to not let things in.

**Entry point:**
Do not begin in the affections domain with condition-creation practices. The precondition for receiving love is the capacity to receive anything — and that capacity requires some degree of safety. Two entry points:

*Lament before gratitude.* The lament framework (honest protest brought to God — Psalms 22, 88) is a lower-risk affections entry than any practice that requires the client to receive God's love. Lament asks only for honesty toward God — not emotional reception from God. It is the affective motion the Path soil client is most capable of because it doesn't require vulnerability toward love, only expression of honest negative internal state. The client who can bring their anger, grief, or protest to God has taken a more significant step toward genuine affective relationship than the client who performs gratitude.

*The somatic anchoring sequence (Part 6 of Soils) applied to love reception.* Any session moment in which the client has a small genuine experience — not of love necessarily, but of safety, relief, or recognition — use the anchoring sequence: slow it down, locate it in the body, amplify, name. You are building somatic evidence that reception is possible and safe.

**Key contraindication:**
Do not introduce gratitude practices, solitude practices, or condition-creation tools in the affections domain before the armor has shown some degree of openness. Asking a Path soil client to engage in practices designed to cultivate reception of God's love without first addressing the self-protective structure will produce performance religion — another form of the armor, now wearing spiritual language. Watch for clients who dutifully complete all assigned practices and report they felt nothing. That is Path soil in the affections domain. The response is not more or different practices. It is the armor.

---

### Rocky Soil — Affections Domain

**What the blockage looks like:**
This is the primary Rocky Soil warning location. Rocky soil clients do not lack affective capacity — they have an abundance of it. They feel God's presence intensely in worship, respond to scripture with genuine emotion, and have real experiences of love for God in high-affect moments. The affections domain problem for Rocky soil is the misidentification of affect as love. The emotional experience is real. It is not the thing itself. And it has no roots: it is available in the mountain-top moment and absent in the ordinary week. Rocky soil clients often organize their entire faith-life around pursuing, reproducing, and confirming these experiences — which is the experiential dependence that the Soils framework diagnoses and interrupts.

**Entry point:**
Restraint is the first move. Do not amplify what is already surface-level intense. The affections domain work for Rocky soil runs counter-intuitively: reduce the emotional activation practices and shift toward behavioral-formational ones.

The reframe to make explicit with the client: *"You are not short on feeling. You are short on roots. And roots grow in the dark, in the ordinary, without a payoff. The practices that form love for the long haul are not the ones that feel like love — they are the ones that build what holds when the feeling isn't there."*

James KA Smith's liturgy principle is the primary tool: obedience forms love; consistent behavioral practices shape the affective disposition over time, regardless of whether the practice "feels" meaningful in the moment. The Rocky soil client doing a 10-minute daily prayer practice without emotional return is doing more formation work than they realize. This needs to be named, because the absence of emotional payoff will read as failure to them.

**Key contraindication:**
Do not deploy awe experiences, intense worship, or any practice that produces emotional amplification as the primary affections tool for Rocky soil clients. This is the specific soil type the Rocky Soil warning exists for. You are not helping them feel more — you are helping them grow deeper. Any practice that feeds the experiential cycle delays the root development. Corporate worship is appropriate but must not be framed as the primary affections formation strategy.

---

### Thorny Soil — Affections Domain

**What the blockage looks like:**
The Thorny soil client's affections domain presents as depletion and capture. They may have genuine love for God — real desire, real history of receiving love at some point — but the affective resources that the affections domain needs are captured by the chronic anxiety, competing demands, and relentless productivity that constitute the thorns. The condition-creation practices (solitude, silence, gratitude, awe) are precisely the practices their life doesn't allow. The obstruction is not wound or misidentification — it is resource competition. What is available for love has been spent.

**Entry point:**
Clearing before cultivating. Before introducing any condition-creation practice, identify which thorns are capturing the affective resources that would otherwise be available for love. The thorns are not only time; they are emotional. Chronic anxiety has an emotional footprint. Relentless performance pressure has one. A relationship that perpetually drains without replenishing has one. Each thorn is consuming something that the affections domain needs.

The diagnostic question: *"Where does your emotional energy go by the end of a normal week? What's consumed it?"* Map the consumption before prescribing the practice. The intervention is strategic reduction — create margin by removing what's competing — before adding anything.

**Key contraindication:**
Do not add affective practices to a Thorny soil client's schedule without first addressing what will need to be removed to make room. Addition without reduction in a depleted system produces guilt and failure — the client tries the practice, can't sustain it, and confirms their belief that they can't do the interior work. The thorns have to give before the conditions can be created. One protected hour of genuine margin is more affections-formative for Thorny soil than five practices they perform anxiously between other demands.

---

### Good Soil — Affections Domain

**What the blockage looks like:**
Good soil in the affections domain — relatively open to receiving love, capable of experiencing it at some level, affective disposition toward God that holds across ordinary experience. The risks are subtler: plateau without deepening, the drift toward emotional attainment even in stable soil, and the unprocessed grief that even Good Soil clients carry without bringing it honestly to God.

**Entry point:**
Continued condition-creation with increasing depth and honesty. Two specific deepening moves:

*Lament as deepening practice, not entry practice.* Good soil clients are ready to bring their honest negative material to God in a way that Path or Rocky soil clients cannot sustain. Unprocessed grief, honest protest, and the prayers Good Soil clients have not yet prayed because they didn't feel they had permission — these are available for God's engagement in a way that forms deeper love than the practices Good Soil has already consolidated. The Psalms of lament (22, 88, 44) as a prayer template, not just a theological category.

*Dry seasons as formation, not failure.* John 15:2 — God prunes fruitful branches. The affections domain for Good Soil will include periods of relative dryness that are not evidence of regression but of pruning. Name this preemptively. The Good Soil client who enters a dry season without the framework for interpreting it will reach for Rocky Soil practices to reproduce the affect they've lost — and will be confused when the tools that worked before don't produce the same result.

**Key contraindication:**
Do not assume the affections work is done because the client is stable and fruitful. Stability in the affections domain is a platform for deeper engagement, not the destination. The pruning work happens to Good Soil clients, and it happens precisely because they have capacity for more than they currently have. Don't protect them from what God is doing by framing stability as arrival.

## Domain 3: The Will
*Divided commitment — the held territories where trust has not followed belief*

The will domain is where the Romans 7 reality lives: the divided self, the client who wants to surrender what they cannot release, the held territory that seems too specific to be about faith but is exactly where faith is being tested. Each soil type has a characteristic will-domain pattern. The coaching error consistent across soil types is prescribing action-stage tools to clients who are not in the action stage.

---

### Path Soil — Will Domain

**What the blockage looks like:**
The Path soil client's will-domain division is different from the others: the conflict is not between competing priorities but between the desire for change and the deep conviction that change means exposure and risk. The held territory is often the armor itself. They cannot surrender what they built for survival without believing something safer is available — and believing that requires the very trust the armor was designed not to extend. This is a pre-contemplative will problem, not a commitment-practice problem.

**Entry point:**
Motivational Interviewing is the primary tool — specifically, evoking the client's own voice for what the armor is costing them before anything else. The question is not "What do you need to surrender?" but "What is keeping things as they are costing you? Not what you think it should cost — what is it actually taking from you?" 

The will does not engage until the cost of non-movement is felt more acutely than the cost of movement. For Path soil, the scale tips toward change only when the protection is seen clearly alongside its price. The MI spirit: the coach does not argue for surrender — the coach evokes the client's own articulation of why staying armored is no longer free. When the client hears their own voice making that case, movement becomes internally generated rather than externally prescribed.

**Key contraindication:**
Do not deploy implementation intentions, habit architecture, accountability structures, or any action-stage will-domain tool with Path soil clients. These assume the client has decided to change. Path soil clients in the will domain are at best in contemplation — often pre-contemplation. Prescribing behavioral commitment to someone who hasn't yet decided to engage produces one of two outcomes: compliance that generates nothing, or resistance that closes the door. Meet them where they are.

---

### Rocky Soil — Will Domain

**What the blockage looks like:**
The Rocky soil client's will is emotionally driven. They make genuine, heartfelt commitments in high-affect moments — in session, in worship, in a conversation that moved them — and the commitments do not hold when the emotion subsides. The will-domain problem is not divided commitment in the classic sense; it is the inability to sustain commitment across emotional weather. They are fully surrendered on the mountain and quietly reclaiming in the valley. They are not being dishonest. They genuinely mean it when they say it. But the will was anchored to the feeling, and when the feeling left, so did the anchor.

**Entry point:**
Commitment structures that run on environmental triggers rather than motivational state. Implementation intentions work here, but with a specific design principle: the if-then plan must trigger on a sensory/environmental cue, not on "when I feel ready" or "when I feel close to God."

*"When I make my first coffee in the morning, I will sit for five minutes before opening my phone."*
Not: *"I will spend time with God when I feel the need for it."*

The environmental anchor makes the commitment independent of the emotional state. The client is not deciding in the moment whether they feel like it — the trigger has already decided for them. Over time, this is what builds the will-domain structure that holds across emotional weather.

Second move: before the session closes on a significant commitment, ask the counter-move question directly — *"What will you do to keep this commitment on the Thursday when the feeling from today is completely gone?"* Surface the gap between the felt commitment and the structural support before they leave the room.

**Key contraindication:**
Do not celebrate will-domain commitments made in high-affect session moments. The commitment is real. The affect it was made in is not a reliable anchor. If you celebrate the breakthrough without addressing the structural question, you have confirmed to the Rocky soil client that the emotional experience of commitment is the thing — which is exactly the pattern being interrupted. Instead: acknowledge, then immediately ask what holds it when the feeling changes.

---

### Thorny Soil — Will Domain

**What the blockage looks like:**
The Thorny soil client has genuine will and genuine desire to surrender — and the will is specifically captured by the competing commitments and identities that constitute the thorns. They are not passive or ambivalent; they are over-extended. The held territory in the will domain is often not a specific sin or struggle but an identity: the person who handles everything, the one everyone depends on, the high-performer who can't stop because stopping would require facing what is underneath the performance. Surrender in the will domain means releasing not just tasks but the self-concept built on doing them.

**Entry point:**
The Secondary Gain framework (Part 4 of Soils) is the direct tool. The Thorny soil client's held territory has a specific payoff — significance, security through competence, belonging that comes from being needed — and the will will not release it until the payoff is named and the trade is evaluated honestly.

The sequence from Part 4: validate the payoff first, make the cost visible and specific, exegete the promise, then invite rather than prescribe — *"You've been paying [the cost] for [the payoff]. Do you actually want to keep that trade?"* The Thorny soil client's will is often ready to move once two things are true: the cost is visible, and the permission to reduce is real. Many Thorny soil clients are waiting for someone to tell them it's okay to stop. That is part of the coaching work.

**Key contraindication:**
Do not add more commitments, accountability structures, or discipline programs to Thorny soil clients in the will domain. This is the specific soil type for whom more structure is harmful — it feeds the over-function pattern and models the problem instead of the solution. The will intervention for Thorny soil is reduction and release, not addition and accountability. Every new commitment prescribed to a Thorny soil client should require the explicit removal of something that was there before.

---

### Good Soil — Will Domain

**What the blockage looks like:**
Good soil in the will domain presents as integrated commitment, genuine surrender in visible areas, growth holding across time. The blockage is subtle: the held territories for Good Soil clients are not obvious failures but competencies. They are the areas where the client has developed sufficient mastery that they can manage effectively without God's governance — and they do. They may pray about these areas and be grateful for the ability, without ever actually surrendering the management of the outcomes. The soil is good; the territory is quietly self-governed.

**Entry point:**
The held-competencies question: *"Where in your life are you genuinely the most capable person in the room — and have you surrendered the governance of that domain to God, or just thanked Him for the ability?"* This is a different question than the one for other soil types. The Thorny soil client holds territory out of fear or identity. The Good Soil client holds territory out of stewardship that has quietly become self-sovereignty.

A second diagnostic: *"Where is the last place you remember God disrupting something that was going well — and what was your response?"* Good Soil's enemy in the will domain is the drift from cooperation with God's governance to management with God's blessing. The disruption response reveals whether sovereignty has been retained.

**Key contraindication:**
Do not assume Good Soil clients have no will-domain work because they appear integrated and fruitful. The enemy's specific attack on Good Soil in the will domain is pride in the fruitfulness itself — the subtle shift from "God is producing this through me" to "I have learned to produce this." Sustained success is a will-domain risk, not a protection from one.

## Domain 4: The Relational
*Neighbor love — presence vs. performance in actual relationships*

The relational domain is where the 1 John circularity is most operationally important. Love for God and love for neighbor are not sequential — they are co-constitutive. Do not coach clients to complete interior love-work before extending relationally. The neighbor-love work *is* interior formation. Obedience forms love; love motivates obedience. The behavioral act of loving the neighbor shapes the person performing it. Hold this loop in every relational domain session.

---

### Path Soil — Relational Domain

**What the blockage looks like:**
The Path soil client's relational armor extends beyond their relationship with God into every relationship they have. The relational domain obstruction is structural: compacted soil produces the same managed surface engagement with people that it produces with truth. Path soil clients typically have relational competence — they can function in social contexts, maintain appropriate relationships, fulfill relational roles — and are rarely genuinely present. The performance of presence has long since replaced the thing itself, and the client may not be aware of the difference.

**Entry point:**
The coaching relationship is the first laboratory. Before prescribing relational love-acts toward others, the coach needs to be the first context where genuine presence is experienced as safe. The corrective relational experience in coaching — being seen, heard, and not abandoned for what is actually inside — is, for Path soil clients, often the most significant relational formation happening in their life. Name it explicitly at appropriate moments: *"What just happened in this conversation — that's what presence actually feels like. That's available to you in other relationships too."*

For external relational work: start small and low-risk. One genuine, non-performing encounter with a person they already have goodwill toward. Not conflict, not forgiveness, not love extended to a difficult person. One conversation where they are actually present rather than managing the room. Specific, small, within the window of tolerance.

**Key contraindication:**
Do not prescribe extensive service commitments, conflict engagement, or forgiveness work as the relational entry point for Path soil clients. These require emotional resources and vulnerability that Path soil hasn't developed. Forgiveness work before the armor has opened can produce intellectual forgiveness that bypasses the actual wound and leaves it intact. The entry is smaller and closer than Path soil clients expect — and often more significant because of it.

---

### Rocky Soil — Relational Domain

**What the blockage looks like:**
Rocky soil clients are often relationally engaging and emotionally present — in the intense moment. Their relational pattern mirrors their spiritual pattern: they show up powerfully in the crisis, the deep conversation, the moment of acute connection — and are less present in the ordinary ongoing relationship. Their neighbor-love is episodic and emotionally driven: high when activated, unavailable when the emotional signal is low. They may be known as the person who shows up when things are hard and is absent the rest of the time — and be confused by this because their heart for people is genuine.

**Entry point:**
Consistency over intensity. The relational work for Rocky soil is not amplifying the already-present emotional engagement — it is building the behavioral structure that holds relationship when the intensity subsides. James KA Smith's liturgy principle applied relationally: the ordinary acts of consistent, low-affect presence over time are more formative of love than the intense episodic moments.

Specific and scheduled relational presence acts: not "be present for your spouse" but "Tuesday evenings, phone down, ask one non-task question and stay with the answer." The specificity removes the emotional-state variable. Rocky soil clients will find the low-intensity consistency boring or inadequate — that is the diagnostic confirming the intervention is correct.

**Key contraindication:**
Do not celebrate relational breakthroughs or intense relational moments with Rocky soil clients in the way you wouldn't celebrate their spiritual breakthroughs. The deep conversation, the emotional reconnection, the breakthrough of presence — these are good, and they are not the thing that forms durable love. Ask after them: *"What will you do to be present in the ordinary week after this?" The Tuesday is the formation. The breakthrough is the signal that the Tuesday is worth showing up for.

---

### Thorny Soil — Relational Domain

**What the blockage looks like:**
The Thorny soil client is often relationally active and relationally depleted simultaneously. They serve. They show up in crises. They fulfill relational roles with consistency. And they are rarely actually present — because their relational engagement is organized around output rather than presence, and because their emotional resources are running at deficit. The people who love them can feel the difference. Thorny soil clients often receive feedback over years that they are "there but not there" — present in function, absent in attention — and attribute it to stress or busyness rather than recognizing it as a structural feature of Thorny soil relational engagement.

**Entry point:**
The arrival practice. Genuine relational presence for Thorny soil often requires a deliberate transition before entering the relationship — a clearing of the background hum before the room. One specific practice: a 5-minute transition before a key relationship encounter (before dinner with family, before a meaningful conversation) of silence, breath, and the explicit question *"What's running in the background right now that isn't this person?"* Name it, set it aside, then enter.

The reframe for the Thorny soil client in the relational domain: the call is not to do more but to be more actually present in the love that is already being attempted. Reduction in relational output may be required to create the attentional and emotional resources for genuine presence. Less service, more arrival.

**Key contraindication:**
Do not prescribe additional relational service commitments, more love-acts, or expanded relational involvement to Thorny soil clients in this domain. They are already over-extended. The relational problem is not insufficient output — it is insufficient presence in the output already happening. Adding more to a depleted system produces burnout, not formation. The one who needs to show up for fewer relationships with genuine presence will form more love than the one who rotates through fifteen relationships with managed attention.

---

### Good Soil — Relational Domain

**What the blockage looks like:**
Good soil in the relational domain presents as genuine presence capacity, relational investment with some depth, and neighbor-love that extends beyond role-fulfillment. The primary risk is consolidation within comfortable relational territory — investing deeply in relationships that are already mutual, reciprocal, and rewarding, while the harder edges of neighbor-love (the difficult person, the conflict that costs something, the enemy) remain unaddressed. Good soil clients can have highly developed love for the neighbor they prefer while the Great Commandment's actual demand — love for the neighbor in front of them, regardless of cost — remains largely theoretical.

**Entry point:**
The challenging neighbor. The Good Soil relational domain work moves toward forgiveness of real cost, conflict engagement that has been avoided, and love extended to those where reciprocity is not guaranteed. The Luke 10 parable specifically identifies the neighbor as the unexpected, inconvenient, costly one. Good soil clients are ready for this work in a way that other soil types cannot sustain.

Specific practices: one piece of forgiveness work that involves a real person and a real cost (not a generic forgiveness exercise); one relationship where conflict has been avoided and presence has been replaced by managed distance; one category of person that has been implicitly excluded from relational availability.

**Key contraindication:**
Do not let Good Soil clients consolidate their relational formation within comfortable, mutually beneficial circles and call the neighbor-love commandment fulfilled. The formation happens at the edges — where love requires something, where the person is hard, where the relationship doesn't give back. The Great Commandment's relational demand is specifically for the neighbor who cannot be loved by preference alone. Push into this work with Good Soil clients who have the capacity to do it.

---

## A Final Note on Sequence

This document treats the soil types as stable for the sake of clarity. In practice, clients cycle. A Thorny soil client working the will domain may reveal Rocky soil structure underneath. A Path soil client beginning to open in the affections domain is entering a transitional zone more unstable than either soil type. The diagnostic overlay and the soil framework are living reads, not permanent labels.

When in doubt: go back to the three tracking channels (Part 5 of the Soils framework), reassess the window of tolerance (Part 6), and let what's actually happening in the room tell you where you are. The soil knows. Watch it.
