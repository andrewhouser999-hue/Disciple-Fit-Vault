# Greatest Commandment — Diagnostic Overlay
*Coach reference — triage tool for identifying love-blockage location*

*Part of: [[Greatest Commandment — Organizing Telos (Coach)]] | Client version: [[Greatest Commandment — Organizing Telos (Client)]]*

---

## Purpose

The telos documents establish where the Disciple Fit system is going. This document tells you where in that direction the client is stuck.

The Greatest Commandment has four components, and clients get blocked in different places. A client whose mind holds a distorted picture of God needs different work than a client whose will is divided. A client who cannot receive love needs different work than a client who is internally present to God but disconnected from every room she walks into. The same commandment. Four distinct blockage points. Four different coaching responses.

This overlay is not a new tool. It is a diagnostic lens applied over the tools you already have. Use it to answer one question before the session builds momentum in a direction: *Where is the obstruction?*

---

## The Four Domains

| Domain | What It Names | Diagnostic Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Mind** | Beliefs about God — the functional picture, not the theological statement | Who is God to you when things go wrong? |
| **Affections** | Capacity to receive love — emotional experience vs. intellectual assent | Does God's love for you land, or does it stay abstract? |
| **Will** | Divided commitment — the held territories where trust hasn't followed belief | Where are you managing outcomes you haven't surrendered? |
| **Relational** | Neighbor love — presence vs. performance in actual relationships | Are you genuinely with the people you're with? |

These are lenses, not compartments. A client blocked in one domain will show stress fractures in others. The diagnostic question is which domain is *primary* — because the primary blockage is the entry point.

---

## Audience Note — 45-55, Male or Female, Self-Identified Christian

This overlay is calibrated for clients who say they are Christian, without assuming the depth that phrase implies. The spectrum is wide: from theologically grounded practitioners to people who believe in God, attend church regularly, and have a long personal history with faith language that has never been examined closely. Do not assume evangelical fluency. Do assume a real belief in God, years of accumulated faith experience, and high-functioning lives that have recently revealed a ceiling.

**At 45-55, three things are consistently true:**

The blockage is invisible by design. Whatever is in the way has been there long enough to present as the normal state of things — a stable pattern, not a visible struggle. The coaching question is not *when did this start?* but *what has this been quietly costing you, and what made you notice it now?*

Life transitions are in play. Career peak or plateau, empty nest, aging parents, midlife identity pressure. These transitions reactivate earlier blockages by destabilizing the structures the client has built to manage around them. The client who functioned well for twenty years arrives at this stage because the structures that held the blockage in place are shifting.

Accumulated frustration is a constant background variable. Many clients at this stage carry a quiet conviction that they should be further along by now. The question is not only *what blocks love* — it is *what blocks love for someone who has done everything right and is still hitting a ceiling.*

**Gender patterns:**

*Male clients* at this stage most often present with will-domain obstruction as the primary entry point — specific areas of life they have been managing competently for decades and have not surrendered because surrender is functionally indistinguishable from failure. The held territory is usually work, finances, a key relationship, or a family member's future.

*Female clients* at this stage most often present with affections-domain depletion as the entry point — decades of serving, giving, and caring for others, with the receptive capacity running at a deficit for so long it has become invisible. The presenting issue is not shame or wound but exhaustion: love has been going out without coming in. In the will domain, the held territory for women at this stage tends to be relational — the emotional wellbeing of their children, spouse, or the people they've spent decades protecting.

These are patterns, not rules. Calibrate to the individual.

## How to Use This Overlay

**When to deploy:** After the soil type has been identified. These questions require enough relational trust that the client can answer honestly rather than perform competence for you.

**Sequencing:** Start with Question 1. Listen for the domain the client naturally pulls toward. Use the targeted domain question (2–5) that sharpens the read. You do not need all five in one session. You need the one that opens the honest conversation.

**What you're looking for:** Not the stated answer — the affect behind it, the pause, the slight shift, what the client says when they stop performing. The diagnostic lives in *how* they answer, not in *what* they answer.

**Clinical calibration:** This overlay identifies obstruction location. Some of what surfaces — especially in the affections and mind domains — points past coaching into therapeutic territory. See the Scope Boundary section.

## The Five Questions

---

### Question 1 — Orientation *(use with every client; all domains)*

> *"When you think about your relationship with God right now — not how you want it to be, not how it should be, but how it actually is — what word would you use to describe it?"*

**What to listen for:**

| Word or Pattern | Domain Indicator |
|---|---|
| Distant, one-sided, formal, transactional | Mind — internal working model is cold or absent |
| Flat, numb, going through motions | Affections — blocked reception; possible depletion |
| Wrestling, guilty, inconsistent, complicated | Will — divided commitment; possible held territories |
| Fine. Private. Not really something I think about. | Relational — love is contained, not extended outward |
| Empty, burned out, like it used to mean something | Affections — depletion pattern; common in female clients at this stage |
| Hurt. Angry. Hard to describe. | Mind + Affections — unprocessed grief or wound; possible therapeutic flag |

This question will not give you a clean diagnosis. It gives you a direction. Follow the affect, not the word.

---

### Question 2 — Mind Domain

> *"When things are hard and you haven't heard from God — what do you usually assume? That you've done something wrong, that He's busy with bigger things, that He doesn't care, or that He's working even when you can't see it?"*

**What to listen for:**

The answer that comes fastest is the functional model — not the theological statement the client is capable of producing on command, but the operating assumption that runs underneath it. A client who can quote Romans 8:28 but immediately says *I assume I've done something wrong* is a mind-domain client. The distortion is in the assumption, not in the stated belief.

**Domain indicators:**
- *"I've done something wrong"* → performance-based picture of God; Lie Eliminator entry needed on God's regard specifically
- *"He's busy with bigger things"* → smallness or insignificance lie; connects to identity work; common in female clients who have spent decades in supporting roles
- *"He doesn't care"* → possible wound projection onto God; look for history with absent, neglectful, or conditional human authority; this is a flag for potential therapeutic referral
- *"He's working even when I can't see it"* → theologically correct answer; if it comes instantly, press: *"Does that feel true, or do you know it's true?"* The gap between those two is the affections domain

**When to flag for referral:** If the God-picture has been shaped by a significant human relationship — a harsh or absent parent, an abusive authority, a pastor who caused harm — and the projection is persistent and severe, this is past coaching scope. The picture of God was formed by someone who was not God. Cognitive tools can help at the margins. The core requires a therapist.

---

### Question 3 — Affections Domain

> *"When you think about God's love for you specifically — not for Christians in general, not for good people, but for you right now in your actual life — does that feel real? Or does it land more like something you know is true but can't quite feel?"*

**What to listen for:**

Most self-identified Christians at this age will have a theological answer immediately available: *Yes, I know God loves me.* Do not stop there. The diagnostic value is in the distinction between knowing it and receiving it — and most clients at this stage have been running on the theological version for years without the received version ever landing.

**Domain indicators:**
- *"I know it but it doesn't feel real"* → classic affections blockage; obstacle removal is the entry; ask the sub-question below
- *"I don't think I believe it the way I used to"* → mind-domain erosion driving affections gap; experience has outpaced theology
- *"Honestly, I haven't thought about it in a while"* → depletion pattern; receptive capacity has been underused or depleted; common in clients who have been serving others for decades; love has been going out, not coming in
- *"Yes, I feel it"* → follow up with the relational question; clients who receive love well but don't extend it are often relational-domain blocked

**The obstacle sub-question** *(use when this domain is primary):*

> *"What gets in the way of that love landing? If you had to name it — shame, distance, doubt, exhaustion, something else — what comes first?"*

**Rocky Soil warning — carry this into every affections conversation:**

Do not frame the goal of this work as *feeling more love for God.* A client who pursues emotional experience as the target will cycle through emotional highs and crashes and call it spiritual growth. That is the Rocky Soil pattern, and this framework must not reproduce it. Frame the work as: *What is blocking reception? Let's remove that.* The emotional experience of love is occasional fruit of that clearing. It is not a target. Keep this framing consistent in every client-facing conversation about the affections domain.

**When to flag for referral:** When the inability to receive love is rooted in early abandonment, severe neglect, or abuse, the obstacle is traumatic — not a discipleship gap. Coaching can work on beliefs and practices. It cannot repair attachment wounds. Know the difference and refer.

---

### Question 4 — Will Domain

> *"Is there an area in your life where, if you're honest, you're managing the outcome yourself — not because you've decided against God, but because trusting Him with that specific thing feels too risky or too slow?"*

**What to listen for:**

The held territory is almost always in a small number of categories: financial security, vocational control, a marriage, a child's future, physical health. A client who is operating in the will domain will recognize the question immediately — there will be a pause, a small recognition, sometimes a deflecting laugh. That is the territory.

**Domain indicators:**
- *Names a specific area quickly* → will domain is primary; the territory is known, at least partially; implementation intention work and MI evocation apply
- *"I've been working on surrendering that"* → partially released; the question is whether the work is genuine release or managed compliance; press gently: *"What would it mean if God actually took it from here?"*
- *"I can't think of anything"* → either full surrender (rare) or the territory is defended enough that they can't see it; follow with: *"What would be hardest to surrender if God asked for it directly?"*
- *"It's been this way for a long time"* → calcified will; this territory has been held for years or decades; progress here will be slow and requires sustained work before movement appears

**Gender note:**

Male clients most often name work, finances, or a specific outcome they've been controlling. Female clients most often name a person — a child, a spouse, a parent — and the control mechanism is relational rather than strategic: over-functioning, protecting, managing the emotional environment. Both are held territories. Both require the same coaching move: help the client name what they are being promised by holding it, and what it is costing them.

**The diagnostic question beneath the question:**

> *"What does holding that territory protect you from?"*

The answer to that is the actual obstruction. The territory is the symptom. What it protects is the wound or fear beneath.

---

### Question 5 — Relational Domain

> *"Think about the people you spend the most time with — your spouse, your family, your closest friend. Are you genuinely present with them, or are you more often performing the role well while being somewhere else in your head?"*

**What to listen for:**

High-functioning clients at this stage are typically very good at performing presence. The question is whether the performance has quietly replaced the thing itself. This question frequently produces a moment of recognition rather than defensiveness — they know. They have known for a while.

**Domain indicators:**
- *"I'm there but distracted"* → ordinary relational deficit; behavioral activation work applies; specific presence practices as starting point
- *"I'm more of a problem-solver than a companion"* → service-organized relationships; the love is real but expressed as function rather than presence
- *"I've been told I'm distant even when I'm in the room"* → relational pattern with history; often ties back to affections domain — a person who cannot receive love cannot be fully present to another person
- *"I'm very present with people but God feels distant"* → relational capacity intact; the obstruction is vertical, not horizontal; redirect to mind or affections domain
- *"I've given so much to others that I'm not sure I have anything left"* → depletion driving relational unavailability; this is an affections-domain entry point, not primarily relational

**The 1 John circularity — do not skip this:**

Relational love and interior love are not sequential. 1 John makes them co-constitutive: you grow in love for God through love for neighbor; love for neighbor is fed by love for God. If the relational domain is primary, the coaching move is not *first fix the interior, then extend outward.* The neighbor-love work *is* interior formation. Obedience forms love; love motivates obedience. Acts of love toward others — before they feel like love — reshape the affective disposition over time. Make this loop explicit. The client doing relational work is not waiting to be ready. They are becoming ready by doing it.

## Domain-to-Tool Map

| Domain | Primary Existing Tool | Specific Application |
|---|---|---|
| Mind | Lie Eliminator | Deploy on beliefs about God's character specifically — not only on self-beliefs |
| Mind | Identity Installer | Entries establishing who God is in relation to this client; received-love architecture |
| Mind | Attachment prayer exercise | "What do I expect from You right now?" — surfaces the functional model |
| Affections | Soils Framework (somatic anchoring, Part 7) | Creates conditions for reception; does not generate the emotion |
| Affections | Gratitude practice | Tracking specific evidence of God's action — not generic gratitude |
| Affections | Lament prayer | Permission to bring unprocessed grief and protest to God; Psalms 22, 88 as templates |
| Will | Implementation intentions (Gollwitzer) | If-then plans for specific surrender practices; surrender as a scheduled act, not a general posture |
| Will | MI evocation | Evoke the client's own voice for why surrendering the territory matters to them; do not argue them toward it |
| Relational | Behavioral activation | Specific love-acts prescribed before feeling them; James KA Smith's liturgy principle: obedience forms love |
| Relational | Conflict engagement | Reconciliation is the concrete form of neighbor-love most often avoided at this stage |
| Relational | Forgiveness work | Offering and receiving; the most emotionally demanding form and the highest-formation one |

## Scope Boundary Markers

Coaching operates in all four domains — within defined limits.

**Flag for referral when:**

*Mind domain:* The God-picture has been significantly shaped by trauma with a human authority figure — parent, pastor, spouse. If the projection is severe and persistent and does not respond to belief-restructuring work over two to three sessions, this requires a therapist. Lie Eliminator entries help at the margins; attachment wounds require repair at the root.

*Affections domain:* The inability to receive love is rooted in early abandonment, abuse, or significant neglect. This is not a discipleship gap — it is a traumatic blockage. Coaching can work on beliefs, practices, and conditions. It cannot repair what attachment wounds broke. Know the threshold and refer clearly.

*Will domain:* The control structure being discussed is covering an active addiction, compulsive behavior pattern, or dissociative symptom. These require clinical support. Naming the territory and working on surrender is coaching-appropriate only when the client is functioning in a range that coaching can address.

*Relational domain:* Conflict engagement or forgiveness work surfaces acute grief, trauma disclosure, or suicidal ideation. The coach's role at that point is crisis connection, not continued session work.

**Coaching-safe territory in every domain:**
- Belief restructuring using the Lie Eliminator format
- Condition-creation practices (solitude, gratitude, attention practices, lament)
- Implementation intention work for specific surrender practices
- Behavioral activation for neighbor-love practices
- Framing the work consistently as obstacle removal and condition creation — never as love generation
