# Narrative & Story — We Are Storied Creatures

The belief and practice files treat formation as primarily propositional and behavioral. A Christian psychologist and theologian would say: identity is fundamentally *narrative*. We do not simply hold beliefs and perform practices — we inhabit a story. And the story we believe we are living determines everything about how we interpret our experience, what we do with our wounds, what our suffering means, and who we understand ourselves to be.

> "God authors our stories. Our calling is to read them well." — Dan Allender

---

## The Core Claim

Humans are not primarily belief-holding, behavior-performing machines. We are **story-inhabiting creatures**. We make sense of our lives through narrative — through a beginning, a middle, and an anticipated end that gives the present meaning. The story we believe we are in is more fundamental than any individual belief we hold, because it is the framework that gives all other beliefs their meaning.

If the story is wrong, correct individual beliefs float disconnected. If the story is right — if we know who we are, where we are in the plot, and how it ends — everything else orients correctly.

---

## Biblical Support

### 1. The Bible Is Structured as Story, Not Systematic Theology

Scripture does not primarily present God's truth as a set of propositions to be organized and memorized. It presents it as a **drama unfolding over time** — with characters, conflict, crisis, and resolution.

- Creation (Act 1)
- Fall (Act 2)
- Israel (Act 3)
- Jesus (Act 4 — the climax)
- Church and New Creation (Act 5 — where we now live)

This is not incidental. God chose narrative as his primary revelation form because humans are storied creatures who understand themselves through story.

---

### 2. "Remember" — Identity Maintained Through Story

The single most repeated command in the Old Testament is some form of **remember**. Israel's identity was not maintained through abstract theology but through the rehearsal of the story.

| Verse | Content |
|---|---|
| **Deut 6:20–25** | When your children ask "What do these commands mean?" — you tell them the story: "We were slaves in Egypt, and the LORD brought us out" — identity is transmitted narratively |
| **Ps 78:1–7** | "I will open my mouth with a parable... We will tell the next generation the praiseworthy deeds of the LORD... so the next generation would know them" — identity is passed through story, not propositions |
| **Ps 136** | The entire psalm is a narrative rehearsal — "He led his people through the wilderness... his love endures forever" — corporate identity maintained through repeated storytelling |
| **Deut 26:5–9** | The first-fruits confession — "A wandering Aramean was my father..." — every Israelite was required to identify themselves *within* the story, not merely assent to it |

---

### 3. Name Changes as Narrative Identity Changes

In Scripture, a name change signals a story change — a new role, a new identity, a new chapter in the plot.

| Character | Before | After | Story Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Abram** | "Exalted father" | Abraham — "Father of many nations" (Gen 17:5) | New role in God's story |
| **Sarai** | Unknown | Sarah — "Princess" (Gen 17:15) | Included in the covenant story |
| **Jacob** | "Deceiver" | Israel — "He who strives with God" (Gen 32:28) | Wounds and wrestling become the story's defining moment |
| **Simon** | "Reed" | Peter — "Rock" (Matt 16:18) | Identity replaced by what he will become in the story |
| **Saul** | Persecutor | Paul — "Small/humble" | Old story died; new story began on the Damascus road |

**Implication:** When God changes a person, he often changes their story — not just their beliefs or behaviors.

---

### 4. Jesus Retells Stories to Transform People

Jesus's primary teaching method is not proposition — it is narrative reframe.

| Text | What He Does |
|---|---|
| **Luke 24:13–35** | Road to Emmaus — the disciples have a broken story ("We had hoped he was the one to redeem Israel"). Jesus walks with them and *retells the entire story of Israel* with himself at the center. Their hearts burn. Their eyes open. The story change produces the transformation |
| **Luke 15** | Three lost-and-found parables — Jesus reframes who God is (the father who runs) and who sinners are (not enemies but lost sons) by telling a story, not making an argument |
| **John 4:1–26** | The Samaritan woman — Jesus retells her story: "You have had five husbands, and the man you now have is not your husband." He names the real story before offering the new one |
| **Luke 19:1–9** | Zacchaeus — Jesus enters his story physically ("I must stay at your house today") before Zacchaeus's story changes ("Today salvation has come to this house") |

---

### 5. Paul's Repeated Retelling of His Own Story

Paul does not argue his way to credibility — he retells his story.

| Text | Content |
|---|---|
| **Acts 22:1–21** | Paul's defense before Jerusalem — he tells his story |
| **Acts 26:1–23** | Before Agrippa — he tells his story again |
| **Gal 1:11–24** | Establishing his authority — he tells his story |
| **Phil 3:4–11** | Countering the Judaizers — he tells his story |

The story is the argument. The story is the formation tool. Paul understands that people are changed by inhabiting a narrative, not by winning a debate.

---

### 6. The Testimony as a Weapon and Formation Tool

| Verse | Content |
|---|---|
| **Rev 12:11** | "They triumphed over him by the blood of the Lamb and by the word of their *testimony*" — the story you tell about your life is a weapon against the enemy. The testimony is not incidental to victory; it is a mechanism of it |
| **Ps 66:16** | "Come and hear, all you who fear God; let me tell you what he has done for me" — the story of God's action in a life is an act of worship and community formation |
| **Ps 34:2** | "I will glory in the LORD; let the afflicted hear and rejoice" — the told story forms others who hear it |

---

### 7. The New Creation as a New Story

| Verse | Content |
|---|---|
| **2 Cor 5:17** | "If anyone is in Christ, the new creation has come: the old has gone, the new is here!" — salvation is not merely a belief update or a behavioral reset. It is a story change. The old story is ended; a new one has begun |
| **Eph 2:1–10** | "As for you, you were dead... but God..." — the gospel is presented as a narrative turn: this was the story, *but God* entered it and changed everything |
| **Rev 21:5** | "I am making everything new" — the ultimate eschatological promise is narrative: the story does not end in tragedy. It ends in renewal |

---

### 8. The Story Must Be Large Enough to Hold Suffering

| Verse | Content |
|---|---|
| **Rom 8:18** | "I consider that our present sufferings are not worth comparing with the glory that will be revealed in us" — suffering is bearable when the story is large enough to contain it |
| **Rom 8:28** | "In all things God works for the good of those who love him" — the promise is not that all things are good, but that God is writing them into a coherent story that ends in good |
| **2 Cor 4:17** | "Our light and momentary troubles are achieving for us an eternal glory that far outweighs them all" — suffering has meaning only within a story large enough to give it proportionality |
| **Ps 22** | Moves from "Why have you forsaken me?" to "He has not despised the affliction of the afflicted" — the psalm itself enacts a narrative reframe *within the suffering*, not after it |

## Psychological and Theological Support

### Dan Allender — *To Be Told*
"God is the author of your story. Your calling is to read it well — and to write the next chapter in accordance with the one who began it." Allender argues that the single most important formation task is learning to read your own story honestly — the harm done to you, the harm you have done, the goodness present, and the redemptive threads running through it. Clients who cannot read their story cannot be formed; they are living inside a narrative they have never examined.

---

### N.T. Wright — *The New Testament and the People of God*
The five-act drama framework: Christians are called to improvise faithfully in Act 5, knowing how Acts 1–4 have unfolded and how Act 5 will end. The entire Christian moral life is an improvisational performance within a known narrative. Virtue, identity, and decision-making all require knowing *what story you are in*.

---

### Paul Ricoeur — Narrative Identity Theory
We are the characters in the stories we tell about ourselves. Identity is not a fixed essence — it is a narrative achievement. "Who am I?" is answered by "What is my story?" Change the story, change the person. The gospel functions precisely this way: it offers a new story large enough, true enough, and good enough to reorganize the self.

---

### Eugene Peterson
"A story is the universe made personal." Peterson's entire pastoral theology — *The Message*, the *Under the Unpredictable Plant*, the *Working the Angles* trilogy — is built on the conviction that pastoral work is fundamentally narrative work: helping people inhabit the right story rather than the small, self-authored stories they default to.

---

### Bessel van der Kolk — *The Body Keeps the Score*
Trauma disrupts narrative coherence. Traumatized people lose the ability to tell a coherent story of what happened to them — the experience is stored in fragments, not integrated into a narrative. Healing involves the reconstruction of a coherent narrative. This is why storytelling, testimony, and memory work are healing practices, not merely reflective ones.

## Key Synthesis

### The Two Story Problems Every Client Has

| Problem | Description |
|---|---|
| **Wrong story** | The story they believe about themselves is false — authored by wounds, lies, and shame rather than by God |
| **Small story** | The story is too small to hold their suffering, their calling, or their God — so suffering collapses into meaninglessness and calling collapses into ambition |

### The Gospel as Story Replacement

The gospel does not merely add new beliefs to the existing story. It ends the old story and begins a new one (2 Cor 5:17). The old story: orphaned, condemned, purposeless, alone. The new story: adopted, justified, called, accompanied.

But clients often hold the new theology while still *living inside the old story* — because the story was installed before the theology, through experience and relationship rather than through information. The coaching task is to help them inhabit the story they claim to believe.

### The Coaching Practice

- Ask "What story are you telling yourself about this situation?"
- Ask "What story are you telling yourself about who you are?"
- Ask "Is that story true? Is it large enough?"
- Help clients locate their specific experience within the larger biblical narrative — not abstractly but personally
- Testimony is not just a church practice — it is a formation tool. Helping clients articulate what God has done in their story is identity work

### The Diagnostic Question

*What story is this client living inside?* Not what do they believe propositionally, but what narrative gives their life its shape — who is the protagonist, what is the threat, what is the hoped-for resolution, and where is God in the plot?
