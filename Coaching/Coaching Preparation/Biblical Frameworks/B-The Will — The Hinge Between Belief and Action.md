# The Will — The Hinge Between Belief and Action

The belief file and the practice file between them describe what we think and what we do. But neither fully accounts for the faculty that stands between them: **the will** — the capacity to choose, to initiate, to direct attention, to consent or refuse. A person can believe the right things and perform the right practices while still having a will that continually chooses against both. The will is distinct from belief and distinct from habit. It must be addressed directly.

> "Do you want to get well?" — John 5:6

Jesus does not begin with a diagnosis or a prescription. He addresses the will first.

---

## The Core Claim

The will is not automatically governed by belief, and it does not automatically produce practice. It is the hinge between them — and it can be broken, enslaved, divided, or misdirected. Formation that does not address the will directly will eventually stall, because a person can know the truth and choose against it, can perform disciplines externally while willing against them internally, and can remain stuck not for lack of knowledge or effort but for lack of genuine willingness.

### 2. The Divided Will — The Will's Primary Problem

The New Testament's most penetrating analysis of the human condition is the divided will — the will that is simultaneously drawn toward God and toward sin, producing incoherence rather than direction.

| Verse | Content |
|---|---|
| **Rom 7:15–19** | "I do not understand what I do. For what I want to do I do not do, but what I hate I do... For I have the desire to do what is good, but I cannot carry it out. For I do not do the good I want to do, but the evil I do not want to do — this I keep on doing" — the most precise biblical description of the divided will |
| **Matt 26:41** | "The spirit is willing, but the flesh is weak" — willingness at one level, weakness at another. The will is not singular |
| **Gal 5:17** | "For the flesh desires what is contrary to the Spirit, and the Spirit what is contrary to the flesh. They are in conflict with each other, so that you are not to do whatever you want" — the divided will is the experiential result of the flesh-Spirit conflict |
| **Matt 6:24** | "No one can serve two masters" — the divided will cannot function. It must be unified or it will default to whatever is stronger |
| **James 1:8** | "A double-minded man is unstable in all he does" — divided will = instability across all domains of life |
| **James 4:8** | "Purify your hearts, you double-minded" — the remedy for a divided will is purification, not more information or more effort |

## Theological Support

### Dallas Willard — *Renovation of the Heart*
Willard describes six dimensions of the human soul that must be transformed: mind, will, body, emotions, social, and spirit. He treats the will as the **deepest self** — the executive center of the person. "The will is the capacity to initiate, to refrain, to reflect on and evaluate action."

Key insight: **the will cannot be directly commanded into change**. You cannot simply will yourself to believe differently or to feel differently. But you can will to engage in *practices* that place you in the position where God can change your will. This is the logic of spiritual disciplines — they are not achievements of the will; they are acts of the will that create the conditions for transformation.

---

### Gerald May — *Addiction and Grace*
May argues that the human will is fundamentally addicted — attached to created goods in a way that competes with attachment to God. "We are all addicts in every sense of the word." The will cannot free itself from its attachments by willpower — that is circular. Liberation comes through grace that loosens attachment at the root level, gradually reorienting the will toward its proper object. May's framework maps directly onto Paul's "slave to sin" language.

---

### John Owen — *On the Mortification of Sin*
Mortification is the willful engagement of the believer in putting sin to death — "by the Spirit." Owen insists on both sides: the Spirit is the agent, but the believer must engage. "Do you mortify? Do it by the Spirit." The will must actively cooperate with the Spirit's work. Passive waiting is not mortification — it is neglect.

---

### Augustine — *Confessions*
Augustine's conversion is famously a story of the will, not the intellect. He knew the truth for years before he chose it. "Grant me chastity and continence, but not yet." The will's resistance to what the mind knows is the central drama of his spiritual biography. His eventual surrender: "Our heart is restless until it rests in Thee" — the will finding its proper resting place.

## Key Synthesis

### The Will's Three Problems

| Problem | Description | Biblical Term |
|---|---|---|
| **Divided** | Simultaneously pulled toward God and away from God | Double-minded (James 1:8) |
| **Enslaved** | Repeated choices have produced bondage to sin/pattern | Slave to sin (John 8:34) |
| **Deflected** | Genuine desire for God but energy consistently directed elsewhere | Loves the world (1 John 2:15) |

### The Will's Formation Path

1. **Honest diagnosis** — identify what the will is actually choosing, not what it intends to choose
2. **Willing to be willing** — sometimes the will cannot choose God directly but can choose to ask God to make it willing (Ps 51:10, Phil 2:13)
3. **Deliberate practice** — engage disciplines that train the will through repeated exercise (1 Tim 4:7)
4. **Surrender** — the will's ultimate act: "Not my will but yours" (Matt 26:39)
5. **Ongoing cooperation with the Spirit** — the will cooperates with what the Spirit is already doing (Rom 8:13)

### Coaching Application

- "Do you want to get well?" is a legitimate coaching question — not cruel, but necessary
- Clients who are stuck often have a will problem, not a belief or practice problem — they know what to do and could do it, but are not willing
- Secondary gain (what the client gets from staying stuck) is a will issue: the will is choosing the comfort of the familiar over the cost of change
- The coaching question: *What is your will actually choosing, beneath what you say you want?*
- Sometimes the most honest prayer a client can pray is: "God, make me willing to be willing"
