# Greatest Commandment — Client-Facing Vocabulary
## Affections Domain and the "As Yourself" Component
*Coach reference — language to use and avoid when working these two domains with clients*

---

## Why This Document Exists

Two parts of the Greatest Commandment framework are most likely to break down in client-facing conversation — not because the concepts are wrong but because the natural language for them produces the wrong result.

"Affections" is the right technical term for the domain, and it is meaningless or clinical to most clients. Worse, any language that implies "feel more love for God" will produce precisely the pattern the framework is designed to interrupt in Rocky Soil clients. The vocabulary has to carry the correct frame or it actively works against the work.

"As yourself" is the most mishandled phrase in the commandment. In a self-help culture, it reads as a command to develop self-love — a therapeutic concept that triggers resistance in male clients and guilt in female clients who've been serving others for decades. For shame-based clients it is functionally unusable: their "as yourself" standard is neglect and criticism, and the commandment tells them to extend that to their neighbor. None of those outcomes serve formation.

This document gives you the language that works — and the language that doesn't, and why.

---

## Part 1: The Affections Domain

### What It Is (Coach's Internal Understanding)

The affections domain is the client's capacity to receive love — specifically, to receive God's love in a way that is more than intellectual assent. It covers the emotional experience of being loved, the interior openness to let God's love actually land, and the conditions that allow or block that reception.

The governing theological reality: love for God is the first fruit of the Spirit (Galatians 5:22). It is produced by the Spirit's presence in cleared ground, not generated by human effort. The human role in the affections domain is obstacle removal and condition creation — identifying what blocks reception and clearing it, and creating the practices that make reception possible. The Spirit produces the love. The client creates conditions.

This distinction is not negotiable in client-facing work. Any vocabulary that implies the client is trying to produce or increase their love for God will generate the wrong behavior in affectively-oriented clients and the wrong frame in all clients.

---

### What to Call It With Clients

Do not use the word "affections" in client-facing conversation. It reads as clinical or archaic.

**Use instead:**
- *"The receiving side"* — simple, functional, directionally correct
- *"Your capacity to receive"* — slightly more expansive, same direction
- *"How love actually lands"* — colloquial, concrete
- *"The interior side"* — acceptable for clients with some depth vocabulary

The framing that orients clients to the domain without producing the wrong goal:

> *"There are two sides to every relationship. There's the giving side — what you extend outward. And there's the receiving side — what you're able to take in. Most people who've been in church for twenty years are much more developed on the giving side than the receiving side. They serve, they give, they show up. What they're less practiced at is actually letting love land on them. That's what we're looking at here — not whether you should feel more, but what's getting in the way of reception."*

This framing does three things: it normalizes the imbalance (most people in this demographic are giving-side developed), it names the domain correctly (receiving, not feeling), and it establishes the right direction of work (obstacle removal, not emotional attainment).

---

### The Core Reframe — Deliver This Before Any Affections Work

Before introducing any practice, condition, or intervention in this domain, deliver this reframe. It sets the governing frame that prevents the Rocky Soil drift.

> *"We're not trying to get you to feel more love for God. That's not actually in your control — and trying to generate it by effort tends to produce a performance cycle where you're monitoring your feelings to see if you're doing it right. What is in your control is removing what's blocking reception and creating the conditions where love can take root. God's love toward you is constant. The question is what's getting in the way of it landing."*

This reframe — love as something to receive, not generate — is the frame that must hold across every affections session. If you drift from it, the client will drift toward emotional attainment as the target.

---

### Language to Use

| Phrase | Why It Works |
|---|---|
| *"What's blocking reception"* | Obstacle removal frame; client is clearing, not generating |
| *"What's getting in the way of it landing"* | Same as above; slightly more concrete |
| *"Create the conditions where love can take root"* | Condition creation frame; uses agricultural language that fits the Soils vocabulary |
| *"God's love toward you is constant — this is about your side of the reception"* | Anchors the work in God's prior action; removes performance pressure |
| *"The receiving side of this"* | Simple domain name; pair it with "the giving side" for contrast |
| *"Let it land"* | Simple, physical; avoids clinical vocabulary |
| *"What would have to clear for this to be real to you, not just true to you?"* | The key diagnostic for intellectual-only reception; opens the obstacle question naturally |

---

### Language to Avoid

| Phrase | Why It Fails |
|---|---|
| *"Feel more love for God"* | Direct Rocky Soil trap; frames emotional experience as the target |
| *"Grow in love for God"* | Implies love generation; sounds like effort produces the fruit |
| *"Develop your affections"* | Both vocabulary and frame are wrong; produces effort-based pursuit |
| *"Work on your emotional life with God"* | Too therapeutic; triggers male resistance |
| *"Cultivate love"* | Sounds like effort produces the product; Willard specifically warned against this misread of disciplines |
| *"Your feelings toward God"* | Reduces the domain to emotional weather; reinforces the monitoring dynamic |
| *"Try harder to connect emotionally"* | Produces exactly the performance cycle the reframe is designed to prevent |

---

### When Clients Use the Wrong Frame

Clients will arrive using phrases that embed the wrong goal. Here is how to redirect without making them feel corrected.

**Client says:** *"I just want to feel closer to God."*

**Redirect:**
> *"That makes complete sense. What we're going to ask is: what's getting in the way? Because what God is extending toward you is constant — it's not like He's moved. The question is what's on your side that's blocking the landing. When we find that and clear it, the closeness tends to follow on its own."*

**Client says:** *"I need to work on loving God more."*

**Redirect:**
> *"Love for God isn't something you build by effort — it's more like a fruit that grows when the soil conditions are right. What you can work on is what's blocking the conditions. What do you think gets in the way of God's love actually reaching you?"*

**Client says:** *"I just don't feel anything when I pray."*

**Redirect (depends on soil type — see soil differentiation document):**
> *"Tell me more about what 'nothing' feels like. Is it flat, distracted, like you're talking to a ceiling? Because those are actually different things pointing to different places. What does the nothing feel like?"*

This last redirect diagnoses the specific obstacle rather than accepting "nothing" as a feelings report. The obstacle is named in the answer.

## Part 2: The "As Yourself" Component

### What It Actually Means (Coach's Internal Understanding)

"Love your neighbor as yourself" (Matthew 22:39) is read by NT scholars including D.A. Carson, John Stott, and N.T. Wright as a comparative standard, not a developmental command. Jesus is not saying "first develop self-love, then extend it." He is saying: "You already know how to attend to your own needs — direct that same natural attentiveness toward your neighbor."

The force is not "develop love for yourself." It is "extend to others the care you already naturally extend to yourself." The assumption embedded in the commandment is that basic self-care is a natural given — not something to be built, but a reference point already present.

This creates two problems in coaching:

**Problem 1 — The self-help misread.** In a therapeutic culture, "as yourself" reads as a command to develop self-love before extending love outward. This triggers resistance in male clients (soft/therapeutic) and guilt in female clients who interpret it as permission to prioritize themselves over others they've been serving for decades.

**Problem 2 — The shame inversion.** For clients with deep shame, the "as yourself" standard is not a positive reference point. Their natural self-regard is harsh self-criticism, neglect, or absence. Telling them to love their neighbor "as themselves" doesn't lift them — it gives them a floor they're already below.

The coaching vocabulary must route around both problems.

---

### The Core Reframe — Deliver This Before Using the Command

Never introduce "love your neighbor as yourself" without this reframe already in place. Without it, the client is working from one of the two problem readings above.

> *"The second part of the commandment — 'love your neighbor as yourself' — tends to get read as 'develop self-love first.' That's actually not what it means. Jesus was using how you naturally care for yourself as a reference point: you already know how to attend to your own needs. Do the same for your neighbor.*
>
> *The more important question for where you are is this: are you able to receive the love God is already extending to you? Because the love the commandment describes flows from received love — not from love you generate from inside yourself. 1 John 4:19 is explicit: we love because He first loved us. The direction is from Him to us first, then outward. If that first movement is blocked, the second one has nothing to flow from."*

This reframe does three things:
1. Corrects the self-help misread without dismissing it
2. Establishes the biblical anchor (1 John 4:19) for the correct direction
3. Shifts the question to the most actionable place for this demographic: *can you receive?*

---

### Language to Use

| Phrase | Why It Works |
|---|---|
| *"Receive the love God is already extending to you"* | The governing reframe; ground everything here |
| *"Love from the place where you know you're loved"* | Locates the "as yourself" reference point in God's love, not self-regard |
| *"The foundation that makes the second command possible"* | Names received love as structural prerequisite, not therapeutic project |
| *"Your receive side"* | Already established in affections vocabulary; consistent language |
| *"You can't give from empty"* | Concrete, non-therapeutic; points to structural need without blame |
| *"The love flows from Him to you first — then outward"* | Directional clarity; avoids self-generated love framing |

---

### Language to Avoid

| Phrase | Why It Fails |
|---|---|
| *"Love yourself"* | Never use this phrase with this demographic. Full stop. Triggers theological resistance, therapeutic suspicion, or — for shame-based clients — produces an instruction they cannot execute |
| *"Self-care"* | Therapeutic vocabulary; triggers resistance in male clients; triggers guilt in female clients at this stage |
| *"You deserve to receive love"* | Entitlement framing; theologically incorrect; will be rejected by clients with any evangelical background |
| *"You have to fill your own cup first"* | Self-help cliché; frames the entire dynamic incorrectly |
| *"Your needs matter too"* | Counseling language; triggers the "this isn't a therapy session" response |
| *"Be kind to yourself"* | Same register as above; produces distance, not engagement |

---

### The Shame Variant

For clients where shame is a primary presenting dynamic — visible through self-critical language, performance orientation, the belief that their needs don't register — the "as yourself" reframe requires an additional move.

The shame-based client's comparative standard is already negative. Telling them to love their neighbor "as themselves" either reinforces the shame (love your neighbor the way you treat yourself — with criticism and neglect) or produces confusion. Neither advances the work.

**Do not introduce the "as yourself" language directly with shame-based clients.** Use this instead:

> *"You are trying to love your neighbor from a position where you haven't yet received what makes that possible. That's not a character problem — it's structural. The source is blocked. Before the second command can become real, we need to ask: what is getting in the way of God's love actually reaching you? Not intellectually — you know it's true. Landing, in your actual interior."*

Then follow with the affections-domain vocabulary above. The "as yourself" formulation becomes accessible after the receiving deficit is addressed — not as a prerequisite to fix before returning to the commandment, but as the specific place where the blockage lives.

The Lie Eliminator is the primary tool for shame-based clients in this domain: entries specifically targeting beliefs about God's regard for this client — not only beliefs about the client's self-worth, but who God is in relation to the client's specific failure and inadequacy. This is where the mind-domain and the affections domain overlap in a shame presentation.

---

### Gender Differentiation

**Male clients at 45-55:**

"Love yourself" lands as either theologically suspicious ("Is that actually in the Bible?") or therapeutically coded ("That sounds like something my wife's counselor would say"). Both responses close the conversation.

The frame that works for male clients is relational rather than self-focused: this is not about their feelings about themselves — it is about whether they have allowed God's love to govern them specifically. The language of *receiving* rather than *loving self* avoids the therapeutic register entirely.

The secondary gain for male clients in not receiving: competence and self-sufficiency. Receiving love — particularly from God, where the receiving is interior rather than practical — requires acknowledging need. Performance-oriented men at this stage have spent decades building the interior equivalent of not needing anything. The question that surfaces this concretely:

> *"When was the last time you let someone do something for you without deflecting it, minimizing it, or immediately doing something in return? Not physically — emotionally. When did you last just let something land?"*

The answer is the diagnostic. The pattern they describe in human relationships maps directly onto their receiving posture toward God.

**Female clients at 45-55:**

For women who've spent decades in caretaking, service, and attending to others' needs, "love yourself" triggers a specific guilt response: it sounds like permission to stop serving — which their theology and identity won't allow without significant resistance. It can also read as a therapeutic challenge to a faith orientation they've held for decades: *"I don't need to love myself more — I need to serve better."*

The frame that works: not "attend to your own needs" but "receive before you extend." The structural argument is more effective than the permission argument:

> *"You've been giving for a long time. The question isn't whether you should give less — it's whether you have something to give from. The love the commandment describes flows from received love. If your receive side has been running at a deficit for years, what's going out is not the love the commandment is talking about. It's something else — obligation, or duty, or the last reserves of a depleted system. What would it mean to actually receive before you extend?"*

For women where the receiving deficit presents as exhaustion rather than shame, pair this with the affections-depletion vocabulary: naming that the affective resources have been going out without coming in is not a spiritual failure — it is a condition with a structural cause and a structural intervention.

---

### Introducing This in Session — Sequence

When the "as yourself" component comes up naturally or is worth introducing, use this sequence:

1. **Deliver the reframe first** (see above) — correct the self-help misread and establish 1 John 4:19 as the governing anchor

2. **Move immediately to the diagnostic question:** *"What gets in the way of God's love for you specifically actually landing? Not as theology — as experience."*

3. **Route the answer to the affections-domain vocabulary above** — you are now in the affections domain with the correct framing. The "as yourself" language served as an entry; the work is in the receiving-side obstacle identification

4. **Only return to the neighbor-love component after the receiving question is engaged** — the sequence matters. The commandment's directionality (God's love → received by client → extended to neighbor) is the structural map for the coaching conversation
