# We Become What We Do — Biblical & Theological Support

Both "we become what we believe" and "we become what we do" are true. They are not competing principles — they form a loop.

> **Belief produces action. Action reinforces and deepens belief. Both shape identity. Neither works without the other.**

The danger of separating them is named explicitly in Scripture:
- Belief without action = self-deception (James 1:22)
- Action without belief = legalism — whitewashed tombs (Matt 23:27)

**Cross-link:** See *We Become What We Believe — Biblical & Theological Support* for the complementary half of this framework.

| Practice | Formation Purpose |

| **Circumcision** (Gen 17) | Covenant identity marked on the body — the doing shapes belonging |

### 10. The Loop — Action and Belief Are Inseparable

The heroes of Hebrews 11 are described entirely by what they *did* because of what they believed. The two cannot be separated — their doing was the evidence and expression of their believing, and their doing deepened and confirmed their believing.

| Verse | Content |
|---|---|
| **Heb 11:8** | "By faith Abraham obeyed and went" — belief expressed in action |
| **Heb 11:17** | "By faith Abraham, when God tested him, offered Isaac" — the doing was the proving ground of the believing |
| **James 2:21–22** | "Was not our father Abraham considered righteous for what he did when he offered his son Isaac? You see that his faith and his actions were working together, and his faith was made complete by what he did" — **faith made complete by action** — the doing deepened and completed the believing |

## Theological Support

### Thomas Aquinas — Virtue Ethics
Moral virtues are *habitus* — stable dispositions formed through repeated acts. We do not first become brave and then act bravely; we become brave by repeatedly doing brave things. Grace perfects nature, but nature is formed through practice. Aquinas explicitly says: "Acts produce habits." The Christian life is the formation of virtuous character through Spirit-empowered, repeated action.

---

### Dallas Willard — *The Spirit of the Disciplines*
Spiritual disciplines are not ways to earn grace — they are ways to train the body, mind, and will to be *capable* of what grace calls us to. "We are not transformed by grace alone — we must train." Key insight: Jesus did not just *believe* rightly — he *practiced* solitude, fasting, prayer, Scripture memorization. Those practices formed his human nature for the mission. The disciplines work because the body has a formative influence on the soul.

---

### James K.A. Smith — *You Are What You Love*
Formation happens primarily through *practice*, not proposition. Liturgies, rituals, habits shape desire at a pre-cognitive level — below belief. The body leads, and the mind follows. We are shaped more by what we repeatedly do than by what we consciously believe. The shopping mall, the stadium, the smartphone each form us through repeated embodied practice — before we ever consciously decide what to believe about them.

---

### N.T. Wright — *After You Believe*
Virtue is character formed through repeated choice until it becomes second nature — *instinct*. Wright grounds this eschatologically: we are practicing now for the new creation, becoming people capable of ruling and reigning in God's kingdom. Practice shapes readiness. The Christian is not merely forgiven — they are being *formed* for a role.

---

### Eugene Peterson — *A Long Obedience in the Same Direction*
Character is not formed in a moment or a crisis — it is formed through sustained, repeated faithfulness in ordinary life. The Psalms of Ascent (Ps 120–134) are the pilgrim's formation through repeated practice of worship, remembrance, and trust. Each step of obedience shapes the person who takes the next step.

---

### Alexander Schmemann — *For the Life of the World*
Liturgical practice — especially Eucharist — forms the person who participates in it. The repeated doing of worship transforms who you are at a level deeper than intellectual agreement. Orthodox theology is built on the conviction that embodied, repeated practice is the primary mechanism of human formation.

---

### Jonathan Edwards — *Religious Affections*
The affections are expressed in action, and action reinforces affection. Obedience to God deepens love for God. The doing is not separate from the believing — they form a reinforcing loop. True affections always produce action; and action, when done faithfully, deepens true affection.

## The Full Integration

| Principle | Primary Mechanism | Key Texts |
|---|---|---|
| **We become what we believe** | Belief → interpretation of reality → action → identity | Prov 23:7, Rom 12:2, 2 Cor 3:18 |
| **We become what we do** | Action → habit → character → deepened belief → identity | Heb 5:14, 1 Tim 4:7, James 2:22 |

### The Loop

```
Belief
  ↓
Action
  ↓
Habit (repeated action)
  ↓
Character (stable disposition)
  ↓
Identity
  ↓
Deepened / confirmed Belief
  ↓
More consistent Action...
```

This is the structure of sanctification. It is also the structure of corruption — Romans 1 shows the same loop running in reverse: suppress truth → believe lies → act on lies → habits of depravity → hardened identity → beliefs that call evil good.

### Which Is Primary?

Belief — but only barely, and only at the start. Here is why:

- You can act your way into a new way of believing (John 7:17 — do and you will know)
- But sustained action without belief eventually collapses — it becomes performance, not formation
- The goal is a reinforcing loop where belief and action feed each other
- James 2:22 is the clearest statement: "His faith and his actions were *working together*, and his faith was made complete by what he did"

### Coaching Application

You cannot only address beliefs and skip practices — and you cannot only assign homework without addressing the beliefs that will either fuel or sabotage those practices. Session work (belief) and between-session work (practice) are both essential, and they feed each other. A client who changes their belief but takes no new action will not be transformed. A client who takes new action without addressing the underlying belief will eventually revert.
