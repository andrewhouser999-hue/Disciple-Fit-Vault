# Reiss Motivation Profile — Theory & 16 Basic Desires

## Authorship

Steven Reiss was a psychologist and professor at Ohio State University who spent five years conducting empirical research with over 6,000 people across four continents before publishing his framework. Rather than building theory through philosophical inquiry, psychoanalytic inference, or the study of isolated populations, Reiss grounded his model in large-scale, cross-cultural scientific surveys — a methodological stance he considered essential to getting motivation right.

His central claim: human behavior is organized around 16 universal basic desires — intrinsic, stable drives that are present in everyone but weighted differently from person to person. Most of these desires, he argued, have genetic origins. They are not triggered by deficits or external conditions; they are simply part of what it means to be human. As Reiss put it: *"These desires are what drive our everyday actions and make us who we are. What makes individuals unique is the combination and ranking of these desires."*

With 16 desires that each exist on a spectrum, Reiss estimated approximately 2 trillion possible desire profiles — meaning no two people are motivationally identical.

Several things set RMP apart from competing theories:

- **Against deficit-based models (Maslow):** Reiss rejected the idea that motivation is primarily about filling gaps or climbing a hierarchy. All 16 desires operate simultaneously and are always active — there is no sequence, no ceiling.
- **Against the intrinsic/extrinsic binary:** Reiss directly challenged the prevailing social psychology view that external rewards undermine intrinsic motivation. He argued the so-called "undermining effect" was a trivial distraction artifact with no meaningful scientific significance — not a reliable law of human psychology.
- **Against normative judgment:** One of Reiss's sharpest contributions was his argument that unconventional motivation profiles are not pathologies. A child who is bright but uninterested in school, a person who prefers solitude over socializing, a workaholic who thrives on 70-hour weeks — these are not problems to fix. They are people whose desire profiles simply differ from cultural expectations. *"It's OK to be non-curious,"* Reiss said. *"As long as the child is not flunking and is meeting minimum standards, parents should ease up."*

He coined the term **self-hugging** to describe the universal human tendency to assume others share your desire profile — and to judge or pathologize them when they don't. Much of interpersonal conflict, in Reiss's view, is not about bad character or dysfunction. It is about unrecognized motivational difference.

The goal his framework points toward is what he called **joyful living**: a life structured around alignment between your actual desire profile and your daily choices — work, relationships, routines, and community.

## The 16 Basic Desires

| # | Desire | Core Drive |
|---|--------|------------|
| 1 | **Power** | Influence, leadership, achievement |
| 2 | **Independence** | Self-reliance, freedom from constraint |
| 3 | **Curiosity** | Learning, truth, intellectual exploration |
| 4 | **Acceptance** | Approval, belonging, positive self-image |
| 5 | **Order** | Structure, organization, predictability |
| 6 | **Saving** | Collecting, ownership, frugality |
| 7 | **Honor** | Loyalty, ethics, character, tradition |
| 8 | **Idealism** | Justice, fairness, social causes |
| 9 | **Social Contact** | Companionship, fun, socializing |
| 10 | **Family** | Raising children, domestic life |
| 11 | **Status** | Prestige, wealth, social standing |
| 12 | **Vengeance** | Competition, winning, getting even |
| 13 | **Romance** | Sex, beauty, aesthetic pleasure |
| 14 | **Eating** | Food, hunger satisfaction |
| 15 | **Physical Activity** | Exercise, physical health, sports |
| 16 | **Tranquility** | Emotional calm, safety, freedom from anxiety |

## Coaching Relevance

RMP is useful for identity work and values clarification — understanding why a client feels unfulfilled often comes down to a chronic mismatch between their desire profile and their actual life (job, relationships, routine). Strong alignment = intrinsic motivation and meaning. Strong misalignment = chronic dissatisfaction with no obvious cause.

The concept of self-hugging is particularly valuable in relational and marriage coaching: most conflict is not moral failure — it is two people with different desire profiles assuming the other is wrong for not wanting what they want.
