# Self-Efficacy Theory — Bandura Framework

## The Honest Assessment

Self-efficacy is the single strongest psychological predictor of sustained performance across virtually every domain studied. Albert Bandura's Social Cognitive Theory is one of the most replicated frameworks in behavioral science — not because he had a good idea, but because the research keeps finding it. This is not "believe in yourself" coaching language. It is a specific, mechanistic account of how confidence is built, maintained, and destroyed.

Most coaches confuse self-efficacy with self-esteem. They are not the same. Self-esteem is a global sense of worth. Self-efficacy is task- and domain-specific: a person can have high self-efficacy for public speaking and low self-efficacy for conflict resolution in the same week. Interventions that target self-esteem globally ("you're enough") miss the mechanism entirely. The four sources of self-efficacy tell you exactly where to apply pressure.

---

## Elaborate Overview

### Definition — What Self-Efficacy Actually Is

Self-efficacy is a person's belief in their *capacity to execute the behaviors required to produce specific outcomes* in a specific context. It is not confidence in the abstract. It is task-specific, domain-specific, and context-specific.

Bandura's core claim: self-efficacy beliefs do not just predict performance — they regulate it. A person with low self-efficacy for a task will:
- Avoid it when possible
- Give up sooner when it gets difficult
- Interpret obstacles as confirmation of incapacity
- Experience higher anxiety during the task
- Cognitively rehearse failure rather than success

A person with high self-efficacy will:
- Engage with challenges rather than avoid them
- Persist longer in the face of setbacks
- Interpret obstacles as problems to solve rather than evidence of inability
- Use the difficulty as information, not verdict

**The critical insight for coaching:** Self-efficacy is not simply a reflection of past performance — it is a *determinant* of future performance. Raising it changes outcomes before the behavior has changed. This is where coaching intervention lives.

---

### The Four Sources of Self-Efficacy

Bandura identified four specific mechanisms through which self-efficacy is built or undermined. These are not metaphors — they are the actual pathways. Know them all.

---

#### Source 1: Mastery Experiences (Enactive Attainment)
**The most powerful source.** Direct personal experience of success — particularly in the face of difficulty — is the most reliable builder of self-efficacy. Conversely, failure experiences, particularly early or repeated, are the most powerful destructors.

**The nuance:** Not all success builds efficacy equally. Success achieved with minimal effort on easy tasks produces little efficacy. Success achieved through sustained effort on increasingly difficult tasks — with the struggle visible and survived — produces durable efficacy.

**Coaching application:** Design the client's early action steps as deliberate mastery experiences. Small wins are not trivial — they are efficacy deposits. The sequencing matters: start below the challenge threshold, succeed, then increase difficulty. This is not coddling — it is the biological mechanism of capability-building.

**The failure intervention:** When a client has experienced failure, the question is not *"how do we restore confidence?"* but *"what attribution is the client making about this failure?"* Failure attributed to lack of effort or strategy is recoverable. Failure attributed to fixed ability is corrosive to self-efficacy and requires direct attention.

---

#### Source 2: Vicarious Experiences (Social Modeling)
Observing *similar others* succeed at a task raises self-efficacy for that task. The key word is *similar*: watching a prodigy perform does not raise a novice's efficacy — it may lower it by contrast. Watching someone perceived as similar struggle and then succeed is the mechanism.

**The similarity principle:** "If they can do it, I can do it" — but only when the observer believes the model is like them in relevant ways.

**Coaching application:**
- Use testimony from clients who have done similar work
- Share relevant examples (without violating confidentiality) that match the client's situation
- Draw on Scripture as a source of viable models — *not* superhero figures of faith, but flawed people who moved forward anyway (Moses, Gideon, Peter, David)

---

#### Source 3: Verbal Persuasion (Social Encouragement)
Being told by a credible person that you have what it takes raises self-efficacy — but less reliably than mastery experience and more vulnerable to being undermined by failure. The *credibility* of the source matters enormously. Hollow encouragement ("you've got this!") from a non-credible source produces nothing or backfires.

**The asymmetry:** Negative verbal persuasion (criticism, dismissal, "you'll never be able to") is more damaging to efficacy than positive verbal persuasion is building. It is easier to tear down than build up through words.

**Coaching application:**
- Your role as a credible coach means your affirmations carry real weight — use them specifically, not generically. *"The way you handled that setback last month was evidence of real resilience"* lands differently than *"I believe in you."*
- Help clients identify and interrupt the internal critic — which is often internalized verbal persuasion from past relationships.

---

#### Source 4: Physiological and Affective States
The body's signals during a task are interpreted as information about capability. A person who experiences anxiety before a performance will often interpret that arousal as evidence that they "can't handle it" — which then actually degrades performance, confirming the belief.

**The reappraisal mechanism:** Research by Alison Wood Brooks (Harvard, 2014) shows that reappraising pre-performance anxiety as excitement ("I'm excited" rather than "I'm nervous") significantly improves performance. The arousal signal is the same; the interpretation determines its effect.

**Sleep, health, and efficacy:** Physiological depletion directly reduces efficacy perceptions — a well-slept client's "I can do this" is measurably different from an exhausted client's. This is not metaphor.

**Coaching application:**
- Help clients learn to *interpret* arousal neutrally or positively before high-stakes tasks
- Don't underestimate the physiological baseline — sleep, movement, and nutrition are efficacy inputs
- Somatic awareness work: *"What does your body do before something important? What would it feel like if that sensation meant readiness rather than fear?"*

---

### Self-Efficacy vs. Outcome Expectancy

Bandura distinguishes between:
- **Self-efficacy beliefs:** "Can I execute the behavior?"
- **Outcome expectancy:** "Will executing the behavior produce the outcome I want?"

A client can have high self-efficacy (I know I can do the work) but low outcome expectancy (I don't believe the work will lead anywhere good). Both predict motivation independently. A client stuck despite competence may be suffering from low outcome expectancy — a belief that effort doesn't translate to outcomes. This is despair, not incapacity.

**For Kingdom coaching:** Low outcome expectancy in faith contexts often presents as *"I can pray, I can read, I can do all the right things — but I don't believe God is actually going to show up."* This is a theological and psychological problem simultaneously, and cannot be resolved with efficacy-building alone.

---

### Generalized vs. Domain-Specific Efficacy

Self-efficacy is domain-specific by nature — but there is evidence for *generalized self-efficacy* as a trait-like disposition (Schwarzer, 1992). Clients with chronically low generalized efficacy tend to interpret failure across all domains as evidence of global incapacity. This is not low self-esteem — it is a global negative efficacy schema, and it requires repeated, diverse mastery experiences to shift.

## What to Ignore

- "Fake it till you make it" as an efficacy strategy — false confidence without behavioral grounding does not produce self-efficacy and often backfires when reality intervenes
- Self-efficacy confused with self-esteem — global worth interventions ("you're amazing") do not build domain-specific efficacy and often feel hollow to clients who know they're struggling in a specific area
- Over-relying on verbal persuasion — coaches tend to lean on encouragement because it's comfortable. It is the weakest source. Design mastery experiences; don't just cheer.
- Attributing low performance solely to effort without addressing systemic barriers — Bandura's model accounts for structural constraints; efficacy coaching in a vacuum ignores context

## Self-Directed Therapy/Coaching Questions

### Mapping Current Efficacy
- "In what areas of your life do you feel genuinely capable — where you think 'I know I can do this'? What built that confidence?"
- "In what areas do you consistently avoid, procrastinate, or expect to fail? How long has that been true?"
- "When you fail at something, what is your first internal explanation? Do you blame ability ('I'm just not good at this'), effort ('I didn't try hard enough'), or strategy ('that approach didn't work')?"
- "Is there a gap between what you can *do* technically and what you believe you *can* do? Where did that gap come from?"

### Building the Four Sources
- "What is the most difficult thing you have succeeded at in the last three years? How did you do it? What does that say about you?"
- "Who do you know — similar to you in meaningful ways — who has done what you're trying to do? What can you observe about how they did it?"
- "Who in your life has told you what they genuinely see in you? Have you believed them? If not, why not?"
- "Before an important task, what does your body do? Do you interpret that as readiness or as warning? What would change if you read it as readiness?"

### Addressing Low Outcome Expectancy
- "On a scale of 0–10, how much do you believe your effort will actually lead to the outcome you want? What's making it that number?"
- "Where does your evidence for 'it won't matter' come from? Is that evidence from your current situation, or is it carried from a different one?"
- "Have you ever put in full effort and had it not work? Have you ever put in full effort and had it work? What does the ratio tell you?"

## Coaching Application — In Session

**The diagnostic question:** *"When you imagine actually doing this — not planning it, actually doing it — what number comes up? How confident are you, right now, that you could execute?"* This is a self-efficacy probe. The answer tells you which of the four sources to target.

**Designing mastery experiences:**
- Break large goals into the smallest executable unit that produces a genuine win
- The win must involve some difficulty — too easy produces no efficacy deposit
- Review wins explicitly: *"You did X this week. That was hard. What does it tell you that you did it?"*

**Vicarious modeling in faith contexts:**
- Use Scripture's own mastery narratives — not as inspiration but as evidence. Moses led 2 million people while telling God he couldn't speak. The failure of the confidence argument is documented in the text; so is what happened anyway.

**Handling attribution after failure:**
- *"What do you think that failure means?"* — surface the attribution
- If effort/strategy: reinforce that ability is intact; adjust approach
- If fixed ability: *"Is there evidence that this is a permanent capacity limit, or evidence that this was a specific situation that could change?"* Challenge the attribution directly.

## Bottom Line

Self-efficacy is the mechanism beneath every "I can't" and "what's the point." It is not attitude — it is a specific, measurable belief built through four specific pathways. Every time you design a client's early action steps carelessly, skip reviewing their wins, or rely on generic encouragement, you are working against the mechanism you're supposed to be engaging. Build mastery experiences first. Everything else supports them.
