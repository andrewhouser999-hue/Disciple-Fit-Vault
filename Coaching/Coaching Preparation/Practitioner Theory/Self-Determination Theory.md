# Self-Determination Theory

## The Honest Assessment

SDT is the most replicated motivation framework in psychology. Thousands of studies across sport, healthcare, education, and workplace contexts. If you understand one motivation theory deeply, make it this one — it explains more of what you'll see in coaching than anything else.

The core insight most coaches miss: motivation is a *quality*, not a quantity. A client can be working extremely hard and still be running on the worst kind of motivation. Recognizing the difference is a diagnostic skill, not an intuition.

---

## Tier 1: The Empirical Foundation

### The Three Basic Needs
Every person needs these to be satisfied — not as preferences, but as psychological necessities:
- **Autonomy** — feeling that behavior is self-chosen and volitional. Not the same as independence. A client can follow your structure and still feel autonomous if they understand why and endorse it.
- **Competence** — feeling capable and effective. Requires optimal challenge — too easy or too hard both undermine it.
- **Relatedness** — feeling genuinely connected and valued as a person, not just a performer. The entry point for any change work.

### The Motivation Continuum
From most to least damaging — know where your clients actually sit:

| Style | What Drives It | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|---|
| **Amotivation** | Nothing | Passive, going through motions |
| **External** | Reward / punishment | Works only when watched |
| **Introjected** | Shame / ego protection | Hard-working but driven by fear of failure. *Most common in high-achievers.* |
| **Identified** | Personal importance | Behavior tied to values, not fear |
| **Integrated** | Core identity | Behavior is an expression of self |
| **Intrinsic** | Pure enjoyment | Rare in serious work contexts |

**Autonomous motivation** = identified + integrated + intrinsic. This is the target, not just "intrinsic."

## What to Ignore

- Treating "intrinsic motivation" as the only goal — most professional performance runs on identified/integrated regulation, and that's fine
- Assuming high effort = healthy motivation — introjected clients are often the hardest workers in the room
- Autonomy support as permissiveness — structure and clear expectations are compatible with autonomy when rationale is given

---

## Coaching Application

**Diagnosing regulatory style — ask:**
- *"Why are you doing this?"* — listen for shame, fear, external pressure vs. values and meaning
- *"What would happen if no one was watching?"* — reveals whether behavior is externally maintained
- *"What does this mean to you personally?"* — opens identified and integrated framing

**The introjected client is your most common high-achiever:**
- Appears motivated, works hard, hard on themselves after setbacks
- Relief after wins, not satisfaction — success feels like dodging failure, not achieving something
- Intervention: don't increase their motivation. Reorient it toward values rather than fear.

**Autonomy support in sessions:**
- Give rationale, not just direction
- Acknowledge their frame before offering yours
- Use *"What would feel right to you?"* not *"Here's what you need to do"*

---

## Bottom Line

The client who is terrified of failure and the client who loves what they do can look identical from the outside. The difference shows up in how they handle setbacks, what winning feels like, and how long they last. SDT gives you the map to tell them apart and coach accordingly.

## Without Biblical Framework — Christian Client

**Verdict: Use with significant caution.** SDT's embedded anthropology is the most subtly dangerous tool in the CBC toolkit — not just incomplete, but directionally wrong for Christian formation.

**The three needs are anthropologically incomplete for a Christian:**
- *Autonomy* as the highest motivational need ≠ surrender and submission as the highest Christian posture
- *Competence* as a basic need ≠ calling and stewardship as the proper frame for capability
- *Relatedness* generically ≠ covenant community and union with Christ as the deepest human belonging

**The intrinsic motivation standard is the sharpest conflict.** SDT's gold standard is intrinsic motivation — doing what you value because you genuinely value it. Christian discipleship frequently requires doing what you don't feel intrinsically motivated to do: confession, forgiveness, service, sacrifice, spiritual disciplines. Obedience as a category does not exist in SDT. A coaching model built on SDT will train clients to pursue only what already feels intrinsically rewarding — conformity to the current self, not transformation into Christ.

**Strong inference:** SDT accelerates a client's commitment to a psychologically healthy version of the old self. It produces observable flourishing that can be spiritually misdirected.

**Fix:** Use SDT to diagnose regulatory style (introjected vs. identified/integrated) — that diagnostic is sound and valuable. Do not use it to set the telos of motivation. Replace the intrinsic motivation standard with obedience-informed motivation: *"What does faithfulness call for here, regardless of felt motivation?"*
