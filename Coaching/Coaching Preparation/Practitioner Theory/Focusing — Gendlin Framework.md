# Focusing — Gendlin Framework

**Purpose:** Practitioner theory reference for Focusing (Eugene Gendlin) — the felt sense framework and its application as both an in-session coach skill and a between-session client practice. Gendlin is the most portable of the implicit-level frameworks: it is non-clinical, client-learnable, and directly accessible to formation work.

**See also:** [[Memory Reconsolidation — Ecker & Neuroscience Basis]], [[Covenant Identity — Stage 4b Implicit Level Practitioner Reference]], [[Insecure Attachment — Nervous System Dysregulation]]

---

## Clinical and Pastoral Significance

Most formation tools operate at the level of explicit meaning — they address what a person knows, believes, or declares. The implicit level holds meaning that has not yet reached language: the pre-verbal, body-held sense of a situation, a belief, or an identity that the organism carries before it can articulate any of it.

Focusing is the discipline of attending to that pre-verbal level with patience and precision until it yields its meaning — and shifts.

Unlike somatic approaches that require clinical training (SE, Sensorimotor), Focusing is designed for self-use. Gendlin developed it explicitly as a learnable skill for anyone who wants to access what their organism already knows. This makes it the most directly deployable implicit-level tool in coaching and formation contexts.

---

## The Experiencing Theory Foundation

Gendlin's framework rests on his *experiencing theory* — the philosophical claim that meaning is not primarily conceptual but pre-conceptual. Before a person can articulate anything about a situation, the body-organism already holds the full complexity of it: felt, but not yet formed into thought or language.

This is not mysticism; it is phenomenology. The person who can't explain why something feels wrong — but something clearly does — is carrying pre-verbal meaning. The person who "knows in their gut" before reasoning arrives is accessing this level. The person who cries before they understand why is being moved by meaning that has not yet reached words.

The felt sense is the form that pre-verbal meaning takes in the body.

---

## The Felt Sense — What It Is and Is Not

The felt sense is the body's holistic, pre-verbal sense of a whole situation. It is:

- **Global, not local** — not a specific emotion like "angry" or "sad" but the whole gestalt of the thing: its texture, its weight, its quality
- **Fuzzy, not sharp** — it resists precise description initially; you know something is there before you can name it
- **Bodily located** — typically somewhere in the chest, stomach, or throat; it has a physical address
- **Dynamic** — it shifts when it is accurately touched; it remains when it is ignored or bypassed

It is **not:**
- An emotion (though it often contains emotion as one element)
- A thought (it precedes thought)
- A sensation (though it has somatic qualities)
- A physical symptom (it is meaning-carrying, not biomedical)

The felt sense forms slowly and requires the practitioner and client to resist the pull toward explanation, analysis, or resolution.

## The Six-Step Focusing Process

Gendlin's structured practice for accessing and working with the felt sense:

**Step 1 — Clearing a Space**
Set aside the concerns and content pressing in from the day. Inventory what is present — name each thing briefly without going into it — and set each one down gently. Create interior spaciousness. This is not emptying the mind; it is making room to attend to one thing at a time.

**Step 2 — The Felt Sense**
Choose one issue. Instead of going into it analytically, turn attention to the body and wait. *Where in the body does this whole thing live?* Not what you think about it — the physical sense of the whole thing, before thinking. Let it form. This takes longer than it seems. The felt sense is not immediate; it arrives.

**Step 3 — Finding a Handle**
When the felt sense is present (even fuzzily), let a word, phrase, image, or gesture emerge that captures its quality. Not a description imposed onto it — something that rises from it. Possibilities: heavy, stuck, tight, like a wall, like something waiting, like being five years old again.

**Step 4 — Resonating**
Hold the handle against the felt sense. Ask: *does this word fit?* Not logically — does the body say yes? If there is a slight release, a settling, a sense of *yes, that's it* — the handle is right. If not, wait for a better one. The resonating check is bodily, not cognitive.

**Step 5 — Asking**
When the felt sense and handle are resonating, invite the felt sense to say more. *What is it about this that makes it feel like [the handle]? What does this need? What is the worst of this?* Wait. The answer does not come from analysis — it comes from the felt sense itself.

**Step 6 — Receiving**
When something comes — a word, an image, a shift, a memory, an unexpected knowing — receive it without immediately analyzing, judging, or explaining it. Let it be present. Protect the space. The meaning that arrives at this step has its own authority; rushing to process it cognitively collapses what was just opened.

## The Felt Shift

When the right handle arrives, or when the felt sense is accurately named or invited to speak, there is a **felt shift** — a physical reorganization. Breath deepening, muscle releasing, a sense of *ah — that's it*, often accompanied by tears or a quiet settling.

The felt shift is the body registering that it has been understood. The meaning that was held implicitly has now been touched, and the organism releases some of the tension that was holding it.

**This is how belief changes at the implicit level through Focusing.** Not through reasoning to a new conclusion, but through the felt sense shifting when accurately touched. The implicit level does not change because the explicit mind argues it toward a new position. It changes when it is genuinely met.

This maps directly onto the reconsolidation mechanism: the felt sense is the implicit belief in its pre-verbal form; the accurate handle or the asking is the prediction error that destabilizes the encoding; the felt shift is reconsolidation.

## How Focusing Differs from Related Practices

**vs. Mindfulness:**
Mindfulness observes experience without judgment — an open, receptive, non-reactive witnessing. Focusing attends to experience *with the intent of listening*: the felt sense is invited to speak, not merely observed. Focusing is dialogical; mindfulness is witnessing. The felt sense is expected to yield meaning; mindfulness does not presuppose this.

**vs. Emotional processing / feeling your feelings:**
Emotional processing works with identified emotions — grief, anger, fear. Focusing works with the pre-verbal layer *beneath* the emotion: the holistic body-sense of the whole situation that may contain emotion as one element but is not reducible to it. You can feel your feelings without ever accessing the felt sense. You can access the felt sense and find that what is there is not primarily emotional but something harder to name.

**vs. Body scan / somatic awareness:**
Body scan tracks physical sensations without seeking meaning. Focusing specifically attends to the meaning-carrying quality of the body's experience: *what is this sensation saying about this situation?* Somatic awareness is the entry point; Focusing continues past it.

## Christian Adaptation — Bio-Spiritual Focusing

Edwin McMahon and Peter Campbell (*Bio-Spiritual Focusing*, 1991) developed a specifically Christian adaptation. Their key moves:

- **The body as the site of the Spirit's working** — the pre-verbal felt sense is not merely psychological; it is the level at which the Spirit often moves first. Attending to the felt sense is a form of interior listening to what God is doing before the mind can name it.
- **The clearing of space as prayer** — Step 1 becomes a form of presenting oneself before God, setting down what has been carried, creating interior space for encounter.
- **The asking as spiritual inquiry** — Step 5 question becomes: *"What does the Lord want me to notice here?"* or *"What is the Spirit saying through this?"*
- **The felt shift as grace** — when the felt sense shifts, something that was bound releases; this can be received as a work of the Spirit, not merely a psychological event.

This framing is coherent with Willard's account of spiritual disciplines as creating conditions for what only the Spirit can do. Focusing creates the interior conditions of attentiveness and waiting that allow the Spirit's work at the pre-verbal level to become accessible.

## In-Session Coach Application

The primary in-session application is a specific move: **slow down and wait instead of interpret**.

When a client arrives at a moment of stuckness, confusion, or partial emotional contact — instead of asking a clarifying question, offering a reframe, or reaching for a tool — the coach slows:

*"As you sit with what we just named — what do you notice in your body right now? Not what you think about it — what you actually sense."*

Then wait. The felt sense forms slowly. The coach's job is to protect the space from premature closure — their own interpretive impulse or the client's return to explanation.

This is the Focusing move at Stage 4b: not directing, not interpreting, not adding content — inviting the client to attend to the pre-verbal level where the implicit belief actually lives, and waiting for it to yield something.

**What makes this distinct from open-ended reflection questions:** Standard reflection ("How does that feel?") invites emotion-naming. Focusing-adjacent attending invites the pre-verbal: the fuzzy, whole, bodily sense of the thing before words. The coach holds space for something that cannot be rushed.

## Between-Session Client Application

Focusing is the most portable implicit-level practice available to clients. Gendlin designed the full six-step process for solo use, and it is teachable in 1–2 sessions.

**Format:** 20–30 minutes, quiet space, no agenda for outcome.

**Teaching sequence:**
1. Walk the client through the six steps once in session with the coach present
2. Provide a written guide for solo practice
3. Debrief the experience in the following session: *What did you notice? Did anything shift?*

**Focusing partnerships:** Two people take turns — one Focuses, one listens with Focusing-trained attention (not interpreting, not asking leading questions, just holding space and occasionally reflecting). This is the peer-practice format and is well-suited to clients who find solo practice difficult.

**Formation framing for clients:** *Between sessions, you carry more than you're aware of. Focusing is a way of slowing down and listening to what your body already knows about what God is working on in you — before your mind has put it into words.*

## Coaching Scope and Evidence

Focusing is non-clinical by design. Gendlin developed it as a self-help practice after research showed that the clients who benefited most from therapy were those who could access the felt sense. He made the process explicit and teachable so more people could learn the capacity.

**No clinical training is required** to teach the basic Focusing practice to clients. It is not trauma processing — it is attentive interior listening. The scope boundary: if Focusing access to the pre-verbal level surfaces trauma-level material (dissociation, flashback-adjacent experience, escalating activation), stop and refer. Focusing is not a trauma-processing modality.

**Evidence:** Focusing has a research base in person-centered therapy outcomes (the Experiencing Scale — Klein et al., 1969 — predicts therapeutic outcome). McMahon & Campbell's Bio-Spiritual Focusing has practitioner-validated but not RCT-validated application in spiritual direction. Coaching deployment is practitioner-grade theory.
