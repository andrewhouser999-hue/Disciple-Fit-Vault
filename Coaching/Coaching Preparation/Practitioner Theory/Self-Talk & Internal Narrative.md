# Self-Talk & Internal Narrative

## The Honest Assessment

Self-talk interventions work — the meta-analysis evidence is solid. But most coaches deliver "think positive" with a vocabulary upgrade, and that's not the same thing. The research shows that *function* and *structure* of self-talk matter more than valence. Telling someone to be positive without matching the tool to the task is noise.

The deeper layer — internal narrative — is where durable change actually happens. A client can learn all the cue words you teach them and still choke, because the underlying story they carry about who they are hasn't changed. Know the difference between intervening at the surface and intervening at the root.

---

## Tier 1: The Empirical Foundation

### Key Research
- **Hatzigeorgiadis et al. (2011) meta-analysis**: 32 studies, N=1,489. Self-talk has a moderate positive effect on performance (d=0.48). The effect varies significantly by task type and self-talk function. This is the anchor citation. High confidence.
- **Instructional vs. motivational self-talk**: Fine-motor and technical tasks respond better to instructional cues ("smooth," "stay low"). Effort and endurance tasks respond better to motivational cues ("keep going," "I've done this"). Applying the wrong type reduces effectiveness.
- **Kross et al. (2014) — distanced self-talk**: Using your own name or third-person pronouns during self-reflection measurably reduces emotional reactivity. Replicated across paradigms including neuroimaging. One of the more actionable, accessible findings in the literature.
- **Oettingen — WOOP research**: Pure positive thinking about outcomes can *reduce* motivation by creating premature psychological arrival. Effective coaching combines positive imagery with realistic obstacle acknowledgment. This directly contradicts most pop coaching.
- **Wegner (1994) — Ironic Process Theory**: Thought suppression increases intrusion of the suppressed thought. Don't tell clients to stop thinking negative thoughts. Reframe or redirect; never suppress.

## What to Ignore

- **"Think positive"** as a standalone instruction — it has no mechanism and can backfire per Oettingen
- **Thought stopping alone** — bridges to what? Always pair with a replacement behavior
- **Generic affirmations** not tied to a specific moment or task — they don't survive pressure
- NLP-based self-talk tools — no rigorous evidence base

---

## Coaching Application

**Assess before intervening.** Ask: *"Walk me through the last 30 seconds in your head before [the thing they struggle with]."* Clients can't access this abstractly — use simulation or role-play.

**Match the tool to the task:**
- Technical precision needed → instructional cue words tied to specific actions
- Effort and persistence needed → motivational self-talk tied to values and identity

**Distanced self-talk in session:** When a client is flooded, shift to third-person. *"What would you say to [their name] right now if you were watching this from outside?"* Practice it in low-stakes moments so it's available under pressure.

**Cue words — selection criteria:**
- Client-chosen, not coach-assigned
- Specific to a moment (pre-performance, mid-setback, post-error)
- Short enough to fire automatically under stress
- Anchored to a physical action (breath, gesture) for conditioned response

**Narrative-level reframe:** *"What's the story you're telling yourself about why this keeps happening?"* Then: *"Is there another story that fits the same facts?"* This is where self-talk work and identity work converge.

**When to refer:** Rumination that is pervasive, mood-congruent across all life domains, and resistant to cognitive intervention is a clinical presentation, not a coaching one. Know the boundary.

---

## Bottom Line

Self-talk is a skill, not a mindset. Vague positivity doesn't hold under pressure. Trained, specific, practiced cues do. The deeper work is the narrative — the story the client carries about who they are determines what their self-talk reaches for when things go wrong.
