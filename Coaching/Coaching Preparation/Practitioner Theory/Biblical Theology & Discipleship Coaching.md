# Biblical Theology & Discipleship Coaching

`[CURRICULUM]`

## The Honest Assessment

Biblical Theology & Discipleship Coaching is not one lane in the CBC model — it is the road the other lanes run on. Without it, the coach is a reasonably skilled secular practitioner with Bible verses appended for Christian audiences. With it, the coach is bringing the actual mechanism of lasting human transformation — not self-improvement supported by Scripture, but the gospel as the operative power beneath every other coaching tool.

The risk of misuse runs in two directions. The first is moralism: using Scripture as a behavior-modification instrument, producing clients who know what they *should* do and feel worse about not doing it. Moralistic coaching treats the cross as inspiration for self-improvement rather than as the mechanism of transformation. The second risk is vagueness: platitudes about "what God says" that never engage the actual text or the client's actual functional theology. The client who hears "God loves you and has a plan for your life" in response to a deep identity struggle has not been served by the biblical framework — they have been dismissed by it.

This file uses theological sources rather than psychological research for its primary evidence base. The evidence structure is the coherence and exegetical fidelity of the theological framework rather than RCTs.

## Elaborate Overview

### The Three-Layer Diagnostic — The Coach's Map of Presenting Issues

Every presenting issue has three layers, and the coach's work requires all three. Failing to engage all three produces interventions that touch the surface and leave the roots intact.

**Layer 1 — Behavior (Surface)**
What the client is doing or not doing. This is the presenting complaint: procrastinating, people-pleasing, avoiding conflict, self-sabotaging, losing control, withdrawing. Behavioral coaching that stays here produces temporary change — the symptom managed without the root addressed.

**Layer 2 — Belief and Desire (Heart)**
What the client functionally believes — about themselves, about God, about what they need to be okay. These are not always conscious beliefs. They are operational: the beliefs the client is *living from* regardless of what they say they believe. The desire layer adds: what does the client ultimately want? What are they actually serving, organizing their life around, seeking to secure?

**Layer 3 — Theological (Worship and Trust)**
What is the client functionally trusting to deliver what only God can provide — worth, safety, belonging, control, meaning? This is the worship layer. Every presenting behavioral pattern has a functional theology beneath it: a working system of who or what is actually sovereign and providing in the client's daily life. The coach who reaches this layer is working with the actual root.

Paul David Tripp's framework (*Instruments in the Redeemer's Hands*): all human problems root ultimately in some form of misplaced trust — trusting self, others, achievement, pleasure, control, or approval to provide what only God can provide. The behavioral symptom is the presenting issue; the misplaced trust is the coaching target.

---

### Functional Theology — The Primary Diagnostic Site

The gap between stated theology and functional theology is where the most significant coaching work happens. Every client has both:

- **Stated theology:** What the client says they believe — "God is in control," "I am accepted in Christ," "my worth is in God, not my performance"
- **Functional theology:** What the client is actually living from — demonstrated by what they fear, what they pursue at cost, what would devastate them if lost, what they organize their decisions around

David Powlison's insight: the diagnostic question is not *what do you believe?* but *what are you living as if is true?* The client who states "my worth is in Christ" but whose life is organized around maintaining others' approval is living from a different theology than the one they profess.

The coaching move: *"What are you most afraid of in this situation? What do you believe would happen if [feared outcome] occurred? What does your response tell us about what you're actually trusting to make life work?"*

---

### Idolatry — The Central Diagnostic Construct

Tim Keller's framework (after Calvin): the human heart is an *idol factory* — it naturally generates functional saviors to do the work that only God can do. An idol is not merely something the client values or desires; it is a desire that has become a demand, a functional necessity, something that — if lost — feels like the end rather than a setback.

**Four primary idol categories (Keller, after Powlison):**
- **Comfort** — safety, privacy, the absence of stress or demand. Idol markers: withdrawal from responsibility, indulgence, passivity
- **Approval** — acceptance, affirmation, being liked or respected. Idol markers: people-pleasing, performance anxiety, approval-seeking, conflict avoidance
- **Control** — order, predictability, having outcomes within one's management. Idol markers: rigidity, anxiety when plans fail, compulsive planning, difficulty with uncertainty
- **Power** — significance, influence, being superior or unchallenged. Idol markers: ambition disconnected from calling, dominance, inability to defer, comparison

**The idol diagnostic questions:**
- *"What do you fear losing most in this situation?"*
- *"What would it take to make you feel okay about yourself? What has to be true about your life for you to feel like it's working?"*
- *"What has the capacity to devastate you — not merely disappoint, but devastate?"*
- *"What are you working hardest to secure or protect?"*

The idol identified is not the target for shame — it is the target for gospel application. The idol exists because a legitimate need has been directed toward an illegitimate source. The coaching move is not to strip the idol without replacement; it is to reconnect the need to its legitimate source.

### The Gospel as Mechanism — Not Decoration

The CBC model's defining distinctive: the cross is not the inspiration for self-improvement; it is the mechanism of change. This requires precision about what the gospel actually does:

**Justification:** The verdict has been rendered. The client is not performing to earn it, and failure does not revoke it. For the client whose performance anxiety is rooted in a working belief that worth is earned, justification applied specifically is not theological information — it is the direct counter-claim to the operative lie.

**Adoption:** The client's identity is filial, not conditional. Jack Miller's sonship framework: the orphan spirit (living as if one must perform for parental acceptance) vs. the adopted child (operating from secured relationship). Most Christian clients live from orphan-spirit functional theology regardless of their stated theology about adoption.

**Union with Christ:** Not improvement of the old self but new creation (2 Cor 5:17). The client's identity is not their history, their failure patterns, or their psychological profile — it is their union with Christ. Coaching that aims at improving the old self misses this entirely.

**Sanctification as progressive realization:** Becoming who you already are in Christ. The coaching frame: the goal is not becoming someone new — it is living increasingly from what is already true. The behavioral work follows from the identity work, not the other way.

**Common grace:** Why secular psychological tools work. God's general grace operating through human observation and discovery means that CBT, ACT, attachment theory, and schema work all capture genuine truths about human functioning. The CBC coach can use them without theological compromise — and with the theological lens that gives them their fullest interpretive depth.

---

### Repentance — The Active Mechanism of Change

Repentance in the CBC framework is not guilt management or emotional distress about sin. It is a specific movement: turning from a false center toward God. 2 Corinthians 7:10 distinguishes *godly sorrow* (which produces genuine repentance leading to salvation) from *worldly sorrow* (which produces only death — self-condemnation that reinforces rather than liberates).

**Repentance has three dimensions — all three need to move:**
- *Cognitive:* recognizing the lie/idol for what it is; seeing it clearly
- *Affective:* genuine sorrow toward God (not self-flagellating shame toward self) — feeling the weight of what the false trust has cost
- *Behavioral:* specific reorientation — not "feel worse about this" but "turn from X toward Y; what does that look like this week?"

The coaching move that distinguishes biblical from moralistic repentance: the motivation is received grace, not improved performance. The client repents not because they have failed to meet a standard, but because they have believed a lie about where life comes from — and the gospel has exposed it.

---

### Discipleship as the Coaching Frame

Biblical coaching is not goal-setting with prayer — it is discipleship with coaching skills. The frame shifts the question from *how do I help this client achieve their goals?* to *how is this person being formed into Christlikeness through this struggle, and how can I serve that formation?*

Dallas Willard's whole-person model (*Renovation of the Heart*): formation requires engagement with the entire person — will, mind, body, soul, and social dimension. A coaching engagement that only addresses the mind (beliefs, cognition) while leaving the body's habits, the social environment, and the soul's relational life with God unaddressed will produce partial results. Comprehensive discipleship-informed coaching asks: which dimensions of this person's life need formation, and which coaching tools serve each dimension?

## What to Ignore

- **Proof-texting.** Pulling a verse to address a presenting issue without understanding its textual context, meaning, or theological function is not biblical coaching — it is theological bypassing. The text must speak for itself before it is applied.
- **Moralistic therapeutic deism.** Using Christian vocabulary while the actual mechanism is self-improvement. The cross as inspiration is not the cross as mechanism. If the coaching would make sense without the resurrection, it is not distinctively Christian coaching.
- **Conflating with formal biblical counseling.** NANC/ACBC-style biblical counseling (nouthetic/biblical counseling movement) is a distinct model with more clinical scope and specific doctrinal commitments. Know the difference; refer when the presenting issue exceeds the coaching lane.
- **Gospel as bypass.** Applying gospel truth before the presenting issue has been heard fully will produce a pleasant theological conversation that changes nothing. The gospel is not a shortcut past the presenting issue — it is what addresses the root of it after the root has been accurately located.
- **Idolatry language without relational warmth.** Naming someone's idol without the pastoral care to honor the legitimate need beneath it is clinical theology without pastoral love. The idol is not the enemy — it is evidence of a legitimate need directed toward an illegitimate source.

## Coach-Safe Scope

**What a life coach CAN do with this modality:**
- Use the three-layer diagnostic (behavior, belief, theological) to understand the full depth of a presenting issue
- Surface functional theology: what the client is living from vs. what they say they believe
- Name idols and functional saviors using Keller/Powlison categories in a coaching context
- Apply the gospel specifically to the presenting issue — not generically, but to the specific lie or misplaced trust named
- Help clients distinguish godly sorrow (genuine repentance leading to change) from worldly sorrow (shame-based self-condemnation)
- Connect presenting behavioral patterns to their heart/theological roots
- Use Willard's whole-person model to assess which dimensions of the client's life need formation attention
- Apply common grace framework to justify secular coaching tools used within a biblical frame

**What requires pastoral training or exceeds coaching scope:**
- Formal biblical counseling (NANC/ACBC level) — requires specific training in the biblical counseling model
- Church discipline situations or pastoral care for acute spiritual crisis
- Demonization/spiritual warfare that exceeds normal Christian experience
- Situations requiring church elder involvement, sacramental care, or ecclesial authority

**Referral indicator:**
When the presenting issue involves theological crisis that is destabilizing the client's relationship to Christian community or the faith itself, referral to a pastor or theologically trained counselor is appropriate alongside or instead of coaching. When the "coaching" conversation has become pastoral care for pastoral situations, the role clarity needs a reset.

## Self-Directed Therapy/Coaching Questions

### Three-Layer Diagnostic
- "What is the presenting behavior? What do you actually do when this comes up?"
- "What do you believe, at the gut level, would happen if the feared outcome occurred? What verdict does that carry about you?"
- "What is this situation telling you about what you are most fundamentally trusting or fearing to lose?"

### Functional Theology
- "What would it take for you to feel okay about yourself? What has to be true about your life for it to feel like it's working?"
- "What are you living as if is true — about God, about yourself, about what you need — that you would not say out loud?"
- "If someone watched your life for a week without hearing your theology, what would they conclude you actually believe?"

### Idolatry and False Saviors
- "What has the capacity to devastate you — not merely disappoint, but devastate?"
- "What are you working hardest to secure or protect right now?"
- "If you imagine your worst-case scenario — losing X — what does that feel like? What does the intensity of that feeling tell you?"

### Gospel Application
- "The verdict that your performance earns — what would change if that verdict had already been rendered and you had been acquitted?"
- "What would it mean for how you live today if your acceptance was already completely secured and could not be improved by anything you do?"
- "Where is the gospel actually good news for the specific thing you're carrying right now — not generically, but for this exact situation?"

### Repentance and Movement
- "What would it look like to turn from [named idol] toward God specifically? Not feel more guilty about it — actually reorient."
- "What are you grieving about what this has cost you in your relationship with God — as distinct from shame about being someone who did this?"
- "What specific step does repentance look like this week? Not a feeling — a direction and a movement."

## Coaching Application — In Session

**Sequencing principle:** Surface, then heart, then theological. Move from the presenting behavior to the belief driving it to the functional trust beneath the belief. Do not skip layers.

**Opening the theological layer:**
After the presenting issue is heard and the heart layer has been explored:
*"I want to go one layer deeper. Underneath that belief — underneath the fear that [named fear] — what is that telling you about what you are most fundamentally trusting to make life work? What is doing the work of a savior here?"*

**Idol identification:**
*"What does the intensity of your response tell you? If losing [X] would devastate you — not disappoint, but devastate — that's a clue about what has become load-bearing in a way it wasn't designed to carry."*

**Gospel application (specific, not generic):**
*"The specific lie you're living from is [named lie]. What does the gospel say directly to that — not in general, but to that specific thing? Let's find it."*

Take the client to a specific text. Do not stay at the level of "God loves you." Find the text that speaks to the exact operational lie.

**Repentance as concrete movement:**
*"Repentance isn't feeling worse about this. It's a turn. What would you be turning from — specifically? What would you be turning toward? What does that look like practically this week, not as a performance requirement but as a genuine reorientation?"*

**Closing with grace, not demand:**
*"This isn't a new list of things to do to earn what you already have. You're already accepted. The invitation is to live from that — to let your behavior catch up with what's already true. That's a different energy than performance. Can you feel the difference?"*

## Bottom Line

Biblical Theology & Discipleship Coaching is not a set of spiritual techniques added to a coaching framework — it is a different account of what is actually wrong and what actually changes it. The coach who holds this framework is working with the roots rather than the symptoms, with the mechanism of transformation rather than its inspiration. Every other modality in the library functions more powerfully when the coach understands the theological layer beneath the presenting issue — what is being functionally trusted, what the gospel specifically addresses, and how the client is being formed through this struggle toward Christlikeness.
