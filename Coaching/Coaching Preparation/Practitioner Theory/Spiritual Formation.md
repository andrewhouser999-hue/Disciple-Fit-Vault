# Spiritual Formation

`[CURRICULUM]`

## The Honest Assessment

Spiritual formation is the operating theory of how lasting change actually happens for the Christian client — and a coach who doesn't understand it will consistently mistake surface behavior change for the real thing. The disciplines of grace are not add-ons to Christian life. They are the structural means by which a person is conformed to the character of Christ over time, at the level of desire and habit rather than just intention and knowledge.

The risk in coaching is dual. The first is productivizing formation: treating the disciplines as a spiritual optimization system, deploying them as techniques for becoming a better-functioning version of oneself. This produces disciplined clients who remain fundamentally unchanged at the desire level. The second is privatizing formation: treating it as solo, invisible, interior — and missing that the body of Christ is not optional to the formation process. Community is not a supplement to formation; it is a primary context for it.

The honest limitation: a coach is not a spiritual director, and spiritual formation coaching is not spiritual direction. The role distinction matters. The coach draws on the formation framework to understand the client's whole-person development and help them structure life more intentionally toward God. The spiritual director walks alongside the client's relationship with God as the primary focus. They are not the same work.

This file uses theological sources rather than psychological research for its primary evidence base.

## Elaborate Overview

### What Spiritual Formation Actually Is

Dallas Willard's definition: spiritual formation is the Spirit-directed process by which the whole person — mind, will, body, soul, and social dimension — is increasingly conformed to the character and likeness of Jesus Christ.

Several elements of this definition deserve attention:

**Spirit-directed:** Transformation is the Spirit's work, not a product the human produces through technique. The disciplines are not the transformation — they are positioning for transformation. The coach and client can organize conditions; God does the forming.

**Whole person:** Formation is not merely cognitive renewal (though it includes that), not merely behavioral habit change (though it includes that), not merely emotional reorientation (though it includes that). It addresses every dimension of the person: what they think, choose, do by physical habit, how they are organized at the soul level, and who they are with and around.

**Increasingly conformed:** Formation is a long-haul process, not an event. It is gradual, non-linear, and requires sustained attention over time. Coaching that expects formation outcomes from single-session interventions has misunderstood the nature of the work.

**To the character of Christ:** The destination is specific — not generic self-improvement, not best-self actualization, not even moral excellence in the abstract. It is Christlikeness. The coaching target is always formation toward love, holiness, and the fruit of the Spirit.

### Willard's Whole-Person Model — The Coach's Diagnostic Map

*Renovation of the Heart* provides the most complete map of the human person for formation purposes. Five dimensions, each requiring formation, each interconnected:

**1. Mind** — The thoughts, images, beliefs, and attitudes that the mind habitually entertains. What one fills the mind with shapes what one naturally thinks, and natural thought produces natural action. Formation of the mind involves Scripture meditation, theology, and the deliberate crowding out of thought patterns contrary to Christ.

**2. Will** — The choosing self; the hinge between belief and action. Many clients know what is right and feel the pull toward it but cannot consistently choose it — the will is not formed to act on what it knows. Will-formation is addressed through practices that develop the capacity to say yes and no with increasing consistency: submission, obedience, and disciplines of abstinence that train the will to deny immediate impulse.

**3. Body** — Physical habits, the automation of action. The body has its own "knowledge" — what it does without deliberate decision. Years of formed bodily habit mean the body carries the person toward or away from Christlikeness without conscious direction. Body-formation involves physical disciplines (fasting, solitude, service) that retrain what the body does by default.

**4. Soul** — The organizing principle that integrates all the other dimensions. When the soul is well-formed, the other dimensions move in the same direction. Soul-care involves deep, sustained practices rather than quick interventions: extended solitude, sustained prayer, long-form Scripture engagement.

**5. Social Dimension** — Who the person is with and around. Formation does not happen in isolation. The social context shapes thought, desire, and habit — which is why community is not optional to the formation process. The body of Christ is a primary formation environment. The question is not just "what disciplines do you practice?" but "who are you becoming with, and is that community forming you toward or away from Christ?"

### James K.A. Smith's Desire/Liturgy Framework

*You Are What You Love* makes a claim that changes how formation coaching works: humans are not primarily thinking beings who need better information — we are desiring, loving beings who need redirected desire.

**The central argument:** We are what we love. What a person habitually moves toward — what they reach for first, what their attention returns to without direction, what they organize their discretionary time around — reveals what they love. And what they love determines who they become, not what they know.

**Secular liturgies:** Every habitual practice that structures attention and desire is a liturgy — whether or not it is labeled spiritual. The morning scroll through social media is a liturgy that forms desire. The shopping mall is a liturgy. Each of these aims the heart at something and shapes what the person automatically loves and pursues. Clients are being formed by their daily liturgies whether or not they have chosen to be.

**The coaching implication:** Behavior change without desire change doesn't stick. A client can decide to act differently and sustain it for a period through willpower — but if the underlying desires are still aimed elsewhere, behavior reverts when willpower runs low. Formation addresses what the person loves, not just what they do. The question for clients is not just "what do you need to do differently?" but "what are your current habits forming you to love? and what practices would aim your desire toward God instead?"

**Sacred liturgies as desire-redirection:** The spiritual disciplines are not exercises in willpower — they are practices that over time redirect desire. Worship aims the heart at God's worth. Scripture meditation fills the mind with God's language. Solitude creates space where the noise of lesser desires quiets. Fasting makes the body's default impulses more visible and more resistible. Each discipline is a liturgy of desire-formation.

### The Spiritual Disciplines — Means of Grace

Foster's classic taxonomy:
- **Inward disciplines:** meditation, prayer, fasting, study
- **Outward disciplines:** simplicity, solitude, submission, service
- **Corporate disciplines:** confession, worship, guidance, celebration

Willard's taxonomy organized by function:
- **Disciplines of abstinence** (creating space): solitude, silence, fasting, frugality, chastity, secrecy, sacrifice. These train the capacity to say no — to impulse, to noise, to consumption — creating the quiet in which God's voice becomes audible.
- **Disciplines of engagement** (filling space): study, worship, celebration, service, prayer, fellowship, confession, submission. These actively fill the person with what is good, true, and God-oriented.

**The training metaphor:** The disciplines are to spiritual formation what physical training is to athletic capacity. A musician does not become capable of beautiful performance by deciding to play beautifully — they practice scales and technique until musical expression becomes natural. The disciplines do not make a person righteous. They create the conditions in which the Spirit forms the person's character over time.

### The Habit vs. Formation Distinction

This distinction is critical for coaching, because habit-change frameworks (CBT, behavioral activation, Atomic Habits) operate at a different level than formation.

**Habits** are behavioral patterns — regularized sequences of cue, response, and reward. Habit science can produce reliable behavior change and is genuinely useful. But habit change alone does not address what the person desires.

**Formation** shapes desire at a deeper level — what the person automatically loves, what they reach for without deliberate decision, what they are aimed at by default. A person can have regular devotional habits and remain fundamentally unchanged at the desire level if the practices are performed rather than received.

**The diagnostic question for coaches:** *"What is this practice doing IN you?"* Not just "are you doing it?" but "what is it forming?" A client who reports doing the practice but feels no change in their desires or their relationship with God is either performing rather than receiving, or the practice itself needs examination.

### Common Formation Pathologies — What to Name in Coaching

**Legalism:** Formation as performance for God's approval. The disciplines become spiritual currency — doing them earns standing with God, failing them produces condemnation. This produces spiritual exhaustion. The gospel correction: disciplines are positioning practices, not merit-generating activities.

**Passivity:** Waiting for God to change them without participating. "I've prayed about it; I'm waiting for God to move." This misunderstands the relationship between Spirit-initiative and human participation. God invites cooperation, not inertia.

**Novelty-seeking:** Always starting new practices, never deepening any. Spiritual consumerism applied to formation. Formation requires sustained, deepened engagement with consistent practices over time.

**Compartmentalization:** Spiritual practices isolated from the rest of life. Formation shapes the whole person across all of life, not a God-slot added to an otherwise unchanged life.

**Community neglect:** Solo formation attempted without the body of Christ. Confession requires another person; corporate worship shapes what private devotion cannot; being genuinely known surfaces what private practice misses.

## What to Ignore

- **Formation as spiritual productivity.** Coaches who bring a productivity frame will count disciplines the way they count reps. Formation is receptivity, not optimization. The disciplines are postures of attention before a Person, not achievements to log.
- **Solo formation without community.** A coach who designs a rule of life containing no community element has designed an incomplete formation plan. Confession, corporate worship, and genuine relational presence in the body of Christ are not optional.
- **Habit science as a proxy for formation.** Atomic Habits and behavioral change frameworks are useful and partially overlapping. They do not address desire. Treating spiritual formation as habit formation with religious labels misses the deepest mechanism.
- **Starting with disciplines before addressing the grace foundation.** A client approaching disciplines under legalism needs the gospel before they need a rule of life. Disciplines practiced in grace are formation; disciplines practiced for merit are exhaustion.
- **Formation work as a substitute for clinical care.** When the presenting struggle involves clinical depression, trauma, or deep psychological wound, formation coaching without appropriate clinical support is incomplete.

## Coach-Safe Scope

**What a life coach CAN do with this modality:**
- Use Willard's five dimensions as a diagnostic framework: which dimensions of this client's life need formation attention?
- Identify common formation pathologies (legalism, passivity, novelty-seeking, compartmentalization, community neglect) and name them
- Conduct a formation audit — what current rhythms are shaping this client's desires, and toward what?
- Ask the secular liturgy question: what habitual practices are currently forming the client without their conscious awareness?
- Help clients design a sustainable rule of life — one or two additions, framed in grace
- Distinguish habit-change (behavioral level) from formation (desire level) and know which the client needs
- Recommend *You Are What You Love*, *Practicing the Way*, or *Celebration of Discipline* as between-session resources

**What requires pastoral training:**
- Acting as a spiritual director — distinct training and distinct relational role required
- Prescribing a full formation program as if the coach has pastoral authority over the client's spiritual life
- Working with acute spiritual crisis: loss of faith, deconstruction, or destabilizing doubt

**Referral indicator:**
When the presenting formation issue is entangled with clinical depression, significant trauma, or spiritual crisis severe enough to be destabilizing, refer to a licensed Christian therapist or pastor. Formation coaching supports healthy Christian growth; it is not treatment for psychological or spiritual crisis.

**The deepest connection:** Formation theology and behavioral coaching are both accounts of how habitual practice changes the person — but at different levels. CBT works with thought patterns and behaviors; formation works with what the person loves. Smith's argument that we are what we love is the theological version of what neuroscience shows about deep neural pathway formation through repetition and attention. The coach who holds both frameworks can address behavior, belief, AND desire — the full depth of what needs to change for lasting transformation. Formation is not the enemy of behavioral coaching; it is its deepest layer.

## Self-Directed Therapy/Coaching Questions

### Whole-Person Audit
- "Which of these dimensions of your life currently feel most formed toward Christ — mind, will, body, soul, or your social community? Which feel most neglected?"
- "What is your mind habitually on when it's not on anything in particular? What does that reveal about what you love?"
- "What does your body do automatically — by habit, without decision? What is that habitual behavior forming in you?"

### Desire and Secular Liturgy
- "What are the habitual practices that structure your attention every day? What are they forming you to love?"
- "What do you reach for first in the morning? What does that aim your heart at before the day begins?"
- "If someone watched how you spend your discretionary time and attention, what would they conclude you love most?"

### Discipline Audit
- "Which of the disciplines do you currently practice — even occasionally? What are they doing in you?"
- "Which disciplines are completely absent from your life? What do you think that absence is costing you?"
- "Are you practicing disciplines from a place of grace or obligation? How do you know the difference?"

### Formation Pathology
- "Is the work you're doing on yourself a form of training or striving? What's the difference in how it feels?"
- "Is there a practice you keep starting and abandoning? What is driving the cycle?"
- "Is your formation work happening in community? Who is forming you alongside you?"

### Rule of Life
- "If you were to add one rhythm that doesn't currently exist — the one that would most address the gap you've named — what would it be?"
- "How would you frame that addition in grace rather than obligation? What would the difference feel like to live?"
- "What would need to change about your current schedule to make room for it?"

## Coaching Application — In Session

**Sequencing principle:** Formation is the long-game frame for all coaching work. Introduce it when the client is asking "why isn't this sticking?" or "why does the same pattern keep returning?"

**The entry:**
*"I want to ask you something different. Not 'what are you doing?' but 'what are you being formed by?' What are the habitual practices in your daily life, and what are they training your desires to love?"*

**The secular liturgy question:**
*"Before we talk about what you want to add, let's talk about what's already forming you. What does a normal weekday morning look like — before you've made any intentional choices? What is that routine aiming your heart at?"*

**The whole-person diagnostic:**
*"There are five dimensions that need formation — mind, will, body, soul, and how you live in community. If you had to honestly assess each one: which is most formed toward Christ? Which is most unaddressed?"*

**Designing the rule of life addition:**
*"I'm not going to give you a list of twelve disciplines. Let's name one — the one that, if it became a consistent rhythm, would most address the gap you've named. What is it? What would need to change to make it sustainable?"*

**The grace frame:**
*"This is positioning work, not earning work. You are not doing this practice to become acceptable to God — you're already accepted. You're doing it to put yourself in the path of what God is already doing in you. What would it feel like to approach it from that posture rather than obligation?"*

## Bottom Line

Spiritual formation coaching operates at a level behavioral coaching cannot reach — the level of desire, of what the person automatically loves and moves toward. The disciplines are not the goal; they are the training field. Transformation is the Spirit's work; the coach helps the client structure their life to position for it. A coach who holds Willard's whole-person model, Smith's desire framework, and the grace foundation of formation is equipped to ask the questions that get beneath behavior to what is actually being formed — and to help the client build a life that is structurally aimed at God, not just intellectually committed to him.
