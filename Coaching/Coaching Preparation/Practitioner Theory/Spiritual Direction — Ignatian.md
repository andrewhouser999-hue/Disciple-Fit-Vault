# Spiritual Direction — Ignatian

`[CURRICULUM]`

## The Honest Assessment

Spiritual direction is not coaching, and a coach is not a spiritual director. The distinction matters and must be held clearly: spiritual direction focuses primarily on the person's relationship with God — the directee's interior life with God is the subject, and the director serves as a trained companion in that territory. Coaching focuses primarily on the person's movement toward goals, with the relationship with God as one dimension of the whole. Conflating the roles creates confusion for the client about what kind of conversation they are in and what kind of help they can expect.

What the Ignatian tradition offers a CBT-informed, faith-based coach is something genuinely distinct: the most sophisticated framework for discernment and interior awareness that the Christian tradition has produced. Five hundred years of rigorous development of consolation/desolation discernment, the daily Examen, and the principles for major decision-making give the coach a vocabulary and a set of practices that no secular framework can supply. A coach who understands even 20% of Ignatius — the Examen and basic consolation/desolation mapping — brings a capacity for interior awareness to their clients that cannot be approximated by self-reflection tools or journaling prompts.

The limitation is the same as it is for spiritual formation work: the coach is not the spiritual director. Elements of the Ignatian framework are coach-deployable; the full Spiritual Exercises require a trained guide and a structured retreat context that is not a coaching relationship.

This file uses theological and spiritual tradition sources rather than psychological research for its primary evidence base.

## Elaborate Overview

### The Ignatian Tradition — What Ignatius Actually Built

Ignatius of Loyola (1491–1556) developed the Spiritual Exercises through his own spiritual experience and decades of accompanying others. The Exercises are a structured program — originally designed as a 30-day directed retreat — organized around four "weeks" (which are not calendar weeks but phases of interior movement):

- **Week 1:** Encountering one's own sinfulness and God's mercy — the foundation of humility and gratitude
- **Week 2:** Contemplating the life and ministry of Christ — deepening knowledge and love of Jesus; major discernment work happens here
- **Week 3:** Accompanying Christ through his passion — solidarity with Christ's suffering
- **Week 4:** Encountering the risen Christ — consolation, mission, love

For a coach, the full Exercises are outside scope — they require a trained spiritual director, extended time, and a depth of guided interior work that is not a coaching relationship. What transfers to coaching practice are the Ignatian principles embedded in the Exercises: the theology of discernment, the tools of consolation/desolation mapping, and the Examen.

---

### Consolation and Desolation — The Central Framework

Ignatius's discernment of spirits is built on the distinction between two types of interior movement:

**Consolation:** Interior movements that draw the person toward God — not necessarily comfort in the emotional sense, but movements of increase: increased faith, hope, and love; peace; attraction toward God and the things of God; tears of devotion; a sense of aliveness, integration, or rightness. Consolation is not the same as feeling good. A person can be in consolation while weeping; a person can be in desolation while experiencing sensory pleasure.

**Desolation:** Interior movements that draw the person away from God — darkness of soul, disturbance, restlessness, diminished faith or hope, attraction toward self-preoccupation or lesser things, a sense of heaviness, flatness, or spiritual withdrawal. Desolation is not the same as feeling bad. Difficult emotions in the context of prayer or good work may be consolation; pleasant feelings in the context of avoidance may be desolation.

**The Rules for Discernment:** Ignatius developed specific rules for interpreting consolation and desolation and for appropriate response to each. Key principles:
- In consolation: bank the energy, make commitments, but do not make major irreversible decisions *in* the consolation itself (because the good feeling may be influencing judgment)
- In desolation: do not change course; do not make major decisions; maintain existing practices; intensify prayer; seek support. Desolation is a signal but not necessarily a directive.
- The source of interior movements matters: both good and evil can produce consolation-like or desolation-like experiences; discernment requires reading the arc, not just the present feeling

**Why this matters for coaching:** Most clients make major decisions based on immediate emotional state — when they feel energized about something, they commit; when they feel flat about something, they withdraw. Ignatian discernment provides a more sophisticated framework: the interior movement is data, not instruction. The question is not "how do I feel about this?" but "what are my interior movements telling me about this, and what is their source?"

### The Daily Examen — The Most Coach-Deployable Ignatian Practice

The Examen is a daily 15-minute prayer of review that Ignatius considered the most important of all the spiritual practices — he recommended it even when other prayers had to be shortened. It is the primary Ignatian tool deployable within a coaching context.

**The five movements of the Examen (Gallagher's contemporary form):**

1. **Recall that you are in God's presence** — not a technique, but an orientation: the review happens in relational context
2. **Review the day with gratitude** — scan for gifts; where was grace present? This is not forced positivity but genuine attention to what was given
3. **Survey the day for interior movements** — where did you notice consolation? desolation? What were the emotional texture and spiritual quality of different parts of the day?
4. **Choose one moment — pray from it honestly** — not analysis, but prayer; bring the most significant moment of consolation or desolation to God directly
5. **Look toward tomorrow** — a brief forward movement: what is coming? what might I need? a short prayer for what is ahead

**The diagnostic function of the Examen:** Over time, the Examen develops a particular kind of self-knowledge — interior awareness that distinguishes the quality and direction of movements rather than just their intensity. A client who has practiced the Examen for three months brings a richer interior vocabulary to coaching sessions. They have learned to distinguish what draws them toward God from what draws them toward self; what animates them from what drains them; what is peace from what is avoidance.

**The Examen is a prayer, not a reflection exercise.** The failure mode is treating it as a self-reflection tool and stripping it of its relational frame. The power of the Examen is inseparable from its being done in God's presence and with God as the primary audience. A coach who teaches it as journaling with a structure has missed the essential element.

### Discernment of Spirits — The Coaching Application

Ignatius's discernment framework is applicable in coaching contexts wherever a client is navigating a significant decision or noticing persistent interior patterns.

**The key diagnostic questions Ignatius trained his directees to ask:**
- *"Which option leaves me with greater interior freedom?"* — Ignatius observed that right choices tend toward freedom; disordered attachments tend toward constriction and anxiety
- *"Which choice draws me toward greater love and service?"* — the directional criterion: movement toward love is typically a positive indicator
- *"If I imagine myself at death looking back on this decision — which choice would I be glad I made?"* — the death-meditation as discernment tool, neutralizing the distortion of short-term feeling
- *"What are the interior movements I notice when I sit with each option?"* — not the first emotional reaction but the sustained interior quality after reflection

**The Two Standards and Election (Week 2 of the Exercises):** Ignatius uses the image of two military standards — the standard of Christ (poverty, humility, service) and the standard of the enemy (riches, honor, pride) — to help directees see which value-system their choices are actually serving. This maps to the coaching construct of functional saviors (Keller/Powlison): what is actually organizing the client's desire at the decision point?

### The Role-Distinction — Spiritual Director vs. Coach

This distinction requires explicit attention because it affects how the coach presents this work to clients:

**Spiritual director:**
- Primary focus: the directee's relationship with God
- The director's role: listen, reflect, occasionally interpret interior movements; primarily creates space for God to speak
- Trained in the Ignatian tradition or another recognized spiritual direction method
- Meets monthly, typically; relationship may span years
- The director does not set goals or design action plans

**Coach:**
- Primary focus: the client's movement toward goals and growth
- Uses discernment tools to inform decision-making and interior awareness within the coaching process
- Deploys the Examen and consolation/desolation framework as tools within a coaching session, not as spiritual direction
- Goal-oriented; session-structured; time-bounded

A client who needs genuine spiritual direction needs to be referred to a trained spiritual director. The coach can introduce the Ignatian vocabulary and practices as self-awareness and decision-making tools while being clear about the role distinction.

## What to Ignore

- **Treating the Examen as a journaling or productivity tool.** It is a prayer — relational, directed to God, conducted in God's presence. Strip out the relational frame and it becomes a sophisticated self-reflection exercise that loses the essential mechanism. Teach it as prayer or don't teach it.
- **Simplifying consolation/desolation to good feeling/bad feeling.** This is the most common distortion. Consolation can involve tears, difficulty, and challenge. Desolation can involve pleasant distraction and comfort. The distinction is directional (toward or away from God), not emotional valence.
- **Applying Ignatius's rules without understanding their limits.** Ignatius himself identified different rules for different spiritual states and warned that sophisticated temptation can mimic consolation. A coach deploying these frameworks should know enough to be humble about interpretation and should never be dogmatic about what a client's interior movements mean.
- **Attempting to guide clients through the full Spiritual Exercises.** The Exercises require a trained director, extended solitude, and a depth of guided interior work that is not a coaching relationship. Do not attempt to adapt them into a coaching series.
- **Using Ignatian language as a theological accent on secular coaching.** The framework has integrity. It is not a vocabulary set to add spiritual texture to coaching questions — it is a structured system for developing interior awareness in the context of a life lived before God.

## Coach-Safe Scope

**What a life coach CAN do with this modality:**
- Teach the daily Examen to clients as a between-session interior awareness practice — always framed as prayer, not journaling
- Use consolation/desolation mapping in session: "When you sit with this option, what do you notice internally? Is it drawing you toward or away from God?"
- Deploy Ignatius's discernment questions for major decision work: freedom, love/service direction, death-bed retrospective, sustained interior quality vs. first emotional reaction
- Help clients develop a vocabulary for interior movements that goes beyond emotional language
- Introduce the concept of *indifference* (interior freedom from outcome) as a preparation for discernment
- Distinguish the coaching role from spiritual direction clearly when clients may need the latter

**What requires spiritual direction training:**
- Guiding a client through the full Spiritual Exercises or substantial portions of them
- Acting as a spiritual director — accompanying the client's relationship with God as the primary focus
- Deep interpretation of consolation/desolation patterns (Ignatius's Rules involve sophisticated distinctions that require training)
- Working with mystical experience or unusual interior phenomena that require discernment training

**Referral indicator:**
A client who needs sustained companionship in their interior life with God — not coaching on goals with spiritual dimension, but a guide for their prayer and relationship with God specifically — should be referred to a trained spiritual director. When the client's need is direction rather than coaching, the role clarity serves them better than trying to do both.

The coach who understands the Examen and basic consolation/desolation discernment can help clients develop this interior attention as a between-session practice that progressively deepens the quality of everything else they bring to coaching — because they have learned to listen more carefully to what is actually happening in them.

## Self-Directed Therapy/Coaching Questions

### Consolation/Desolation Mapping
- "When you sit with this option — not just think about it, but stay with it — what do you notice internally? Is the quality of what you notice drawing you toward God or away?"
- "In the last week, when were you most alive, most integrated, most genuinely at peace? What was happening then?"
- "When were you most flat, most restless, most pulled toward distraction or self-preoccupation? What was that context?"

### Examen-Based Questions
- "If you reviewed today in God's presence — where did you notice moments of grace? Where did you notice moments of resistance, heaviness, or moving away?"
- "What is the one moment from this week that most deserves to be brought before God in prayer — not analyzed, but prayed from?"
- "What were you most grateful for today? Not what should you be grateful for — what did you actually notice?"

### Discernment Questions
- "Which of these options leaves you with greater interior freedom — not comfort, but freedom?"
- "If you imagine yourself at the end of your life looking back on this decision — which choice would you be glad you made?"
- "Which direction draws you toward greater love and greater service? Which draws you toward self-protection, control, or comfort?"
- "What are you attached to in this decision that is distorting your ability to see clearly? What would it mean to hold it more lightly?"

### Role and Referral Awareness
- "What you're describing sounds like it goes beyond decision-making — it sounds like you need someone to walk alongside your interior life with God specifically. Have you ever worked with a spiritual director?"

## Coaching Application — In Session

**Sequencing principle:** Ignatian practices are most useful in three coaching situations: (1) major decision work where standard pros/cons frameworks are insufficient, (2) helping clients develop interior awareness as a continuous practice, and (3) clients who are spiritually sophisticated and can engage the vocabulary directly.

**Introducing the Examen as a between-session practice:**
*"I want to give you something to practice between sessions — not homework in the productivity sense, but a prayer practice that will develop your interior awareness over time. It's called the Examen, and it takes about 15 minutes at the end of the day. Here's how it works..."*

Walk through the five movements. Emphasize it is a prayer, not a reflection exercise. Frame what they should bring to the next session: "What one moment in the week stood out — in consolation or desolation — that is worth paying attention to?"

**Consolation/desolation mapping for major decisions:**
*"I want to try something with you. Rather than talking about the pros and cons of this decision — which you've already done — I want to sit with the interior quality of each option. Let's take Option A. Sit with it for a moment. Don't analyze it — just notice what happens internally. What do you notice?"*

After the client responds, distinguish: "Is that drawing you toward or away from God? Is it peace or avoidance? Is it aliveness or comfort?"

**The death-bed question:**
*"There's a question Ignatius used for major decisions: imagine yourself at the end of your life, looking back. Which decision would you be glad you made? Not which feels better now — which would you be glad you made then? What does that perspective show you?"*

**When to name the role distinction:**
*"What you're describing — the need for someone to walk alongside your interior life with God, not just make decisions — sounds like what a spiritual director does rather than what a coach does. Those are different relationships. I can help you with discernment tools in our sessions, but if you want the deeper companionship in your prayer life, I'd want to help you find a good spiritual director alongside this work."*

## Bottom Line

Ignatius gave the world the most disciplined and practically sophisticated system for interior awareness that the Christian tradition has produced. A CBC coach who learns even 20% of it — the Examen and basic consolation/desolation discernment — brings a capacity to clients that no secular framework can supply: a vocabulary and a practice for noticing how God communicates through the interior life, and for distinguishing movements toward God from movements away. The coach who holds this framework is not doing spiritual direction — but they are helping clients develop the interior attention that makes all of their other formation and coaching work more deeply rooted.
