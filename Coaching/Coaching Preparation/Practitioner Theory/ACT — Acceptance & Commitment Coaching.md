# ACT — Acceptance & Commitment Therapy/Coaching

## The Honest Assessment

ACT (pronounced as one word, not initials) is not a technique — it is a comprehensive psychological model of how human minds trap us and how to break free. It has an RCT evidence base rivaling CBT for a wide range of presentations. The distinction: CBT targets the *content* of thoughts (change what you think); ACT targets the *relationship* to thoughts (change how you relate to what you think). Both are necessary. Neither alone is complete.

For Kingdom coaching, ACT is uniquely powerful: its core mechanisms — defusion, acceptance, values-clarified committed action — are structurally isomorphic with Christian spiritual formation. The degree to which Christians already practice aspects of ACT under different language (die to self, take every thought captive, walk by faith not sight) makes it the most naturally integrated evidence-based framework for your work. The danger: you can use the language without the mechanism. Know the mechanism.

## Elaborate Overview — The ACT Hexaflex

ACT is built on six interlocking psychological flexibility processes. The goal is not symptom reduction — it is *psychological flexibility*: the ability to contact the present moment fully, without unnecessary defense, and move toward what matters.

### 1. Cognitive Defusion
**What it is:** The capacity to observe thoughts as mental events rather than literal truth or commands to act.

The opposite — *cognitive fusion* — is the default human state. A fused client is not *having* the thought "I'm a failure" — they *are* the failure. The thought is treated as fact, as self, as reality. Defusion creates distance.

**Defusion in practice:**
- Naming: *"I'm noticing the thought that I'm a failure"* (vs. *"I'm a failure"*)
- Thanking the mind: *"Thanks, mind, for that helpful commentary"*
- Singing the thought to a tune — makes the content absurd without suppressing it
- Putting thoughts on leaves floating down a stream (mindfulness imagery)

**Kingdom parallel:** *"Take every thought captive to the obedience of Christ"* (2 Corinthians 10:5). This is not thought suppression — it is exactly what defusion describes: the capacity to observe a thought, evaluate it, and choose not to obey it.

---

### 2. Acceptance
**What it is:** Willingness to have difficult internal experiences (emotions, sensations, memories, urges) without fighting them.

Acceptance is not approval, resignation, or tolerance. It is the active, deliberate choice to *make room* for difficult experience rather than spend energy trying to eliminate it. The irony: the more energy spent eliminating an unwanted feeling, the more space it occupies. Acceptance reduces the secondary suffering (suffering about the suffering).

**The experiential avoidance trap:** Clients in avoidance mode organize their lives around not feeling certain things. This narrows their world progressively. The coaching work is naming the avoidance, then building willingness in the direction of values.

**Kingdom parallel:** *"Count it all joy when you fall into various trials, knowing that the testing of your faith produces patience."* (James 1:2–4). The command is not to feel happy about pain — it is to hold it without avoidance, trusting its purpose.

---

### 3. Present-Moment Awareness (Contact with the Present)
**What it is:** Flexible, voluntary attention to the current moment — what is actually here now, not the mental narrative about it.

Most clients live primarily in the past (rumination, regret) or the future (worry, planning). The present moment is where values are acted on. If a client cannot contact the present, they cannot choose — they are simply reacting to mental content.

**Kingdom parallel:** *"Be still and know that I am God."* (Psalm 46:10). Presence is the prerequisite for divine encounter. The anxious or ruminating mind is not available to hear.

---

### 4. Self-as-Context (The Observing Self)
**What it is:** The capacity to experience oneself as the *observer* of thoughts, feelings, and sensations — not as their content.

Most clients are fused with their self-concept: *"I am my anxiety, my failures, my trauma."* Self-as-context establishes a stable perspective from which everything else can be observed without threat. This is the self that cannot be destroyed by circumstance.

**The chessboard metaphor:** Thoughts and feelings are the chess pieces — black and white, good and bad, moving around the board. The observing self is the board itself. It holds everything. It is not threatened by any piece.

**Kingdom parallel:** The indwelling Spirit as the stable, unchanging identity beneath all performance, emotion, and circumstance. *"I have been crucified with Christ; it is no longer I who live, but Christ lives in me."* (Galatians 2:20). The ego-self that can be wounded is not the deepest self.

---

### 5. Values Clarification
**What it is:** Identifying what genuinely matters — not goals, not obligations, not what should matter — but what the client actually cares about deeply.

ACT distinguishes values from goals: goals are achieved or not; values are directions, not destinations. You can live toward a value every day regardless of outcome. *Integrity* is a value. *Getting promoted* is a goal. Values survive failure. Goals don't always.

**The tombstone question:** *"What do you want people to say about the kind of person you were — not what you accomplished?"* This is values territory, not goals territory.

**Kingdom parallel:** The Great Commandment (love God, love neighbor) is a values statement — a direction of life, not a destination. Sanctification is values-clarified committed action over a lifetime.

---

### 6. Committed Action
**What it is:** Taking specific, concrete behavioral steps in the direction of values — even in the presence of difficult thoughts and feelings.

The ACT model does not require the client to feel confident, motivated, or free of fear before acting. Committed action is *willingness to act while carrying the difficulty*. This is what breaks the avoidance cycle: the client learns that difficult feelings can be present and they can still move.

**Kingdom parallel:** *"Faith without works is dead."* (James 2:17). Faith is not a feeling — it is committed action toward what is believed and valued, carried forward even through doubt.

## The Psychological Flexibility Model — How the Six Work Together

```
Psychological Inflexibility (the trap):
Fusion + Avoidance + Dominance of the conceptualized past/future +
Self-as-content + Unclear values + Inaction/impulsive action

Psychological Flexibility (the target):
Defusion + Acceptance + Present-moment contact +
Self-as-context + Clear values + Committed action
```

The six processes are bidirectional — working any one of them moves the others. A values clarification exercise also increases acceptance. Defusion work also strengthens self-as-context. You don't need to work all six explicitly in every session.

## Tier 1: Empirical Foundation

| Framework Element           | What the Research Says                                                                                                                                                   |
| --------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| RCT evidence base           | 300+ RCTs across anxiety, depression, chronic pain, substance use, work stress. Meta-analyses consistently show ACT equivalent or superior to CBT on many presentations. |
| Transdiagnostic application | Works across presentations without requiring diagnosis — ideal for coaching. The mechanism (psychological flexibility) operates regardless of presenting issue.          |
| Values-action gap           | Research shows values clarification alone, without committed action, produces minimal behavior change. Both components are necessary.                                    |
| Defusion vs. restructuring  | Multiple studies comparing CBT restructuring vs. ACT defusion show equivalent outcomes — suggesting relationship to thoughts matters as much as content of thoughts.     |

## What to Ignore

- ACT presented as "mindfulness plus positive thinking" — it is neither. The acceptance component is often butchered into "just let it go," which is avoidance with a spiritual veneer.
- Mixing ACT defusion with CBT restructuring carelessly — they have different theoretical bases. You can use both, but know which you're doing and why.
- Confusing acceptance with endorsement — especially critical in faith contexts. Accepting a sinful impulse as a mental event is not the same as acting on it.
- Pop "ACT in 5 minutes" content — defusion done incorrectly (as distraction, humor without depth) backfires.

## Self-Directed Therapy/Coaching Questions

### Defusion
- "What is the most frequent thought that shows up and tells you that you can't, shouldn't, or aren't enough? Can you write it down exactly?"
- "If that thought were a person sitting in the chair across from you — what would it look like? How long has it been following you?"
- "What is that thought trying to protect you from? What does it think will happen if you don't obey it?"
- "Can you hold that thought at arm's length — like a sign someone else is carrying — and still take one step in the direction you care about?"

### Acceptance
- "What feeling are you spending the most energy trying not to feel? What does managing that feeling cost you per week?"
- "If avoiding that feeling weren't an option anymore — not that it's gone, but that you've agreed to carry it — what would you do differently?"
- "What have you given up, cancelled, or avoided in the last month in order to not feel that feeling?"
- "What would willingness look like — not liking it, just making room for it to be there?"

### Present-Moment Awareness
- "Right now, in this moment — not yesterday, not tomorrow — what is actually true?"
- "Where does your mind go when you're trying to pray or sit quietly? What does it pull you toward?"
- "What would you notice about your life if you spent one full hour only responding to what is here — not planning, not reviewing?"

### Self-as-Context
- "You have thought thousands of different thoughts in your life. You have felt thousands of different feelings. You have had many different opinions about yourself. What is the part of you that has been present for all of that? Can you sense that?"
- "If the most painful belief about yourself turned out to be completely wrong — would there still be a 'you'? What would remain?"

### Values Clarification
- "Imagine your funeral — people who knew you well are speaking. What do you most want them to say about the kind of person you were? Not your accomplishments — the kind of person."
- "In the last year, when did you feel most like yourself? Most alive? What were you doing, and what did it say about what you value?"
- "What are you consistently moving *away* from? What is the thing you'd be moving *toward* if you weren't afraid?"

### Committed Action
- "What is one step you could take this week — a real, behavioral step — that moves in the direction of what you've named as important? Not when you feel ready. Now."
- "What difficult feeling will probably show up when you take that step? Are you willing to carry it and act anyway?"
- "On a scale of 1–10, how willing are you to feel uncomfortable in order to live this value? What would make that number one point higher?"

## Coaching Application — In Session

**ACT is not a technique set — it is a conversation orientation.** The posture is: the problem is not the thoughts or feelings themselves but the client's struggle against them and the narrowing of life that results.

**Key entry phrases:**
- *"What does your mind tell you about that?"* (vs. *"What do you think about that?"*) — creates distance from thoughts as thoughts
- *"What has the cost been of managing/avoiding that feeling?"* — opens experiential avoidance
- *"What matters to you about this — if the fear weren't in the room?"* — opens values beneath the avoidance
- *"What would someone who lived their life by that value do next?"* — creates committed action from values, not from emotion

**Fusion marker in clients:** Certainty, absoluteness, the need to be right, catastrophic language. Watch for "I *am*" statements — *"I am a failure,"* *"I am broken."* These are fusion.

**Avoidance markers in clients:** Procrastination, distraction, overwork, substance use, excessive reassurance-seeking, topic avoidance in sessions. These are experiential avoidance strategies — narrowing the life to avoid the feeling.

**When to use defusion vs. restructuring:** If the thought is inaccurate, CBT restructuring is appropriate. If the thought might be accurate but is paralyzing, defusion is the move. *"You might be right that this is hard. Can you hold that and still act?"*

## Bottom Line

ACT is the framework that explains *why* people fail to change even when they know better and want to. The mechanism — psychological inflexibility: fusion plus avoidance — is operating in virtually every client you will ever coach. You have been working around it without naming it. Name it. The six hexaflex processes are your map from the trap to movement.
