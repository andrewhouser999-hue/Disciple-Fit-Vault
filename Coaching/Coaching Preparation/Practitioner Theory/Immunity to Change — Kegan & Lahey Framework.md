# Immunity to Change — Kegan & Lahey Framework

`[CURRICULUM]`

## The Honest Assessment

Most coaching frameworks assume that once a person understands what to change and commits to changing it, the obstacle is motivation, skill, or environment. Immunity to Change (ITC) challenges that assumption at the root. Kegan and Lahey's finding — developed over 15 years of research at Harvard with thousands of leaders and professionals — is that smart, motivated, genuinely committed people fail to change because they are holding *competing commitments* they don't know they have. The change goal and the competing commitment are in direct conflict, and the competing commitment always wins until it is surfaced and examined.

This is not a motivational framework. It is a structural one. The ITC four-column map is one of the most precise diagnostic instruments available to a coach — and one of the most misused when reduced to "limiting beliefs work." Know the difference: a limiting belief is a thought about what's possible. A competing commitment is an active, protective system that produces specific behaviors because it is keeping something safe. The mechanism is more like an immune system than a mindset.

Evidence quality: **Supported.** Based on practitioner research with large populations, not RCTs. The conceptual framework is grounded in Kegan's empirically validated adult development theory (Subject-Object theory). The ITC model itself is primarily validated through case evidence and longitudinal practitioner research rather than controlled trials. Use it with confidence in the change mechanism; hold the developmental stage claims with appropriate tentativeness.

## Elaborate Overview

### The Developmental Foundation — Subject-Object Theory

ITC does not stand alone. It is the applied expression of Robert Kegan's Subject-Object theory of adult development — arguably the most sophisticated model of adult psychological growth available.

The core distinction: things we are *subject to* are things we cannot see or examine because we are embedded in them — they operate on us invisibly. Things we hold as *object* are things we can step back from, examine, and reflect on.

Development is the progressive movement of things from subject to object. A child cannot reflect on their impulses — they are subject to them. An adult can observe their impulses without acting on them — impulses have become object. But most adults still have large domains of their mental life that remain subject — particularly the assumptions that define the self.

The competing commitment and the big assumption are structures that have remained *subject* — they operate invisibly, running the behavioral system without the person's awareness that they are doing so. The ITC map moves them from subject to object, which is why it can produce change that sheer willpower cannot.

---

### What "Immunity" Actually Means

Kegan and Lahey chose the immune system metaphor deliberately. An immune system does not malfunction when it attacks a foreign body — it is working exactly as designed. When a person "fails" to change, the system is not broken. It is protecting something it has been designed to protect.

The mechanism: the person's improvement goal is genuine, but taking behavioral steps toward it triggers anxiety — because the change would threaten something the system has been built to protect (status, safety, belonging, control, self-image). The system responds by generating contrary behaviors that make the anxiety go away. The person experiences these as laziness, avoidance, lack of discipline, or failure — but they are actually the immune system doing its job.

The implication for coaching: applying more motivation, more accountability, or more skillbuilding to a client running an immune response will not work. The immune response must first be surfaced and examined. Only when the big assumption beneath it is tested — and found to be either false or no longer relevant — does the system begin to relax.

### The Four-Column Map

**Column 1 — Improvement Goal**
What the client genuinely wants to change, improve, or become. Stated as a positive commitment: *"I want to be a more present and decisive leader."*

The goal must be genuine — something the person has actually tried to change and failed, not something they merely wish were different.

**Column 2 — Contrary Behaviors**
What the person is doing (or not doing) that directly works against the Column 1 goal. Specific and behavioral — not explanations or interpretations.

*"I defer to others in meetings. I avoid giving direct feedback. I spend time in low-priority tasks instead of the hard decisions."*

The key move: Column 2 is not failure to implement Column 1. It is active behavior in the *opposite* direction. The system is doing something purposeful here.

**Column 3 — Competing Commitment**
The hidden commitment being served by the Column 2 behaviors. The diagnostic question: *"What might you be protecting by doing these things?"*

Column 3 is framed as a commitment, not a fear: *"I am also committed to not being seen as domineering / not being wrong in public / not risking relationships by being direct."*

This is the pivot point. The Column 2 behaviors are not random — they are serving Column 3. Once Column 3 is visible, the contrary behaviors make complete sense. The client was never failing to change — they were successfully protecting something.

**Column 4 — Big Assumption**
The underlying belief that makes the Column 3 commitment feel necessary and urgent. Framed as: *"I assume that if I [stopped the Column 2 behaviors], then [feared outcome]..."*

*"I assume that if I am direct and decisive, people will see me as arrogant and I will lose the relationships I depend on."*

The big assumption is not obviously false — it often has real historical roots. But it has never been *tested* in the current context. It operates as established truth when it is actually an untested hypothesis. This is the target of the behavioral experiment.

---

### The Big Assumption — Deeper

Big assumptions share a common structure: they predict catastrophe in response to the very change the person is trying to make. They are protective hypotheses that have never been allowed to be disconfirmed.

Common big assumption categories (Strong inference — not Kegan's taxonomy but consistent with the pattern):
- *If I succeed at this, I will lose the relationships / belonging I depend on*
- *If I am seen clearly, I will be found to be fundamentally defective*
- *If I take this risk and fail, I will confirm that I am not capable*
- *If I stop performing/protecting/accommodating, something bad will happen to me or others*

The theological resonance is direct and significant. These assumptions are, at their root, security bids — attempts to manage safety in a world without guaranteed protection. They reveal where a person is functionally depending on something other than God for their security.

---

### The Behavioral Experiment

Insight alone does not break an immune system. The client can understand their big assumption perfectly and still be subject to it — because understanding it doesn't test it.

The behavioral experiment is designed to test the assumption in a small, low-risk, concrete way. Not a declaration to change everything — a single specific action that violates the big assumption just enough to generate data.

Design criteria:
- Small enough to be genuinely doable despite anxiety
- Directly relevant to the big assumption (not the goal — the assumption)
- Specific: what, when, with whom
- Observable: the client can report what actually happened afterward

*"This week, give one direct piece of feedback to one person in one meeting — and notice what actually happens, not what you assumed would happen."*

The experiment is not a behavior change strategy. It is a truth-testing strategy. The client is not trying to become different — they are finding out whether the feared thing is actually true. If it is not, the assumption begins to weaken. If it partially is, the next experiment is designed around what was learned.

## What to Ignore

- Reducing ITC to "limiting beliefs" work. A limiting belief is a thought about what's possible. A competing commitment is an active protective system producing specific behaviors. They are not the same thing, and the intervention is different.
- Running the four-column map as a journaling exercise without skilled facilitation. Columns 3 and 4 require careful probing to surface accurately. Self-administered, most people stop at Column 2 and never reach the real competing commitment.
- Using ITC as a motivational tool. If the goal is to increase motivation, use MI. ITC is for diagnosing persistent structural failure to change despite genuine motivation.
- Confusing the behavioral experiment with accountability for the Column 1 goal. The experiment is not trying to achieve the goal — it is testing whether the big assumption is true.

## Coach-Safe Scope

**What a life coach CAN do with this modality:**
- Introduce and explain the ITC framework and immune system metaphor
- Run the full four-column map as a coached conversation
- Help identify specific, observable contrary behaviors (Column 2)
- Ask probing questions to surface the competing commitment (Column 3): *"What might you be protecting by doing that?"*
- Help articulate the big assumption (Column 4): *"What are you assuming would happen if you stopped?"*
- Design a specific, small behavioral experiment to test the assumption
- Debrief experiments at the following session
- Apply to any presenting issue where persistent failure to change is evident

**What requires clinical training:**
- Interpreting big assumptions that are rooted in significant developmental trauma — the competing commitment often has real historical roots, and if those roots are active trauma, the four-column map can open territory the coach cannot safely work in
- Working with the Column 3 commitment when it reveals significant psychological distress, personality-level self-protection, or clinical anxiety

**Referral indicator:**
If the behavioral experiment itself triggers significant anxiety, panic, or a flood of traumatic memory — or if the big assumption is deeply fused with early relational wounds — pause and refer. The map surfaced what was there; the coach's job is to hand it to the right person.

### Without Biblical Framework — Christian Client

**Verdict: Fixable.** Two specific points of failure for the Christian client.

**1. Assumption-testing is evidence-based, not revelation-based.** The behavioral experiment tests big assumptions by gathering empirical data: *"What actually happened?"* But many big assumptions for Christians are theological claims — "God won't provide," "I'm fundamentally too broken," "suffering means abandonment." Empirical experiments cannot disconfirm these. They require revelation-based counter-claims (the cross, covenant promise, declared identity in Christ), not experimental data. Secular ITC stops short of the only source that can actually address the assumption at its root.

**2. Competing commitments may be sin, not adaptive protection.** ITC frames all competing commitments as understandable protective structures deserving compassion — the system is working as designed. For a Christian, some competing commitments are sinful attachments to idols. They require repentance and mortification, not just reframing. ITC will generate compassion toward a commitment that Scripture would name as flesh. The diagnosis determines the intervention: insight and reframing are not interchangeable with repentance.

**Fix:** At Column 4, add a theological diagnostic — *"Is this assumption best addressed by testing it empirically, or by what God has declared?"* At Column 3, ask whether the competing commitment is a protective response or a sin pattern requiring repentance. See [[11 — Repentance Framework]] for the latter.

## Self-Directed Therapy/Coaching Questions

### Improvement Goal (Column 1)
- "What is something you have genuinely wanted to change for more than six months — and haven't?"
- "Where do you keep starting over? What is the change that won't seem to stick?"
- "What would your best self be doing that your current self keeps failing to do?"

### Contrary Behaviors (Column 2)
- "What are you actually doing — or not doing — that works directly against that goal? Be specific."
- "If someone filmed you for a week, what behaviors would they see that contradict what you say you want?"
- "What do you do instead, every time you don't do the thing you said you'd do?"

### Competing Commitment (Column 3)
- "What might those behaviors be protecting you from? What are they keeping safe?"
- "If you imagine doing the opposite of those behaviors — what is the first feeling that arises?"
- "What is the thing you are also committed to, that those behaviors are serving?"
- "What would you have to give up or risk if you fully committed to the Column 1 goal?"

### Big Assumption (Column 4)
- "What are you assuming would happen if you stopped doing those things?"
- "Complete this sentence: 'If I [stopped the contrary behavior], then...'"
- "Where did that assumption come from? When did you first learn that this was the danger?"
- "Has the assumed consequence ever actually happened? On what evidence are you operating?"

### Behavioral Experiment
- "What is the smallest possible test of that assumption you could run this week?"
- "What would it look like to take one step in the direction of your goal — and observe what actually happens?"
- "If you're wrong about the assumed consequence, what would that change?"

## Coaching Application — In Session

**Setup — framing for the client:**
*"You've told me this is something you've genuinely tried to change and haven't. I want to try a different diagnostic — not asking what you should do differently, but asking what you might be protecting. Can we try a four-column exercise?"*

The framing matters: this is diagnostic, not accountability. The client is not failing — they are protecting something. That reframe is often itself significant.

**Running the map:**
- Column 1: get a specific, positive commitment statement. If the client gives a vague aspiration, push for specificity.
- Column 2: do not accept explanations. *"I've been busy"* is not a contrary behavior. *"I cancel the hard conversations when they appear on my calendar"* is.
- Column 3: this is the longest column. Expect resistance — the competing commitment is not obvious. Ask: *"What are you protecting? What would feel scary about doing the opposite?"* Reflect back tentative language: *"It sounds like you might be committed to not being seen as..."*
- Column 4: frame as a hypothesis, not a verdict. *"So the assumption underneath this might be... does that land?"*

**Designing the experiment — key principle:**
The experiment is not accountability for Column 1. It is a test of Column 4. Ask: *"What is the smallest thing you could do that would give you real data about whether that assumption is actually true right now?"*

**Debrief — the most important session:**
*"What actually happened? Not what you expected — what happened? What does that tell you about the assumption?"*

## Bottom Line

ITC answers the question that every other change framework sidesteps: *why do motivated, intelligent people keep failing to change what they say they want to change?* The answer is not a character flaw — it is a structural one. They are holding a competing commitment that is producing the very behaviors working against their goal, in service of a big assumption they have never been allowed to test. The four-column map surfaces that structure. The behavioral experiment begins to dismantle it. For the CBC coach, the model's deepest value is the theological parallel it reveals: what ITC calls the big assumption, Scripture calls the heart's hidden commitments — and both traditions agree that surfacing them requires something more than willpower alone.
