# Beatitude Diagnostics — Quick-Select Index

## Status: Provisional — Redesign Likely After Field Use

This index was built by synthesizing the cross-references and design patterns that surfaced while building all nine beatitude diagnostics, not from any actual session experience using them — none of the nine has been used with a real client yet. Treat the Quick-Select table below as a starting hypothesis about how presenting signals map to tools, not a validated triage system. Revisit after enough sessions to know: whether the presenting-signal language here actually matches how clients or coach notes describe things in practice, whether the cross-references in the map below get followed or sit unused, and whether any of the three-way counterfeit maps in the individual documents need a fourth category or should collapse to fewer. This document should be edited, not treated as settled, once that data exists.

## Cross-Reference Map

Connections identified across the builds, in the order they surfaced. Each is documented more fully inside the individual diagnostics — this is a navigation layer, not a replacement for reading them.

- **5:3 ↔ 5:5 (shared root):** "Poor in spirit" (*ʿānî*) and "meek" (*ʿānāw*) share a Hebrew root family. Distinct facets, not duplicates — 5:3 is posture toward God (dependence for standing), 5:5 is posture toward others and circumstance (restraint under provocation).
- **5:4 ↔ 5:6 (shared anchor):** Both draw on Isaiah 61:1, which names "the poor" and, by extension, mourners as its first beneficiary categories — real textual overlap, not just thematic adjacency.
- **5:3 ↔ 5:6 / 5:3 ↔ 5:7 (root-cause routing):** Self-sufficiency (5:3's opposite) is frequently what's underneath a stalled hunger for righteousness (5:6) or an unmerciful posture (5:7) — a client with nothing to extend has usually received nothing to extend it from.
- **5:3 ↔ 5:8 (justification/sanctification bridge):** Both engage "clean hands, and a pure heart" (Ps 24:4 background for 5:3's territory via Isaiah 66:2, and directly for 5:8) — but Psalm 24's existing CIC-track use answers the justification question (how does an unqualified person get standing at all), while the 5:8 diagnostic works entirely downstream of that, in ongoing sanctification. See the 5:8 document's "Relationship to Psalm 24" section — this is the one connection in the map with real risk if blended carelessly.
- **5:8 ↔ 5:9 (James 3:17 ordering):** "First pure, then peaceable" — false peace (5:9's Hermit and False Noble counterfeits) frequently traces back to impure motive (5:8's own counterfeits: fear, self-protection, image management).
- **5:7 ↔ 5:9 (adjacent but distinct safety risk):** Both carry real-harm contraindications around pressuring reconciliation with someone unsafe — but they're not identical. 5:7's risk is forgiveness/reconciliation conflation in the client's own grievance; 5:9's sharper, more distinct risk is compulsive third-party mediation as a trauma-adapted role, not a copy of 5:7's shape.
- **5:5 ↔ 5:9 (shared mechanism):** Both required a three-way Warrior/Hermit/False Noble counterfeit map and two-part (behavior + motivation) probes, because in both cases the same outward behavior — staying calm, not engaging conflict — can come from trust, fear, or performance, and those are indistinguishable without asking what's underneath.
- **5:9 ↔ 5:10-12 (the sequence's own logic):** Costly, genuine peacemaking (5:9) can itself be what provokes opposition (5:10-12) — "for righteousness' sake" isn't only about doctrinal conviction; actively pursuing genuine reconciliation in a hostile system can cost the peacemaker exactly the kind of opposition 5:10-12 describes.

## Recurring Design Patterns Across the Series

Noted here for anyone extending or revising this series later — these are the load-bearing decisions the individual builds made, collected in one place.

- **Three-way counterfeit maps** (Warrior / Hermit / False Noble) appear in five of the nine builds (5:4, 5:5, 5:7, 5:8, 5:9). In every case, the wound type that's the "obvious opposite" of the beatitude turned out to be the *least* dangerous false positive — the ones that mimic the beatitude's surface behavior (Hermit's withdrawal, False Noble's performance) are the ones that actually fool a coach.
- **Two-part probes** (behavior, then the motivation underneath it) were required wherever the same outward behavior could come from trust, fear, or performance — meekness and peacemaking specifically. A single session-ready question isn't enough in those cases.
- **Contraindications framed as coach-conduct rules, not client-suspicion lenses**, wherever a real-harm/safety risk exists (mercy, peacemaking, persecution). The lesson, finalized during the 5:10-12 build: when a contraindication exists to prevent a practitioner's misuse of a tool, word it as a constraint on the practitioner, not a screening test applied to the client — same protective content, opposite posture, and posture is what keeps it from undermining trust.
- **Vault-check-before-build** caught two overlap risks with existing CIC-track documents — 5:3 with Isaiah 66:2b, 5:8 with Psalm 24 — resolved differently depending on whether the existing document was itself diagnostic (5:3: build a short companion, defer the exegesis) or served a different function like late-stage declaration (5:8: full build plus an explicit relationship section, since the risk was blending two different jobs, not duplicating content).
