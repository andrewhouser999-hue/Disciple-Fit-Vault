## Purpose

This is a practitioner reference for in-session diagnostic use when a client's response to conflict — their own, or conflict between others they're caught inside — is a live question. Andrew uses it during session to select and sequence diagnostic probes. It is not a scoring rubric and is not shown to clients. It belongs in the diagnostic layer of the coaching mechanism: after the relationship has created a safe container, before covenant truth application.

**Read the Contraindication section before deploying probes.** This tool carries two distinct risk profiles, not one — read both before using it.

*Part of a series covering all nine Beatitudes (Mt 5:3–12) — see [[_Beatitude Diagnostics — Quick-Select Index]] for the presenting-signal lookup and cross-reference map across all eight diagnostics.*

## Governing Reframe

**This tool diagnoses whether a client's response to conflict is costly, Christlike reconciling work — not whether conflict is present or absent.**

Colossians 1:20 uses the verb form of this beatitude's exact word — Christ "making peace" (*eirēnopoiēsas*) "by the blood of his cross." Ephesians 2:14-17 names the mechanism: he killed the hostility between God and humanity, and between hostile human groups, through costly cross-work — not by avoiding conflict, and not by imposing peace through power. Jesus's own audience lived under Pax Romana, peace made by conquest; this beatitude is a direct, pointed subversion of that pattern.

This creates a trap that runs in two directions, and both need guarding against — unusual among the beatitudes in this series, where most misreadings run one way:

- Read one way, "be a peacemaker" becomes an instruction to avoid conflict, smooth over tension, and never disturb the peace — which is not peacemaking at all, it's the absence of the costly engagement the cross itself required.
- Read the other way, it becomes an instruction that the client is personally responsible for resolving every conflict they're near, regardless of whether it's theirs to resolve or whether it's safe to try — which produces exactly the compulsive mediation pattern named in Contraindication, below.

A note on scope, held honestly: Matthew 10:34 has Jesus say "I have not come to bring peace, but a sword" — his coming divides households (10:35-36). This is real tension with 5:9, not smoothed over here. The more defensible resolution distinguishes peace-with-truth (which sometimes requires conflict when people reject Christ) from peacemaking-as-reconciling-ministry (what this beatitude commends) — this tool works entirely within the second category.

## Entry Signal

Deploy when one or more of the following appears in session:

- The client describes a specific conflict — their own, or between people close to them — and their response to it is a live diagnostic question
- The client identifies as "the peacemaker" of their family or friend group, and this identity warrants a closer look rather than automatic affirmation
- Affections or Character Wound work has surfaced a pattern of managing others' conflicts, and it's unclear whether this is costly love or anxious hypervigilance
- The client avoids a specific, nameable conflict and frames the avoidance as keeping the peace

## Contraindication

**This document has two distinct contraindications. Read both — they are not the same risk.**

**1. Do not pressure reconciliation with someone unsafe.** Romans 12:18 qualifies the peace-pursuit command directly: "if possible, so far as it depends on you" — peacemaking is bounded by what's actually possible and within the client's control, not an unconditional obligation regardless of the other party's danger or unwillingness. If the client's own conflict involves abuse, ongoing danger, or an unrepentant and unsafe party, this tool is not permission to press toward reconciliation. See [[Mercy — Practitioner Diagnostic (Mt 5_7)]] for the closely related forgiveness/reconciliation distinction — the two beatitudes carry adjacent but not identical risk here.

**2. Do not affirm compulsive third-party mediation as peacemaking.** This is the sharper and more common risk, and it is distinct from the first. A client who grew up in a high-conflict or addicted household often carries a "family peacemaker" role — managing, predicting, and pre-empting conflict *between other people* — not from costly Christlike love, but because their nervous system learned early that conflict signals danger and their survival depended on preventing it. This looks like devotion to peace and is actually hypervigilance. Affirming it as the beatitude in action reinforces a trauma-driven role rather than naming it. Listen for: conflict-management that activates around *other people's* disputes specifically, anxiety (not compassion) as the presenting affect when conflict arises nearby, and a family or relational history where the client's safety once depended on managing tension between others.

**Timing matters for fresh or escalating conflict.** A client in the middle of an acute, unresolved dispute is not a candidate for this diagnostic yet — assess once the immediate situation has enough stability to be examined rather than just survived.

## Diagnostic Probes

Peacemaking, like meekness, resists direct self-report in both directions — a person avoiding conflict rarely experiences it as avoidance (it feels like wisdom or grace), and a person compulsively mediating others' conflicts rarely experiences it as anxiety (it feels like responsibility or love). Probes are two-part: behavior, then the motivation underneath it. Probes also cover two contexts — the client's own conflicts, and their role in others' — since peacemaking operates differently in each.

---

### Layer 1 — Response to Conflict (Biblical Counseling diagnostic)

#### The Engagement Question
*Source: Romans 12:18 — peace pursuit bounded by what's possible and within the client's control.*

**Session-ready, part one:** "Think of a real, specific conflict you're in or have been in — not hypothetical. What did you actually do?"

**Session-ready, part two:** "And underneath that — was it costly for you to do that? Did it require something real from you? Or was it mostly about making the discomfort go away as fast as possible?"

**Why this works:** Part one gathers the behavior; part two locates whether it was genuine reconciling work (costly, engaged) or conflict-avoidance dressed as peace (fast, discomfort-driven). Genuine peacemaking, per Ephesians 2:14-17's pattern, costs something.

**Listen for:** Costly engagement — naming the hard thing, staying in the discomfort long enough for something real to happen — is the beatitude functioning. Fast resolution driven by discomfort-relief, or conflict simply never engaged at all, signals avoidance regardless of how it's described.

---

### Layer 2 — Counterfeit Mapping (Biblical Counseling / Spiritual Formation diagnostic)

Peacemaking's counterfeits map across all three wound types — but check for Hermit-pattern avoidance first. In a Christian coaching context specifically, conflict-avoidance is frequently culturally reinforced as virtue ("keeping the peace," "not being contentious"), making it the most common real-world presentation by a wide margin — not equally likely alongside the other two.

| Counterfeit | Wound type | What it looks like | What's actually driving it |
|---|---|---|---|
| Forced resolution | Warrior | Peace achieved by winning, overpowering, or forcing compliance | Pax-Romana-style peace — control, not reconciliation |
| Conflict avoidance *(check this first)* | Hermit | Disengagement, silence, "I don't like conflict," conflict never actually addressed | Avoidance called peace; the discomfort of engagement is what's actually being managed |
| Performed mediation | False Noble | Visible reasonableness, credited as "the peaceful one," surface resolution | Social credit for appearing peaceable; underlying issues remain unresolved |

**Why this works:** Warrior's forced resolution is the easiest to spot — it looks like conflict, not peace. Hermit and False Noble both *look like* the beatitude from outside, which is exactly why they're the dangerous ones, and why Hermit specifically needs to be checked first given how normalized conflict-avoidance is in many church cultures.

**Listen for:** With Hermit-pattern presentations, ask what specifically would have to happen for the conflict to actually be addressed — a client who can't answer, or who says it's already resolved when nothing has actually been discussed, is showing avoidance. With False Noble-pattern presentations, check whether the "resolution" holds up under any real pressure or reappears in a different form later.

### Layer 3 — Third-Party Mediation (Biblical Counseling / Spiritual Formation diagnostic)

This layer targets the second context peacemaking operates in — not the client's own conflicts, but their role managing conflict between other people.

#### The Family Peacemaker Question
*Source: this is the Contraindication's second item, operationalized as a probe.*

**Session-ready, part one:** "Do you find yourself managing or smoothing over tension between other people — even when it's not really your conflict to solve?"

**Session-ready, part two:** "When you do that — what's actually happening in you? Is it compassion for both sides? Or is there something more like dread, like you need it to stop?"

**Why this works:** The same behavior — stepping into others' conflicts — can be costly Christlike mediation or anxious hypervigilance, and the difference is entirely in what's driving it. Dread and urgency point toward the trauma-adaptive pattern named in Contraindication; compassion without urgency points toward genuine ministry of reconciliation (2 Cor 5:18-20).

**Listen for:** "I care about both of them and want to help" without physiological urgency is closer to genuine peacemaking. "I can't stand it when there's tension, I have to fix it" is closer to the family-peacemaker survival pattern — treat this as trauma-adjacent material, not a virtue to affirm.

## What to Do with What Surfaces

**Costly engagement present in the client's own conflicts** — the beatitude is functioning. Affirm it, and note the connection to 2 Corinthians 5:18-20's "ministry of reconciliation" — this may be a capacity worth naming as growing, not just present.

**Warrior-pattern forced resolution surfaces** — move toward Affections Discovery to locate what winning or control is protecting.

**Hermit-pattern avoidance surfaces, in the client's own conflicts** — name the avoidance gently as avoidance, not peace; connect to [[Purity of Heart — Practitioner Diagnostic (Mt 5_8)]] if fear or self-protection (rather than genuine trust) is driving the avoidance — James 3:17's "first pure, then peaceable" ordering suggests impure motive is often what's underneath false peace.

**False Noble-pattern performed mediation surfaces** — name the gap between the visible resolution and what's still actually unresolved underneath.

**Compulsive third-party mediation surfaces** — this is not the beatitude functioning; it's a trauma-adaptive role. Do not affirm it as virtue. Explore its origin (see Contraindication) and consider whether Character Wound or attachment-history work is warranted before continuing.

**Reconciliation pressure toward an unsafe party is being considered** — stop. See Contraindication, item 1.

## What This Tool Is Not

- Not a scoring rubric. There is no passing result, and no expectation that every conflict resolves.
- Not permission to pressure reconciliation with someone unsafe — see Contraindication, item 1, and [[Mercy — Practitioner Diagnostic (Mt 5_7)]].
- Not an affirmation of compulsive conflict management as virtue — see Contraindication, item 2.
- Not a claim that the client is responsible for resolving conflicts that aren't theirs to resolve.
- Not a client-facing document. The theological grounding is for Andrew's comprehension; clients receive the stripped-down session-ready questions, not the framework.

## Peacemaking in Life Experience

### What It Is

Costly, active engagement with real conflict that moves it toward genuine resolution — not the absence of conflict, and not conflict resolved by winning. It's recognized by what it costs the peacemaker to do it: staying present through discomfort, naming what's actually true, and doing the relational work reconciliation requires, bounded by what's actually possible and within their control.

### Not to Be Mistaken For

- **Conflict avoidance** — disengagement or silence called "keeping the peace" when nothing has actually been addressed; see Layer 2's Hermit-pattern mapping.
- **Forced or dominated resolution** — peace achieved by winning, overpowering, or compliance rather than genuine reconciliation; see Layer 2's Warrior-pattern mapping.
- **Performed mediation** — visible reasonableness credited socially while the underlying issue remains unresolved; see Layer 2's False Noble-pattern mapping.
- **Compulsive third-party mediation** — anxious management of others' conflicts driven by hypervigilance or a trauma-adaptive family role, not compassion; see Contraindication, item 2.
- **Unconditional pursuit of reconciliation regardless of safety** — peacemaking is bounded by Romans 12:18's "if possible, so far as it depends on you," not an obligation to reconcile with someone unsafe or unrepentant; see Contraindication, item 1.
