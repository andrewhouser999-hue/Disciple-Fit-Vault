## Purpose

This is a practitioner reference for in-session diagnostic use when a client's appetite for righteousness — its presence, direction, or quality — is a live question. Andrew uses it during session to select and sequence diagnostic probes. It is not a scoring rubric and is not shown to clients. It belongs in the diagnostic layer of the coaching mechanism: after the relationship has created a safe container, before covenant truth application.

---

## Governing Reframe

**This tool reveals where appetite goes — not whether appetite exists.**

Matthew 5:6 is a *description*, not a *test*. Jesus names hunger for righteousness as characteristic of Kingdom citizens — a mark of what is already true, not a standard to achieve. Using this diagnostic to evaluate whether a client "hungers enough" inverts the beatitude's logic and produces a performance loop. The right question is never "do you hunger?" It is: **what is the hunger actually going toward, and what is blocking or rerouting it?**

Retain this posture through every probe. The goal is to illuminate appetite direction and formation conditions — not to render a verdict on the client's spiritual state.

---

## Entry Signal

Deploy when one or more of the following appears in session:

- Client questions the genuineness of their desire for God or righteousness ("I'm not sure I actually want to change")
- Client's stated values and actual pursuits are visibly misaligned and the gap hasn't been named
- Client expresses spiritual flatness, lack of growth, or indifference they can't account for
- Client is pursuing formation disciplines but experiencing no movement — the conditions question needs to precede the discipline question
- Affections work (via the Affections Discovery Process) has surfaced a false covenant object and the client needs diagnostic clarity on what righteousness hunger looks like in contrast

Do not deploy as a routine intake question. These probes are best used when there is already enough relational trust that the client can receive a question about their interior appetite without deflecting into performance.

---

## Contraindication

**Shame-prone clients require adjusted delivery.** A client with a high inner critic and a pattern of self-condemnation will use these questions as evidence against themselves rather than as diagnostic data. In this population, the Grief Inventory and Hiddenness Test are particularly dangerous — they can activate a shame spiral instead of surfacing appetite information. Adjustments:

- Lead with the Trajectory Audit rather than the Grief Inventory (trajectory is gentler; it asks about direction rather than failure)
- Preface probes with the governing reframe stated plainly: "I'm not asking whether you hunger — I want to understand where the hunger goes"
- If shame is activated mid-probe, name it directly and pivot: "It sounds like that question landed as an accusation. That's not what I'm after. Can we look at it differently?"

## Diagnostic Probes

Organized by mechanism function. Use one or two per session, not all five. Select based on presenting signal.

---

### Layer 1 — Affections Surfacing (Biblical Counseling diagnostic)

These questions surface what the appetite is actually pursuing — its real object, not its stated object.

---

#### The Pursuit Question
*Source: Isaiah 55:2 — "Why do you spend your money for what is not bread, and your wages for what does not satisfy?"*

**Session-ready:** "When you're depleted, restless, or off — when something in you is hungry — where do you actually go? What do you reach for?"

**Why this works:** Genuine hunger for righteousness drives toward what actually feeds it. Substitute appetites route toward comfort, approval, control, or status — things that feel like satisfaction but leave the hunger intact. Isaiah 55:2 is God's own diagnostic question, asked of people who are hungering in the wrong direction. The probe surfaces false covenant objects by tracking the actual movement of appetite rather than asking the client to evaluate their desires in the abstract.

**Listen for:** Does the client name anything oriented toward God, Scripture, prayer, community, obedience, or justice? Or do they name comfort behaviors, distraction, relational validation, achievement, or control? What is conspicuously absent? A client who reaches for approval when depleted is demonstrating where the deepest hunger is lodged, regardless of stated values.

---

#### The Grief Inventory
*Source: Psalm 119:136 — "Streams of tears flow from my eyes, for your law is not kept." 2 Corinthians 7:10–11.*

**Session-ready:** "What sins or failures in your own life genuinely grieve you — not just embarrass you, but break something in you? And separately: is there anything in the world around you — injustice, unfaithfulness, compromise — that produces the same grief?"

**Why this works:** Active appetite for righteousness produces grief over its absence — in the self and in the world. Paul distinguishes godly grief (which produces repentance and change) from worldly grief (which produces shame, avoidance, or self-protection). The distinction between embarrassment and grief is diagnostic: embarrassment is reputation-protective; grief is righteousness-oriented. Psalm 119:136 shows a psalmist grieved not only over his own sin but over others' violation of God's law — a broadened appetite that is oriented toward righteousness itself, not just personal performance.

**Listen for:** Does the client's grief name the offense against God's character or order — or does it name the consequences (being caught, relational damage, loss of face)? Grief over consequences is worldly grief. Grief that names the breach of righteousness itself is godly grief — evidence of appetite that is oriented toward the right object. Also note: a client who cannot identify any grief may be operating with a suppressed affective register, which is its own diagnostic signal (formation conditions question, not absence of hunger).

---

### Layer 2 — Appetite Direction (Biblical Counseling / Spiritual Formation diagnostic)

This question tests whether the appetite survives the removal of social reward — the most reliable behavioral marker of what the hunger is actually after.

---

#### The Hiddenness Test
*Source: Matthew 6:1–6 — Jesus's contrast between hypocrites who perform for an audience and those whose practice survives in secret.*

**Prequalifier (ask first):** "Is there anything in your life right now that you'd call a spiritual practice — prayer, reading Scripture, serving someone, anything?" If the client has no practice to evaluate, table this probe and use the Pursuit Question instead. Without an existing practice, the Hiddenness Test produces shame about absence rather than data about appetite direction.

**Session-ready:** "In whatever you do spiritually — however small — how much of it continues when no one would notice? What falls away when the audience disappears?"

**Why this works:** The appetite that requires an audience is approval hunger. The appetite that persists in secret is righteousness hunger. Matthew 6 draws the contrast sharply: hypocrites perform for social reward; those who practice before the Father alone are expressing a different orientation. This probe is not about the quantity of hidden practice but about its existence and the client's awareness of what disappears without an audience.

**Listen for:** Hesitation, deflection, or shame when this question is asked is itself diagnostic — it often signals awareness of performance motivation, which is valuable data. A client who says "honestly, not much survives when I'm alone" is showing self-awareness, not absence of hunger. What matters most: do they experience the absence of hidden practice as *loss* (hunger signal — they know something is missing) or as *indifference* (appetite is not oriented toward righteousness in the first place)?

---

### Layer 3 — Formation Trajectory (Spiritual Formation diagnostic)

This question evaluates the Spirit's work over time. It shifts the diagnostic from a fixed-point snapshot — which produces pass/fail anxiety — to a directional question, which reveals whether formation is occurring.

---

#### The Trajectory Audit
*Source: Proverbs 4:18 — "The path of the righteous is like the light of dawn, which shines brighter and brighter until full day."*

**Session-ready:** "Not asking about perfection. But looking back two or three years — are you more or less oriented toward righteousness than you were? Not just in behavior: in what you want, what troubles you, what you're drawn toward?"

**Why this works:** The Spirit's work produces trajectory, not a static condition. Proverbs 4:18 describes a path that moves — incrementally, sometimes slowly, but directionally. A diagnostic that asks only about current state cannot distinguish a client in early formation from one who is not moving at all. The trajectory question surfaces movement (or its absence) and anchors the diagnostic in what the Spirit is doing over time rather than what the client achieved today. Note: the question asks about *what the client wants* and *what troubles them* — appetite markers, not behavior markers. Behavioral change can precede or follow appetite change; the question aims at the interior.

**Listen for:** Direction, not degree. "I care about things I didn't used to care about" is an appetite expansion signal. "I'm troubled by things that used to roll off me" is a grief activation signal — both indicate growing hunger. A flat or declining trajectory, or a client who cannot identify any movement, is a formation conditions signal: the hunger may be present but the disciplines that create conditions for the Spirit's work are absent or misdirected.

## What to Do with What Surfaces

After one or more probes, match what emerged to the appropriate next move.

**Appetite routing toward substitutes** — the client's hunger is going toward comfort, approval, control, or status rather than righteousness. This is affections/false covenant object territory. Move toward the Affections Discovery Process.

**Shame about the quality of the hunger** — the client is evaluating themselves against a standard rather than receiving the beatitude as description. This is a false identity structure. The appropriate move is covenant truth application: Matthew 5:6 itself as a declarative truth the client can receive. The beatitude does not say "blessed are those who hunger perfectly." It says hunger is the mark of Kingdom belonging — not a test to pass.

**Genuine grief with no formation traction** — the appetite is present and oriented correctly, but the client is not moving. This is a between-session discipline question. The hunger exists; the conditions for the Spirit's work need attention. Ask: "What would it look like to create regular space where this hunger could be fed rather than suppressed?"

**Flat trajectory, indifference, no grief** — do not immediately conclude absence of genuine hunger. First ask what the appetite *is* going toward. Something occupies the position righteousness ought to hold; finding it is the diagnostic work. Use the Pursuit Question if not already deployed. If the pursuit question also surfaces nothing, this may indicate suppressed affective access — a formation conditions and attachment question, not a verdict on regeneration.

---

## What This Tool Is Not

- Not a scoring rubric. There is no passing result.
- Not a regeneracy test. Do not use it to evaluate whether a client is genuinely saved.
- Not a client-facing document. The theological grounding is for Andrew's comprehension; clients receive the stripped-down session-ready question, not the framework.
- Not a formation tool. Between-session formation practices (Psalm 119 as a mirror; Isaiah 55 as reflection) are a downstream artifact from this diagnostic — not part of it.
