# Andrew Houser — Career Themes Profile

*Synthesized from full work history for use in career feedback and vocational clarity conversations.*

---

## Theme 1: I Am the Person Who Wins Revenue Through Relationship Trust, Not Pressure or Volume

You don't outperform by working more calls or harder pitches. You outperform by building genuine understanding of what someone needs and aligning to it. The close rates (48.9% vs. 30% national average at Ecowater; top 5% at Schwab), the long contract portfolios (23 clients on 12–18 month contracts at FOX 42), the 300% above-average client satisfaction scores — all of this points to a sales style that is fundamentally relational and consultative. You win trust before you win the deal.

---

## Theme 2: I Am the Person Who Extracts Principle from Practice, Then Teaches It

The pattern repeats in multiple roles: perform at the top, then get selected to train others, then build the curriculum. You don't just do things well — you understand *why* they work and can systematize it for transfer. Ecowater commissioned you to train. Schwab made you the onboarding trainer after you ranked #1, and you built the department's first formal curriculum from scratch. The coaching methodology and the RPA framework are the same tendency applied independently. This is a consistent intellectual habit, not just a job function.

---

## Theme 3: I Am the Person Who Uses Human Behavior as the Primary Analytical Lens

Every domain you've worked in, you've approached through the question of how people actually function. In sales: psychology-based selling, personality-adaptive communication. In coaching: affections, identity, attachment. In consulting: psychological safety, implicit behavioral patterns, organizational dynamics. The industries change; the lens doesn't. This is an intellectual interest, not just a skill.

---

## Theme 4: I Am the Person Who Synthesizes Across Disciplines Rather Than Specializing in One

Your best work is integrative. The coaching model pulls from spiritual formation, biblical counseling, and life coaching — not one, but all three in a specific relationship. The RPA evidence base draws from neuroscience, organizational behavior, clinical research, and hospitality industry data. You are drawn to problems that require thinking across fields, not going deeper into one.

---

## Theme 5: I Am the Person Who Is Drawn to Deep Change, Not Surface Behavior Adjustment

In coaching, you're not working on habits or goals — you're working at the level of identity, belief, and desire. In sales and training, you weren't teaching scripts — you were working on how people think and relate. There's a consistent pull toward transformation that goes to the root rather than managing the symptom. This is both an interest and a value.

---

## Theme 6: I Am the Person Who Needs Latitude to Perform at the Ceiling

Your peak performance stories are all in roles or contexts where you controlled the relationship, the approach, and the execution. The entrepreneurial thread is persistent — Houser Consulting, Disciple Fit, RPA. This doesn't mean you can't function inside an organization — Schwab proves otherwise — but the pattern suggests you are a self-directed performer who designs his own approach within whatever structure he's given. Tightly managed, process-heavy environments are not where you produce your best work.

---

## Theme 7: I Am the Person Who Treats Communication as a Studied Discipline, Not Just a Natural Gift

You've invested deliberately in communication precision — deep listening training, adaptive communication across personality types, message structure and delivery. The results show up in the data (client satisfaction scores, fund growth rates). The more important signal is that you treat communication as something to be improved, not just leveraged. That's a practitioner mindset applied to a relational skill.

---

## Where These Themes Intersect

Themes 1, 3, and 7 all point to the same thing from different angles: you are deeply attuned to people at the level of motivation, trust, and meaning, and you've built your commercial effectiveness on that attunement rather than on volume or process.

---

## The Tension Worth Naming

Themes 4 and 6 together raise a real question — you operate best when you can integrate broadly and work with high discretion. That's a strong profile for independent practice or a senior consultative role, and a harder fit for managed, process-heavy employment. A feedback conversation probing vocational fit will likely surface this directly.
