## Discovery Call Flow

### Stage 1 — Small Talk & Scene Setting (2-3 min)
*"Tell me a little about yourself and what you've been up to."*

> "I've spent my career at the intersection of sales, communication, and developing people. Most recently at Schwab — left after the restructuring post-merger, used the time to be intentional about what's next. When this came across my desk I took a closer look and it checked boxes most roles don't."

Then stop. Let them respond.

---

### Stage 2 — They Pitch the Role (5-7 min)
Listen. Collect ammunition for later. When they pause, ask:

> "What does the profile look like for someone who's succeeded in this role versus someone who struggled?"

---

### Stage 3 — They Dig Into You (10-15 min)

**"Walk me through your sales background."**
> "I've consistently been at the top of every sales environment I've been in — 3:1 over peers in revenue production, half of total territory sales by year three. The through-line has always been the quality of the conversation, not the volume of activity. Which fits exactly what you described."

**"Why Facet specifically?"**
> "Flat-fee fiduciary is a high-conviction sell — you're actually acting in the client's interest. I can't say that about every sales role I've looked at. What I appreciate about Facet is they take a stand for something, not just a commission."

**"How do you handle rejection or a slow pipeline?"**
> "I've never been fueled by external pressure — deadlines, quotas, recognition. What keeps me moving is internal. I'm motivated by becoming world-class at what I do. A slow day is a problem to solve, not a reason to stall."

**"Are you interviewing elsewhere?"**
> "I'm being selective. This one moved to the top of the list quickly."

---

### Stage 4 — The Comp Conversation

**Anchor at $95k — above their ceiling. $90k becomes the compromise.**

> "Based on my track record I'm targeting $95k base. I know that's above your posted range — I also know what I bring to a role like this."

If they push back to $90k:
> "I can work with $90k if everything else aligns. But I want to be clear — I'm not a $70k performer and I don't think you'd want to bring me in at that number. It sets the wrong tone for both of us."

Hold the line once more, then let the silence breathe.

**Why $95k:**
- Anchoring at their ceiling ($90k) leaves no room — they can only negotiate down
- $95k makes $90k feel like a concession on your part — you get what you wanted
- The published range is a starting position, not an absolute ceiling
- State one number, never a range — ranges signal willingness to negotiate before it begins

---

> "One thing I'd add — I've naturally moved into training and developing people in every role I've been in. Not because I was asked, but because I saw the gap and filled it. If there's a path here that opens into leadership or a broader scope, that's something I'd want to understand."

## Q16 — Tell Me About Yourself (Full Robinson Version)

*For formal interview stages — hiring manager and beyond. Longer and story-driven. Not for recruiter small talk.*

> "I figured something out early in sales that most people in my field never did — and it took me a while to understand why it worked.
>
> I was sitting with a flooring company owner — his seven-year-old at the table — when his character of a wife appeared in the doorway, looked at both of us, and said, 'We don't have any money,' and kept walking. The boy looked up contemplatively: 'I **thought** we were broke.' The owner and I glanced at each other and laughed — the nervous kind.
>
> I didn't say anything. I just waited. He started talking. He'd been burned before — costly sales pitches, still recovering, afraid of making another bad decision. But underneath all of it, he knew he needed effective marketing or nothing else would move. Once that surfaced, I built a plan around his actual growth strategy. He became a client.
>
> That happened over and over. Not because I was better at closing — because I was willing to go into the fear, the past disappointment, the thing nobody else would sit with. Most salespeople treat that as an obstacle. I treated it as the real conversation finally starting.
>
> That habit made me nationally ranked. 48.6% close rate. Three times my peers at the next company. I eventually got formally trained in what I'd been doing intuitively — coaching and cognitive behavioral frameworks.   Not a detour.   A sharpening.
>
> That's why this role fits. The people calling Facet have probably been sold financial products before. They're carrying skepticism, maybe some past disappointment. What they need first isn't a feature explanation. It's to feel like this time is genuinely different. That's the work I know how to do."

[The Six Primal Brian Stimuli are found in this communication. The Persuasion Code 
Deep Canvassing: What makes deep canvassing different from standard persuasion: it targets the emotional and identity layer first. You're not trying to insert better information — you're trying to make it psychologically safe enough for the person to examine their own reasoning. How Minds Change by David McRaney]
   
*~220 words / ~100 seconds*

**Delivery notes:**
- Pause after "the nervous kind." Let it land before moving on.
- Slow down on "the real conversation finally starting." That's the turn.
- The Facet close is a statement, not a plea. Say it like you mean it and stop.

## HNW Story — Schwab / $30M Client

*For: "What's your experience with high net worth clients?" / "What makes you different?" / "Walk me through your Schwab experience."*

> "I had a client at Schwab — thirty million dollars — who called in about a routine maintenance issue on a smaller account. Standard call. But early in the conversation he started talking about the state of the world. Poor conditions. General unease.
>
> — I've learned that macro dissatisfaction is often a signal. Not always — but often it's a proxy for something more personal. So I stayed with him in his unease, then made connection to the economy and pivot: 'How is your portfolio doing?'
>
> That opened it. He wasn't happy with his advisor. Poor decisions, neglect, opportunities missed — it had been costing him real revenue potential. He hadn't set out to have that conversation. He called about maintenance. But the right question at the right moment surfaced what was actually there.
>
> I probed, offered a parallel path — not a pitch. First, educate him on how we work. If it's a fit, move forward. We'd handle the transfer. He moved all 30 million.
>
> What I've learned about high net worth clients is they rarely state the real problem directly. They signal it. My job is to hear the signal.
>
> That's why Facet's model matters to me. The people calling aren't just looking for financial advice — they're often looking for a reason to finally trust someone in this industry again. I know how to create that opening."

*~200 words / ~90 seconds*

**Delivery note:** Slow down on *"macro dissatisfaction is often a signal."* That's the moment the interviewer realizes you're operating at a different level. Don't rush past it.
