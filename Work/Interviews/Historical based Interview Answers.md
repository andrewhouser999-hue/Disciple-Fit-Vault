## Question 1
**"300% above company average on Client Service Index. What specifically did you do differently from the reps scoring average?"**

### Answer

"I made it a personal mission to become the best at what the best in financial services considered the gold standard of client communication. Not competent — the best. That meant treating skill development as a serious, long-term discipline.

Most reps look at a structured call framework — one that breaks every interaction into phases with skills mapped at beginner, intermediate, and advanced levels — and do what's natural: pick a few skills they're comfortable with and stop. I organized my implementation deliberately, sequencing skills over years, building in layers, and never treating myself as finished. My manager told me I had the highest relational and emotional intelligence of anyone he had hired. That didn't happen by accident.

I also challenged the standard way performance was measured. The company's satisfaction metric didn't fully capture what I was seeing on my calls, so I tracked something more precise — the percentage of total calls each quarter that earned a perfect score across all metrics. The company average is 4.37%. My highest quarter was 14%. My 2025 average was 12.12%.

What drove those numbers was the ability to meet any client exactly where they were — guiding them, building their confidence, and persuading them to hold a perspective on trading and investing that actually served their decision-making rather than their anxiety. That's what deliberate, sustained development looks like over time."

## Question 2
**"You mentored new reps to close deals 10x the standard size. Walk me through one of those deals — who was involved, what was the process, what did you actually coach them on?"**

### Answer

"When I was hired, the training I received was product-focused — transactional. Here's the product, here's the price, here's how to close. From day one, that wasn't the approach I was going to take. My sole interest was selling consultatively, because I knew that's where the real numbers lived and honestly, it's the kind of work I find meaningful.

While my peers were closing deals at the $10,000 level, I was consistently closing at $100,000 and above. That kind of gap gets noticed. New reps started coming to me asking what I was doing differently.

What I walked them through was a complete reframe — how to qualify a prospect, conduct a real discovery call, perform a genuine needs assessment, co-design the solution collaboratively with the client, close with confidence, and follow through. But in practice, most of the refinement work happened right at the front end: how you introduce yourself and how you run the discovery process. If those two things fall short, nothing downstream works. Get those right and the rest of the process has a real chance. Most reps took it from there.

Management noticed and approached me about moving into a leadership role. I declined. At that point I was already earning more than my manager, and my production was on track to increase substantially from there. It wasn't the right move financially — and I knew I had more to build as a producer."

## Question 3
**"At FOX 42 you were selling for the 4th-ranked station. How did you overcome that in competitive situations where a prospect could go to the top 3?"**

### Answer

"Selling for the 4th-ranked station in the market sounds like a disadvantage — and it would be, if you're selling the way most reps sell. Leading with ratings, reach, audience share. That's a race I was never going to win against the top three.

So I didn't run that race.

Consultative sales is built on a different premise entirely. The goal isn't to pitch a product — it's to understand a client's problem deeply enough to build a solution around it, define what results should look like, and involve the client in designing the answer so they own it. When you do that well, the client isn't choosing between station rankings. They're choosing between a vendor and a partner.

Most clients are sophisticated enough to see through a brand pitch. They're not buying the #1 station because it's popular — they're trying to solve a business problem. The rep who takes the time to understand that problem, earns their trust, and builds something that actually serves their goals will win that client. Every time.

That's how I generated $650,000 in local advertising revenue for a 4th-ranked station, built a portfolio of 23 clients on 12 to 18 month contracts, and by year three accounted for 50% of total local advertising sales. The station's rank never changed. My approach did all the work."

## Question 4
**"48.9% close rate vs a 30% national average at Ecowater. What were you doing that the other 200+ reps weren't?"**

### Answer

"When I started at Ecowater, we were working from a 20-page script. Management eventually refined it to an 8-page presentation. The core of it was emotional — creating a visceral awareness of what was in the water clients were drinking and cooking with, and letting them experience firsthand the difference soft water makes. That emotional foundation was real and it worked.

But emotion alone doesn't close at 48.9%.

What I believed — and what I think separated me from the national average — was that most reps avoid objections. They sense resistance and either back off or try to talk past it, because they're afraid surfacing an objection will cost them the sale. I did the opposite. I went looking for objections. Courageously, directly. Because an objection isn't a wall — it's a question the client hasn't had answered yet.

Once you surface it, you address it with a logical, achievable solution. Not pressure, not deflection — a real answer. And then you ask for the business. That sequence — emotional foundation, proactive objection handling, logical resolution, direct close — is what I was doing that most reps weren't.

The national average was 30%. I closed at 48.9%. That gap is what happens when you stop being afraid of the word no."

---

## Question 5
**"Tell me exactly what the framework you built at Schwab looked like. What problem was it solving, how did you design it, and what pushback did you get getting it adopted by the department?"**

### Answer

"The problem I was solving wasn't really a skills problem — it was an identity problem. Most onboarding trains new reps on what to do. Nobody addresses who they're becoming. If a rep doesn't see themselves as someone pursuing mastery, skills training is just information they'll underuse.

So I built the framework around that gap.

I started by having new recruits listen to calls side by side — top performers against average performers. Not just to analyze technique, but to feel the difference. I'd ask them how each call made them feel as a listener. That question alone shifts something. Then we'd connect it to their own experiences as clients — the moments they felt genuinely served versus processed. Now it's personal. Now they have a reference point.

From there, I worked with the Regional Manager to frame what they were being invited into. This wasn't standard onboarding. This was a class of professionals pursuing what Schwab called World Class — something rare in financial services and rare in any industry.

Once that identity was planted, I addressed the strategy problem directly. I showed them the approach most reps naturally take — pick a handful of skills, get comfortable, stop developing. I laid out exactly where that leads. Then I presented the alternative: a deliberate sequencing strategy that makes skill adoption more achievable over time and builds on itself. The contrast made the right path obvious.

Management didn't push back. Their response was essentially — this is a logical step we hadn't taken. The department adopted it because it addressed something the existing training had been missing entirely."

## Question 6
**"You ran your own coaching business from 2013 to 2017, then moved to Schwab. What happened? Why did you close it?"**

### Answer

"The coaching work during that period was real — I spent four years training, researching target markets, gathering content, and evaluating where the business could go. I was coaching people I knew and building the foundation. But I wasn't generating revenue at a level that could support the household long-term.

My wife's income had given me the runway to build it. When she decided she wanted to step away from work to caretake full time, that dynamic changed. I made a clear-eyed decision to pause the coaching until a future date rather than force it before it was ready.

At the same time, I had developed a serious interest in the Thinkorswim platform and had been watching TD Ameritrade closely. What drew me in wasn't just the industry — it was their explicit commitment to being Best in Class in client service and training. That language meant something to me. I had spent years developing communication and relational skills that I believed were directly transferrable, and I wanted to put them in an environment that would actually value and reward that level of development.

The shift wasn't a retreat from what I was building. It was a practical decision made for the right reasons, in the direction of work I was genuinely drawn to."
