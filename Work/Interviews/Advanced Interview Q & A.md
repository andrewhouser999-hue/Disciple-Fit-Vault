## Difficult Relationship — How Did You Handle It?

**Situation**
When I stepped into an Onboarding Mentor role, I was paired with a full-time colleague to co-lead support for new hires. Within the first few days a pattern emerged that concerned me — his behavior was noticeably different depending on who was in the room. When people of influence were present, he was professional. When they weren't, the dynamic looked very different. He diluted a private conversation I requested by inviting three others without notice. He handed me a presentation I had never seen and told me to stop worrying and just read from the slides — which didn't meet the quality standard the role required. He shared opinions on my work with others before ever bringing them to me. And on more than one occasion he made his displeasure visibly — eye rolls, grunts, walking away — in front of the very people we were there to develop.

**Task**
Onboarding outcomes were a shared responsibility. When new recruits started noticing the tension, it stopped being a personal dynamic and became a business problem. I felt accountable for addressing it before it cost them anything.

**Action**
I flagged the pattern to leadership early — I wasn't going to absorb it quietly and hope it resolved. I also gave it some time after that, assuming leadership awareness might shift things. It didn't. When it became clear the dynamic wasn't changing, I recognized I was waiting for something that required a direct conversation.

I requested a private meeting and kept it entirely focused on specific behaviors and their impact. I said: *"I noticed when I asked you to lunch to discuss our working relationship, you invited three others — that fell outside the request. When I asked you to walk me through the presentation I was handed to deliver, you told me to stop worrying and read from the slides — but that doesn't meet the quality standard the role requires. When you share your assessment of my work with others before bringing it to me, it removes my opportunity to learn from you or speak to your perceptions. And when you express displeasure in front of new recruits — visibly, in the moments they're watching us — it creates tension they can feel, and that affects their experience."* No character judgments, no assumptions about intent — just what I observed and what it was costing us.

**Result**
He said he genuinely hadn't realized his behavior was landing that way. After that conversation the dynamic shifted — communication became more direct, collaboration became functional, and we finished the engagement as a cohesive team. Leadership asked me to extend a seventh month before my scheduled return to my home team.

**What I took from it:** I escalated to leadership early but still waited too long to have the direct conversation — I treated them as alternatives when they both needed to happen. Escalation protects the record. The direct conversation changes the dynamic. Since then I don't wait for one to do the work of the other.

## Getting Buy-In for an Idea Others Initially Disagreed With

**Situation**
When I joined the New Client Department at TD Ameritrade — a team of twelve, mostly senior agents — I noticed the team wasn't functioning well together. The environment was competitive and siloed. Escalations to specialists were frequent, and peer support was essentially absent.

**Task**
I wasn't in a formal leadership role, and I was new. But my manager confirmed he'd been looking for a solution and hadn't found one. I decided the problem was mine to own.

**Action**
I built a structured case for collaboration — why it would benefit individual performance, reduce escalation dependency, and improve the team's standing. I ran it by my manager first, then brought it to the team across three separate meetings.

It didn't move them. Engagement was nearly absent across all three sessions. The only response I got was one team member who said he was satisfied with the existing level of collaboration and didn't see the need for more. He said what the rest of the room was thinking.

That told me the logical argument was never going to be the lever. These were senior agents with established habits. I hadn't built credibility yet, and I was asking them to see a problem they didn't believe existed.

So I stopped presenting and started asking. In a group setting I shifted the conversation entirely — away from what I thought the team needed, toward what they believed made a team worth showing up to. I asked them to define it. Trust, responsiveness, peer support — those were their words, not mine.

Once the standards came from them, I drew out the behaviors that would reflect those standards in practice. Small, concrete actions — like answering questions in the Teams channel rather than defaulting to a specialist. Because they built the standard, accountability followed without me enforcing it.

**Result**
Within 30 days the Teams channel was active with peer-to-peer problem solving. Over the next three months specialist escalations dropped roughly 40% and average handle time dropped about a minute per call — with no changes to formal process.

**What I took from it:** Three prepared meetings couldn't do what one honest question did. People don't commit to standards handed to them — they commit to standards they authored. The pivot wasn't about finding a better argument. It was about getting out of the way and letting the team lead themselves to the conclusion.

## What Have You Created That Is Still in Operation?

**Situation**
Charles Schwab had a baseline communication framework for client interactions — the EDGE Model. It established the standard but didn't close the gap between how new associates naturally communicate and what Schwab defines as World Class. That gap was showing up in client satisfaction scores.

**Task**
I was given responsibility for designing and delivering a training module that would move new recruits from baseline competency to genuine mastery — in a way that actually stuck beyond the training room. The feedback from managers was consistent: the EDGE Model was comprehensive but too large to adopt all at once. Associates were overwhelmed before they could build momentum.

**Action**
Rather than adding more content on top of the existing model, I rebuilt the delivery from the ground up around two core design decisions.

First, I sequenced the complexity. I identified three behaviors to anchor on every call — a manageable foundation associates could execute without thinking. Once those were automatic, a second tier of skills was introduced progressively. This gave people a path to mastery instead of a wall of expectations.

Second, I built identity into the structure. I partnered with a Director to formally open each class — not as an orientation, but as an invitation into a community of professionals being trained at a level unlike anything the company had offered before. The module closed with a graduation ceremony that acknowledged what they had done and who they were now expected to be. The goal was for associates to see themselves as part of something worth aspiring to, not just completing a compliance requirement.

I also didn't build it in isolation — I pulled in managers and team leads across departments throughout development. Their input confirmed the diagnosis: the model needed to be easier to sequence and more likely to be adopted. That feedback shaped the final structure.

**Result**
We ran the module across three classes — 75 new hires total. Those cohorts averaged 30% more calls scoring at 100% client satisfaction compared to all previous classes. That metric — the percentage of perfect scores across a quarter — is a more reliable reflection of consistent execution than aggregate satisfaction averages. The module continued to run for four additional classes after I delivered it and was still active in the onboarding curriculum when I transitioned out of the role.

**What I took from it:** The EDGE Model wasn't the problem — the delivery was. When you sequence complexity so people can build real competency before adding load, and when you frame mastery as an identity worth claiming rather than a checklist to complete, adoption takes care of itself. Compliance-based training has a ceiling. Aspiration doesn't.
