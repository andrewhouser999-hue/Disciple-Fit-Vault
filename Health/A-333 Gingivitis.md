# A-333 Gingivitis — Holistic, Naturopathic & Functional Medicine Protocol

---

## UNDERSTANDING THE TERRAIN FIRST

Gingivitis is not primarily a hygiene problem — it's an inflammatory and microbial dysbiosis problem. The gums are a window into systemic health.

**The functional medicine framing:**
- Gingivitis = bacterial biofilm + immune/inflammatory failure to clear it
- Root causes: oral microbiome dysbiosis, systemic inflammation, nutrient deficiencies, mouth breathing, saliva dysfunction, gut dysbiosis (the oral-gut axis is real)
- Antibiotics and chlorhexidine mouthwash kill bacteria indiscriminately — they wipe the microbiome and create rebound dysbiosis

**The goal:** Shift the oral environment so pathogenic bacteria can't thrive, strengthen the gum tissue's own defenses, and resolve underlying systemic inflammation.

## TIER 1 — MECHANICAL FOUNDATION (Non-Negotiable)

No protocol works without this. Supplements and herbs cannot penetrate biofilm that isn't being physically disrupted.

### Brushing

- Electric toothbrush — Oral-B or Sonicare. Soft bristles only.
- Technique: 45-degree angle to the gumline, small circular motions. Don't scrub.
- Duration: 2 full minutes, 2x/day minimum
- Timing: After meals. Wait 30 min after acidic foods before brushing.

### Flossing

- Daily. Non-negotiable.
- Technique: C-shape around each tooth, slide below the gumline
- If flossing causes bleeding every time: that is active gingivitis, not a reason to stop. Consistent flossing resolves it within 2 weeks.
- Alternatives: Waterpik/oral irrigator (medium pressure), interdental brushes (Piksters, GUM brand)

### Tongue Scraping

- Copper tongue scraper, AM before eating or drinking
- The tongue is the largest reservoir of anaerobic bacteria in the mouth
- Scrape 7-10 times from back to front, rinse between passes

### Gum Massage

- Use a clean finger or gum stimulator tool
- Small circles along the gum line after brushing
- Increases circulation, promotes healing, stimulates collagen remodeling
- 2-3 min daily

## TIER 2 — OIL PULLING (Apothecary Anchor Practice)

One of the most well-evidenced traditional oral practices. Comparable to chlorhexidine in some studies — without destroying the microbiome.

### Protocol

- Oil: Coconut oil (lauric acid — antimicrobial) or sesame oil (traditional Ayurvedic)
- Amount: 1 tablespoon
- Timing: First thing in the morning, before eating, drinking, or brushing
- Duration: 10-20 minutes (start with 5, build up)
- Technique: Swish, push, pull through teeth — don't gargle. Keep jaw relaxed.
- Spit into trash — not the sink
- Rinse with warm salt water, then brush

**Enhancement:** Add 1-2 drops of clove or oregano essential oil to the coconut oil before pulling.

## TIER 3 — TOPICAL HERBAL PROTOCOLS

### Saltwater Rinse

- Foundation rinse. Simple, effective, free.
- 1/2 tsp sea salt or Himalayan salt in 8oz warm water
- Rinse 30-60 seconds, 2x/day after brushing
- Creates alkaline, high-osmolarity environment hostile to anaerobic pathogens

### Herbal Antimicrobial Rinses

**Clove (Eugenia caryophyllata)**
- Eugenol: one of the most potent natural antimicrobials against periodontal pathogens
- 1-2 drops clove essential oil in 4oz water as a rinse
- Clove powder paste: mix with coconut oil, apply to gumline, leave 5-10 min, rinse
- Direct application to inflamed tissue on a cotton swab (dilute — it's strong)

**Neem (Azadirachta indica)**
- Disrupts bacterial biofilm formation; antimicrobial against S. mutans and P. gingivalis
- Use: Neem toothpowder, neem-based toothpaste, or neem twig (traditional chew stick)
- Neem mouthwash: steep neem leaves in hot water, cool, use as rinse

**Myrrh (Commiphora myrrha)**
- Traditional apothecary medicine for gums — centuries of use
- Anti-inflammatory, astringent, antimicrobial
- Myrrh tincture: 10-15 drops in water as a rinse, or apply to gums with a cotton swab

**Goldenseal (Hydrastis canadensis)**
- Berberine content — potent antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory
- Rinse: 1 tsp tincture in 4oz water
- Available as toothpowder

**Green Tea**
- EGCG: reduces gingival inflammation, inhibits bacterial growth
- Brew strong green tea, cool, use as a rinse after brushing
- Or use cooled green tea in the Waterpik reservoir

**Aloe Vera**
- Direct application of pure aloe vera gel to gum line
- Anti-inflammatory, promotes tissue repair, antimicrobial
- Clinical studies show comparable results to chlorhexidine for gingivitis reduction
- Apply with finger to gumline, leave on

**Turmeric Paste**
- Curcumin: powerful anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial
- Mix turmeric powder with coconut oil into a paste
- Apply to gumline, leave 5-10 min
- Will stain temporarily — rinse well. Don't use on white/composite work.

### DIY Herbal Combo Rinse

- 4oz distilled or filtered water
- 5 drops clove essential oil
- 5 drops tea tree oil
- 1 tsp aloe vera gel
- 1/4 tsp sea salt
- Optional: 5 drops myrrh tincture

Shake before each use. Rinse 30-60 seconds after brushing. Do not swallow.

## TIER 4 — TOOTHPASTE / POWDER PROTOCOL

### What to Avoid

- SLS (sodium lauryl sulfate) toothpaste — strips mucosal lining, increases permeability to bacteria
- Triclosan — endocrine disruptor
- Carrageenan — inflammatory
- Glycerin — may inhibit enamel remineralization

### Better Options

**Tooth powder (DIY)**
- 2 tbsp baking soda + 1 tbsp bentonite clay + 1 tsp neem powder + 10 drops clove oil + 10 drops peppermint oil
- Baking soda alkalizes oral pH, directly hostile to acid-producing bacteria

**Commercial alternatives:**
- Revitin (prebiotic — supports oral microbiome)
- Dr. Bronner's All-One Toothpaste (simple, SLS-free)
- Davids Natural Toothpaste
- Risewell (hydroxyapatite-based — remineralizing)

**Hydroxyapatite toothpaste**
- Biomimetic — same mineral teeth are made of
- Remineralizes enamel comparably to fluoride
- Does not disrupt oral microbiome
- Brands: Risewell, Boka, Dr. Collins Restore

## TIER 5 — NUTRITIONAL PROTOCOL

### Vitamin C (The Most Critical)

- Collagen synthesis is entirely dependent on vitamin C — gum tissue is collagen-rich
- Subclinical deficiency causes bleeding gums at a lower level (pre-scurvy)
- Dose: 1,000-3,000mg/day in divided doses
- Form: Ascorbic acid, sodium ascorbate (buffered), or liposomal (highest absorption)
- Food sources: Bell peppers (highest), kiwi, citrus, strawberries, broccoli, parsley

### CoQ10 (Coenzyme Q10)

- Concentrated in gingival tissue; measurably depleted in gingivitis and periodontitis
- Anti-inflammatory, supports cellular energy in gum tissue
- Dose: 100-200mg/day (ubiquinol form — better absorbed than ubiquinone)
- Topical: Open a capsule, mix with coconut oil, apply directly to gumline 2-3x/week

### Vitamin K2 (MK-7)

- Supports alveolar bone density — bone loss underlies gum recession
- Works synergistically with D3
- Dose: 100-200mcg MK-7 daily

### Vitamin D3

- Immunomodulatory — regulates the inflammatory response in gum tissue
- Deficiency associated with increased risk of periodontal disease
- Dose: 2,000-5,000 IU/day (same as testosterone protocol — one supplement, dual benefit)

### Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA/DHA)

- Directly reduces gingival inflammation through specialized pro-resolving mediators (resolvins, protectins)
- Studies show omega-3 supplementation reduces gingival index scores
- Dose: 2-3g EPA+DHA/day (therapeutic anti-inflammatory dose)
- Form: Triglyceride form fish oil or algae-based omega-3

### Probiotics (Oral-Specific)

**Systemic gut probiotics** — indirect benefit via oral-gut axis
- Lactobacillus reuteri, L. acidophilus, B. longum

**Oral-specific probiotics** — direct benefit
- Lactobacillus reuteri (ATCC 55730 + ATCC PTA 5289) — strongest clinical evidence for periodontal disease
- Streptococcus salivarius K12 — outcompetes pathogenic bacteria for adhesion sites
- Form: Chewable tablets or lozenges — swallowed capsules bypass the mouth
- Timing: After brushing at night — field is mechanically clear, let probiotics colonize
- Brands: BioGaia Prodentis (L. reuteri combo), BLIS K12 (S. salivarius)

### Supporting Micronutrients

- Magnesium: bone mineralization in jaw, reduces systemic inflammation
- Zinc: wound healing and immune function in gum tissue; zinc citrate mouthwash inhibits tartar

## TIER 6 — SYSTEMIC FACTORS (Root Causes)

### Mouth Breathing

One of the most underaddressed causes of gingivitis. Mouth breathing:
- Desiccates gum tissue
- Raises oral pH to range favorable for pathogenic bacteria
- Reduces saliva's antimicrobial function
- Causes gum tissue to recede anteriorly

**Interventions:**
- Mouth tape at night — Somnifix or 3M Micropore surgical tape
- Nasal breathing training during the day (Buteyko method)
- Address structural causes: deviated septum, nasal polyps, chronic congestion
- Neti pot (saline nasal rinse) to clear nasal passages

### Saliva Quality

Saliva is the mouth's primary defense system — antimicrobial proteins, pH buffering, remineralization.

- Hydration — most people are chronically mildly dehydrated
- Eliminate medications that cause dry mouth if possible (consult physician)
- Chew xylitol gum — stimulates saliva flow; xylitol inhibits S. mutans and cariogenic bacteria
- Avoid mouth breathing (above)

### Gut Dysbiosis / Leaky Gut

The oral-gut microbiome axis is bidirectional. Pathogenic oral bacteria seed the gut; gut dysbiosis drives systemic inflammation that manifests in the gums.

- Address if: IBS, bloating, irregular bowel, food sensitivities
- L-glutamine (5g/day) supports gut lining integrity
- Fermented foods: sauerkraut, kimchi, kefir, yogurt

### Blood Sugar Regulation

Diabetes and insulin resistance are among the strongest risk factors for periodontal disease. Elevated blood glucose:
- Impairs immune cell function in gum tissue
- Promotes glycation of collagen (weakens gum tissue)
- Feeds pathogenic bacteria (glucose is their preferred substrate)

- Interventions: low-glycemic diet, resistance training, berberine (500mg 2-3x/day)

### Chronic Systemic Inflammation

Gingivitis is often a local manifestation of systemic inflammatory burden.
- Address: gut permeability, food sensitivities (gluten, dairy common), chronic stress, sleep deprivation

### Biological/Holistic Dentist

A biological or functional dentist will:
- Use phase contrast microscopy to identify specific pathogens
- Avoid mercury amalgam fillings
- Offer ozone therapy
- Not default to chlorhexidine

### Ozone Therapy

- O3 is a powerful, targeted antimicrobial with no microbiome disruption
- Kills pathogens at 99.9% without harming commensal bacteria
- Administered as gas blown into gingival pockets or ozonated water rinse
- Home ozone water generators available for daily rinse use

### Professional Cleaning (Scaling and Root Planing)

- If subgingival calculus is present, no protocol above will fully work until it's removed
- Professional cleaning is mechanical debridement — not antimicrobial warfare
- After cleaning, all protocols above work significantly better
- Schedule every 3-4 months if gingivitis is active (instead of standard 6)
