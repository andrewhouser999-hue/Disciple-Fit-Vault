# A-02 Absence Seizures — Treatment & Management Reference Guide

**Child Age:** 10 | **Symptom:** 3-second "brain pause," resumes activity normally afterward
**Compiled:** April 2026 | For informational purposes — not a substitute for medical evaluation

---

## Part 1 — Alternative & Functional Medicine

### Ketogenic Diet — Strongest Non-Pharmaceutical Evidence

Clinically validated and used even within conventional neurology for drug-resistant epilepsy. Changes the brain's fuel source from glucose to ketones, which stabilizes neuronal activity.

- High fat, very low carbohydrate eating pattern
- **Evidence:** Reduces seizures in 50%+ of epilepsy cases; some children achieve full remission
- Modified Atkins Diet is a less restrictive alternative with similar research support
- Requires medical supervision — especially in a child

---

### Nutritional & Supplement Support

| Supplement | Rationale | Evidence Level |
|---|---|---|
| Magnesium glycinate | Reduces neuronal excitability; deficiency lowers seizure threshold | Moderate |
| Vitamin D | Deficiency linked to seizure disorders | Moderate |
| Omega-3 / Fish oil | Anti-inflammatory, neuroprotective | Low-moderate |
| CoQ10 | Mitochondrial energy production | Low-moderate |
| B vitamins (B2, B6) | B6-dependent seizures are a recognized subtype | Legitimate for specific cases |
| Zinc | Involved in GABA regulation | Low |
| Taurine | Inhibitory neurotransmitter support | Anecdotal |
| Carnitine | Mitochondrial fatty acid transport | Low |

---

### Gut-Brain Axis

Emerging research links microbiome health to neurological function and neuroinflammation.

- Probiotic protocols
- Elimination of common food sensitivities (gluten and dairy most common targets)
- Address intestinal permeability ("leaky gut")

---

### Blood Sugar Regulation

Glucose spikes and crashes directly affect neurological stability.

- Reduce refined sugars and processed carbohydrates
- Consistent, regular meal timing throughout the day

---

### Additional Functional Investigations

- **Heavy metals panel** — Lead, mercury, aluminum (especially with unusual exposure history)
- **Full thyroid panel** — TSH, Free T3, Free T4, antibodies
- **Organic acids test** — Mitochondrial function markers
- **Food sensitivity panel (IgG)** — Identify inflammatory triggers

---

### Mind-Body Approaches

| Modality | Notes |
|---|---|
| Neurofeedback | Trains brainwave patterns; growing evidence for seizure reduction |
| Yoga / Breathwork | Reduces stress triggers; small studies show seizure frequency reduction |
| Acupuncture | Limited seizure-specific evidence; stress reduction benefit is well-supported |

---

### Herbal Approaches

> **Caution:** Some herbs lower the seizure threshold. Do not use herbal protocols in a child without practitioner guidance.

| Herb / Compound | What Is Claimed |
|---|---|
| Bacopa monnieri | Neuroprotective, adaptogenic support |
| Ashwagandha | Stress and cortisol regulation |
| CBD (Cannabidiol) | FDA-approved (Epidiolex) for specific epilepsy types — requires medical oversight |

---

### Trigger Management

| Trigger | Recommended Action |
|---|---|
| Hyperventilation | Monitor; also how neurologists induce absence seizures during EEG |
| Sleep deprivation | Consistent sleep schedule is non-negotiable |
| Stress / Anxiety | Reduce environmental and emotional stressors where possible |
| Flickering light | Less common trigger with CAE than other epilepsy types |
| Illness / Fever | Lowers seizure threshold; manage proactively |
| Blood sugar instability | Consistent meals, reduce sugar significantly |

---

## Part 2 — Allopathic (Conventional) Medicine

> **Critical note:** Early diagnosis is the single most important intervention. Undiagnosed absence seizures can cause dozens of micro-interruptions per day — compounding learning loss silently over months or years.

---

### Diagnosis Protocol

1. **Pediatric neurologist referral** — first and most urgent priority
2. **EEG (electroencephalogram)** — diagnostic standard; looks for 3 Hz spike-and-wave discharge
3. **MRI** — rule out structural causes if EEG is inconclusive

---

### First-Line Medications

70–80% of children with Childhood Absence Epilepsy (CAE) achieve full seizure control with medication. Many outgrow CAE by mid-adolescence.

| Medication | Status |
|---|---|
| Ethosuximide | Preferred first-line treatment |
| Valproic acid | Alternative first-line option |
| Lamotrigine | Third-line option |

---

### School & Learning Protections

- Notify the teacher immediately — describe what absence seizures look like (brief staring, not dramatic)
- Request a **504 plan or IEP** accommodations through the school
- Accommodations: extended test time, preferential seating, written seizure action plan on file
- Educate all relevant staff — absence seizures are easily mistaken for daydreaming or inattention

---

### Prognosis

- CAE is one of the more favorable epilepsy diagnoses in children
- Many children achieve full remission by mid-adolescence
- Early treatment correlates with consistently better long-term outcomes

---

## Integrated Priority Roadmap

| # | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Confirm diagnosis — EEG with pediatric neurologist |
| 2 | Address sleep, blood sugar, and stress immediately (zero downside) |
| 3 | Begin magnesium glycinate + Vitamin D (low risk, plausible benefit) |
| 4 | Work with neurologist on medication decision if diagnosis is confirmed |
| 5 | Seriously consider ketogenic or modified Atkins diet |
| 6 | Explore neurofeedback as an adjunct therapy |
| 7 | Run functional medicine labs to identify underlying deficiencies |
| 8 | Protect the learning environment with school accommodations |
