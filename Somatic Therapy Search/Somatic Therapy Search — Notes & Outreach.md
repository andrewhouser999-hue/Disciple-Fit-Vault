# Somatic Therapy Search — Notes & Outreach

---

## Finding Somatic Therapists

**The most targeted directories — start here:**

1. **Somatic Experiencing International (SEI)** — `traumahealing.org/find-a-practitioner` — This is the gold standard directory for SE practitioners. Filter by state or telehealth availability. These therapists are trained in Peter Levine's nervous-system model.

2. **Sensorimotor Psychotherapy Institute** — `sensorimotorpsychotherapy.org/find-a-therapist` — Body-based trauma and attachment work. Strong clinical training.

3. **NARM Training Institute** — `narmtraining.com/find-a-therapist` — NeuroAffective Relational Model. Strong for developmental/complex trauma.

4. **Hakomi Institute** — `hakomiinstitute.com/find-a-hakomi-therapist` — Mindfulness-based somatic work.

5. **Psychology Today** — `psychologytoday.com/us/therapists` — Filter by *Somatic* under "Issues" or "Types of Therapy." Broad but large pool. Good for telehealth filter.

6. **USABP** — `usabp.org/find-a-therapist` — United States Association for Body Psychotherapy. Covers multiple somatic modalities.

Since open nationally, prioritize telehealth. Most SE and Sensorimotor therapists now offer it. This dramatically expands access and removes geography as a constraint.

## How a Somatic Therapist Reads This Profile

**Green flags (strong candidacy):**
- High somatic literacy — already naming sensations, tracking activation, recognizing hypervigilance onset in real time. That's rare in a first-time therapy client and significantly accelerates early work.
- Self-initiated, not crisis-driven — examining from a place of readiness because conditions finally allow it, not because of collapse. That signals sufficient window of tolerance.
- Spouse's 3 years of somatic work is a *clinical asset* — witnessed the process, have a somatic-literate partner, and the relational environment is stabilizing rather than actively compounding.
- The mid-lift discovery is significant — it signals the body is already surfacing material. Somatic therapists find that meaningful.

**What they'll hold carefully:**
- No prior therapy + complex layering = pacing will be their primary responsibility early on. Expect them to go slower than you think necessary.
- Secondary trauma from the marriage is still proximate, even if improving. Active relational context means the nervous system isn't fully in past-tense yet.
- Developmental origin (paternal neglect + periodic hypervigilance) means early patterning — this is attachment-level work, not just event-processing.

## How the Three Modalities Map to the Specific Layers

| Layer | Best Fit |
|---|---|
| Paternal neglect + hypervigilance (attachment/developmental) | **NARM** — built specifically for this. Addresses the survival strategies and core needs (attunement, trust, connection) that get shaped in early attachment. |
| Job loss PTSD + compounding single/acute traumas | **SE** — designed for this. Works by completing the incomplete defensive responses stored in the body from discrete traumatic events. |
| Secondary trauma from marriage + relational patterning | **Sensorimotor** — most relationally oriented of the three. Works at the intersection of nervous system, attachment, and how relational dynamics live in the body. |

---

## Strategic Reality

The developmental layer (paternal neglect) is almost certainly the **root architecture** — the other traumas compounded on top of a nervous system that was already shaped by early relational patterns. That tends to make **NARM the deepest entry point**, even if SE or Sensorimotor handles what's more surface-accessible first.

Cross-training is common. Many experienced somatic therapists hold certifications in two or more of these. SE and Sensorimotor share lineage (Pat Ogden trained under Levine). NARM practitioners often carry SE training as a foundation.

**What to look for:** Therapists who list SE as a base plus NARM or Sensorimotor as an additional modality. That combination would cover the full profile without needing to sequence therapists.

---

## Questions to Ask in Consultation

1. **"I have both developmental/attachment trauma and discrete traumatic events — how do you typically sequence that work?"** — A good answer shows they understand the layering and won't just chase acute symptoms while leaving the root untouched.

2. **"Are you cross-trained across modalities, and how do you decide which approach to use in session?"** — You want flexibility, not ideological rigidity.

3. **"My wife has been in somatic therapy for three years — our relational dynamic is part of the picture. How do you hold relational context in individual work?"** — This screens for therapists who think systemically, not just individually.
