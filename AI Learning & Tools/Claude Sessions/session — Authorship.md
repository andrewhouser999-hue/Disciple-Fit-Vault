## Context
Andrew is building a portfolio of articles for his GitHub Pages job search site. Articles are written to strategically inform prospective employers — demonstrating how he thinks, decides, leads, communicates, and diagnoses human behavior. The writing lens is psychology (how people decide, communicate, relate, think, feel, shift, lead, persuade). Articles map to specific interview questions defined in the Article-Interview Mapping file.

**Obsidian article folder:** `Andy's Life/Articles/`

## Current State
Writing style established: Housel + Grant + Lewis blend.
- Morgan Housel: compression, short punchy paragraphs, implication-heavy endings, behavioral observation
- Adam Grant: counter-intuitive framing, research-grounded, flips conventional assumptions
- C.S. Lewis: the "turn" — deeper philosophical explanation that lands with weight

First article drafted and saved in this style: "Most Leadership Problems Are Identity Problems in Disguise" (~900 words). Saved to Obsidian and ready to be published to GitHub as a standard post (not featured). GitHub site has two sections: Featured Articles and The Full Body of Work. Featured slot stays with "Leadership Is a Diagnostic Discipline" (evidence-based, personal stories). New article goes in the full list.

## Key Decisions & Notes
- **Shorthand for style blend:** "Write in the house style" = Housel + Grant + Lewis default. Adjust with: "lean Housel," "more Lewis on the turn," or "Grant-heavy" for register shifts.
- Style blend: Housel + Grant + Lewis — do not deviate without Andrew's direction
- Target length: ~900 words per article
- Tone: secular professional — psychological lens carries the front; no overt faith register
- Article purpose: demonstrate how Andrew thinks to hiring managers before the interview
- Featured section: keep "Leadership Is a Diagnostic Discipline" as the featured article — it has personal proof points
- New articles go into "The Full Body of Work" as standard posts unless Andrew says otherwise
- Opening move: behavior first, not concept — show someone doing the thing before naming it
- Ending move: invitation or unresolved question — never a summary
