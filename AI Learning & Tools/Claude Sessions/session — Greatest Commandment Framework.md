### Frame Challenge First

Before I go to the four categories: **you may be answering the wrong question.**

You're asking how to add the Greatest Commandment to your framework. But the Greatest Commandment isn't an addition to the framework — it's the *telos* the framework already implies. Every soil-work session is trying to produce ground that can grow love. Every lie you eliminate is a lie that distorts love. Every identity you install is an identity grounded in being loved. You don't need to *add* the commandment — you need to *reveal* that it was already the destination.

The question to answer is: **Does this become the organizing goal of the whole system, or a separate intervention module?** That decision changes everything about how to build it. Right now your request reads as both, and that will produce structural confusion. Decide before building.

### 1. Theological Accuracy

**Steelman:** The Greatest Commandment (Matt 22:37-40; Mark 12:29-31; Luke 10:27) is the most defensible possible addition to any Christian discipleship framework — Jesus called it first, Paul called it the fulfillment of the law (Romans 13:9-10), John organized an entire epistle around it, and Augustine's entire theology is summarized as "our heart is restless until it rests in Thee." The dual-agency framing (Holy Spirit + human cooperation) maps cleanly onto Philippians 2:12-13, the most precise dual-agency text in the NT. "God knows what to sanctify when" is supported by John 15:2, Philippians 1:6, and broad evangelical/Reformed pneumatology.

**Most dangerous finding — Overreach on the fourfold faculty system.**

The heart/mind/soul/strength breakdown in the Shema is *intensification language*, not a clinical taxonomy. It means "with everything you have" — the list amplifies totality. The synoptic gospels don't even agree on the terms: Matthew has *three* (heart, soul, mind — 22:37), Mark has *four* (heart, soul, mind, strength — 12:30), Luke has *four* with a different arrangement (10:27). **Strong inference:** NT scholars including N.T. Wright, Gordon Fee, and Dallas Willard all read this as a holistic devotion formula, not a faculty partition. If you build a system that maps heart → emotion, mind → cognition, soul → spirit, strength → behavior, you are imposing a modern psychological taxonomy onto an ancient intensification device. The system is internally coherent but exegetically overbuilt.

**Fix:** Use the fourfold structure as *emphasis clusters* with explicit acknowledgment that these are lenses, not discrete faculties. The intensification reading actually strengthens the application — Jesus isn't segmenting love into parts, he's saying nothing is held back. That framing supports your dual-agency model more powerfully than a faculty map does.

**Second risk — "Things you can work on to develop love" slides toward Pelagianism without a structural guardrail.**

1 John 4:19: "We love because he first loved us." Galatians 5:22: love is a *fruit of the Spirit* — it is produced by the Spirit's presence, not by human cultivation effort. Augustine, Aquinas, Calvin, Wesley, and Willard all agree: love for God is a derivative response to God's prior love, not a capacity generated by discipline. The disciplines create *conditions* for the Spirit to produce love — they do not produce it. **Fact:** Dallas Willard's most important contribution in *The Spirit of the Disciplines* is exactly this distinction: "Grace is not opposed to effort; it is opposed to earning." Effort creates conditions. Earning is the heresy.

**Fix:** Reframe the entire framework as *obstacle removal and condition creation* rather than *love development*. You are not teaching someone to generate love. You are helping them identify what blocks love from being received and expressed, and create conditions where the Spirit can produce it. This is more theologically precise and more practically honest.

**"God knows what to sanctify when" — Solid, with a pastoral risk.**

Scripturally supported (Phil 1:6, John 15:2, Romans 8:28-30). The risk is pastoral: it can become a client's reason to avoid hard internal work. "If God wants to change it, He will" is a real misapplication. Keep this framing tethered to the human-agency counterpart: God initiates and sustains; humans respond and clear ground.

**Verdict: Fixable.** Core framing is defensible. Two points need structural correction: (1) treat the fourfold terms as intensification/lenses rather than discrete faculties; (2) reframe human agency as obstacle-removal and condition-creation, not love-generation.

### 2. Clinical Accuracy

**Steelman:** The mind/affections/will triad maps cleanly onto the three domains that evidence-based psychology identifies as the components of sustainable change: cognitive restructuring (mind), motivational/emotional work (affections), and behavioral commitment (will). This is convergent validity — multiple independent frameworks landing on the same structure. The relational component (love neighbor as yourself) addresses the social domain that is notably underdeveloped in the current framework.

**Most dangerous finding — "Experience love emotionally" as a coaching goal will produce Rocky Soil behavior in clients who don't already have it.**

If the framework's affections category produces an objective like "I should feel more love for God," you've created a pursuit that maps exactly onto the Rocky Soil pattern: chasing emotional experience as proof of the real thing. Clients already prone to experiential dependence will use this framework to justify cycling through emotional highs and crashes while calling it spiritual growth. **Strong inference:** The NT definition of love is primarily volitional (1 Cor 13 is behavioral; John 14:21 ties love to obedience), not primarily affective. Emotional experience of love is real and valuable — but it's an *occasional byproduct*, not a goal to pursue.

**Fix:** The affections category should be framed as *obstacle removal* (what blocks me from receiving God's love) and *responsive cultivation* (what conditions allow the Spirit to produce affective experience), not *emotional attainment* (how do I feel more love). The difference is structural: removing blockages is within human agency; generating the feeling is not.

**Scope creep risk — "Learning to receive love" is therapy, not coaching, for many clients.**

A significant portion of clients who can't receive or experience love for God have attachment wounds, abuse histories, or religious trauma that makes this a clinical issue, not a discipleship gap. The inability to receive love is not a matter of underdeveloped spiritual disciplines — it's a traumatic blockage that requires therapeutic intervention. The current framework (Soils, Part 7 crisis protocol) already flags this. The Greatest Commandment addition needs the same explicit referral boundary: "coaching can work on belief restructuring and condition-creation; when the blockage is traumatic, this requires a therapist."

**Tool mapping — what holds clinically:**

| Domain | Sound Tool Mapping | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Mind / Belief about God | Attachment theory (internal working models); CBT (cognitive distortions about God); Narrative therapy (reauthoring the God story) | Mind-reading God's intentions is a common distortion — handle with care |
| Affections / Receiving love | Somatic anchoring (already in framework); gratitude practices (Emmons & McCullough 2003 — **Fact**, strong evidence base); awe experiences (Keltner — **Strong inference**, growing evidence) | Cannot generate emotion directly; fasting/solitude create conditions |
| Will / Commitment | Implementation intentions (Gollwitzer — **Fact**, well-replicated); SDT autonomy support (Deci & Ryan — **Fact**); habit architecture (BJ Fogg — **Strong inference**) | Duty-based compliance produces opposite of intrinsic love |
| Neighbor love | Behavioral activation (loving acts before feeling them — **Strong inference** supported by James KA Smith's liturgy argument); altruism research (Post — **Strong inference**) | "As yourself" is clinically loaded for shame-based clients |

**Verdict: Fixable.** The main clinical error is treating affective experience as a goal rather than an occasional outcome. Redraw the affections category around obstacle removal and condition creation. Add explicit scope boundaries around attachment wounds.

You cannot sequence this framework as "develop internal love first, then express it relationally." The sequence is interactive. **Fix:** Build in explicit circularity — a feedback loop where relational love-acts reshape internal experience and vice versa. James KA Smith's key insight (borrowed from Aristotle): you form virtue by practicing virtuous acts, not by first attaining the virtue internally. Obedience forms love; love motivates obedience. Make that loop explicit.

The dual-agency model needs to match the Soils framework's level of integration — not God's work vs. my work, but the Spirit working through and with human agency in an irreducibly cooperative process (Phil 2:12-13 — both simultaneously true).

**Verdict: Fixable.** Decide the architectural role. Build in circularity. Align the dual-agency model with the Soils framework's more integrated pneumatology.

### The High-Effort Breakdown

#### PREAMBLE: The Foundational Reframe

You do not generate love for God by working harder at it. You remove what blocks the reception of His love, and create conditions where the Spirit produces love as fruit. The human agency in love-formation is not production; it is *preparation* and *cooperation*. Dallas Willard: "You can't do the work God does. But you can put yourself in the place where He can." — **Strong inference, widely supported across theological traditions.**

The Greatest Commandment is not an achievement. It is the description of what a person looks like who has received God's love and has nothing competing with their response to it.

---

#### DOMAIN 1: THE MIND — Beliefs, Attention, Mental Models

##### Sub-Category 1A: Your Internal Working Model of God

The single most important cognitive factor in love for God is not what you *believe about* God theologically — it is who God *is* to you functionally when you are under pressure, lonely, or failing.

**Theological ground:** Psalm 62 (God as fortress), Hosea 11 (God as tender parent), Romans 8:38-39 (nothing can separate) — these are relational representations, not propositional statements. The psalms of lament (22, 88) show that honest protest to God is a form of love, not its absence.

**Psychological ground (Fact):** Attachment theory (Bowlby, Ainsworth) establishes that early caregiver relationships form *internal working models* — templates for all subsequent relationships, including relationship with God. Research by Pehr Granqvist and Lee Kirkpatrick demonstrates that adult God attachment style correlates significantly with early parental attachment style. Anxious attachment → clingy, performance-based relationship with God. Avoidant attachment → intellectualized, distant, prayer-avoidant relationship with God.

**Practical diagnostic question:**
> *"Who is God to you right now — not who you believe He is theologically, but who He actually is in your experience this week?"*

**Practices that recalibrate the internal model:**
- Lectio Divina on passages that reveal God's character specifically (John 17:23; Zeph 3:17; Rom 8:31-39) — receiving them slowly, not analyzing them
- "Evidence file" for God's faithfulness — tracking specific historical instances of God's faithfulness in their life (builds object constancy — the cognitive ability to trust someone is good even when they feel absent)
- The attachment prayer exercise: sit in silence and ask "What do I expect from You right now?" — what surfaces is the functional model, not the theological one

---

##### Sub-Category 1B: Cognitive Distortions About God

**Fact:** CBT research establishes that cognitive distortions (overgeneralization, all-or-nothing thinking, mind-reading, etc.) operate in the domain of God-relationships just as they do in human relationships. Beck's cognitive model applies to theology: automatic thoughts about God shape emotional responses to God.

Common distortions specific to God-relationship:
- *All-or-nothing:* "If God loved me, He would have prevented ___."
- *Mind-reading:* "I know what God thinks of me" (usually: disappointment, distance, judgment)
- *Fortune-telling:* "God won't come through on this."
- *Personalization:* "This happened because I'm not holy enough."

**Practices:**
- The Lie Eliminator structure already in the framework deployed specifically on lies about God's character, not just lies about self
- Cognitive-behavioral journaling targeted at automatic thoughts during prayer (what am I expecting when I pray? What do I think God is doing right now?)

---

##### Sub-Category 1C: Attention Architecture

**Strong inference (supported by neuroscience and theology convergently):** You love what you repeatedly attend to. This is Augustine's restless heart principle (*Confessions*: "our heart is restless until it rests in Thee") stated as a cognitive-behavioral principle. Neuroplasticity research (Siegel, Hanson) establishes that sustained attention shapes neural architecture — what you attend to literally builds the brain's response patterns.

2 Corinthians 3:18 is the theological anchor: "beholding as in a mirror the glory of the Lord, we are being transformed into the same image." The mechanism is *beholding* — sustained attentive gaze — and the result is *transformation*, not the other way around.

**Critical distinction (Willard's most important insight — Strong inference):** Attention-practices do not produce love. They create conditions for the Spirit to produce transformation. The Pharisees attended to the law obsessively and it did not produce love. Attention must be directed toward the *person of God*, not the *performance of religious duties*.

**Practices:**
- Contemplative prayer / Scripture meditation — reading a short passage slowly, sitting with it, letting it be received rather than analyzed
- "Sacred reading" (Lectio Divina) as attention practice, not information-intake
- Removal of competing attention captures (the thorny-soil application — you cannot cultivate attention for God while every available attentional resource is captured by anxiety, screens, or demand)

#### DOMAIN 2: THE AFFECTIONS — Desire, Feeling, Emotional Experience of Love

**Critical reframe (must come first):** The goal of this domain is *not* to feel more love for God. It is to remove what blocks receiving God's love and to create conditions where the Spirit can produce affective love as fruit. This distinction is not semantic — it determines whether this domain produces Rocky Soil behavior or actual formation.

##### Sub-Category 2A: Obstacle Removal — What Blocks the Reception of Love

The most common obstacles are:

**1. Unhealed wounds that project onto God**
Wounds from human authority, abandonment, betrayal, or harsh judgment do not stay contained to human relationships — they are unconsciously projected onto God. A person who was abandoned by their father will find it neurologically difficult to experience God as present and reliable, regardless of what they believe theologically. **Fact:** This is well-established in the psychology of religion (Granqvist, Exline) and is one of the clearest coaching/therapy boundary lines: when the obstacle is traumatic, referral is the coaching move.

Practices within coaching scope:
- Identify the "presenting God" vs. the "scriptural God" (the Soils framework's Dimension 3 — God representation under suffering — already does this)
- Journal specifically: "Where did my picture of God come from? When was it shaped by someone who was not God?"

**2. Performance-based shame**
A shame-based relational posture makes genuine love impossible because it filters every transaction through the question "am I acceptable?" Genuine reception of love requires the capacity to be seen without terror — which shame prevents.

Practices:
- Lie Eliminator entries specifically on shame-based beliefs about God's regard
- Brennan Manning's "ragamuffin" framing — experiencing God's love in failure, not only in success
- Distinguish conviction (specific, proportionate, leads to restoration) from condemnation (global, excessive, leads to shutdown) — 1 John 3:20 is the key text

**3. Unprocessed grief and lament**
Many clients carry grief, anger, or disappointment at God that they have never expressed — because they believe honest negative emotion toward God is sin or faithlessness. This unprocessed material creates an emotional dam that blocks both the expression and reception of love.

**Theological ground (Fact):** The Psalms are the model. Psalms 22, 88, 44, 77 express abandonment, rage at God, confusion, and despair — all addressed *to* God. Lament is not the opposite of love; it is a form of intimate engagement that presupposes love enough to protest. Scholars including Walter Brueggemann (*The Psalms and the Life of Faith*) and Soong-Chan Rah (*Prophetic Lament*) establish this as the proper use of lament in spiritual formation. **Fact.**

Practices:
- Guided lament prayer: "What have you never said to God that you've felt?" — give explicit permission to be honest
- Psalms of lament as a prayer template (not just psalms of praise)

---

##### Sub-Category 2B: Condition Creation — What Cultivates Affective Love

These are not guaranteed to produce emotional experience of love. They create conditions where the Spirit can produce it.

**1. Fasting**
Theologically: breaks the grip of competing desires (Matt 6:16-18; Isaiah 58:6). Fasting for love-cultivation is not fasting from food specifically — it is strategic deprivation of what is competing with the hunger for God. **Strong inference:** The mechanism is desire-clarification: you discover what you actually depend on, and create hunger for what you're not feeding.

**2. Solitude and silence**
Dallas Willard calls solitude the "foundational spiritual discipline" — not because it directly produces love, but because it removes the noise that prevents you from hearing and experiencing what's already present. **Strong inference:** Contemplative practices (Thomas Merton, Henri Nouwen, Dallas Willard) are in convergent agreement here. Note: some evangelical traditions have concerns about contemplative prayer specifically — flag this when relevant.

**3. Gratitude practices**
**Fact (well-replicated):** Emmons & McCullough (2003) established that weekly gratitude journaling produces measurable increases in positive affect, prosocial behavior, and life satisfaction compared to control conditions. Robert Emmons' subsequent work (*Thanks!*) extends this to the vertical dimension — gratitude toward God as a form of relational engagement.

Practice: gratitude journaling specifically tracking God's faithfulness (not generic gratitude — specific recognition of God's action in daily life)

**4. Beauty and awe**
**Strong inference:** CS Lewis's concept of *Sehnsucht* — the longing triggered by beauty, which he argued is a longing for the transcendent source of beauty (*The Weight of Glory*). Jonathan Edwards (*Religious Affections*) develops the most rigorous theological account of beauty as a sense of the divine. Neuroscience of awe (Dacher Keltner, *Awe: The New Science of Everyday Wonder*) establishes that awe experiences reduce self-salience, increase prosocial orientation, and produce a sense of presence beyond the self — **Strong inference** that this maps onto the experience the tradition calls "the fear of the Lord."

Practices: deliberate encounters with beauty (nature, music, art, liturgy) — not as entertainment but as attention to what the beauty points to

**5. Corporate worship**
Not generating feeling through collective emotion (Rocky Soil risk), but participation in a communal practice that creates conditions. James KA Smith's liturgy argument (*You Are What You Love*): habitual participation in practices that form the body/emotions shapes desire over time, regardless of whether it "feels" meaningful in the moment. **Strong inference.**

#### DOMAIN 3: THE WILL — Choices, Commitments, Surrender, Perseverance

##### Sub-Category 3A: The Divided Will — The Romans 7 Problem

Paul names the divided will as the normal human condition: "what I want to do I do not do, but what I hate I do" (Rom 7:15). The most common coaching error in this domain is treating a divided will as a commitment problem to be resolved by stronger commitment. It is not. It is the condition of a person in whom conflicting desire-systems are active simultaneously.

**Clinical application:** Motivational Interviewing (Miller & Rollnick — **Fact**, most evidence-backed tool for ambivalence) addresses this precisely. The MI principle: *you do not argue the client toward the right side of the ambivalence — you evoke the client's own voice for the desired change.* Applied to love for God: don't prescribe greater commitment; evoke the client's own articulation of why they want to love God more. The voice they use becomes the internal resource for movement.

**Practical diagnostic question:**
> *"You want to love God more. You also want things that compete with that. What does each side promise you?"*

---

##### Sub-Category 3B: Commitment Science — Building on What Actually Works

**Implementation intentions (Gollwitzer — Fact, well-replicated):** Already in the framework. The application here: every love-practice commitment should end in an if-then plan. Not "I will pray more" but "When I make my morning coffee, I will open my journal and write one honest sentence to God."

**Habit architecture (BJ Fogg — Strong inference):** Tiny Habits model — start smaller than you think is useful. A 2-minute prayer practice maintained is more formation-productive than a 30-minute practice that gets skipped when it's hard.

**SDT (Deci & Ryan — Fact):** Intrinsic motivation requires autonomy (you chose this, it wasn't imposed), competence (you have enough skill to succeed), and relatedness (it matters in a relational context). Love commanded under external pressure produces compliance, not love. The coaching posture must support autonomy — the client's intrinsic reasons for wanting to love God more must be evoked, not imposed.

**Critical insight for love specifically:** The more you *should* something, the less motivationally available it becomes. Duty-framing produces obedience; only desire-framing produces love. This is not in conflict with biblical command — the goal of the command is to produce desire, not compliance. Willard again: "You cannot love on command. But you can create conditions where love becomes natural."

---

##### Sub-Category 3C: Surrender as Active Practice

**Theological ground:** Romans 12:1-2 — "present your bodies as a living sacrifice" — this is an active, ongoing, volitional act. The Greek *paristēmi* (present) is a deliberate offering, not passive drift. Surrender is not passivity; it is the most demanding act of the will.

**Structural model (Phil 2:12-13 — the clearest dual-agency text):**
> "Work out your salvation with fear and trembling, *for* it is God who works in you both to will and to work for his good pleasure."

The grammar makes the human agency the *expression of* the divine agency, not an independent parallel to it. This is the cleanest resolution of the passive/active tension. You work because He is working in you; your work is His work through you.

**Areas of self-sovereignty as coaching targets:**
One of the highest-leverage coaching practices in this domain is identifying the "held territories" — specific areas where a client has not surrendered control to God. These are not necessarily sinful areas; they are often areas where a client has decided they will manage the outcome without trusting God's governance.

**Diagnostic question:**
> *"Where in your life, if you're honest, are you telling God: 'I'll handle this one'? Not because you've decided against Him — but because surrendering this one feels too risky."*

---

##### Sub-Category 3D: Obedience as Love Formation (Not Just Love Expression)

**Theological ground (Fact — textual):** John 14:21 — "Whoever has my commandments and keeps them, he it is who loves me." John does not say obedience *expresses* love. He says obedience *is* love, and it is also *how you are known* as loving. This is a formation claim, not just a behavioral one.

**James KA Smith's liturgy principle (Strong inference, based on Aristotle's virtue ethics):** You form desire by practicing desired acts. Aristotle: you become courageous by doing courageous things before you feel courageous. You become loving by doing loving things before you feel loving. This is counter-intuitive but well-supported: the behavioral act shapes the affective disposition over time. *Liturgy* is Smith's word for habitual practice that forms the body and affections before the mind consciously engages.

**Application:** Specific love-acts practiced consistently — serving without recognition, forgiving when it costs something, giving beyond comfort, praying for enemies — reshape the affective disposition toward love. These are not acts performed to prove love; they are acts performed to form it.

#### DOMAIN 4: LOVE NEIGHBOR AS YOURSELF — The Relational/Social Dimension

##### Sub-Category 4A: The "As Yourself" Problem

For shame-based clients, "love your neighbor as yourself" is a command they cannot execute from the inside out — because their self-regard is negative. The traditional interpretation is that self-love is assumed, not commanded — Jesus is using it as a reference point, not an instruction. **Strong inference:** Most NT scholars, including DA Carson and John Stott, read the "as yourself" as a comparative standard, not a prerequisite to develop.

The coaching application is not "learn to love yourself first." It is: "Receive the same love God extends to you — and relate to your neighbor from that place of received-love." The foundation is God's love, not self-regard.

For clients where self-regard is extremely negative, this is a clinical boundary point: deep shame about self requires therapeutic work, not a command to love themselves more.

---

##### Sub-Category 4B: Love for God and Love for Neighbor — The Circular Dependency

**Fact:** 1 John 3-4 explicitly makes love for God and love for brother co-constitutive:
- 1 John 4:20: "Whoever does not love his brother whom he has seen cannot love God whom he has not seen."
- 1 John 3:17: "If anyone has the world's goods and sees his brother in need, yet closes his heart against him, how does God's love abide in him?"

The sequence is not *first internal, then relational.* It is simultaneous and mutually reinforcing: obedience to the neighbor-love command produces and deepens love for God; growth in love for God produces more genuine love for neighbor.

**Strong inference:** This is also what the behavioral activation research suggests (Post's *Altruism Research*): acts of love toward others produce neurological and psychological changes in the giver, including increased meaning, increased positive affect, and reduced self-focus. The act shapes the person performing it.

**Application:** Do not build this framework so that clients spend all their time in the interior love-work and delay the neighbor-love work until they "feel more love internally." The neighbor-love *is* the interior work.

---

##### Sub-Category 4C: Social Engagement as Love Formation

**Practices:**
- Deliberate acts of service that exceed convenience (not what's easy — what costs something)
- Conflict engagement rather than avoidance — reconciliation is the concrete form of neighbor-love most often avoided
- Forgiveness work — both offering and receiving — the most emotionally demanding form of love
- Hospitality: opening your space, time, and resources to someone outside your immediate circle

#### THE DUAL AGENCY MAP — Final Architecture

| What the Human Does | What the Spirit Does | The Interface |
|---|---|---|
| Attends (prayer, Scripture, worship) | Produces encounter | The attention creates conditions; the Spirit fills them |
| Removes obstacles (repentance, lament, forgiveness) | Heals wounds, liberates from bondage | Human identifies and clears; Spirit transforms what's uncovered |
| Creates conditions (solitude, fasting, gratitude) | Produces fruit (Gal 5:22: love, joy, peace...) | Conditions don't cause the fruit — they stop choking it |
| Practices love-acts (obedience, service, forgiveness) | Forms the person through the practice | The body performing the act is the Spirit's instrument of formation |
| Surrenders the will (active presentation — Romans 12:1) | Works in the will to will (Phil 2:13) | Surrender is the act; the Spirit is the power received through it |
| Identifies held territories (the honest accounting) | Initiates the sanctification of what's surrendered | Human confession of what's held creates the opening |

**The master principle:** The human does not produce love. The human removes what blocks love, creates conditions for love, and practices love-acts that form the person into someone capable of greater love. The Spirit produces the transformation, the fruit, and the power. The cooperation is not 50/50. It is 100/100: complete dependence and complete engagement, simultaneously.
