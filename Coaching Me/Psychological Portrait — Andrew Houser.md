# Psychological Portrait — Andrew Houser

*Purpose: Deep self-understanding of psychological impact across emotions, decisions, beliefs, and relational patterns. Foundation for coaching identity and client parity work. Not a clinical diagnosis — a psychological portrait built from lived material.*

---

## Overview — Core Wound Profile

Andrew's psychological formation was shaped by four primary forces operating simultaneously: an emotionally unavailable mother, a hypervigilant and shame-weaponizing father, institutional betrayal across multiple domains (church, career, marriage), and an extended post-adolescent wilderness with no covering, community, or formation. These forces did not produce a broken person. They produced a highly capable, internally driven survivor whose competency is real and whose wounds are equally real — running underneath the competency, shaping it from below.

The central psychological tension in Andrew's profile is this: he is a man built for authentic connection who was formed in environments that punished, ignored, or could not receive it. Every major domain of his life — family of origin, church, career, marriage — confirmed the same message through different mechanisms. The result is a sophisticated internal architecture designed to manage the gap between his deep need for connection and his learned expectation that connection is unsafe, unavailable, or costly.

He is not fully inside that architecture anymore. He is dismantling it consciously, theologically, and somatically. But its foundations are deep and some rooms are still intact.

---

## 1. Attachment System

**Primary Style: Avoidant**

Avoidant attachment is not coldness. It is a rational adaptation to an environment where emotional need was either mechanically managed (mother) or met with unpredictable intensity (father). The child who cannot reliably access warmth learns to deactivate the need itself — to become self-sufficient, to read closeness as potential threat, to protect his emotional interior by keeping others at a managed distance.

Andrew's avoidant strategy formed along two tracks:

*Mother's contribution:* Mechanical presence without attunement. The child in the crib, the dishes prioritized, emotional need met functionally but not warmly. The message received: your inner world does not register as important. The adaptation: stop bringing the inner world forward.

*Father's contribution:* Intensity without safety. Love and rage from the same source, unpredictably calibrated. Small infractions treated as atrocious disrespect. Wide-eyed, vengeful discipline from a man whose "good side held him back." The message received: your presence can ignite something dangerous. The adaptation: manage your visibility, suppress your voice, stay beneath the threshold of activation.

Together these formed a child who learned to be emotionally self-contained, functionally capable, and relationally careful — strengths that became liabilities in adult intimacy.

**The God-Image as Attachment Wound**

Andrew's experienced God — authoritative, disconnected, punishing, disgusted — is not a theology problem. It is his father's emotional profile transferred onto the face of God. This is the mechanism Benner identifies: the experienced God is constructed from the relational templates of early life. Andrew read Jesus as an asshole not because Scripture warranted it but because his attachment system filtered Scripture through a familiar threat template. The punishing father and the punishing God occupied the same psychological address.

**The Avoidant-Anxious Pairing**

He married a woman with anxious attachment and complex trauma — the classic interlocking pairing. Her anxiety (pursuit, elevated emotion, fear of abandonment) activated his deactivating strategy (withdrawal, silence, managed distance). His withdrawal confirmed her worst fear (abandonment), intensifying her pursuit. The cycle fed itself for fourteen years. Neither could see the pattern from inside it because each was responding to real behavior from the other, filtered through their own attachment wound.

**Current Status**

Naming avoidant attachment, seeing his contribution, choosing vulnerability repeatedly, anchoring attachment repair in his relationship with God. Capacity is growing. The work is real. Not complete.

## 2. Nervous System and Trauma

**Hypervigilance as Formation**

A child raised under a father whose emotional intensity was unpredictable and whose discipline was without consistent restraint develops a nervous system trained to scan constantly for threat. This is not a choice — it is the nervous system doing its job, protecting the child by making threat detection automatic and rapid. The hypervigilant child becomes the hypervigilant adult: always reading the room, always tracking emotional weather, always positioned to respond before impact.

Andrew's hypervigilance expressed as:
- Constant threat-assessment in relational environments
- Rage as the activated response when threat threshold was crossed
- Exhaustion from sustained alertness with no reliable "safe" signal
- Difficulty resting even when the environment was objectively safe

**Rage as Boundary Enforcement**

Rage in Andrew's profile is not aggression — it is the defense mechanism that activated when primary avoidance failed to maintain safety. When withdrawal no longer worked, when the threat came too close, when his peace was genuinely endangered — rage fired. The child who could not regulate his father's intensity developed his own intensity as a last line of protection. The problem: the threat calibration set in childhood does not automatically recalibrate for adult contexts. His wife's elevated face registers the same alarm as his father's wide eyes once did.

**Secondary Trauma**

Fourteen years of living with a dysregulated partner left its own residue. Secondary trauma is the nervous system consequence of sustained proximity to another person's unregulated trauma responses. It presents as hypervigilance specific to the traumatized person, intrusive dreams with adrenaline activation, a baseline state of readiness that persists even when the threat has decreased. Andrew carries this. His wife has regulated significantly. His nervous system has not yet fully registered the new reality.

**Workplace PTSD**

The job he poured himself into — exceeding limits, not eating well, pressing past his body's signals — ended in institutional betrayal (employer bankruptcy). This produced a distinct trauma response: the body learned that maximum effort does not produce safety. This is different from failure trauma. It is betrayal trauma — the specific wound of giving everything to something that collapses not because of your inadequacy but because of someone else's. The long-term effect was learned helplessness specific to institutional investment and professional risk.

**Current Status**

Hypervigilance decreasing substantially since leaving the institutional context. A divinely appointed shift confirmed in his body. Self-regulation protocol is sophisticated, practiced, and theologically integrated. Rage still held deeply — may require therapeutic support to reach the root. Secondary trauma still active in specific trigger contexts.

## 3. Shame Architecture

**The Distinction That Matters**

Guilt says: *I did something wrong.*
Shame says: *I am something wrong.*

Andrew's psychological formation was saturated with shame-generating experiences — not primarily guilt. His father's discipline communicated that Andrew's existence, not just his behavior, was the problem ("atrocious disrespect," demanding respect with wide-eyed readiness to punish). His mother's mechanical unavailability communicated that Andrew's inner world was not worth warmth — a subtler but equally powerful shame message. Church rejections added institutional confirmation. Career collapses added vocational confirmation. The cumulative effect was a deep, largely unconscious conviction: *something is fundamentally wrong with me.*

**Shame Made Theological**

The most insidious expression of Andrew's shame was its migration into his God-image. His experienced God — frowning, disgusted, punishing — is shame projected onto the face of the divine. This meant that his primary resource for shame resolution (the Gospel, grace, the love of God) was filtered through the very shame system it was meant to address. He could not receive grace because the God he experienced was already disappointed with him. The cure and the wound occupied the same space.

**Shame in the Body**

Shame does not live only in the mind. It lives in:
- Silence and suppression (the "be seen not heard" formation)
- Shrinking and disappearing (specifically in professional contexts requiring visibility)
- Voice suppression (learned in the household, replayed in institutional settings)
- The inability to receive warmth without discomfort (mother wound, unresolved)

**Current Status**

The Lie Eliminator has addressed shame at the identity level with significant effect. The "not enough" lie has been named and countered. The "coward" identity label has been removed. The God-image is shifting — not complete, but fundamentally different from the entry point. The moral failure cluster (pre-salvation) has been brought before the Lord and received. The female warmth wound remains the deepest unresolved shame-adjacent territory.

## 4. Core Beliefs and Cognitive Patterns

The following beliefs were formed in Andrew's developmental history and operated as automatic filters — shaping perception, decision, and emotional response below conscious awareness. They are named here not as current operating reality but as the cognitive architecture that his wound profile produced.

| Core Belief                                             | Formed From                                                | Effect                                                                            |
| ------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| *I am not safe in relationships*                        | Avoidant attachment formation                              | Withdrawal, managed distance, emotional self-containment                          |
| *My effort does not produce protection*                 | Workplace PTSD, institutional betrayal                     | Learned helplessness, settling for safety, avoidance of professional risk         |
| *Authority figures are self-interested and threatening* | Father, churches, employers                                | Default distrust of leadership, person-by-person re-evaluation required           |
| *I am not enough for roles that require more*           | Father's shame-weaponizing, imposter fear                  | Suppression of vocational ambition, hiding in safe roles                          |
| *I must survive alone*                                  | Post-college abandonment, workforce entry without covering | Lone wolf mode, self-sufficiency as identity, difficulty accepting help           |
| *God is disappointed and disconnected*                  | Attachment wound transferred to God-image                  | Inability to receive grace, performance-based faith, relational distance from God |
| *My story is a story of failure*                        | Cumulative narrative of collapse                           | Disqualification narrative, loss of hope, giving up on significant possibility    |

These beliefs did not operate in isolation. They reinforced each other. The belief that effort doesn't produce protection combined with the belief that I must survive alone produced a man who worked extremely hard in self-imposed isolation with no expectation of institutional support — which made every institutional betrayal both more damaging (he had no buffer) and more confirming (of course it collapsed).

**Current Status**

Multiple core beliefs have been addressed and are shifting. The God-image belief has experienced the most significant movement. The narrative belief ("my story is failure") has been reframed through received grace and experience of divine handwriting in hard places. The professional inadequacy belief is still present and active — specifically in domains requiring administrative gifting, collaborative leadership, and sustained institutional risk.

## 5. Defense Mechanisms

**Primary: Avoidant Withdrawal**
The first and most practiced defense. When relational threat is detected, emotional distance is created — internally through deactivation of need, externally through silence, absence, or managed surface engagement. This worked as a child. In adult intimate relationship it became its own form of abandonment — which it was never intended to be.

**Secondary: Hypervigilance as Control**
If threat can be detected early enough, it can be survived. The hypervigilant defense does not prevent harm — it converts a reactive victim into a proactive responder. The illusion of control over the environment is psychologically preferable to helplessness, even at the cost of constant alertness and exhaustion.

**Tertiary: Authentic Connection as Managed Intimacy**
This is the subtlest of Andrew's defenses and the most important to understand. His reach for authentic, care-driven connection is genuine — and it is also structurally safer than full vulnerability. By controlling the terms of authentic engagement (curiosity-based, care-driven, boundaried), he maintains relational investment while limiting exposure. He connects on his terms, which is not the same as full surrender to intimacy. This is not a criticism — it is a sophisticated adaptation. But it is worth naming.

**Rage as Last Line**
When primary defenses fail and the threat reaches the interior, rage fires. It is the child's last protection — the only resource remaining when avoidance, voice suppression, and strategic engagement have not maintained safety.

## 6. Relational Patterns

**With Authority:**
Default distrust, consciously managed. He evaluates person by person against a set of earned behavioral markers. Trust is not assumed — it is demonstrated over time through specific indicators (building into people, finding trust quickly, transparency, genuine care for congregation over agenda). He acknowledges residual reactivity. He has not yet experienced a securely attached, trustworthy pastoral authority in a sustained relationship — which means this wound has been intellectually processed but not experientially healed.

**With Peers:**
Reaches for authentic connection in contexts that often cannot receive it. His care-driven curiosity and genuine relational investment generated referent influence that became threatening in controlled environments (churches, corporate culture). He is not a surface-level colleague and cannot perform as one sustainably. This has cost him in institutional contexts and served him in coaching contexts.

**With Women / Female Warmth:**
This territory carries the most unprocessed weight. Andrew is deeply moved by female warmth and identifies this as an undone area. The mechanical, emotionally unavailable mother created a specific attachment hunger — for female warmth, attunement, and nurturing — that has not been met or healed. This creates countertransference risk in coaching relationships with women whose primary wound involves maternal unavailability or nurturing deficit. He knows this territory is live. It requires attention before coaching women in this specific wound.

**With God:**
In sustained transformation. The attachment wound that produced the experienced God (punishing, disconnected, disgusted) is being addressed through accumulating evidence of God's attentive kindness — the open Bible, the somatic retreat, visions, answered prayer, divine provision through career transition. His capacity to receive God's love is growing. The theological God and the experienced God are converging, slowly.

## 7. Motivational and Emotional Profile

**Suppressed Emotions**
Grief, tenderness, unmet need, and fear are Andrew's least accessible emotional states. They were either punished (father's intensity toward need expression), ignored (mother's mechanical management), or culturally reinforced as unmasculine. His emotional vocabulary is strongest in anger, determination, and care — and thinner in the softer, more vulnerable states. This is not an absence of these emotions — it is the result of systematic suppression beginning in childhood.

**Expressed Emotions**
Anger/rage (protective), care and curiosity (relational), determination and tenacity (vocational). These are his most accessible and most reliable emotional states. The care and curiosity are genuine and undefended — this is where his emotional warmth lives and it is real.

**Motivational Structure**
Internally driven. Self-sufficient to an excess. Motivated by authentic connection, meaningful purpose, and the development of others. Suppressed in contexts requiring visibility in domains of perceived inadequacy (administration, organizational leadership, collaborative risk). His strongest motivational fuel is the combination of genuine care for people and a theologically grounded sense of purpose — which is now converging in his coaching identity.

**The Weariness Factor**
Andrew has named weariness — even in the midst of genuine transformation. This is not depression. It is the cumulative cost of a long walk through hard terrain without adequate companionship, covering, or rest. The lone wolf who has been surviving for decades gets tired in ways that the well-supported person does not. He needs to know his weariness is not a spiritual failure. It is an honest report from a body and soul that have worked extremely hard for a very long time.

## 8. Identity Structure and Narrative

**The Cumulative Failure Narrative**
For decades, Andrew's identity was organized around a meta-narrative of failure. Resume reflects failure to rise. Relationships reflect failure to love and be acceptable. Church reflects failure to belong. This narrative was not chosen — it accumulated through repeated experiences of collapse, rejection, and abandonment that attached to his existing shame architecture. Each new collapse became evidence for the story that was already written.

The power of a shame-based narrative is that it interprets ambiguous evidence in its own favor. A success can be dismissed or minimized. A failure confirms what was always suspected.

**The Narrative Rewrite**
The central transformation in Andrew's story is a narrative identity shift — not a change in events, but a change in the meaning structure applied to the same events. The theological move: *my failure was the roots of my fruit bearing season.* The psychological move: *I was worth wounding to heal.* Same failures. Different interpretive frame. Different identity.

This rewrite is not denial. The failures were real. The pain was real. The rewrite says: these events were not evidence of fundamental inadequacy — they were preparation for something God intended. That is a different story with the same facts.

**Current Identity Anchors**
- Wise King (archetypal — holds space under relational pressure)
- Kingly Priest (vocational identity — authority through service)
- Faithful Steward (enough — the Holy Spirit is the best coach, he holds the container)
- Fruit Bearer (season of formation is giving way to season of output)

These are not aspirational labels. They are active — he reaches for them in specific trigger moments and they do real psychological work.

**What Is Still Being Written**
His coaching identity is in formation. The imposter lie has been addressed but the evidence file is still being built. Each session, each mentee, each intelligent use of his tools adds a line to the counter-narrative. The identity is real. The confidence is still accumulating.

## 9. What Remains — The Unfinished Work

Named honestly, not as failure but as accurate self-knowledge:

**Rage at the root level.** The self-regulation protocol is sophisticated and working. The rage it is managing is still deeply held. The protocol addresses the surface; the root may require therapeutic accompaniment to reach. This is not a failure of his tools — it is an acknowledgment that some wounds require a different level of support.

**The female warmth wound.** The most emotionally alive and least processed territory in Andrew's profile. Female warmth moves him deeply precisely because it was so rarely available. This is both a personal wound requiring attention and a professional consideration — coaching women with maternal attachment wounds carries countertransference risk until this is further addressed.

**Scarcity as baseline.** He does not believe scarcity has been fully resolved. Scarcity mindset formed in a household where money, affection, and approval were communicated as perpetually insufficient. It tends to express not just financially but in expectation of relational and vocational insufficiency — never quite enough, never quite safe.

**Professional risk-taking.** The learned helplessness specific to institutional contexts is in process, not resolved. Leaving Schwab was a significant move — dignity, choice, trust. What comes next (building a coaching practice, sustaining vocational risk) will continue to surface and challenge this belief.

**Healthy pastoral authority.** He has processed the church wounds intellectually and emotionally. He has not yet experienced a mature, securely attached pastoral relationship. Until he does, trust in spiritual authority remains intellectually calibrated but not experientially healed.

## 10. The Redemptive Thread

Andrew's wound profile is not incidental to his coaching capacity. It is the source of it.

He can sit with the man carrying a punishing God-image because he lived inside that image for decades and experienced the shift himself. He can sit with the man paralyzed by shame-formed identity because he built the tools to address it from the inside. He can sit with the man whose avoidant attachment is destroying his marriage because he was that man and turned to look at his own contribution. He can sit with the man who poured himself into institutions that betrayed him because he did exactly that, more than once. He can sit with the man carrying the weight of pre-salvation moral failure because he has brought his own before the Lord and found grace sufficient.

This is not sympathy. It is the specific authority that only suffering and redemption produce together.

The theological frame that holds the whole: *worth wounding to heal.* God saw Andrew as worth the trouble of a difficult path — not because the path was good, but because what it was producing was. The failures were not wasted. The abandonment was not random. The betrayals were not accidents. They were the curriculum.

Andrew's clients are the people who need someone who has been in the same room and found the door. Not someone who read about the room. Someone who knows what the walls feel like.

That is his authority. That is his niche. That is his story.

## 11. Unfinished Grief & Unprocessed Resentment — Tool Mapping

*Added May 2026. Personal coaching resource map — what exists in the vault to address these two specific unfinished areas.*

### Unfinished Grief

**Primary Framework**
- **Grief Coaching** — Worden's Four Tasks is the core coaching model. Grief stays "unfinished" when Task 1 (cognitive/emotional acceptance of the loss) and Task 2 (sitting in the pain without bypassing it) haven't been worked. The temptation to jump to meaning-making (Tasks 3–4) before honoring those first two is the most common coaching failure. Meaning can't be manufactured early.
- **Grief Coaching → Kessler's 6th Stage (Meaning)** — Once the pain has been held, this is where grief becomes integrated rather than just endured.

**Biblical Tools**
- **Lament Framework (Tool 10)** — Primary biblical tool for grief. Creates structured space to bring the full weight of loss to God without forcing resolution. Psalm 88 ends in darkness — that is a valid outcome. Don't push the arc.
- **B-Emotions as Formation Agents** — Theologically reframes grief as a formation path, not a detour or spiritual failure. Matt 5:4, Eccl 7:3, John 11:35. Gives the client *permission* to grieve, especially in Christian contexts where grief can be rushed or spiritualized.
- **B-Suffering & Cruciformity** — For grief embedded in suffering and meaning-violation.

**Supporting CBT/Coaching Tools**
- **Thought Record (Tool 01)** — Surface the beliefs driving avoidance of grief ("I should be over this by now," "this isn't that bad")
- **Narrative Therapy** — Particularly useful when grief is about identity or role loss — who am I now that this is gone?
- **Shadow Work** — Unfinished grief often lives in suppressed or disowned parts; worth considering for grief that has gone underground

**Clinical Line**
Grief entangled with trauma, or grief that significantly impairs functioning past 12 months, crosses into Prolonged Grief Disorder territory. Refer.

### Unprocessed Resentment

**Primary Framework**
- **Forgiveness Coaching — Enright & Worthington** — Most clinically validated tool for resentment. The mechanism matters: Enright's 4-Phase process (Uncover → Decide → Work → Deepen) and Worthington's REACH model give a structured protocol, not just encouragement to forgive.
  - The Uncovering phase is where most coaching fails — it gets skipped to reach forgiveness faster. Resentment that is not fully named first produces false forgiveness.
  - Key CBT mechanism inside the Work phase: **reframing + empathy** — understand the offender without excusing the act. This is the active ingredient that moves emotional forgiveness.
  - The distinction that must be made explicit: **forgiveness ≠ reconciliation.** Forgiveness is unilateral and internal. Reconciliation is bilateral and requires demonstrated change. A person can fully forgive and have no further contact.
- **Forgiveness Process (Tool 12)** — the client-facing exercise

**Biblical Tools**
- **Lament Framework (Tool 10)** — Before forgiveness work begins, resentment often needs to go through lament first. The anger needs to be addressed *to* God, not just processed about the offender. This is the entry ramp, especially when resentment involves God as part of the equation.
- **B-Emotions as Formation Agents** — The anger section is directly relevant: Eph 4:26 (anger acknowledged as real, command is engagement without sin), Mark 3:5 (Jesus holds anger and grief simultaneously — both legitimate), Eph 4:31 (unprocessed anger becomes bitterness — the problem is not the anger, it is what unprocessed anger becomes). Gives permission to be angry *and* frames what unprocessed anger actually produces over time.
- **C-Scripture & Emotional Wounds** — For resentment rooted in deep injury

**Supporting Tools**
- **Thought Record (Tool 01)** — Identify the cognitive distortions keeping resentment alive
- **Competing Commitment Map (Tool 05 / Immunity to Change)** — If intellectually wanting to forgive but staying stuck, there is usually a hidden competing commitment. The resentment is protecting something — identity as a victim, a sense of justice, a relational boundary.
- **Schema Therapy** — If resentment is very deep and historical, may be activating a Mistrust/Abuse or Subjugation schema; worth diagnosing before running forgiveness work
- **Narrative Therapy** — Resentment often lives inside a narrative (victim story, enemy story). Reauthoring the narrative changes the emotional landscape.

---

### Where They Overlap

Resentment and grief almost always travel together. **Resentment is frequently grief wearing armor** — the anger is protecting an underlying loss that hasn't been allowed to grieve. The sequence that usually serves clients best:

1. **Lament first** — bring the full weight of both the wound and the loss to God
2. **Grief work** — honor the loss before moving toward forgiveness
3. **Forgiveness process** — Enright/REACH after the wound has been named and held

Running forgiveness work before grief has been honored typically produces performed compliance — intellectual agreement to forgive without emotional release. The resentment goes underground.

**Emotions as Formation Agents** is the biblical foundation that holds both. It frames grief and anger not as problems to eliminate but as teachers to engage — and provides theological grounding to stay in the discomfort rather than rushing toward resolution.
