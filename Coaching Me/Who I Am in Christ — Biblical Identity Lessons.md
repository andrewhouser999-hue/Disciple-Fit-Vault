# Who I Am in Christ — Biblical Identity Lessons

A growing collection of short lessons. Each lesson addresses a common identity misread — a false label, a misapplied text, or a habitual phrase that roots identity in the wrong place — followed by the biblical corrective and the statement that replaces it.

---

## Lesson 1: "Sinner" Is Not My Identity

**The misread:** *"I'm just a sinner saved by grace"* — or citing Paul: *"It is no longer I who sins but sin that dwells within me"* (Rom. 7:17), yet still carrying "sinner" as a defining self-label.

**What Paul actually distinguishes:**

Romans 7:15–25 separates two things:

- **The "I"** — the renewed self, the inner man who wills what is right (v. 21), who delights in God's law (v. 22), who *hates* what sin does through the flesh (v. 15). This self opposes sin; sin acts against it.
- **Sin that dwells in the flesh** — the unredeemed residue in embodied existence, operating against the true self until glorification (Rom. 8:23).

Paul's point is that sin is the intruder, not the expression of who you are. The "I" he names has been changed by regeneration.

**The biblical corrective — Romans 6–8:**

- *"Reckon yourselves dead to sin and alive to God in Christ Jesus."* (Rom. 6:11) — λογίζομαι: count as true what God has declared true. This is an active refusal to let the flesh define you.
- *"You who were once slaves of sin have become... slaves of righteousness."* (Rom. 6:17–18) — past tense; transfer of masters is complete.
- *"There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus."* (Rom. 8:1) — condemnation is gone. "Sinner" as a permanent category carries legal weight that no longer stands.
- *"If anyone is in Christ, he is a new creation. The old has passed away; behold, the new has come."* (2 Cor. 5:17)
- *"You were washed, you were sanctified, you were justified."* (1 Cor. 6:11) — all past tense; the old category has been replaced.

**What about 1 John 1:8?**

*"If we say we have no sin, we deceive ourselves."* — John is requiring honest acknowledgment of specific sinful acts, not assigning "sinner" as an identity category. The same letter says: *"No one born of God makes a practice of sinning, for God's seed abides in him."* (1 John 3:9). These are not in conflict — they operate at different levels:

- *I sin* → confession of behavior, requiring cleansing (1 John 1:9)
- *I am a sinner* → a claim about essence — covenantally false in Christ

**The replacement statement:**

> I am a saint who still sins. I am not a sinner who sometimes does good.

**Why this matters:**

Identifying as "a sinner" anchors identity in the flesh — reading who you are from residual behavior rather than from covenant standing. Paul calls this a failure of reckoning: refusing to count as true what God has declared true in Christ. The popular phrase "just a sinner saved by grace," however well-meaning, treats the old self as the baseline and grace as an add-on. Paul's logic runs the opposite direction: the new self in Christ is the baseline; remaining sin is the intruder.

## Lesson 2: "It Is No Longer I Who Live" Does Not Mean I Am Erased

**The misread:** *"It is no longer I who live, but Christ who lives in me"* (Gal. 2:20) read as the abolition of personal identity — as if the self is dissolved into Christ, leaving no "I" with agency, preferences, desires, or will. Practically: *"I shouldn't assert my own wants or needs; I'm just a vessel."*

**What Paul actually says — phrase by phrase:**

*"I have been crucified with Christ"* (συνεσταύρωμαι — Greek perfect passive): A completed past event with ongoing present effect. The self that was crucified is the old Adamic self — the self defined by the flesh, by law-performance as the ground of standing, by the old age. This is the same "old self" of Romans 6:6. Not Andrew Houser as a person; the old-self-defined-by-sin-and-law as a governing system.

*"It is no longer I who live"*: The "I" that no longer lives is that old self — the self under sin's dominion and the law's condemnation. Paul does not mean that the person is annihilated. He is writing this letter. He argues, chooses, suffers, rejoices, and asserts throughout Galatians with unmistakable personal force. The old animating principle is gone; the person is not.

*"But Christ who lives in me"*: The indwelling Christ by the Spirit (cf. Rom. 8:9–11) is the new animating source. Before regeneration: the flesh was the operating center. Now: Christ is. This is transformation of the source, not erasure of the person.

*"And the life I now live in the flesh I live by faith"*: Paul still says *"the life I now live."* He still lives. He still acts. "In the flesh" here means in bodily existence — not the sinful flesh of Romans 7, but mortal embodied life. And he lives it actively: *by faith*. This is not passivity; it is directed, intentional agency flowing from union with Christ.

*"In the Son of God, who loved me and gave himself for me"*: The identity anchor is explicit and personal. Christ did not give himself for humanity in the abstract — he gave himself *for me*. This is not depersonalization; it is the most personal statement in the letter. You are specifically loved and specifically purchased.

**The biblical corrective:**

- *"For we are his workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand, that we should walk in them."* (Eph. 2:10) — Workmanship implies a distinct, crafted self with particular works to do; not a dissolved vessel.
- *"But by the grace of God I am what I am, and his grace toward me was not in vain. On the contrary, I worked harder than any of them, though it was not I, but the grace of God that is with me."* (1 Cor. 15:10) — Paul holds both: "I worked" and "it was the grace of God." He does not collapse them into one. The person acts; Christ empowers.
- *"I can do all things through him who strengthens me."* (Phil. 4:13) — The "I" who does things is still present; Christ is the strength, not the replacement.

**The distinction:**

| Old self (crucified) | New self (alive in Christ) |
|---|---|
| Animated by the flesh | Animated by the indwelling Christ |
| Standing before God earned by performance | Standing before God secured by union |
| Identity derived from sin, law, and failure | Identity derived from covenant love and purchase |
| Self-generated striving | Active faith from an indwelt source |

The crucifixion removes the old animating center. It does not remove you.

**The replacement statement:**

> The self that sin and law defined has been crucified. What lives now is me — transformed, indwelt, and loved specifically by Christ. I act; Christ empowers. I live; Christ is the source.

**Why this matters:**

The "vessel" misread produces two opposite errors: passivity ("I shouldn't act — Christ should act") and self-erasure ("my desires and personality are obstacles to Christ's work in me"). Both are wrong. Paul's most vigorous letter was written by the same man who said Christ lives in him. The point of Galatians 2:20 is not the disappearance of Andrew Houser but the displacement of the old governing center — sin, law, flesh — by a new one: the living Christ. The person who remains is more fully himself, not less, because the distorting center has been removed.

## Lesson 3: "No Condemnation" Is a Verdict, Not a Feeling — and It Does Not Fluctuate

**The misread:** Romans 8:1 is known but not reckoned. A believer can quote *"There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus"* while functionally living as condemned — approaching God only after getting right first, reading failure as evidence of God's displeasure, treating conviction as condemnation, or living under chronic guilt that no amount of repentance fully clears.

**What "condemnation" actually means:**

The Greek κατάκριμα is a legal verdict — guilty, with attendant punishment. It is not a feeling of guilt; it is the judicial sentence itself. Paul uses the same word in Romans 5:

- *"The judgment following one trespass brought condemnation."* (Rom. 5:16)
- *"One trespass led to condemnation for all men."* (Rom. 5:18)

That is the Adamic verdict: the sentence of guilt and death that entered through Adam and applied to all. Romans 8:1 is the explicit reversal of that sentence for those in Christ. The verdict is not reduced or suspended — it is gone.

**The "therefore" — what it connects to:**

The word "therefore" (ἄρα νῦν — lit. *so then now*) is doing legal work. It draws a conclusion from everything Paul has argued:

- Romans 5: justification by faith — peace with God, reconciliation, the Adamic condemnation reversed
- Romans 6: death to sin, transfer of masters
- Romans 7: even in the midst of ongoing flesh-conflict, the believer is not condemned

That last point is critical. Paul does not say "there is no condemnation once the Romans 7 conflict is resolved." He says it *in the middle of* that conflict. The verdict stands alongside the battle, not after it.

**The "now" (νῦν) — present tense, present reality:**

Not *"will be"* at glorification. Not *"was"* at justification only. Is — now — for those in Christ. The condemnation that was the Adamic inheritance belongs to the old age. The believer has been transferred into the new.

**The "in Christ Jesus" — location determines standing:**

The condition is not behavioral performance. It is union — being *in Christ Jesus*. You do not earn "no condemnation" by sinning less or repenting more thoroughly. Your standing is determined by where you are: in Christ. This is why failure does not re-activate the verdict. The verdict belongs to those outside Christ; you are not outside Christ.

**The conviction / condemnation distinction:**

The Spirit convicts (John 16:8) — he brings specific sin to awareness and draws the believer toward repentance. This is not condemnation. The difference:

- **Conviction** — Spirit-initiated, specific, leads toward repentance and restoration, does not call into question your standing
- **Condemnation** — verdict of guilt with punishment, calls your standing into question, produces shame that drives away from God rather than toward him

Condemnation says: *you are guilty and God is against you.* Conviction says: *you have sinned; turn, and find grace.* Romans 8:1 eliminates the first. It does not eliminate the second. Conflating them produces a believer who either numbs conviction (to avoid the shame of condemnation) or who cannot distinguish the Spirit's call to repentance from an accusation about their standing.

**The supporting texts:**

- *"Who shall bring any charge against God's elect? It is God who justifies. Who is to condemn? Christ Jesus is the one who died — more than that, who was raised — who is at the right hand of God, who indeed is interceding for us."* (Rom. 8:33–34) — Paul asks who could successfully lodge a condemnation verdict. The answer: no one. The Judge has already justified; the Advocate is interceding.
- *"Whoever hears my word and believes him who sent me has eternal life. He does not come into judgment, but has passed from death to life."* (John 5:24) — past tense: already passed.
- *"Let us then with confidence draw near to the throne of grace, that we may receive mercy and find grace to help in time of need."* (Heb. 4:16) — the throne is a throne of grace, not a throne of condemnation. You do not need to get right before approaching it.

**The replacement statement:**

> The verdict is not-guilty, and it does not change when I fail. I may be convicted — called by the Spirit toward repentance — but I am never condemned. The throne I approach after failure is the same throne of grace I approach at any other time.

**Why this matters:**

Performance-based approach to God is one of the most common identity distortions in believers, and it almost always rests on a functional belief that condemnation is still operative — that failure puts you back under God's adverse verdict until you sufficiently repent or perform. Romans 8:1 dismantles this at the foundation. The verdict has been rendered. It is not provisional. It is not re-opened by sin. Living as though it is re-opened is not humility; it is a failure to reckon what God has declared in Christ — the same failure Paul diagnoses in Romans 6:11.

## Lesson 4: "New Creation" Is a Covenantal Declaration, Not an Experiential Report

**The misread:** *"Therefore, if anyone is in Christ, he is a new creation. The old has passed away; behold, the new has come."* (2 Cor. 5:17) read as a claim about interior experience — that the believer should no longer struggle with old patterns, old desires, or old responses, because the old has passed away. When those things persist, the conclusion is either that the verse isn't really true for me, that I haven't yet had the right experience or breakthrough, or that something is wrong with my faith.

**What "new creation" (καινὴ κτίσις) actually means:**

Paul is drawing on Jewish apocalyptic language, not psychological categories. In the Jewish background he writes from, the "new creation" is the new age — the eschatological renewal of all things that God would bring at the end of history, reversing the effects of the Fall and inaugurating the world as it was meant to be. Isaiah's "new heavens and new earth" is the conceptual background (Isa. 65:17).

Paul's claim is that the death and resurrection of Christ has *inaugurated* that new creation — the new age has broken into the present. To be "in Christ" is to be located in the sphere of the new creation, participating in the age to come even while still living in the present body in the present world. This is cosmological and covenantal, not primarily psychological. The new creation is bigger than your inner life; it is the age you now belong to.

**The context — what grounds the declaration (vv. 14–16):**

Paul doesn't drop this statement from nowhere. The logic:

- Christ died for all → all died (v.14) — the old humanity died with him
- Therefore we no longer live for ourselves but for him (v.15)
- Therefore *"we regard no one according to the flesh"* (v.16) — kata sarka, by worldly, performance-based, external categories

Then: *therefore* — new creation. The declaration flows from the change of age and the change of epistemology. We no longer know *anyone* — including ourselves — according to the flesh. We know ourselves in light of what Christ has accomplished and the age we now inhabit.

**What "the old has passed away" refers to — and what it does not:**

"The old" (τὰ ἀρχαῖα) is an age-category, not a personal-history category. What has passed away:

- The old Adamic standing — defined by sin, law, and condemnation
- The old-age governing principle — the flesh as the source and center
- Knowing people (including yourself) kata sarka — by external, worldly, performance-based categories
- Your old position: outside Christ, under the Adamic verdict

What "the old has passed away" does *not* mean:

- Your memories are erased
- Your wounds are instantly healed
- Your characteristic patterns of response are gone
- Your history no longer shapes you
- You should not struggle

These are not what Paul's category of "old" refers to. Applying this verse to those things produces a spirituality of denial — pretending wounds don't exist because "the old passed away" — or a crisis of faith when old patterns resurface.

**The already / not yet frame:**

The new creation is inaugurated, not complete. Paul says so explicitly in the same letter and the same letter collection:

- *"We ourselves, who have the firstfruits of the Spirit, groan inwardly as we wait eagerly for adoption as sons, the redemption of our bodies."* (Rom. 8:23) — we groan. The new creation doesn't eliminate groaning; it reframes its meaning.
- *"The creation itself will be set free from its bondage to corruption."* (Rom. 8:21) — the new creation is still coming to fullness.
- *"For in this tent we groan, longing to put on our heavenly dwelling."* (2 Cor. 5:2) — Paul, in the same chapter, acknowledges ongoing groaning.

The new creation is already your identity and standing. Its full experiential realization awaits glorification. Ongoing struggle is not evidence against new creation; it is the normal experience of living as a new-creation person in a not-yet-fully-redeemed body in a not-yet-fully-redeemed world.

**The epistemological shift — no longer kata sarka:**

Verse 16 is the key to reading verse 17 correctly. We no longer know anyone "according to the flesh." Applied to yourself: you no longer know yourself by what the flesh reports. The flesh reports performance, failure, patterns, history, wounds. Knowing yourself *kata sarka* is reading identity from those reports. The new creation changes the vantage point: you know yourself in light of what Christ has accomplished and what God has declared about you in him.

This is precisely what the false self does — it reads identity from the flesh-reports: what I've done, what's been done to me, how I perform, how I'm perceived. The new creation doesn't deny those reports exist; it changes what they are *allowed to determine*.

**The supporting texts:**

- *"For neither circumcision counts for anything, nor uncircumcision, but a new creation."* (Gal. 6:15) — new creation is the only category that matters in the new age; external categories have been displaced.
- *"Put off your old self, which belongs to your former manner of life... be renewed in the spirit of your minds... put on the new self, created after the likeness of God."* (Eph. 4:22–24) — the new self is already created (declarative: "created after the likeness of God"), and is also being put on (process: "be renewed"). Both are true simultaneously.
- *"And we all, with unveiled face, beholding the glory of the Lord, are being transformed into the same image from one degree of glory to another."* (2 Cor. 3:18) — transformation is ongoing (present tense), not completed. New creation is both status and trajectory.

**The replacement statement:**

> I am a new creation — not because I feel new in every moment or no longer struggle, but because I am in Christ, who inaugurated the new age. The old Adamic standing, the old governing center, the old epistemology of knowing myself by flesh-reports — these have passed away. What has come is my place in the new creation: a different standing, a different source, a different way of being known. The full experience of that awaits glorification; the reality is now.

**Why this matters:**

Collapsing "new creation" into an experiential claim produces one of two errors. Either the believer chases a subjective experience of newness they believe they're supposed to have (and haven't yet received), or they dismiss the verse as aspirational future-language that doesn't describe them now. Both errors misread the genre of the declaration. Paul is not describing what you feel; he is declaring what is true about your standing, belonging, and the age you inhabit. The corrective is to ask not *"Do I feel new?"* but *"In whom am I? What age do I belong to? By what standard am I now known?"* The answers to those questions — not the emotional report from the flesh — are what 2 Corinthians 5:17 addresses.
