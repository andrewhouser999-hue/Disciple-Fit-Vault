# Lie Eliminator — Schema Recognition Reference
*Personal diagnostic reference. Not a clinical assessment instrument.*

---

## Purpose

This document serves two functions:

**Gap-finding:** Cross-mapping your named lies against the 18 Early Maladaptive Schemas (Young) surfaces schema-territory not yet explicitly named in your Lie Eliminator. Those are candidate lies — not guaranteed active, but worth examining.

**In-operation recognition:** When something is activating but you can't name which lie it is, schemas give you a recognition vocabulary — a characteristic emotional signature, trigger type, and behavioral pattern to match against what you're experiencing.

Read it as a field guide — looking for what fits, not completing a checklist.

---

## Activation & Referral

Some schemas involve early relational deprivation or harm — particularly Abandonment, Mistrust/Abuse, Emotional Deprivation, Defectiveness/Shame, and Enmeshment. When working through a schema activates something that feels larger than reflection can hold — emotional flooding, persistent intrusion, material from a specific early relationship coming up with significant charge — bring it to a therapist rather than working it solo through the Lie Eliminator sequence. The referral is not a ceiling; it is a container recommendation for material that benefits from a relational witness.

## The 18 Schemas

Organized by domain. Each entry: core belief → what it sounds like internally → when it activates → emotional/somatic signature → what it drives behaviorally → gap status → recognition signals.

---

### Domain 1 — Disconnection & Rejection

*Core expectation: needs for security, nurturance, empathy, and belonging will not be met by others.*

---

#### 1. Abandonment / Instability (AB)

**Core belief:** "People I depend on will leave, become unavailable, or be too unstable to count on — connection is temporary."

**Sounds like:** *"They'll pull away eventually. I should pull back first. Don't count on this lasting."*

**Activates when:** perceived emotional distance from someone close; silence read as withdrawal; someone becoming less available or attentive than before.

**Feels like:** panic or urgency disproportionate to what actually happened; a hollow feeling when someone leaves even briefly; preemptive grief before any loss occurs.

**Drives:** clinging or relationship-testing to confirm security, or pre-emptive withdrawal to beat the other person to leaving; relational intensity that pushes people away and confirms the belief.

**Gap status:** *God-directed version covered by "Lie of Abandoned by God." The horizontal version — expecting people to leave — is a gap.*

**Recognition signals:**
- Feeling destabilized by ordinary distance (unreturned message, cancelled plans, someone seeming less warm than usual)
- Relational intensity or urgency that doesn't match the actual stakes of the situation
- Pattern of either holding on too hard or pulling away first before loss can happen
- Difficulty believing relational security is stable without ongoing confirmation

---

#### 2. Mistrust / Abuse (MA)

**Core belief:** "People will hurt me, use me, manipulate me, or betray me — it is only a matter of when."

**Sounds like:** *"Watch for the angle. They want something. No one does something for nothing. Don't let your guard down."*
For me: "People lack any semblance of commitment or loyalty"

**Activates when:** receiving help or generosity (what do they want?); vulnerability in a relationship; perceived inconsistency or hidden motive in someone's behavior.

**Feels like:** guardedness, low-level suspicion even when nothing is wrong; difficulty relaxing into trust; a readiness that doesn't turn off.

**Drives:** testing people to see if they'll betray, emotional distance as protection, preemptive self-protection, interpreting ambiguous actions as hostile by default.

**Gap status:** *God-directed version covered by "Lie of God as Disconnected Authoritarian." The horizontal version — expecting people to harm or use you — is a gap.*

**Recognition signals:**
- Difficulty receiving help without wondering about the motive behind it
- Pattern of waiting for people to show their true colors — and reading normal ambiguity as evidence of something hidden
- Minor betrayals that confirm the belief disproportionately (it finally proved itself)
- Anger or withdrawal when someone gets too close without having been tested

---

#### 3. Emotional Deprivation (ED)

**Core belief:** "No one will truly understand me, nurture me, or protect me — I will always be essentially alone inside."

Three forms:
- *Nurturance deprivation:* absence of warmth, care, and attention
- *Empathy deprivation:* no one truly listens or understands the inner world
- *Protection deprivation:* no strong presence to lean on, guide, or stand with you

**Sounds like:** *"No one actually knows what this is like. I'm on my own with this. There's no point in saying it — it won't land."*

**Activates when:** being with others and not feeling truly known; experiencing pain in silence; needing emotional support and not asking, because you already expect it won't be there.

**Feels like:** a quiet sadness or emptiness in relationships, even good ones; longing without a clear object; resignation; occasional eruption of anger at not being seen.

**Drives:** not asking for what is needed emotionally (expecting deprivation to be confirmed), suppression of emotional needs, chosen self-sufficiency, resentment that builds from unacknowledged longing.

**Gap status:** *Gap. "Scarcity" covers material provision. Relational-emotional deprivation — not being known, nurtured, or protected by others — is not explicitly named. A specific lie entry may be worth adding.*

**Recognition signals:**
- Feeling alone even when surrounded by people who genuinely care
- Difficulty asking directly for emotional support — defaulting instead to managing alone
- Pattern of giving care generously while not receiving it, without noticing this as a problem
- A quiet resignation in relational contexts rather than expectation or hope

---

#### 4. Defectiveness / Shame (DS)

**Core belief:** "I am fundamentally flawed at my core — bad, inferior, unlovable. If anyone really knew me, they would reject or abandon me."

*Distinction from guilt: guilt is "I did something bad." Defectiveness/Shame is "I am something bad."*

**Sounds like:** *"If they knew or could see the real me — what I'm actually like underneath — they'd pull away. I have to manage how I come across."*

**Activates when:** criticism; intimacy that requires real self-exposure; situations where the true self might be visible; comparison.

**Feels like:** acute shame (the desire to disappear, feeling exposed at the worst possible level), hypersensitivity to perceived criticism, chronic self-consciousness.

**Drives:** hiding and concealment of true self; perfectionism as a strategy to prevent exposure; hypersensitivity to perceived judgment; attacking self before others can.

**Gap status:** *Well-covered. This is your most addressed schema domain — present in "I Am Not Enough / Imposter," "Too Connected to Sinful Heritage," "Rejectable," "Coaching Imposter," and "Hidden Light." Defectiveness/Shame as content is not a gap; it may have specific expressions not yet named.*

**Recognition signals:**
- Shame response (wanting to disappear) rather than guilt (wanting to repair) when you fail
- Chronic management of how you come across, even with people who have shown they're safe
- Difficulty receiving genuine compliments without deflecting or immediately discounting them
- Internal critic that attacks your character and being, not just your behavior

---

#### 5. Social Isolation / Alienation (SI)

**Core belief:** "I am fundamentally different from other people. I don't belong to any group or community. I am an outsider everywhere."

*Distinction from Defectiveness/Shame: DS is "I am bad and will be rejected." SI is "I am simply different — not of the same kind as others." The alienation is categorical, not moral.*

**Sounds like:** *"I don't quite fit here. They have something I don't have. I can be in the room without being in it."*

**Activates when:** group contexts; watching others connect naturally and easily; being in social environments where belonging is visible.

**Feels like:** a pane of glass between you and others; going through the motions of social participation; loneliness in the middle of a crowd; a persistent sense of watching from outside.

**Drives:** avoidance of group settings, surface engagement without real presence, maintaining a persona rather than showing self, a private sense of difference that is not disclosed to others.

**Gap status:** *Gap. Not on your list.*

**Recognition signals:**
- Feeling like an observer in social situations, including ones you navigate successfully
- Sense that others have access to natural belonging that doesn't come naturally to you
- Ease in one-on-one that disappears or diminishes in group settings
- Adapting presentation to fit in rather than being yourself — not dishonestly, but instinctively

### Domain 2 — Impaired Autonomy & Performance

*Core expectation: inability to function independently or perform successfully in the world.*

---

#### 6. Dependence / Incompetence (DI)

**Core belief:** "I am not capable of handling everyday responsibilities on my own — I am fundamentally incompetent without significant help."

**Sounds like:** *"I can't manage this. I need someone to tell me what to do. What if I get it badly wrong?"*

**Activates when:** new tasks without clear guidance; situations requiring independent judgment; operating without a competent authority figure nearby.

**Feels like:** helplessness, anxiety about competence, relief when someone else takes charge.

**Drives:** excessive reassurance-seeking, over-reliance on others for decisions, avoidance of responsibility, paralysis when support is unavailable.

**Gap status:** *Covered. "Lie of I Cannot Do It" and "Lie of It Cannot/Will Not Be Done Through Me" address this schema's content.*

**Recognition signals:**
- Difficulty making decisions without checking with someone else first
- Feeling more competent following someone else's direction than operating independently
- Anxiety when beginning something new without a clear roadmap
- Relief when someone else steps in — even at a cost to your own autonomy

---

#### 7. Vulnerability to Harm or Illness (VH)

**Core belief:** "Something could go catastrophically wrong at any moment — I am in constant danger of being overwhelmed by disaster I cannot handle."

Three forms:
- *Medical:* fear of sudden illness or physical catastrophe
- *Emotional:* fear of psychological breakdown or losing emotional control
- *External:* fear of financial collapse, accidents, crime, or circumstances spiraling out of control

**Sounds like:** *"What if something goes badly wrong and I can't handle it? I need to stay ready. I can't let my guard down."*

**Activates when:** health symptoms; unexpected bad news; situations with unclear outcomes; any loss of control over external circumstances.

**Feels like:** low-grade chronic dread; hypervigilance; inability to fully rest; waiting for the other shoe to drop even when things are going well.

**Drives:** excessive caution, worst-case research, checking and reassurance-seeking, over-preparation, avoidance of anything that carries risk of going wrong.

**Gap status:** *Gap. Not explicitly on your list.*

**Recognition signals:**
- Difficulty being at rest when circumstances feel uncertain or unresolved
- Preparing for catastrophic scenarios more than the situation actually warrants
- Inability to fully inhabit good moments — "this won't last" runs underneath them
- Health monitoring or checking behavior that is disproportionate to actual symptoms

---

#### 8. Enmeshment / Undeveloped Self (EM)

**Core belief:** "My identity is so entangled with another person or role that I don't know who I am apart from them — or I cannot function as a genuinely separate self."

**Sounds like:** *"I don't know what I actually think about this, apart from what [person] would think. Who am I if I'm not in this role?"*

**Activates when:** being asked your own opinion after long deference to another; making independent choices; experiencing separation from the person or role the self has been organized around.

**Feels like:** anxiety when making independent decisions; emptiness or identity confusion outside the person or role; feeling suffocated within the relationship but without a clear self to bring to independence.

**Drives:** excessive closeness with parent or partner, difficulty identifying own preferences independently, vicariously living through another, inability to hold a separate opinion under social pressure.

**Gap status:** *Gap. Not on your list. More relevant if early identity formation was strongly shaped by a dominant parental template or external role expectation.*

**Recognition signals:**
- Difficulty answering "what do you want?" without referencing someone else's expectations first
- Identity that feels contingent on a role (pastor, husband, coach, son) — uncertainty about who you are outside it
- Pattern of adopting the opinions, preferences, or values of whoever you're closest to at a given time
- Anxiety when expected to have an independent view or make a significant unilateral choice

---

#### 9. Failure (FA)

**Core belief:** "I have failed and will fail again — I am a failure compared to my peers in areas that matter."

**Sounds like:** *"Others have figured out something I haven't. I'm behind. Eventually this will be exposed."*

**Activates when:** performance contexts; visible comparison with peers; mistakes; attempting something new where failure is publicly possible.

**Feels like:** shame around achievement, dread of trying, a persistent sense of being behind regardless of what has been accomplished.

**Drives:** giving up prematurely to avoid confirmed failure, not attempting visible things, underachievement, or compensatory overachievement that never satisfies because the belief does not update when the standard is met.

**Gap status:** *Partially covered by "Lie of False Risk / I Will Fail" and "Lie of Coaching Imposter." The schema is broader — it can run in domains unrelated to coaching, and it tends to persist even after success.*

**Recognition signals:**
- Discounting accomplishments by comparison ("others have done more, further along")
- Reluctance to attempt visible things where failure is a real possibility
- Feeling behind peers even when evidence does not support the assessment
- Achievement that does not produce satisfaction — the bar moves immediately after success lands

---

### Domain 3 — Impaired Limits

*Core pattern: insufficient internal limits, inadequate responsibility to others, or absence of long-term orientation.*

---

#### 10. Entitlement / Grandiosity (ET)

**Core belief:** "I am special — normal rules and limits do not apply to me; I deserve more or different treatment than others receive."

**Sounds like:** *"Why is this happening to me? I shouldn't have to deal with this. They should recognize what I bring."*

**Activates when:** being treated like everyone else; limits imposed; not receiving recognition proportional to self-perceived contribution.

**Feels like:** frustration or rage at constraint, chronic sense of being underappreciated or misunderstood.

**Drives:** demanding behavior, dominance in conversation, disregard for others' needs when they conflict with one's own, conflict with institutional rules or authority.

**Gap status:** *Not represented in your lies. Based on your document's posture — oriented toward humility, sufficiency from God, and service — this is probably not an active schema domain for you. Assess and skip if it does not resonate.*

**Recognition signals:**
- Frustration that feels unfair specifically when treated like everyone else
- Difficulty accepting limits without an internal sense that they are unjust
- Sense that your problems or contributions are categorically more significant than others'
- Recurring conflict with rules, institutions, or authority figures who "don't understand"

---

#### 11. Insufficient Self-Control / Self-Discipline (IS)

**Core belief:** "I cannot tolerate frustration, discomfort, or delay — self-control is beyond me in this area."

**Sounds like:** *"I just can't keep doing this. The discomfort is too much. I need relief now."*

**Activates when:** tasks requiring sustained effort against discomfort; situations calling for delayed gratification; frustration without available resolution.

**Feels like:** restlessness, irritability, urgency for relief that overrides prior intention.

**Drives:** quitting tasks when they become uncomfortable, impulsive short-term relief choices, avoidance of anything requiring sustained discomfort, emotional outbursts when frustration exceeds threshold.

**Gap status:** *Partially covered by "Lie of Giving Up." The schema is broader — it can appear in any domain where discomfort triggers premature exit, not just quitting a task.*

**Recognition signals:**
- Pattern of beginning things with energy and abandoning when difficulty increases
- Impulsive choices in moments of emotional discomfort that look different in hindsight
- Difficulty staying with an uncomfortable internal state long enough to actually process it
- "I deserve a break" reasoning used to justify exit from something that is actually avoidance

---

### Domain 4 — Other-Directedness

*Core pattern: excessive focus on others' needs, desires, and reactions — at the expense of the self.*

---

#### 12. Subjugation (SB)

**Core belief:** "If I express what I really want, need, or think, I will be punished, controlled, humiliated, or abandoned. My will must be suppressed to keep things safe."

**Sounds like:** *"I can't say that — it will cause problems. Just go along with it. What I want doesn't matter here."*

**Activates when:** conflict or perceived disapproval; situations where asserting your own preference would require standing against someone else's expectation; being asked to comply with something you disagree with.

**Feels like:** anger underneath compliance; a trapped or suffocated feeling in situations where you are expected to go along; resentment that builds slowly without being named.

**Drives:** excessive compliance, inability to say no without significant anxiety, suppression of preferences and genuine opinions, passive-aggressive expression of anger rather than direct expression, rebellion in unrelated areas as an outlet.

**Gap status:** *Gap. Not on your list. High-probability gap given the patterns in your existing lies around approval and relational safety.*

**Recognition signals:**
- Chronic difficulty saying no — not from generosity, but from anxiety about the consequence of refusal
- Pattern of agreeing in the moment and feeling resentment or regret afterward
- Expressing disagreement indirectly or after the fact, rather than in the moment it matters
- Anger that seems disproportionate to its apparent trigger — often it is displaced from earlier suppression

---

#### 13. Self-Sacrifice (SS)

**Core belief:** "My role is to meet others' needs — focusing on my own needs is selfish and will harm others or cause them to disapprove of me."

*Distinction from Subjugation: SS is driven by guilt or responsibility — the giving is voluntary. SB is driven by fear of consequences — compliance is compelled. Both suppress the self, but for different reasons.*

**Sounds like:** *"I can handle it. They need this more than I do. It would be selfish to make this about me right now."*

**Activates when:** someone else is in need; your own needs become visible; opportunities to receive care or attention arise.

**Feels like:** guilt when attending to your own concerns, a hollow emptiness from chronic giving without receiving, virtue attached to depletion.

**Drives:** compulsive prioritization of others' needs, difficulty receiving care or help without deflecting, chronic over-extension, resentment that goes unnamed because naming it would feel selfish.

**Gap status:** *Gap. Not on your list. Particularly relevant in vocational contexts — pastoral and coaching identity can sustain this schema invisibly, framing depletion as faithfulness.*

**Recognition signals:**
- Difficulty receiving care, help, or attention without deflecting or minimizing your own need
- Guilt when taking rest, enjoyment, or focus on your own concerns
- Identity that feels more stable when giving than when receiving
- Exhaustion followed eventually by distance — the unacknowledged resentment creates withdrawal without a named reason

---

#### 14. Approval-Seeking / Recognition-Seeking (AS)

**Core belief:** "My worth depends on the estimation others have of me — I need their approval, admiration, or recognition to feel okay about myself."

*Distinction from Defectiveness/Shame: DS fears rejection of the true self. AS actively seeks positive input as a primary source of worth — even when rejection is not feared. The mechanism is different: one is defensive (avoiding exposure), the other is appetitive (seeking validation).*

**Sounds like:** *"How did that land? Do they think well of me? I need to know they approve."*

**Activates when:** being evaluated, judged, or compared; absence of expected recognition or praise; receiving ambiguous feedback.

**Feels like:** elation at approval that is disproportionate to the situation; deflation at its absence; anxiety before being evaluated even when you have prepared well.

**Drives:** changing yourself to fit others' expectations, excessive attention to status and how you are perceived, performing rather than simply being, seeking validation as a primary rather than secondary motive in decisions.

**Gap status:** *Partial. "Lie of Rejectable" covers the fear of being cast out. Approval-Seeking is a distinct mechanism — it is about actively pursuing positive input as an identity source, independent of rejection fear. The two can run simultaneously but have different roots and different interventions.*

**Recognition signals:**
- Checking how something landed more than whether it was true, right, or faithful
- Mood that tracks closely with expressed approval or disapproval from others
- Identity that feels more stable after recognition and more uncertain in its absence
- Adjusting opinions or presentation based on the audience — not dishonestly, but automatically

---

### Domain 5 — Overvigilance & Inhibition

*Core pattern: suppression of natural feelings, impulses, and responses; internalized rules that prevent spontaneous living.*

---

#### 15. Negativity / Pessimism (NP)

**Core belief:** "Things will go wrong — the negative aspects of experience are more real and lasting than the positive. Expecting good is naive."

*Distinction from Hopelessness: Hopelessness is acute ("there is no future; there is no way out"). Negativity/Pessimism is a chronic baseline orientation — a filter, not a crisis. Good things can be happening while the schema still frames them as temporary or fragile.*

**Sounds like:** *"Don't get too comfortable with this. It will change. Something will go wrong. Better to expect it and not be caught off guard."*

**Activates when:** things are going well (the schema flags the good as temporary); uncertainty; situations where a downside is a genuine possibility.

**Feels like:** chronic low-level dread or worry; difficulty resting inside good moments; hypervigilance to what could go wrong even when nothing is currently wrong.

**Drives:** rumination on worst-case scenarios, underweighting positive evidence, chronic worry used as a form of emotional control ("if I've already imagined it, it won't surprise me"), difficulty celebrating or receiving good things fully.

**Gap status:** *Partial. "Lie of Hopelessness" covers the acute crisis version — no future, no way out. The chronic pessimism baseline — expecting negative as the default orientation toward life — is a gap.*

**Recognition signals:**
- Difficulty staying fully inside good moments without background awareness of how they could end
- More mental energy spent on what could go wrong than on what is currently going well
- Surprise when things turn out well, but not surprise when they don't
- Worry that is present as a near-constant companion, regardless of actual threat level in the environment

---

#### 16. Emotional Inhibition (EI)

**Core belief:** "Expressing emotion is dangerous, shameful, or will overwhelm or harm others — I must keep it under control."

Three forms:
- *Anger inhibition:* anger is too dangerous or destructive to express
- *Positive emotion inhibition:* expressing joy, enthusiasm, or excitement is embarrassing or invites disappointment
- *Grief/vulnerability inhibition:* sadness or pain must not be shown — it is a burden or weakness

**Sounds like:** *"Pull it together. Don't make this emotional. This isn't the time or place. You'll overwhelm them if you let this out."*

**Activates when:** emotional situations that call for expression; intimacy requiring genuine vulnerability; moments of grief, joy, or anger that are appropriate and present.

**Feels like:** emotional flatness or a distancing from your own experience; a pane between you and what you are actually feeling; discomfort watching others express freely.

**Drives:** intellectualizing feelings (analyzing rather than having them), over-controlled presentation, discomfort with others' emotional expression, suppression of spontaneity, being experienced by others as contained, careful, or hard to read.

**Gap status:** *Gap. Not explicitly named. "Hidden Light / Too Little to Offer" touches the inhibition of expression — Emotional Inhibition is likely the broader schema underlying it in some expressions.*

**Recognition signals:**
- Tendency to analyze or explain an emotion to yourself or others rather than staying present in it
- Discomfort in situations that seem to call for vulnerability or visible emotional response
- Spontaneous joy, grief, or anger that gets immediately managed or redirected rather than felt
- Others describing you as contained, controlled, private, or difficult to read emotionally

---

#### 17. Unrelenting Standards / Hypercriticalness (US)

**Core belief:** "I must perform at a very high level at all times — anything less than exceptional is unacceptable, regardless of the cost."

Three forms:
- *Perfectionism:* output must meet a very high internal standard
- *Rigid rules:* specific requirements about how things must be done — the right way
- *Time urgency:* productivity must be maximized; wasting time is intolerable

**Sounds like:** *"This isn't good enough yet. I can't stop — there is still more to do. Why would I rest when this isn't finished?"*

**Activates when:** any performance context; perceived mistakes or substandard output; comparison with an internal ideal that shifts upward when approached.

**Feels like:** relentless internal pressure, chronic dissatisfaction with output even when it is objectively good, difficulty resting even when rest is appropriate and warranted.

**Drives:** excessive effort and time investment, perfectionism, inability to prioritize (everything is urgent), rigidity about process, compulsive reviewing or improving, difficulty receiving the work as finished.

**Gap status:** *Partial. "Lawful Thinking / Ego / Resentment" covers the outward, judgment-toward-others version. The inward Unrelenting Standards — the hypercritical pressure applied to self, the bar that moves — is a gap.*

**Recognition signals:**
- The bar moves immediately after achieving something, before satisfaction can register
- Difficulty distinguishing between what genuinely matters and what is simply not yet perfect
- Rest or leisure that produces guilt or restlessness rather than restoration
- Others can receive your work as good long before you can

---

#### 18. Punitiveness (PU)

**Core belief:** "People who make mistakes — including me — deserve severe punishment. There is no room for grace, understanding, or forgiveness for significant failures."

*Two directions — both may run simultaneously:*
- *Outward Punitiveness:* others deserve punishment for their failures
- *Inward Punitiveness:* I deserve punishment for mine

**Sounds like — outward:** *"They should know better. They don't deserve grace for that. There need to be consequences."*

**Sounds like — inward:** *"I should have known better. I don't deserve to feel okay about this yet. I need to make this right before I can rest."*

**Activates — outward:** others fail, violate a standard, or behave in ways that feel grossly wrong.
**Activates — inward:** personal moral failure, mistakes with consequences, situations where conviction is present but refuses to resolve.

**Feels like — outward:** rage at failure, inability to extend grace, cold clarity about what others deserve.
**Feels like — inward:** self-attack that goes beyond appropriate conviction; guilt that does not resolve into rest; a sense that feeling okay again must be earned, not received.

**Drives — outward:** harsh judgment, withholding grace, anger at weakness in others.
**Drives — inward:** self-punishment extending well past what repentance calls for, inability to receive forgiveness as sufficient for this specific failure, chronic guilt that does not lift.

**Gap status:** *Partial. "Disgust of Others" covers outward Punitiveness. "Lawful Thinking" covers the justice-demand dynamic. The inward self-directed version — punishing self beyond what repentance warrants — is a gap. This is where Romans 8:1 is known in doctrine but not lived in practice.*

**Recognition signals (inward):**
- Conviction that does not resolve into repentance and rest — it keeps returning and re-accusing
- Difficulty believing God's forgiveness is sufficient for this particular failure, even while accepting it in general
- Self-punishment (withdrawal, restriction, extended guilt) as a way of paying for the failure before allowing yourself to feel okay again
- The gap between "there is therefore now no condemnation" (Romans 8:1) as a theological statement and as a lived reality

## How to Use for Gap-Finding

1. Work through the gap status line for each schema.
2. For schemas marked *Gap* or *Partial gap*, ask: "Does any part of this description fit a recurring pattern in me?"
3. If yes — use the recognition signals to confirm it is active. Then write a new lie entry in the Lie Eliminator, or expand an existing one to capture the gap territory.
4. Do not write a lie entry for schemas that do not produce recognition. More named lies is not the goal. More accurate lies is.

## How to Use for In-Operation Recognition

When something is activating and you cannot yet name which lie it is:

1. Match the emotional or somatic signature to a schema domain first — disconnection, autonomy/performance, limits, other-directedness, inhibition.
2. Within that domain, match the *sounds like* voice to what you are hearing internally.
3. Identify the schema. Cross-reference with your named lie list — work an existing lie or flag a new one for naming.
4. Open the Lie Eliminator for the matching lie. If no matching lie exists yet, use the *sounds like* and *core belief* fields above to draft "The Lie I've Believed" and begin the sequence from there.
